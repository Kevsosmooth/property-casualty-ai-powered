Part 4: Other Property Coverages

Flood Insurance and Earthquake Coverage

Overview NFIP Flood Coverage SFHA Zones WYO Program Earthquake Comparison

Why Separate Coverage?

Flood and earthquake are EXCLUDED from standard property insurance policies. These catastrophic perils require separate, specialized coverage due to their concentrated and unpredictable nature.

Exam Alert!

This part covers two completely separate types of coverage: federal flood insurance (NFIP) and private earthquake insurance. They have different rules, different providers, and different characteristics. Pay close attention to which is which!

Catastrophic Losses

Single events can cause billions in damage to thousands of properties simultaneously, making them uninsurable under standard models.

Geographic Concentration

Risks are concentrated in specific areas (floodplains, fault zones), preventing effective risk pooling across diverse locations.

FLOOD INSURANCE

National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)

1. National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)

What is NFIP?

  • Federal program created by Congress in 1968
  • Administered by FEMA (Federal Emergency Management Agency)
  • Provides flood insurance to property owners in participating communities

Why Federal?

Private insurers historically wouldn't offer flood coverage due to catastrophic and concentrated risk. The government stepped in to make coverage available.

Community Participation Requirements

Communities Must:

  • • Participate in NFIP
  • • Adopt floodplain management regulations
  • • Enforce building standards in flood zones

In Exchange:

  • • Residents can buy flood insurance
  • • Federal disaster assistance available
  • • Lower risk through better planning

Real-World Scenario: NFIP Participation

The Setup: Rivertown decides whether to join NFIP. The town has experienced several floods but currently has no flood insurance available to residents.

What Happens: Rivertown agrees to participate, adopts floodplain ordinances requiring elevated construction in flood zones, and enforces strict building codes.

The Result: Residents can now purchase federally-backed flood insurance through private insurers. When the next flood hits, homeowners receive claims payments from NFIP. The town also qualifies for federal disaster aid.

2. NFIP Coverage Details

Building Coverage

• Building structure
• Electrical and plumbing systems
• Central air conditioning
• Furnaces, water heaters
• Permanently installed carpeting
• Built-in appliances (dishwashers, stoves)
• Detached garages
Property Type Building Coverage Contents Coverage
Residential $250,000 $100,000
Commercial $500,000 $500,000

Contents Coverage - SEPARATE Policy!

Contents coverage is NOT automatic. It must be purchased separately from building coverage.

Covers: Personal belongings, furniture, electronics, clothing, portable appliances

What's NOT Covered

✗ Finished basements (limited coverage)
✗ Currency, precious metals
✗ Property outside building
✗ Additional Living Expenses (ALE)
✗ Business interruption losses
✗ Vehicles
✗ Land and land values
✗ Trees, shrubs, plants

Real-World Scenario: Building vs Contents Coverage

The Setup: David owns a home worth $400,000 in a flood zone. He purchases NFIP building coverage for $250,000 (the maximum) but decides to skip contents coverage to save money.

What Happens: A hurricane causes flooding. The house sustains $180,000 in structural damage. All furniture, electronics, and personal belongings are destroyed (worth $60,000).

The Result: NFIP pays $180,000 for the building damage. David receives $0 for contents because he didn't purchase contents coverage separately. He must replace all his belongings out of pocket. Additionally, NFIP doesn't cover hotel costs while repairs are made (no ALE coverage).

3. Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHA)

FEMA creates Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) that designate flood zones based on flood risk. These zones determine insurance requirements and rates.

HIGH-RISK ZONES (Mandatory Insurance)

Zone A

High-risk areas with no base flood elevations determined

Zone AE

High-risk areas with base flood elevations established

Zone V

High-risk coastal areas with wave action (velocity zones)

Zone VE

High-risk coastal with base flood elevations and wave action

MODERATE TO LOW-RISK ZONES (Optional Insurance)

Zone X

Areas of moderate to low flood risk

Zones B & C

Older designations for moderate/low risk areas

Mandatory Purchase Requirement

Flood insurance is REQUIRED for properties with federally-backed mortgages located in SFHA zones (A, AE, V, VE).

Lenders require proof of flood insurance and it must be maintained throughout the entire loan term.

Base Flood ("100-Year Flood")

A flood with a 1% chance of occurring annually. This is the standard used to determine Special Flood Hazard Areas.

Note: "100-year flood" doesn't mean it happens once every 100 years - it means 1% annual probability!

Real-World Scenario: Zone Designation Impact

The Setup: Sarah is buying a $350,000 home with an FHA mortgage (federally-backed). The home is located in Zone AE (high-risk SFHA with established base flood elevation).

What Happens: The lender reviews the FIRM and discovers the SFHA designation. Before closing, the lender requires Sarah to purchase NFIP flood insurance with at least $250,000 building coverage.

The Result: Sarah must maintain flood insurance for the life of the mortgage. If she cancels, the lender can force-place insurance at Sarah's expense. Her neighbor across the street in Zone X (low risk) has the same mortgage company but isn't required to buy flood insurance.

4. Write Your Own (WYO) Program

How WYO Works

The Write Your Own program allows private insurance companies to sell and administer NFIP policies using their own company name.

Private Insurer

Sells policy

→

Federal Government

Backs coverage

→

Policyholder

Gets coverage

Critical WYO Facts

  • ALL WYO policies have IDENTICAL coverage - coverage is standardized by FEMA
  • Rates are set by FEMA - not by the private insurer
  • Claims paid from federal funds - the government bears the risk
  • Insurers handle administration - selling, servicing, and processing claims

Direct NFIP

Purchase directly from federal government through NFIP servicing companies

Write Your Own (WYO)

Purchase through private insurers (State Farm, Allstate, etc.) - same coverage, same price

Real-World Scenario: WYO Shopping

The Setup: Jennifer needs flood insurance for her home in Zone AE. She gets quotes from three companies: State Farm (WYO), Allstate (WYO), and direct from NFIP.

What Happens: All three quotes are identical: $1,200/year for $250,000 building coverage. The coverage terms, exclusions, and deductibles are exactly the same across all three options.

The Result: Jennifer chooses State Farm because she already has her auto insurance there for convenience. But she gets the exact same NFIP coverage at the exact same FEMA-set rate. When a flood occurs, her claim is handled by State Farm but paid with federal funds.

5. Waiting Periods & Key Definitions

30-Day Waiting Period (Standard)

There is a 30-day waiting period from purchase date to coverage effective date. This prevents people from buying coverage only when a flood is imminent.

Exceptions to 30-Day Wait

0 Days

Purchased at same time as mortgage closing

0 Days

When lender requires it (mandatory purchase)

1 Day

Purchased in connection with FIRM map revision

NFIP Definition of "Flood"

For a flood event to be covered, ALL of the following criteria must be met:

1. General and temporary condition of flooding
2. Partial or complete inundation of normally dry land
3. From overflow of inland/tidal waters, unusual accumulation of surface water, or mudflow
4. Affects TWO OR MORE properties OR two or more acres

MUDFLOW - COVERED

A river of liquid mud flowing on surfaces of normally dry land

Caused by accumulation of water, covered by NFIP

MUDSLIDE - NOT COVERED

Mass movement of earth down a slope

Earth movement, excluded from NFIP

Covered Scenario: Two-Property Requirement

The Setup: Heavy rains cause a stream to overflow. Water floods Mike's property and his neighbor Susan's property.

What Happens: Both homes sustain water damage from the overflow.

The Result: This qualifies as a NFIP flood because it affected two or more properties. Both Mike and Susan can file claims under their NFIP policies.

NOT Covered Scenario: Single Property

The Setup: Tom's sprinkler system malfunctions, flooding his basement with 3 feet of water. No other property is affected.

What Happens: Tom files a claim with NFIP for the water damage.

The Result: Claim DENIED. This doesn't meet NFIP's definition of flood because it only affected one property. Tom's homeowners policy might cover this as water backup, but NFIP won't.

Waiting Period Scenario

The Setup: Lisa sees a hurricane forecast for her coastal town in 5 days. She immediately purchases NFIP flood insurance on September 1st.

What Happens: The hurricane hits on September 7th, causing massive flooding. Lisa's home is destroyed.

The Result: Claim DENIED. Lisa's coverage doesn't start until October 1st (30 days after purchase). The 30-day waiting period exists specifically to prevent this type of last-minute purchase.

EARTHQUAKE INSURANCE

Private Market Coverage for Seismic Activity

6. Earthquake Coverage Basics

Why Separate Coverage?

  • • Excluded from standard property policies
  • • Catastrophic, concentrated losses in specific geographic areas
  • • Available as endorsement to property policy OR separate policy
  • • NEVER mandatory - always optional (unlike flood in SFHA)

What Earthquake Insurance Covers

Building Damage

Structural damage from earthquake shaking

Personal Property

Contents damaged by earthquake

Loss of Use

Additional living expenses while home is uninhabitable

Fire Following

May be included (varies by policy)

What's NOT Covered

✗ Flood from earthquake (tsunami, dam break)
✗ Land and land values
✗ Vehicles
✗ External masonry veneer (sometimes excluded)

Real-World Scenario: Earthquake Coverage

The Setup: Carlos owns a home in California worth $500,000. He has a standard homeowners policy but adds an earthquake endorsement with $500,000 coverage and a 15% deductible.

What Happens: A 6.5 magnitude earthquake strikes. The house sustains $200,000 in structural damage. All contents (worth $80,000) are destroyed. The family must live in a hotel for 3 months ($12,000).

The Result: Total loss = $292,000. Deductible = $75,000 (15% of $500,000). Earthquake policy pays: $217,000 ($292,000 - $75,000). This covers building repairs, contents replacement, and hotel costs. Carlos must pay the $75,000 deductible himself.

7. Percentage Deductibles

How Percentage Deductibles Work

Unlike standard property policies (which use dollar deductibles like $1,000), earthquake policies typically use percentage deductibles ranging from 10% to 25% of the coverage limit.

Deductible Calculation Examples

$300,000 coverage with 15% deductible

= $45,000 deductible

$400,000 coverage with 15% deductible

= $60,000 deductible

$500,000 coverage with 25% deductible

= $125,000 deductible

Why Such High Deductibles?

  • • Catastrophic nature of earthquake losses
  • • Keeps premiums affordable for policyholders
  • • Encourages loss prevention and mitigation
  • • Shares risk between insurer and insured

Real-World Scenario: Percentage vs Dollar Deductible

The Setup: Nina has a $400,000 home. Her homeowners policy has a $2,000 deductible. She adds earthquake coverage with a 15% deductible, thinking it's similar.

What Happens: An earthquake causes $100,000 in damage to her home.

The Result: Nina is shocked to learn her deductible is $60,000 (15% of $400,000), not $2,000! The earthquake policy pays $40,000 ($100,000 damage - $60,000 deductible). Nina must cover the first $60,000 herself - 30 times her homeowners deductible.

Real-World Scenario: CEA Coverage Choice

The Setup: Patricia owns a $600,000 home in Los Angeles. She's deciding between CEA deductible options: 5% ($30,000) or 25% ($150,000).

What Happens: The 5% option costs $1,800/year. The 25% option costs $600/year - a $1,200 annual savings.

The Result: Patricia chooses the 25% deductible to save money, figuring she'll self-insure the first $150,000 since major earthquakes are rare. She saves $1,200/year but accepts the risk of paying the first $150,000 if a big quake hits.

9. Fire Following Earthquake

Critical Distinction

Standard property policies DO cover fire damage that occurs following an earthquake, even though earthquake itself is excluded!

Standard Property Policy Covers:

  • • Fire damage from earthquake
  • • Explosion damage from earthquake
  • • Smoke damage from fire following earthquake

Earthquake Policy Covers:

  • • Shake damage to structure
  • • Foundation damage
  • • Crack damage from tremors

Covered by Standard Property: Fire Following Earthquake

The Setup: Marcus has a homeowners policy (no earthquake coverage). An earthquake shakes his home, cracking the foundation. The shaking also breaks a gas line.

What Happens: The broken gas line ignites, causing a fire that completely destroys the house.

The Result: The homeowners policy pays for the fire damage ($400,000). The foundation cracks are NOT covered because earthquake damage is excluded. If Marcus had earthquake coverage, it would pay for the foundation - but the standard policy covers the fire regardless.

Split Coverage: Both Policies Apply

The Setup: Elena has both a homeowners policy and earthquake coverage. An earthquake causes $80,000 in structural damage and breaks a gas line, starting a fire that causes $200,000 additional damage.

What Happens: Elena files claims with both insurers.

The Result: Homeowners policy pays $200,000 for fire damage. Earthquake policy pays for the $80,000 shake damage (minus the percentage deductible). Total recovery covers both types of loss.

Exam Trap Alerts

1. Flood Definition - Two Properties Required

A flood must affect two or more properties OR two or more acres to qualify under NFIP. One flooded backyard doesn't count! This trips up many test-takers who think any water damage qualifies.

2. Mudflow vs Mudslide

Mudflow = COVERED (river of liquid mud). Mudslide = NOT COVERED (earth movement). Know the difference - they sound similar but have opposite coverage!

3. 30-Day Waiting Period

Standard NFIP waiting period is 30 days. You can't buy flood insurance when a hurricane is approaching and expect coverage! Exception: 0 days at mortgage closing or when lender requires it.

4. Contents Coverage Separate

NFIP contents coverage must be purchased separately from building coverage. It's NOT automatic like in homeowners policies. Many people assume building coverage includes contents - it doesn't!

5. No Living Expenses in NFIP

NFIP does NOT cover Additional Living Expenses (hotel, meals, etc.) like homeowners policies do. This is a major exclusion that surprises flood victims.

6. Percentage Deductible Math

Earthquake uses percentage, not dollar deductibles. 15% of $400,000 = $60,000 deductible! This shocks many policyholders. Don't confuse with standard property dollar deductibles.

7. Fire Following Earthquake

Standard property policy covers fire that occurs after an earthquake, even though earthquake itself is excluded. The shake damage requires earthquake coverage, but fire damage doesn't.

8. SFHA Mandatory Purchase

Flood insurance is only mandatory with federally-backed mortgages in SFHA zones (A, AE, V, VE). Not all mortgages - just federal ones. And only in high-risk zones.

9. WYO Same Coverage

All Write Your Own insurers offer identical NFIP coverage at FEMA-set rates. There's no shopping around for better coverage or lower prices - it's all the same!

10. Basement Limitations

NFIP has very limited basement coverage. Finished basements are mostly excluded. Don't assume your finished basement rec room is fully covered!

11. Zone V = Velocity

Zone V is coastal high-risk with wave action (velocity zones). Higher rates than regular Zone A because waves cause more damage than just flooding.

12. CEA Minimum Deductible

California Earthquake Authority minimum deductible is 5%, not 10%. It goes 5%, 10%, 15%, 25% - know all four options.
