Part 3: Inland Marine

Mobile Property & Property in Transit - Nothing to Do with Water!

Overview History Filed vs Unfiled Personal Floaters Commercial Comparison

1. What is Inland Marine Insurance?

Despite the name, Inland Marine insurance has nothing to do with water. It evolved from ocean marine insurance to cover property that is mobile, in transit over land, or has special characteristics that make it unsuitable for standard property policies.

Why "Marine" if There's No Water?

Historical Context: Ocean Marine insurance covered goods on ships. When coverage was needed for goods traveling over LAND, insurers extended the concept - calling it "Inland" Marine because it covered the continuation of ocean journeys onto land. The name stuck, even though it now covers things that never go near water.

Property Must Have One of These Characteristics:

Property in Transit

Being transported from one place to another

Mobile Property

Designed to be moved frequently

Bailee Property

Held by someone other than the owner

Transportation/Communication Tools

Bridges, tunnels, towers, pipelines

Real-World Scenario: Standard Property vs Inland Marine

The Setup: Sarah is a professional photographer with $50,000 in camera equipment. She works at clients' homes, wedding venues, outdoor locations, and her studio.

The Problem: Her Homeowners policy only covers property AT her home. Once she leaves with her cameras, they're not covered.

The Solution: A Personal Articles Floater (Inland Marine) covers her cameras WORLDWIDE, whether at home, in transit, or at shooting locations. It "floats" with the property wherever it goes.

Why Inland Marine Was Necessary

Standard property policies have a fundamental limitation: they cover property at a fixed location only. This doesn't work for:

  • • Goods being transported by truck or train
  • • Equipment that moves from job site to job site
  • • Personal valuables you carry with you
  • • Property held temporarily by repair shops or cleaners

3. Filed vs Unfiled Forms

Filed Forms

Standard ISO forms filed with state regulators for common exposures.

Characteristics:

  • • Standardized coverage
  • • Pre-approved by regulators
  • • Used for typical risks
  • • Consistent across insurers

Examples:

  • • Personal Articles Floater
  • • Personal Property Floater
  • • Standard equipment coverage
  • • Standard builders risk

Unfiled Forms

Custom forms created by insurers for unique or unusual exposures.

Characteristics:

  • • Customized coverage
  • • Greater flexibility
  • • Tailored to specific risks
  • • Varies by insurer

Examples:

  • • Unique equipment types
  • • Specialized business needs
  • • Unusual transit risks
  • • Industry-specific coverage

Nationwide Definition of Marine Insurance (NAIC)

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) established criteria to determine what can be classified as Inland Marine:

1. Property in domestic transit

2. Bailee customer property

3. Mobile equipment and property

4. Transportation/communication tools

5. Jewelry, furs, similar items

6. Fine arts

7. Valuable papers and records

4. Personal Inland Marine (Floaters)

Personal floaters are called "floaters" because the coverage floats with the property - it's covered wherever it goes, not tied to a specific location.

Personal Articles Floater (PAF)

The most common personal inland marine policy - covers scheduled personal property with superior protection.

Open Perils Coverage

All risks except specific exclusions

Worldwide Coverage

Covered anywhere on Earth

No Deductible

Usually zero deductible

Agreed Value

Pays scheduled amount, no depreciation

Common Items Scheduled:

Jewelry Furs Cameras Musical instruments Fine arts Golf equipment Silverware Stamp/coin collections

Real-World Scenario: Why Scheduled Coverage Matters

The Setup: Jennifer has a $15,000 engagement ring. Her Homeowners policy has a $1,500 sublimit for jewelry theft and would pay actual cash value (depreciated).

What Happens: While on vacation in Paris, her ring is stolen from her hotel room.

The Result: With a Personal Articles Floater, Jennifer receives $15,000 (the scheduled amount) with no deductible, no depreciation, even though the loss occurred in France. Without it, her Homeowners policy would only pay $1,500.

Personal Property Floater

Covers ALL personal property with one blanket limit (not scheduled).

Example: Someone with extensive collections who doesn't want to list each item individually.

Personal Effects Floater

Designed for travelers - covers luggage and personal effects during tourist travel.

Example: A family taking a European vacation purchases this to cover their luggage, cameras, and travel items.

Wedding Present Floater

Temporary 90-day coverage for wedding gifts before and immediately after the wedding.

Example: Covers gifts from the shower, during display at reception, and while being transported to the new home.

5. Commercial Inland Marine Coverage

Contractors Equipment Floater

Covers mobile construction equipment - typically open perils coverage for equipment at job sites and in transit.

What's Covered:

Cranes, bulldozers, excavators, forklifts, compressors - heavy equipment that moves from site to site

Coverage Options:

Scheduled (specific items listed) or blanket (all equipment covered)

Real-World Scenario: Mobile Equipment Risk

The Setup: ABC Construction owns a $200,000 excavator that works at different job sites each week.

What Happens: While working at a residential site, the excavator hits an underground utility line and flips over, causing major damage.

The Result: The Contractors Equipment Floater pays for repairs because it covers equipment at job sites. A standard Commercial Property policy would NOT cover this - it only covers property at the insured's fixed location.

Builders Risk Coverage

Covers buildings DURING construction - insures the structure, materials, and supplies at the construction site.

Key Features:

  • • Coverage limit increases as building progresses
  • • Covers materials stored at the site
  • • Can include property in transit to the site
  • • Coverage ends when: completed, occupied, sold, or policy expires

Real-World Scenario: Construction Coverage

The Setup: XYZ Developers are building a $2 million office building. Construction is 60% complete when a severe windstorm hits.

What Happens: The partially built structure suffers $300,000 in damage. Lumber and materials stored at the site are also destroyed ($50,000).

The Result: Builders Risk policy pays $350,000 to repair the structure and replace materials. Once the building is complete and occupied, a regular Commercial Property policy takes over.

Motor Truck Cargo & Transit Policies

Motor Truck Cargo

Covers goods being transported by truck - protects the trucking company (carrier).

Example: A trucking company hauling furniture cross-country. If the cargo is damaged in an accident, this policy responds.

Trip vs Annual Transit

Trip Transit: Covers single shipment. Annual Transit: Covers all shipments during the year.

Example: A business shipping products regularly uses Annual Transit. A one-time art shipment uses Trip Transit.

Installation Floater

Covers equipment/materials during transit AND installation at customer's location.

Real-World Scenario:

Setup: An HVAC company delivers and installs a $25,000 commercial air conditioning unit. What Happens: While being lifted to the roof, the unit falls and is destroyed. Result: Installation Floater covers the loss during the installation process.

Bailee Coverage

Protects businesses that hold customer property - covers damage to customers' goods in your care, custody, or control.

Common Bailees:

Dry cleaners Repair shops Storage facilities Parking garages Tailors Furriers

Real-World Scenario: Bailee Responsibility

The Setup: Elite Dry Cleaners has 200 customers' garments in their shop, with an average value of $150 per item.

What Happens: An overnight fire destroys the shop and all the customers' clothing inside.

The Result: Bailee Coverage pays for the customers' property ($30,000). Without it, the dry cleaner would be personally liable for all customer losses. Note: The building and business equipment would be covered under a separate Commercial Property policy.

Jewelers Block Policy

Comprehensive all-risk coverage for jewelers - covers owned inventory AND customers' property.

Example: Covers jewelry in the store, in transit to trade shows, sent to appraisers, and customer pieces being repaired or sized.

Camera & Musical Instrument Dealers

Covers dealer inventory in store and in transit - can include customers' property for repair.

Example: A music store's inventory of guitars, both displayed in the store and being delivered to customers.

Electronic Data Processing (EDP)

Covers computer hardware, software, data, and business income from EDP breakdown.

Example: Server failure destroys customer database - covers data restoration costs.

Accounts Receivable

Covers money owed if records are destroyed - pays amounts that can't be collected plus collection costs.

Example: Fire destroys billing records showing who owes what - policy pays the uncollectable amounts.

Valuable Papers & Records

Covers cost to reconstruct important documents - research and restoration costs.

Example: Architectural firm's blueprints destroyed - pays to recreate drawings from survey notes.

7. Standard Property vs Inland Marine

Feature Standard Property Inland Marine
Location Fixed location only Mobile/transit - anywhere
Coverage Type Named perils or open perils Usually open perils (all risk)
Territory Policy location only Worldwide possible
Valuation ACV typical Agreed value common
Scheduling Blanket typical Often scheduled items
Deductible Standard deductible applies Often $0 for personal items

When Standard Property Works

Your office building, inventory stored in your warehouse, furniture at your retail location - property that stays in one place.

Example: A retail store's merchandise stays on shelves at 123 Main Street - Commercial Property policy is perfect.

When Inland Marine is Needed

Construction equipment at job sites, property in transit, customer property you're holding, valuable items you travel with.

Example: A contractor's bulldozer moves between job sites daily - Contractors Equipment Floater is essential.

Exam Trap Alerts

1. "Marine" Does NOT Mean Water

Inland Marine covers land transit and mobile property, NOT boats or ocean shipping. Ocean shipping is "Ocean Marine" - a completely different type of coverage. Don't let the name trick you.

2. Agreed Value = No Depreciation Debate

Personal Articles Floater pays the scheduled amount with no depreciation. If a $10,000 camera is scheduled, you get $10,000 - not replacement cost minus depreciation. This is different from ACV coverage.

3. Builders Risk Covers DURING Construction Only

Coverage ends when the building is completed, occupied, sold, or the policy expires - whichever comes first. Completed buildings need regular Commercial Property insurance.

4. Bailee vs Bailor - Know the Difference

Bailee = the business HOLDING the property (dry cleaner, repair shop). Bailor = the customer who OWNS the property. Bailee Coverage protects the business against liability for customer property damage.

5. Motor Truck Cargo Covers the CARGO, Not the Truck

The truck itself is covered under Auto insurance. Motor Truck Cargo covers the goods being transported - damage to the shipment, not the vehicle.

6. Personal Articles Floater = Worldwide Coverage

Unlike most property policies with limited territories, PAF covers your scheduled items anywhere on Earth. Your $20,000 engagement ring is covered in Tokyo, Paris, or Antarctica.

7. Filed vs Unfiled Forms

Filed = standardized ISO forms approved by regulators. Unfiled = custom forms created by insurers for unique exposures. Both are legal and proper, just used for different situations.

8. Contractors Equipment ≠ The Contractor's Building

Contractors Equipment Floater covers mobile equipment like bulldozers and cranes - NOT the contractor's office building. The office is covered under standard Commercial Property.

9. Installation Floater Covers Transit AND Installation

This covers the property while being transported AND during the installation process. If an HVAC unit is damaged while being lifted onto a roof, it's covered.

10. NAIC Nationwide Definition Determines Eligibility

Not everything can be classified as Inland Marine. It must meet NAIC criteria: property in transit, mobile property, bailee property, transportation/communication tools, jewelry/furs, fine arts, or valuable papers.
