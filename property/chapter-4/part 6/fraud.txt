Fraud
The following are considered violations of the New Jersey Insurance Fraud Prevention Act:

Knowingly presenting any written or oral false or misleading statement as part of, or in support of, or opposition to a claim for payment or other benefit;
Concealing or knowingly failing to disclose the occurrence of an event which affects any person's initial or continued right or entitlement to any insurance benefit or payment or the amount of any benefit or payment to which the person is entitled;
Preparing or making any statement intended to be presented to any insurance company or producer for the purpose of obtaining a motor vehicle insurance policy, stating that the person to be insured maintains a principal residence in this state when, in fact, that person's principal residence is in another state;
Preparing or making any statement intended to be presented to any insurance company or producer for the purpose of obtaining an insurance policy, knowing that the statement contains any false or misleading information concerning any fact or thing material to an insurance application or contract;
Concealing or knowingly failing to disclose any evidence, written or oral, which may be relevant to a finding that a violation of the provisions has or has not occurred.
Knowingly assisting, conspiring with, or urging any person or practitioner to violate any of the provisions of this act;
Knowingly benefiting, directly or indirectly, from the proceeds derived from a violation of this act, due to the assistance, conspiracy or urging of any person;
For an owner, administrator or employee of any hospital, knowingly allowing the use of the hospital facilities by any person in furtherance of a scheme or conspiracy to violate any of the provisions of this act;
Directly or indirectly soliciting a person to engage, employ, or retain him/herself or any other person to manage, adjust, or prosecute any claim or cause of action against a person for damages for negligence or for pecuniary gain;
Directly or indirectly soliciting other persons to bring causes of action to recover damages for personal injuries or death, or for pecuniary gain; or
Directly or indirectly soliciting other persons to make a claim for personal injury protection benefits.
Whenever the Commissioner determines that a person has violated any provision of the New Jersey Insurance Fraud Prevention Act, the Commissioner may do the following:

Bring a civil action (lawsuit) against the offender; or
Assess a civil administrative penalty and order restitution.
In lieu of these actions, the Commissioner may request the Attorney General to bring a criminal action under applicable criminal statutes, or refer the matter to appropriate state licensing authorities, including the insurance producer licensing section in the Department of Banking and Insurance, for consideration of licensing actions, including license suspension or revocation.

The penalty for violation the New Jersey Insurance Fraud Prevention Act is up to $5,000 for the first violation, $10,000 for the second violation, and $15,000 for each subsequent violation.

In addition to any other penalty, individuals guilty of committing insurance fraud are also subject to a surcharge of $1,000. If a legal proceeding is resolved through a monetary settlement, the guilty party must pay a surcharge in an amount equal to 5% of the total settlement payment. All surcharges must be made payable to the Treasurer of the State of New Jersey.
