### 1. Property -- The Big Picture

**Property** = The rights that one person has against all others to the possession, use, and enjoyment of a thing. It is not the thing itself -- it is the *legal rights* you have over the thing.

In plain English: When we say you "own property," we really mean you have a bundle of legal rights (to use it, sell it, give it away, exclude others from it, etc.).

**Two Main Categories:**

- **Real property (realty)** = Land, buildings, and things permanently attached to land. Think "real estate."
- **Personal property** = Everything else -- your car, furniture, bank account, stocks, clothing, etc.

**Three Core Concepts:**

- **Ownership** = The complete bundle of rights in property. The owner has the right to possess, use, enjoy, and dispose of the property. Ownership is between the owner and *everyone else in society*.
- **Title** = Evidence of ownership. It is the legal link between the owner and the property. Think of it as "proof you own it."
- **Possession** = Physical custody or control of property. You can possess something without owning it (borrowing a friend's car), and you can own something without possessing it (your car is in the shop).

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example:**
- **The Setup:** Sarah buys a painting at a gallery and receives a receipt (title). She then loans the painting to a museum for a 6-month exhibit.
- **What Happens:** Sarah has ownership (she bought it) and title (receipt proves it), but the museum has possession (physical custody).
- **The Result:** If someone steals the painting from the museum, Sarah -- as the owner -- has the legal right to sue for its return, even though she did not have physical possession.

### 2. Intellectual Property Rights

**Intellectual property rights** = Legal rights to the products of one's own mind and intellect. These are intangible (you cannot touch them), but they are enormously valuable.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] -- Intellectual property law has evolved significantly since 2008, particularly around digital content, software patents, AI-generated works, and internet copyright issues.

#### A. Copyrights

**Copyright** = An exclusive legal right to reproduce, publish, sell, or distribute a literary, musical, or artistic work. Created automatically when the work is fixed in a tangible form (written down, recorded, etc.).

- Protects: books, music, software, art, films, photographs, architectural designs
- Does NOT protect: ideas, facts, titles, short phrases
- Duration: Generally the author's life + 70 years [2026 UPDATE NEEDED -- verify current terms]
- Registration with the U.S. Copyright Office strengthens your rights but is not required

**Fair use** = A legal doctrine that permits limited use of copyrighted material without permission. Evaluated by four factors:
1. The purpose and character of the use (commercial vs. educational)
2. The nature of the copyrighted work
3. The amount used relative to the whole
4. The effect on the market value of the original

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** A college professor copies 3 pages from a 400-page textbook to hand out in class.
- **What Happens:** The publisher claims copyright infringement.
- **The Result:** This likely qualifies as fair use -- it is for education, it is a small portion of the whole, and it does not replace the need to buy the textbook.

#### B. Patents

**Patent** = A government-granted exclusive right to make, use, or sell an invention for a limited time (generally 20 years from filing for utility patents).

- Protects: inventions, processes, machines, manufactured items, compositions of matter
- Requires: novelty, non-obviousness, and usefulness
- Must be applied for and granted by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** An engineer invents a new type of brake pad that lasts 3x longer than existing designs and obtains a patent.
- **What Happens:** A competitor starts manufacturing an identical brake pad without a license.
- **The Result:** The engineer can sue for patent infringement and collect damages. The competitor must stop production or negotiate a licensing deal.

### 3. Accession

**Accession** = When someone adds value to another person's personal property without the owner's consent. The legal question: Who owns the improved property?

In plain English: You accidentally (or intentionally) improve someone else's stuff. Who gets to keep it?

**General Rule:** The original owner keeps the property AND the improvements.

**Exception:** If the improver acted in good faith (honestly believed the property was theirs) and the improvement substantially increased the value, the improver may gain ownership but must compensate the original owner.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** A carpenter mistakenly takes lumber from a neighbor's yard (thinking it was his own) and builds beautiful custom cabinets worth $5,000. The lumber was worth $200.
- **What Happens:** The neighbor discovers the mistake and demands the cabinets.
- **The Result:** Because the carpenter acted in good faith and the value increased dramatically ($200 to $5,000), the carpenter likely keeps the cabinets but must pay the neighbor $200 for the lumber.

### 4. Confusion

**Confusion** = The mingling of goods belonging to different owners so that the individual properties can no longer be identified or separated.

In plain English: Your stuff gets mixed with someone else's stuff and nobody can tell whose is whose.

**Rules:**
- If confusion happens by **agreement or accident**: Owners become co-owners (tenants in common) of the mixture, each sharing proportionally.
- If confusion happens by **one party's intentional wrongdoing**: The innocent party gets to claim the entire mixture (harsh penalty for the wrongdoer).

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** Farmer A stores 1,000 bushels of wheat in a grain elevator. Farmer B stores 500 bushels of identical wheat. The elevator operator accidentally mixes them together.
- **What Happens:** The wheat cannot be separated.
- **The Result:** Farmer A owns 2/3 and Farmer B owns 1/3 of the 1,500-bushel mixture (proportional shares as co-owners).

### 5. Bailments

**Bailment** = A legal relationship where one person (the bailor) transfers possession of personal property to another person (the bailee) for a specific purpose, with the understanding that the property will be returned or disposed of according to the bailor's instructions.

Key distinction: A bailment transfers POSSESSION, not OWNERSHIP. The bailor keeps ownership.

**Three parties/terms to know:**
- **Bailor** = The owner who hands over possession. ("I bail my property to you.")
- **Bailee** = The person who receives possession. ("I hold your property in bailment.")

**Three types based on who benefits:**
1. **Sole benefit of bailor** -- Example: "Store my boat in your garage for free." Bailee has minimal duty of care (only liable for gross negligence).
2. **Sole benefit of bailee** -- Example: "You can borrow my lawn mower." Bailee has highest duty of care (liable even for slight negligence).
3. **Mutual benefit** -- Example: Dry cleaning, car repair, storage rental. Bailee must exercise reasonable care.

#### Bailee's Rights and Duties

- **Duty to care for the property** -- Standard of care depends on the type of bailment
- **Right to possess** -- Bailee can sue third parties who interfere with their possession
- **Lien (possessory lien)** = The bailee's right to hold the property until paid for services. If you fix my car, you can keep it until I pay the bill.

**Lien** = A legal claim or charge on property as security for a debt or obligation. In plain English: "You owe me money, so I have a legal grip on your stuff until you pay."

**Possessory lien** = A lien that depends on physical possession. Once the bailee gives the property back, the lien is usually lost.

#### Bailor's Rights and Duties

- **Right to return of property** in the same condition
- **Duty to warn** the bailee of known defects in the property
- For **mutual benefit bailments**: Duty to warn of defects the bailor *should have known* about

#### Special Bailments (Higher Standards)

Three types of bailees have EXTRA-HIGH duties imposed by law:

1. **Common carrier** = A transportation company that offers services to the general public (airlines, trucking companies, railroads).
   - **Shipper** = The person sending the goods
   - **Factor** = An agent who receives and sells goods for the shipper
   - Common carriers are **virtually insurers** of the goods -- liable for almost ALL loss or damage, with only 5 exceptions: acts of God, acts of public enemy, acts of the shipper, acts of public authority, and the inherent nature of the goods
   - Liability runs from receipt at the freight terminal until the recipient has reasonable time to inspect and remove goods

2. **Hotelkeepers** = Liable for failure to exercise reasonable care in protecting guests' property. Can limit liability by providing safes for valuables.

3. **Warehouse operators** = Do not insure stored goods. Can limit liability in the warehouse receipt.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** You ship a crate of electronics via FedEx (a common carrier). During transport, a FedEx driver leaves the truck unlocked at a rest stop and the crate is stolen.
- **What Happens:** You file a claim for the loss.
- **The Result:** FedEx (common carrier) is liable because they are held to the highest standard. Theft does not fall under any of the 5 exceptions. They must compensate you for the full value.

### 6. Gifts

**Gift** = A voluntary transfer of property from one person to another without payment or consideration.

- **Donor** = The person giving the gift
- **Donee** = The person receiving the gift

**Three requirements for a valid gift (MUST MEMORIZE):**
1. **Donative intent** -- The donor must intend to make a gift (not a loan, not under duress)
2. **Delivery** -- The donor must actually transfer possession to the donee (or to a third party for the donee)
3. **Acceptance** -- The donee must accept the gift (generally presumed for valuable items)

Once a gift is complete (all 3 elements met), it is **irrevocable** -- the donor cannot take it back.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** Grandma tells her grandson "I want you to have my diamond ring" but keeps wearing it and never hands it over. She dies, and her will leaves everything to charity.
- **What Happens:** The grandson claims the ring was a gift to him.
- **The Result:** No valid gift occurred. While there may have been donative intent, there was no delivery. The ring goes to the charity as directed by the will.

### 7. Real Property Estates

**Real property estate** = The extent of one's legal interest in real property. Not everyone who "has" real property has the same level of rights.

**Interest** = Any right in real property, from full ownership down to a temporary right to walk across the land.

#### Types of Estates:

**Fee simple estate** = The most complete form of ownership. The owner can use, sell, lease, gift, or will the property with no time limit. This is what most people mean when they say they "own" a house.

**Life estate** = Ownership that lasts only for the duration of someone's life. A **life tenant** can use and enjoy the property during their lifetime, but cannot destroy it or waste it because it must pass to someone else when they die.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** Dad's will says "My house goes to Mom for her lifetime, and then to our children."
- **What Happens:** Mom can live there, rent it out, and collect income, but she cannot tear it down or let it fall apart.
- **The Result:** When Mom dies, the house automatically passes to the children. Mom had a life estate; the children had a "remainder interest."

#### Concurrent Estates (Multiple Owners)

**Tenancy** = An interest in real property. When multiple people own property simultaneously, it is called a concurrent estate.

**Joint tenancy** = Two or more people own equal shares with the **right of survivorship**. When one owner dies, their share automatically goes to the surviving owner(s) -- it does NOT pass through the will.
- Requirements: Same time, same deed, equal shares, equal right of possession
- Key feature: Right of survivorship

**Tenancy by the entirety** = A special joint tenancy available ONLY to married couples. Neither spouse can sell or mortgage the property without the other's consent. Includes right of survivorship.
- Dower (wife's interest) and curtesy (husband's interest) are historical terms for a surviving spouse's rights in the other's property
- Divorce bars dower and curtesy rights

**Tenancy in common** = Two or more people own property, but:
- Shares can be unequal (one person can own 70%, another 30%)
- NO right of survivorship -- when one owner dies, their share goes to their heirs through their will
- Owners do not need to acquire their interests at the same time or from the same source

**EXAM TIP -- Key Distinction:**
- Joint tenancy = SURVIVORSHIP (deceased owner's share goes to other joint tenants)
- Tenancy in common = NO survivorship (deceased owner's share goes to heirs via will)

**Community property** = A system used in about 9 states (including California, Texas, Arizona, Louisiana) where most property acquired during marriage is owned equally by both spouses, regardless of whose name is on the title. Each spouse owns a 50% interest.

> **Insurance Relevance:** In community property states, both spouses may need to be named on property insurance policies. If one spouse causes a liability claim, community property assets may be at risk. This affects how insurers assess insurable interest and how claims are settled.

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: Community property laws continue to evolve. Some states have adopted community property trusts (e.g., Alaska, Tennessee, South Dakota allow opt-in community property for tax benefits). The interaction between community property and insurance coverage -- particularly for auto liability and homeowners -- varies significantly by state.

#### Cooperative Ownership

**Cooperative ownership** = A corporation owns the building. Residents buy shares in the corporation that entitle them to occupy a specific unit. You do not own your apartment -- you own stock.

#### Condominium Ownership

**Condominium** = Each unit owner owns their individual unit outright (fee simple) PLUS an undivided interest in common areas (lobby, hallways, pool, parking). Unlike a co-op, you actually own real property.

### 8. Real Property Sales

#### A. Contracts of Sale

A contract for the sale of real estate must contain **three essential elements:**
1. **Must be in writing** (Statute of Frauds requirement)
2. **Essential terms**: Description of the premises and the price
3. **Nonessential terms**: Items like closing date, contingencies, etc.

The parties:
- **Vendor** = The seller
- **Vendee** = The buyer

#### B. Deeds

**Deed** = The legal document that actually transfers title (ownership) to real property from one person to another.

The parties:
- **Grantor** = The person transferring ownership
- **Grantee** = The person receiving ownership

**Requirements for a valid deed (MUST MEMORIZE):**
1. Must be in writing
2. Grantor must be legally competent and must sign
3. Must name the grantee
4. Must state the consideration (what is given in exchange)
5. Must contain words of transfer (stating a transfer is occurring)
6. Must contain a description of the property
7. Must be dated
8. May contain a paragraph about the transferor and recording info
9. Some states require grantor's signature under seal
10. Some states require witnesses
11. Must be delivered
12. Most states require notary acknowledgment

**Types of Deeds (ranked from MOST to LEAST protection for buyer):**

**General warranty deed** = The gold standard. Grantor guarantees clear title and promises to defend against ALL claims, even those arising before the grantor owned the property.

**Special warranty deed** = Grantor only guarantees against defects that arose during THEIR period of ownership. Does not protect against problems from before they owned it.

**Bargain-and-sale deed** = Implies the grantor has title, but makes NO warranties about defects. Buyer beware.

**Quit-claim deed** = The weakest form. Grantor transfers whatever interest they MIGHT have. Makes zero guarantees. Could be transferring nothing at all.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** You buy a house with a quit-claim deed. Later, a bank shows up and says the previous owner had an unpaid mortgage on the property.
- **What Happens:** The bank has a valid lien on the property.
- **The Result:** You are stuck. The quit-claim deed gave you no warranty protection. With a general warranty deed, the grantor would have been required to clear the title or compensate you.

### 9. Recording and Priorities

**Why recording matters:** Without recording a deed, no public notice exists that the property has been transferred. If the seller fraudulently sells the property to a second buyer who does not know about the first sale, the second buyer (who records first) may win.

**General rule:** First to record = first in priority.

**Unrecorded interests** are not valid against good-faith purchasers or creditors who have no knowledge of the unrecorded claim.

### 10. Real Estate Security Interests

#### A. Mortgages

A **mortgage** is a security interest in real property given by a borrower to a lender to secure repayment of a loan.

- **Mortgagor** = The borrower (property owner who gives the mortgage)
- **Mortgagee** = The lender (bank that receives the mortgage as security)

**Mortgagee's rights:**
- Right to **foreclosure** if the loan is not repaid. **Foreclosure** = A legal process where the property is sold to satisfy the debt.
- Right to obtain **deficiency judgments** if the sale price does not cover the full debt
- Right to collect deficient payments from subsequent purchasers after foreclosure

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: Since the 2008 financial crisis, mortgage law has changed dramatically. The Dodd-Frank Act (2010) created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), which regulates mortgage lending. Key changes: Qualified Mortgage (QM) rules requiring ability-to-repay verification, restrictions on predatory lending, mandatory loss mitigation review before foreclosure, and TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure (TRID) rules. Many states enacted additional foreclosure protections including mandatory mediation programs.

#### B. Trust Deeds

**Trust deed (deed of trust / trust indenture)** = An alternative to a mortgage, most suitable when large loans are involved. Three parties: borrower, lender, and a trustee. The borrower conveys title to the trustee, who holds it as security for the lender. If borrower defaults, trustee can sell without court foreclosure (faster process).

#### C. Land Contracts

**Land contract** = The seller finances the purchase directly. The buyer makes payments over time but does not receive the deed until the full price is paid. Used when buyers have poor credit ratings or cannot make large down payments.

### 11. Mechanics' Liens on Real Property

**Mechanic's lien** = A lien available to anyone who contributes labor or materials to the improvement of real property. Protects contractors, subcontractors, and suppliers.

- The claimant must file notice of the lien
- State law determines priorities of mechanics' liens relative to other liens
- **Foreclosure** is the remedy: forcing a court sale of the property to recover payment

**Waiver of lien** = A contractor may agree in advance to waive the right to file a mechanic's lien (sometimes required by lenders before funding construction).

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** A roofer installs a new roof on Jessica's barn. Jessica refuses to pay the $8,000 bill.
- **What Happens:** The roofer files a mechanic's lien on the property.
- **The Result:** The roofer can now force a court sale of the property to recover the $8,000. The lien attaches to the real property, and Jessica cannot sell or refinance without satisfying the lien.

### 12. Incidental Real Property Rights

#### A. Adverse Possession

**Adverse possession** = A way to gain legal title to someone else's land WITHOUT their permission, by occupying it for a long period of time under specific conditions.

**Requirements (MUST MEMORIZE):**
1. **Exclusive possession** -- Occupy and use it as your own (in the usual way)
2. **Open and obvious** -- Possession must be visible (not secret)
3. **Adverse/hostile** -- Without the owner's permission
4. **Continuous** -- For a statutory period, usually 10-20 years depending on the state

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** A farmer builds a fence 10 feet onto his neighbor's land and farms that strip for 25 years. The neighbor never objects.
- **What Happens:** After the statutory period, the farmer claims adverse possession.
- **The Result:** If all four requirements are met, the farmer gains legal title to the 10-foot strip. The original owner loses it.

**IMPORTANT:** If the true owner gives permission to use the land, there is NO adverse possession (it is not hostile/adverse).

#### B. Rights Under, Above, and on the Land's Surface

A landowner's rights extend:
- **Above** the surface (airspace, within reasonable limits)
- **Below** the surface (mineral rights, unless separately sold)
- **On** the surface

#### C. Lateral and Subjacent Support

**Lateral support** = The support that soil receives from the land next to it. If your neighbor excavates their land and YOUR land collapses as a result, you have a claim.

**Subjacent support** = The support that the surface of the land receives from the earth beneath it. If someone mines under your land and it caves in, they are liable.

#### D. Water Rights

**Riparian owner** = A person who owns land bordering a natural waterway (river, stream, lake). Riparian owners have the right to reasonable use of the water.

#### E. Ownership of Fixtures

**Fixture** = An item of personal property that has been attached to real property in such a way that it is now considered part of the real property.

**Three tests to determine if something is a fixture:**
1. **Cannot be removed without substantial injury** to the real property
2. **Specially constructed or fitted** for use in the building, or installed in the building
3. **Intent** of the party who attached it -- did they intend it to become permanent?

**Trade fixture** = An item attached to real property by a TENANT for use in their business. Trade fixtures can be removed by the tenant when the lease ends (they do NOT become the landlord's property).

**Improvements and betterments** = Changes made by a tenant that increase the property's value and cannot be removed (like new flooring or walls). These become part of the real property.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
- **The Setup:** A restaurant tenant installs a commercial pizza oven bolted to the floor and also tiles the entire kitchen.
- **What Happens:** The lease ends, and both tenant and landlord claim the oven and tiles.
- **The Result:** The pizza oven is a trade fixture (installed for the tenant's business) -- the tenant can remove it. The tile flooring is an improvement/betterment (cannot be removed without destruction) -- it belongs to the landlord.

### 13. Land Use Restrictions

#### A. Incorporeal Interests

**Incorporeal interest** = A right in real property that does not include the right to possess it. You have a right "in" the property, but you do not possess it.

**Easement** = The right to use someone else's land for a specific purpose. Example: Right to cross your neighbor's property to access your own (right of way).
- Can be created by: express words, implication, or prescription (similar to adverse possession)
- Easements "run with the land" -- they remain even when the property is sold

**Profits a prendre** = The right to enter someone else's land and remove something of value (minerals, timber, crops, fish, game).

**License** = Permission to use someone else's land. Unlike an easement, a license is:
- Temporary and revocable
- Personal (cannot be transferred)
- Example: Permission to park in someone's driveway

#### B. Government Controls

**Zoning** = Government laws that regulate how land can be used in specific areas (residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural).

Key zoning concepts:
- **Spot zoning** = Singling out a small parcel for treatment different from surrounding land. Generally disfavored by courts.
- **Exclusionary zoning** = Zoning designed to keep certain groups out (e.g., requiring very large lot sizes to price out lower-income buyers). Can be struck down as unconstitutional.
- **Special exception** = Permission for a use not normally allowed in a zone (e.g., a church in a residential area).
- **Variance** = Permission to deviate from zoning rules due to unique circumstances.
  - **Hardship variance** = Granted because strict compliance would cause undue hardship to the owner
  - **Use variance** = Allows a completely different use than what the zone permits
- **Nonconforming use** = A use that existed BEFORE the zoning law was enacted. Generally allowed to continue (grandfathered in) but cannot be expanded.

**Building codes** = Regulations governing construction quality and safety standards (electrical wiring, plumbing, structural integrity, fire safety).

**Eminent domain** = The government's power to take private property for public use, but MUST pay fair market value (just compensation). The legal process is called a **condemnation proceeding**.

### 14. Landlord and Tenant

#### Key Terms:

- **Tenancy at will** = Can be terminated by either party at any time. No fixed duration.
- **Estate for years** = A lease for a fixed, definite period (e.g., a 2-year lease).
- **Periodic tenancy** = Automatically renews for successive periods (month-to-month, year-to-year) unless either party gives notice to terminate.
- **Holdover tenant** = A tenant who stays after the lease expires without the landlord's consent.
- **Tenant at sufferance** = Another term for a holdover tenant -- they have no legal right to be there.
- **Security deposit** = Money paid by a tenant as security for performance of lease obligations. Usually refundable if the tenant complies with the lease terms.

#### Landlord's Rights and Duties:

- **Primary duty**: Deliver possession of the premises to the tenant on the lease inception date
- **Right to rent**: Landlord is entitled to receive rent as agreed
- **Remedies against a breaching tenant**:
  - Evict the tenant
  - Apply to a court for help
  - Seize the tenant's property and hold it (**distraint** = seizing a tenant's property for unpaid rent)
  - Impose a non-possessory lien on the tenant's property for rent due

**Constructive eviction** = When a landlord's actions (or inaction) make the property uninhabitable, effectively forcing the tenant out. The tenant can terminate the lease without penalty.

#### Tenant's Rights and Duties:

- **Primary right**: To occupy the premises
- **Primary duty**: Leave the premises in the same condition as when the lease started (except for reasonable wear and tear)

#### Liability of Parties:

- **Landlords** are liable for injuries to third parties if the injury results from the landlord's negligent acts or latent (hidden) defects on the premises
- **Tenants** are liable for injuries to third parties occurring in areas under the tenant's control

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: Landlord-tenant law has evolved significantly since 2008. Many jurisdictions enacted eviction moratoriums during the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2021), some of which have influenced permanent reforms. Security deposit regulations have been strengthened in many states (limits on amounts, required interest, itemized deductions). Several states and cities have adopted "just cause" eviction protections, rent stabilization measures, and mandatory habitability standards. Source-of-income discrimination protections (requiring landlords to accept housing vouchers) have also expanded.

## Review Questions & Answers

### Q1: Distinguish between the rights of ownership and possession. (pp. 7.3-7.4)

**Answer:** Ownership is a relationship between the owner and the rest of society that includes rights specific to the property. Possession is the exercise of custody or control over property, and is not, in itself, ownership. You can own without possessing (your car at the mechanic's shop), and you can possess without owning (borrowing a friend's book).

---

### Q2: What problems do the rules of accession and confusion address? (pp. 7.5-7.6)

**Answer:** Accession addresses the problem of one person adding value to another person's personal property without consent. Confusion addresses the problem of goods belonging to different owners being mingled so they cannot be separated. Both sets of rules determine who owns the resulting property.

---

### Q3: Explain what interests and rights a bailee has in property. (pp. 7.7-7.8)

**Answer:** A bailee has possession only and has the right to hold the property (possessory lien) as security for payment. A bailee can sue third parties who interfere with their possession.

---

### Q4: Explain the extent of a bailee's insurable interest. (p. 7.9)

**Answer:** The bailee has an insurable interest in goods because of the legal duty to care for them and return them to the bailor. A bailee must hold insurance proceeds paid for bailed property's damage in trust for the bailor. However, absent a statute or contract requirement, a bailee has no duty to obtain insurance on bailed goods.

---

### Q5: Compare the extent of liability of common carriers, hotelkeepers, and warehouse operators. (pp. 7.10-7.11)

**Answer:**
- **Common carriers**: Highest liability -- virtually insurers of goods. Liable from receipt at terminal until recipient has reasonable time to inspect and remove. Only 5 exceptions (acts of God, public enemy, shipper, public authority, inherent nature of goods).
- **Hotelkeepers**: Liable only for failure to exercise reasonable care in protecting guests' property. Can limit liability by providing safes for storing valuables.
- **Warehouse operators**: Do not insure stored goods and can limit liability in the warehouse receipt issued for goods received.

---

### Q6: Describe the three requirements for a gift. (p. 7.11)

**Answer:** (1) **Donative intent** -- the donor must intend to make a gift. (2) **Delivery** -- the donor must physically transfer possession. (3) **Acceptance** -- the donee must accept the gift.

---

### Q7: How are dower and curtesy rights affected by: (p. 7.13)
**a. A will?** Even though a spouse's will gives the surviving spouse less than the rightful dower or curtesy portion, the surviving spouse can elect to receive the dower portion.
**b. Divorce?** Divorce bars dower and curtesy rights.

---

### Q8: Distinguish between joint tenancies and tenancies in common. (pp. 7.12-7.14)

**Answer:** In **joint tenancies**, the estate goes entirely to the surviving joint tenant upon one owner's death; joint tenants hold equal shares; all conditions apply equally; and the same deed must name all joint tenants as owners. In **tenancies in common**, there is no survivorship; parties may own unequal shares; and parties do not need to derive their interests from the same deed or at the same time.

---

### Q9: What does a condominium unit owner own? (pp. 7.15-7.16)

**Answer:** A condominium unit owner owns an individual unit (or separate, defined area) as well as an undivided interest in common or public areas that serve all individual units.

---

### Q10: What essential terms must a contract for the sale of real estate contain? (p. 7.16)

**Answer:** Three essential elements: (1) The contract must be in writing. (2) Must list the essential terms -- description of the premises and the price. (3) Must list the nonessential terms, such as the time of closing.

---

### Q11: Would a buyer prefer a general warranty deed or a special warranty deed? (p. 7.17)

**Answer:** A buyer would prefer a **general warranty deed** because a special warranty deed does not protect the buyer against earlier defects in the property. The general warranty deed guarantees against ALL claims, not just those arising during the seller's ownership.

---

### Q12: What are the requirements of a deed? (p. 7.17)

**Answer:** The deed must: be in writing; be signed by a legally competent grantor; name the grantee; state the consideration; contain words of transfer; contain a property description; be dated; be delivered. Additional requirements may include grantor's signature under seal, witnesses, and notary acknowledgment.

---

### Q13: Why is the recording of a deed important? (pp. 7.17-7.18)

**Answer:** Without recording, no public notice exists that the transfer of real property has occurred. An unrecorded deed is not valid against good-faith purchasers or creditors who have no knowledge of the prior transfer.

---

### Q14: What rights does a mortgagee have against: (pp. 7.18-7.19)
**a. The owner?** A mortgagee has the right to foreclose against the owner if the loan is not paid when due, and to obtain deficiency judgments for the remainder of the loan after the property is sold.
**b. A subsequent purchaser?** A mortgagee may also collect deficient payments from a subsequent purchaser after foreclosure.

---

### Q15: In what situations are the following most suitable? (pp. 7.19-7.20)
**a. Trust deeds** -- Most suitable when large loans are involved.
**b. Land contracts** -- Used when buyers have poor or inadequate credit ratings or do not have enough money for down payments.

---

### Q16: What are the requirements to obtain title by adverse possession? (pp. 7.22-7.23)

**Answer:** (1) Exclusive possession and use in the usual way. (2) Open and obvious possession. (3) Adverse, hostile, and without the owner's permission. (4) Continuous for a statutory period (usually a lengthy period such as 20 years or more).

---

### Q17: Explain the tests for whether particular property is a fixture. (pp. 7.24-7.25)

**Answer:** Three tests: (1) The article cannot be removed without substantial injury to the realty. (2) The article is specially constructed or fitted for use in the building, or installed in the building to enable people to use the building. (3) The party who attached the item intended it to become part of the land or building.

---

### Q18: How can easements be created? (p. 7.25)

**Answer:** Easements can be created by express words, by implication, or by prescription (similar to adverse possession).

---

### Q19: Give some examples of profits a prendre. (p. 7.26)

**Answer:** The rights to mine coal, remove sand and gravel, or cut down trees are profits a prendre.

---

### Q20: What matters do zoning laws and building codes, respectively, typically control? (p. 7.27)

**Answer:** Zoning laws regulate building construction, occupancy, and land use according to a comprehensive plan. Building codes regulate technical construction details, such as electrical wiring and heating.

---

### Q21: What is the landlord's primary duty? (p. 7.29)

**Answer:** The landlord's primary duty is to deliver possession of the premises to the tenant on the lease's inception date.

---

### Q22: What are a landlord's remedies against a tenant? (pp. 7.29-7.30)

**Answer:** A landlord's remedies include: the right to evict the tenant, apply to a court for help, seize the tenant's property and hold it (distraint), and impose a nonpossessory lien on the tenant's property for unpaid rent.

---

### Q23: What are a tenant's primary rights and duties? (p. 7.30)

**Answer:** A tenant's primary right is the right to occupy the premises. A tenant's primary duty is to leave the premises in the same condition they were in at the lease inception, except for reasonable wear and tear.

---

### Q24: Who is liable for injuries to third parties on rented or leased property? (pp. 7.30-7.31)

**Answer:** Landlords are liable for injuries to third parties if the injury is the result of a landlord's negligent acts or latent defects on the premises. Tenants are liable for areas under their control.

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Q1: Common Carrier Bailment

**Scenario:** Felicia, the driver for Yale Company (a common carrier), parked her truck at a roadside diner. While she ate lunch, thieves broke into the locked truck and stole the contents.

**a. Based on general bailment principles, what is Yale Company's liability to the owners of the contents?**

**Answer:** This is a bailment for mutual benefit. Yale Company (through Felicia) was the bailee of the goods and had the duty to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances. If a bailee exercises such care, any loss or damage falls upon the bailors who hold title to the goods. However, as a common carrier, Yale is held to an even higher standard -- virtual insurer of the goods. Theft does not fall under the 5 exceptions (acts of God, public enemy, shipper, public authority, inherent nature of goods), so Yale is likely liable.

**b. Would your answer be different if the contract specifically provided the carrier was not liable for damage or loss not caused by its negligence?**

**Answer:** A contract might limit a common carrier's liability to a shipper, although the carrier might not escape liability for negligence completely. Since there was no negligence indicated here (the truck was locked), the contractual provision for release of liability would likely be effective.

---

### Application Q2: Marital Property & Deeds

**Scenario:** During his marriage, Harry purchased a home in his name only.

**a. In a common-law state:** If applicable state law allows a spouse to own property individually, Harry can sell with his signature only.

**b. In a community property state:** The wife's signature would be required because community property states imply that married couples jointly own all property acquired during marriage.

---

### Application Q3: Recording Priorities

**Scenario:** Doug borrowed $15,000 from his parents (unrecorded mortgage), then took a $150,000 mortgage from Bank Y (recorded), and later Jerry obtained a $5,000 judgment.

**Priority order (highest to lowest):**
1. **Bank Y** -- Recorded mortgage, first priority. An unrecorded mortgage is not valid against good-faith subsequent mortgagees without knowledge.
2. **Jerry's claim** -- Jerry won a lawsuit creating a lien. His claim takes priority over the parents' unrecorded mortgage.
3. **Doug's parents' claim** -- The parents failed to record, giving no public notice. Parties who acquire subsequent interests without notice should not suffer from the parents' inaction.

---

### Application Q4: Mechanic's Liens & Adverse Possession

**Scenario:** Jessica's father died 30 years ago. Her mother lived on the family farm until her recent death. Jessica is the sole beneficiary.

**a(1). Roofer's right to payment:** The roofer can obtain a mechanic's lien on the property. A mechanic's lien can be claimed by any person who contributes labor and material to the improvement of real property.

**a(2). Roofer's remedy if Jessica refuses to pay:** The roofer can foreclose, forcing a court sale of the property and receiving a share of the proceeds equal to the amount owed.

**b. Adverse possession by Matt:** Even though Matt lived in an outbuilding for 20 years, he did NOT have exclusive possession of the farm because Jessica's mother lived in the main farmhouse throughout that time. Matt's adverse possession claim would fail on this basis.

---

### Application Q5: Deed Restrictions

**Scenario:** In 2005, Bryan sold land to Oliver with deed restrictions prohibiting automobile garages and manufacture of glue, gun powder, or fertilizers.

**Answer:** Bryan can place restrictions on the use of the property as long as those restrictions are not discriminatory or otherwise unenforceable (such as requiring an illegal use). However, state law may limit the restrictions to a period of years, and changed economic conditions in the area can make restrictions unenforceable. For example, if the surrounding land has been developed into businesses including auto garages, the restriction might end. Similarly, if manufacturing glue or fertilizers becomes the most effective economic use due to changed conditions, a court might release the restriction.

## Exam Tips

### Must-Memorize Lists

**Three requirements for a valid gift:**
1. Donative intent
2. Delivery
3. Acceptance

**Four requirements for adverse possession:**
1. Exclusive possession
2. Open and obvious
3. Adverse/hostile (without permission)
4. Continuous for the statutory period

**Three tests for whether property is a fixture:**
1. Cannot be removed without substantial injury
2. Specially constructed or fitted for use in the building
3. Intent of the party who attached it

**Five exceptions to common carrier liability:**
1. Acts of God
2. Acts of public enemy
3. Acts of the shipper
4. Acts of public authority
5. Inherent nature of the goods

**Requirements for a valid deed:**
- Writing, competent grantor signs, names grantee, states consideration, words of transfer, property description, dated, delivered, (some states: seal, witnesses, notary)

### Likely Exam Traps

1. **Ownership vs. Possession** -- These are NOT the same thing. A bailee has possession but NOT ownership. Do not confuse them.

2. **Joint Tenancy vs. Tenancy in Common** -- The critical difference is SURVIVORSHIP. Joint tenancy = surviving owners get the deceased's share. Tenancy in common = the deceased's share goes to their heirs. Know which is which.

3. **General Warranty Deed vs. Special Warranty Deed** -- A general warranty protects against ALL defects; special warranty only protects against defects during the current owner's period.

4. **Quit-claim deeds provide NO guarantees** -- The grantor is only transferring whatever interest (if any) they might have. Could be zero.

5. **Trade fixtures belong to the TENANT** -- Unlike regular fixtures which become part of the real property, trade fixtures remain the tenant's property and can be removed.

6. **Common carriers are virtually insurers** -- They are liable for almost all loss. Do not confuse with hotelkeepers (reasonable care only) or warehouse operators (limited liability).

7. **Adverse possession requires hostility** -- If the owner gives permission, it is NOT adverse possession. Also, the possessor must be open about it (no secret occupation).

8. **Recording priority** -- First to record generally wins. An unrecorded interest loses to a good-faith purchaser without knowledge.

9. **Easement vs. License** -- An easement is permanent and runs with the land. A license is temporary and revocable.

10. **Constructive eviction** -- This is about the LANDLORD's actions making the property uninhabitable. It is a defense for the tenant, not an action by the landlord.

11. **Mortgagor vs. Mortgagee** -- Mortgagor = BORROWER (gives the mortgage). Mortgagee = LENDER (receives it). The "-or" suffix = the person doing the action (giving the mortgage).

12. **Confusion with intentional wrongdoing** -- If one party intentionally mingles goods, the INNOCENT party gets to claim the entire mixture. Harsh penalty.

## Quick-Reference Summary

**Personal Property:**
- Property = legal rights, not the physical thing
- Ownership = full rights; Possession = custody/control; Title = evidence of ownership
- Copyright = creative works; Patent = inventions
- Accession = adding value to another's property (good faith improver may keep it but must pay)
- Confusion = mingling goods (proportional shares if innocent; wrongdoer loses all)
- Bailment = transfer of possession, NOT ownership (bailor = owner, bailee = holder)
- Three bailment types: sole benefit of bailor (lowest care), mutual benefit (reasonable care), sole benefit of bailee (highest care)
- Common carriers = highest liability (5 exceptions only)
- Gift = donative intent + delivery + acceptance (irrevocable once complete)

**Real Property:**
- Fee simple = fullest ownership; Life estate = ownership for a lifetime
- Joint tenancy = right of survivorship; Tenancy in common = no survivorship
- Tenancy by the entirety = married couples only, both must consent
- Condominium = own your unit + share of common areas; Co-op = own stock, not real property
- Real estate contract: must be in writing, describe property, state price
- Deeds (best to worst): General warranty > Special warranty > Bargain-and-sale > Quit-claim
- Recording = public notice; first to record wins
- Mortgage: mortgagor (borrower) gives, mortgagee (lender) receives; remedy = foreclosure
- Trust deed = 3 parties (borrower, lender, trustee); faster foreclosure
- Land contract = seller finances; buyer gets deed only after full payment
- Mechanic's lien = for labor/material providers; remedy = forced court sale
- Adverse possession = exclusive + open + hostile + continuous for statutory period
- Fixture tests: cannot remove without injury, specially fitted, intent to make permanent
- Trade fixtures = tenant's property (removable)
- Easement = permanent right to use land; License = temporary, revocable permission
- Zoning controls land use; Building codes control construction quality
- Eminent domain = government takes land with fair compensation
- Landlord's duty = deliver possession; Tenant's duty = leave property in same condition
- Landlord remedies: eviction, court help, distraint, lien
- Landlord liable for negligent acts/latent defects; Tenant liable for areas they control
