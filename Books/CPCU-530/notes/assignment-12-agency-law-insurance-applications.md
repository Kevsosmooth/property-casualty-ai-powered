### A. Agents

**Key Concept:** An **insurance agent** is someone authorized to act on behalf of an insurer (the principal). In agency law, the insurer is the principal and the agent represents the insurer.

There are three types of insurance agents:

#### 1. General Agent
- **Definition in plain English:** An agent with the broadest authority of all agent types. A general agent can enter into insurance contracts, manage an office, hire staff, and handle a wide range of business on behalf of the insurer.
- **Think of it as:** The insurer's "regional manager" -- they can do almost anything the insurer could do in that territory.

> **Setup:** Mega Insurance Co. appoints Sarah as their general agent for the tri-state area.
> **What Happens:** Sarah has authority to bind coverage, issue policies, collect premiums, settle small claims, and appoint sub-agents -- all without calling the home office for permission each time.
> **Result:** Sarah's actions within her broad authority legally bind Mega Insurance as if the company itself acted.

#### 2. Special Agent
- **Definition in plain English:** An agent with LIMITED authority, restricted to specific tasks outlined by the insurer. This is the most common type of insurance agent today.
- **Think of it as:** An agent on a "short leash" -- they can only do what the insurer specifically says they can do.

> **Setup:** Tom is appointed as a special agent for ABC Insurance. His contract says he can solicit applications, collect initial premiums, and issue binders for auto insurance only.
> **What Happens:** A customer asks Tom to bind a homeowners policy.
> **Result:** Tom has NO authority to bind homeowners -- only auto. If he does it anyway, ABC Insurance may not be bound (unless apparent authority applies).

#### 3. Soliciting Agent
- **Definition in plain English:** The most limited type of agent. A soliciting agent can ONLY solicit applications for insurance, forward them to the insurer, and perform related activities. They CANNOT bind coverage or issue policies.
- **Think of it as:** Basically a "salesperson" -- they can find customers and take applications, but they cannot make promises about coverage.

> **Setup:** Angela works as a soliciting agent in Helen's insurance agency. Helen (a general agent) steps out for an hour.
> **What Happens:** A worried customer calls asking if her coverage is still active after a late premium payment. Angela reassures her, "I'm sure everything will be all right."
> **Result:** Angela had NO authority to affirm coverage. As a soliciting agent, she cannot bind the insurer. If the policy was actually lapsed, Angela may have personal liability for her unauthorized statement.

---

### B. Brokers

**Key Concept:** A **broker** is fundamentally different from an agent. While an agent represents the INSURER, a broker legally represents the CUSTOMER (the insurance buyer).

- **Definition in plain English:** A broker is an independent intermediary who shops the insurance market on behalf of the customer, finding the best coverage and price. The broker's loyalty is to the customer, not to any particular insurer.

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION for the exam:**

- **Agent** = represents the INSURER (principal)
- **Broker** = represents the CUSTOMER (buyer)

**But here is the tricky part:** A broker can sometimes act as the insurer's agent. This happens through **agency by estoppel** -- for example, if an insurer sends a broker numerous blank copies of its automobile policies and receives from the broker numerous copies of policies the broker has issued, the broker would be considered the insurer's agent by estoppel.

> **Setup:** Jane is an insurance broker. XYZ Insurance sends her blank policy forms, rate manuals, and regularly accepts the policies she writes on their paper.
> **What Happens:** Jane writes a homeowners policy for a customer on XYZ's forms. XYZ later tries to deny the policy, saying "Jane is just a broker, not our agent."
> **Result:** A court would likely find that XYZ created an agency by estoppel. By providing blank forms and consistently accepting Jane's business, XYZ made it look like Jane was their agent. XYZ is bound.

### B2. Surplus Lines Broker

**Surplus Lines Broker**: A specialized broker licensed to place coverage with **non-admitted (unlicensed) insurers** when coverage is not available in the standard admitted market. Surplus lines brokers must typically demonstrate that the coverage was "declined" by admitted insurers before placing it with a surplus lines carrier. They have additional regulatory requirements including filing and tax obligations.

> **Setup:** A fireworks manufacturer needs liability insurance, but no admitted insurer will write the policy due to the high risk.
> **What Happens:** A surplus lines broker can place this coverage with a non-admitted insurer (like a Lloyd's syndicate) that specializes in unusual risks.
> **Result:** The fireworks manufacturer gets the liability coverage it needs through the surplus lines market, and the broker handles the additional filing and tax requirements that come with non-admitted placements.

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: The Nonadmitted and Reinsurance Reform Act (NRRA), part of the Dodd-Frank Act (2010), significantly changed surplus lines regulation. Key changes: the home state of the insured now controls taxation and regulation of surplus lines transactions (ending multi-state tax allocation), and only the home state can require surplus lines premium taxes. This simplified compliance for surplus lines brokers operating across state lines.

### C. Producers' Authority

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: Many states have moved toward a unified **'producer' license** under the NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act, rather than maintaining separate 'agent' and 'broker' licenses. While the legal distinctions between agents and brokers remain important for exam purposes, in practice many states now use a single producer category.

The term **insurance producer** is the broad umbrella term covering both agents and brokers. A producer's authority determines what they can legally do on behalf of the insurer.

#### 1. Actual Authority
- **Definition in plain English:** The authority the insurer ACTUALLY gives the producer, either explicitly in writing (express authority) or by reasonable implication (implied authority).
- **Express authority** = What the contract/appointment letter specifically says the agent can do
- **Implied authority** = What is reasonably necessary to carry out the express authority

> **Setup:** An insurer's agency contract says Agent Mike can "sell and service auto insurance policies."
> **What Happens:** A customer asks Mike to issue a temporary binder while the formal policy is being processed.
> **Result:** Even though the contract does not specifically mention binders, issuing temporary binders is reasonably necessary to sell auto insurance. Mike has implied authority to issue the binder.

#### 2. Apparent Authority
- **Definition in plain English:** Authority that the INSURER (not the agent) creates by making a third party REASONABLY BELIEVE the agent has authority, even when the agent actually does not.
- **Key point:** Apparent authority comes from the PRINCIPAL'S actions, not the agent's claims.

> **Setup:** Friendly Insurance has always allowed Agent Dave to offer 15% multi-policy discounts. The insurer quietly revokes this authority but never tells customers.
> **What Happens:** Dave continues offering the discount to a new customer, who relies on it.
> **Result:** The insurer is bound by the discount because of apparent authority. The insurer created the appearance that Dave could offer discounts, and the customer reasonably relied on that appearance.

**How an insurer can inadvertently create apparent authority (and get bound to unauthorized acts):**
- Ratifying (accepting the benefits of) an agent's unauthorized acts
- Failing to object when learning of unauthorized acts
- Consistently allowing certain practices that go beyond the written contract

> **EXAM TRAP:** An insurer can ratify the acts of an insurance agent who lacks the authority to bind the insurer, and thus be bound. Ratification = the insurer says "OK, we'll accept that" AFTER the unauthorized act.

### D. Extent of Producers' Authority

#### 1. Producers' Status
- General agents have the BROADEST authority
- Special agents are the most COMMON type today
- Soliciting agents have the most LIMITED authority
- A broker represents the customer, NOT the insurer (unless agency by estoppel applies)

#### 2. Producers' Notice and Knowledge

**This is a heavily tested concept:**

- **The Imputation Rule:** If a producer knows something, that knowledge is **imputed to** (legally treated as known by) the insurer-principal.
- In plain English: If your agent knows it, the law says YOU (the insurer) know it too.

> **Setup:** Agent Lisa knows that applicant John has had 3 DUI convictions. John's application does not mention them. Lisa submits the application anyway.
> **What Happens:** The insurer issues the policy. Later, a claim occurs and the insurer discovers the DUIs.
> **Result:** The insurer CANNOT deny the claim based on the DUI non-disclosure. Lisa knew about the DUIs, and her knowledge is imputed to the insurer. The insurer is deemed to have known.

**TWO EXCEPTIONS where the insurer is NOT bound by the producer's knowledge:**

1. **When no actual agency relationship exists** between the producer and insurer
2. **When the agent has supplied false information** -- i.e., the agent is acting fraudulently AGAINST the insurer's interests (colluding with the applicant)

> **Setup:** Sally, a soliciting agent, is told by applicant Albert that he has a serious health condition. Sally and Albert AGREE to hide this from the insurer.
> **What Happens:** Sally submits the application without the health information. The insurer issues the life insurance policy.
> **Result:** Sally's knowledge is NOT imputed to the insurer because Sally conspired to defraud the insurer. She abandoned her role as agent and violated her duty of loyalty. The insurer can void the policy.

#### 3. Producer's Authority to Bind Coverage

- **Oral insurance contracts are VALID.** Insurance contracts do not have to be in writing.
- Why? Because the need for immediate insurance coverage arises frequently, and the lengthy underwriting process needed for a written contract is not time-efficient.
- A general agent typically has authority to bind coverage orally.
- A special agent's binding authority depends on what the insurer specifically authorizes.
- A soliciting agent generally CANNOT bind coverage.

> **Setup:** A customer calls Agent Rodriguez at 4:55 PM on Friday and says, "I just bought a new car. Can you put coverage on it right now?" Rodriguez says, "You're covered."
> **What Happens:** The customer gets into an accident Saturday morning. No written policy has been issued yet.
> **Result:** If Rodriguez had binding authority (actual or apparent), the oral binder is valid. The insurer must cover the claim.

#### 4. Appointment of Subagents

- **General rule:** An insurance agent cannot delegate agency duties to another person when the duties involve the producer's individual care, skill, and judgment.
- **Exception:** Other duties CAN be delegated to subagents (clerical work, routine tasks).
- Subagents are typically appointed by general agents.

---

### E. Termination of Producer Authority

**How does an insurance agency relationship end?**

- Usually terminates under **circumstances specified in the contract** (expiration date, mutual agreement, etc.)
- Can also terminate by an **act of one of the parties** that the other might reasonably construe as showing intent to terminate
- The insurer can revoke the agent's authority
- The agent can renounce the agency

**Important:** After termination, the producer's apparent authority may continue until third parties are notified. The insurer should notify customers that the agent no longer represents them.

> **Setup:** Mega Insurance terminates Agent Bob's contract but does not notify Bob's customers.
> **What Happens:** Bob continues writing policies using old Mega Insurance forms. Customer Carol buys a policy from Bob, not knowing he was terminated.
> **Result:** Mega Insurance may be bound by apparent authority. Carol had no reason to know Bob was terminated. The insurer should have notified customers.

### F. Producers' Duties and Liability

#### 1. Duties and Liability to Insurance Customers

**Standard of Care:** An insurance producer must adhere to a **standard of reasonable care and skill** in relation to customers, even those who have little or no knowledge of insurance matters.

**What does this mean in practice?**

- The producer must exercise the care and skill that a reasonably competent insurance professional would exercise
- The producer must understand the coverages they sell
- The producer must follow through on requests for coverage

**Can the customer sue the agent?** YES, but they must prove that the producer **agreed or undertook** to obtain insurance coverage.

> **Setup:** Customer Maria asks Agent Frank to get her a commercial liability policy. Frank says, "I'll take care of it." Frank then forgets to submit the application.
> **What Happens:** Maria's business is sued, and she discovers she has no coverage.
> **Result:** Frank is liable. He undertook the obligation to procure coverage and failed. Maria can sue Frank for damages.

**BUT -- important nuance:**
An agent who promised a policy, contacted the insurer on the customer's behalf, and then advised the customer of the insurer's decision NOT to provide coverage would have **no liability**. The agent followed through -- the insurer just said no.

**Duty to Determine Insurer's Solvency:**
- The producer SHOULD make reasonable attempts to inquire into prospective insurers' solvency
- The producer should disclose to the customer any information revealing a weak financial condition
- This is NOT an absolute guarantee -- the producer must make "reasonable" efforts

**Duty to Advise About Insurance Needs:**
- Generally, an agent has a duty to advise a client about insurance needs when **the client has inadequate or no insurance coverage** for an unanticipated event, OR when the insured incorrectly believes something was covered
- Some courts find this duty exists; others are more restrictive

> **Setup:** Long-time customer Bill asks Agent Sarah to "make sure I'm fully covered." Bill's business has grown significantly, but Sarah never reviews or updates his policy limits.
> **What Happens:** Bill suffers a major loss that exceeds his outdated policy limits.
> **Result:** Sarah may be liable for failing to advise Bill about his inadequate coverage, especially given their long-standing relationship and Bill's reliance on her expertise.

**Insured's Failure to Read the Policy:**
- Some state courts do NOT allow the "failure to read" defense
- Reasoning: The customer has the right to rely on the producer's expertise, and policy language is often difficult to understand
- This is a producer-UNFRIENDLY rule -- producers cannot simply say "the customer should have read the policy"

#### 2. Defenses to Liability

Producers may defend against liability claims by arguing:

- **Customer never requested coverage**: "You never asked me for flood insurance."
  > Example: A homeowner sues their agent after a flood, claiming the agent should have recommended flood coverage. If the agent can show the homeowner never requested flood coverage and the agent had no special duty to recommend it, this is a valid defense.

- **Producer fulfilled all obligations**: "I did exactly what you asked."
  > Example: A business owner asks for $1M in liability coverage. The agent procures exactly that. When a $3M claim hits, the owner sues the agent for not recommending higher limits. The agent can show they fulfilled the customer's specific request.

- **Customer's contributory negligence**: "Your own carelessness contributed to the loss."
  > Example: An agent sends a renewal notice 60 days before expiration. The customer ignores it, lets the policy lapse, and then has a loss. The customer's failure to act on the renewal notice is contributory negligence.

#### 3. Duties to Third Parties

**Key Rule:** A third party who intends to sue an insurance customer CANNOT sue a producer who wrongfully failed to assist the customer in obtaining insurance coverage for the event that is the subject of the suit.

> **Setup:** A pedestrian is injured by Driver Dan. Dan's Agent forgot to renew Dan's auto liability insurance.
> **What Happens:** The pedestrian wants to sue the Agent for failing to get Dan insured.
> **Result:** The pedestrian CANNOT sue the agent. The agent's duty was to Dan (the customer), not to random third parties. The pedestrian must sue Dan directly.

#### 4. Duties and Liability to Insurer

- The producer owes **fiduciary duties** to the insurer (the principal)
- Must handle premiums properly
- Must follow the insurer's underwriting guidelines
- Must comply with the insurer's instructions

**Producer's liability for failure to comply with insurer instructions:**
- If a producer fails to comply with specific directions (like canceling a policy), the producer is liable to the insurer for the **full amount of the loss** the insurer must cover.

> **Setup:** The insurer tells Agent Pat to cancel a high-risk commercial policy immediately. Pat delays and does not cancel it.
> **What Happens:** A covered loss occurs before Pat gets around to canceling.
> **Result:** Pat is personally liable to the insurer for the full amount of the claim the insurer has to pay.

### G. Errors and Omissions Coverage

**Errors and Omissions (E&O) Liability Policy:**
- **Definition in plain English:** A professional liability insurance policy that protects insurance producers against claims arising from their mistakes or failures in performing professional duties.
- This is essentially "malpractice insurance" for insurance agents and brokers.
- Covers claims like: failing to procure requested coverage, placing coverage with an insolvent insurer, failing to advise about coverage gaps, making misrepresentations about policy terms.

> **E&O Claim Example:**
> **Setup:** A business owner asks their agent to add an umbrella policy with $2M in coverage. The agent says "I'll take care of it" but forgets to submit the application.
> **What Happens:** Three months later, a delivery truck owned by the business causes a multi-vehicle accident with $1.8M in damages. The underlying auto policy only covers $500K.
> **Result:** The business is exposed for $1.3M that the umbrella policy would have covered. The business owner sues the agent for negligence. The agent's E&O policy responds to cover the agent's liability for the gap the agent negligently created.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] E&O coverage requirements have expanded significantly since 2008. Many states now require minimum E&O coverage for licensed producers. Cyber liability and data breach exposures are now common E&O add-ons.

### H. Insurer's Liability for Producer's Acts and Omissions

**Key Concept:** Sometimes the INSURER becomes liable for the producer's mistakes.

- This happens when a customer sues the insurer in a lawsuit alleging that **the producer acted on the insurer's behalf**, and the insurer is therefore responsible for the producer's conduct.
- Under **respondeat superior** (from Assignment 11), if the producer is the insurer's agent acting within the scope of authority, the insurer is vicariously liable.

> **Setup:** Agent Tom, acting within his authority as ABC Insurance's agent, incorrectly tells a customer that flood damage is covered under their homeowners policy.
> **What Happens:** A flood occurs. The customer files a claim. ABC Insurance denies it (flood is excluded).
> **Result:** The customer sues ABC Insurance directly, arguing that Tom (ABC's agent) misrepresented the coverage. The insurer may be held liable because Tom acted on their behalf.

## PART 2: CLAIM REPRESENTATIVES

---

### A. Classification of Claim Representatives

There are several types of claim representatives (adjusters):

#### 1. Staff Claim Representative
- **Definition in plain English:** An adjuster who is a direct EMPLOYEE of the insurance company.
- Works exclusively for one insurer
- Most common type used by insurers for routine claims

#### 2. Independent Adjuster
- **Definition in plain English:** A self-employed adjuster or an employee of an adjusting firm who handles claims for MULTIPLE insurers on a contract basis.
- Not an employee of any one insurer
- Often used for catastrophe claims or in geographic areas where the insurer has no local staff

> A self-insured company most likely would work with an **independent adjuster** (since they do not have their own claims staff the way an insurance company would).

#### 3. Public Adjuster
- **Definition in plain English:** An adjuster hired by the INSURED (policyholder) to represent the insured's interests in a claim against the insurer.
- Works for the customer, NOT the insurance company
- Typically takes a percentage of the settlement as a fee

---

### B. Claim Settlement Authority

#### 1. Actual Authority
Adjusters derive their authority from:
- Employment agreements
- Company manuals
- Insurer's memoranda
- Supervisor's verbal instructions
- Express authority grants (e.g., "you can settle claims up to $10,000")

#### 2. Apparent Authority
- An adjuster can settle a claim under **implied authority** when the adjuster reasonably believes the insurer-principal wants them to take a certain action, **despite the lack of specific instructions.**
- An adjuster can also settle a claim by **express authority** when following instructions in an employment agreement, a company manual, an insurer's memorandum, or a supervisor's verbal instructions.

**Critical Exam Point:**
> A claim adjuster's statement to an insured motorist involved in an accident, "We'll handle your claim," does NOT, by itself, bind the insurer to pay the claim. It is simply an acknowledgment that the claim will be processed, not a promise of payment.

#### 3. Waiver and Estoppel in Insurance Claims

Claim representatives must be careful not to inadvertently waive policy defenses or create estoppel.

- **Waiver**: If an insurer's claim representative acts in a way that voluntarily gives up a known right (e.g., continuing to investigate a claim without reserving rights when there is a clear coverage defense), the insurer may be deemed to have waived that defense.
- **Estoppel**: If the insurer's conduct leads the insured to reasonably rely on a representation (e.g., telling the insured "don't worry, you're covered" when there is actually an exclusion), the insurer may be estopped from later denying coverage.

**Key Protection Tools**:
- **Reservation of Rights Letter**: A written notice from the insurer to the insured stating that the insurer will investigate and possibly defend a claim, but RESERVES the right to later deny coverage based on specific policy provisions. This prevents waiver.
- **Nonwaiver Agreement**: A signed agreement between the insurer and insured acknowledging that the insurer's investigation does not waive any policy defenses. Requires the insured's signature (unlike a reservation of rights letter, which is unilateral).

> **Setup:** A claim comes in that may involve an excluded peril.
> **What Happens:** The adjuster should IMMEDIATELY send a reservation of rights letter before investigating. If the adjuster investigates for 6 months without reserving rights, a court may rule the insurer waived the exclusion defense.
> **Result:** By failing to reserve rights promptly, the insurer loses the ability to deny coverage based on the exclusion -- even if the exclusion clearly applies. The reservation of rights letter is the insurer's most important protective tool.

### C. Claim Representatives' Duties and Liability

#### 1. Duties to Insurers
- Must act within their authority
- Must follow company procedures and manuals
- Must exercise reasonable care and skill in investigating and settling claims
- An insurer's manual that sets appropriate procedures for claim management relates to the adjuster's **duty to follow instructions**

#### 2. Duties to Insureds and to Third-Party Claimants
- Must act in good faith
- Must investigate claims promptly and thoroughly
- Must not misrepresent policy provisions
- Must make fair settlement offers

**Unfair Claim Settlement Practices Act:**
- **Definition in plain English:** A state law (based on the NAIC model act) that sets rules for how insurers and their claim representatives must handle claims. Violations can result in regulatory penalties.
- Typically enforced by the **state department of insurance**
- The unfair claim settlement practices acts regulate **insurers'** conduct, and redress should be against the insurers. Only the insurer and insured have a relationship under the policy -- the claim representative is NOT a party to that agreement.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Most states have adopted updated versions of the NAIC Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act. Many states have added specific timeframes for claim acknowledgment (often 15 days), investigation (30 days), and payment (varies by state). Some states now allow direct private causes of action against insurers.

**First-Party Bad Faith Lawsuit:**
- **Definition in plain English:** A lawsuit by an INSURED against their OWN insurer for unreasonably denying, delaying, or underpaying a legitimate claim.
- Damages can include: **compensatory damages, emotional distress, attorney's fees, and sometimes PUNITIVE damages**
- This is a powerful weapon for policyholders against insurers that mistreat their own customers

**Why most jurisdictions do NOT allow personal lawsuits against claim representatives for alleged unfair claim settlement practices:**
- Because the claim representative's actions are on behalf of the insurer
- The proper defendant is the insurer, not the individual adjuster
- **Exception:** A claim representative CAN be personally liable if their actions were **willful, intentional, or reckless**

---

### D. Claim Representatives' Personal Liability

**Excess Liability:**
- **Definition in plain English:** When an insurer is held liable for an amount EXCEEDING the policy limits because the claim representative (or insurer) acted in bad faith in failing to settle a claim within policy limits.

> **Setup:** Insured has a $100,000 liability policy. A claimant offers to settle for $95,000 (within policy limits). The adjuster unreasonably refuses.
> **What Happens:** The case goes to trial and the verdict is $500,000.
> **Result:** The insurer may be liable for the full $500,000 (not just $100,000) because of the adjuster's bad faith failure to settle within limits. This is "excess liability."

A **default judgment** can also create problems -- this occurs when the insurer fails to defend its insured, and the claimant wins by default.

## Review Questions & Answers

**1. Who has the broadest authority of all agents to enter into insurance contracts?**
A **general agent** has the broadest authority of all agents to enter into insurance contracts. (p. 12.4)

**2. What type of agent is typical of most of today's insurance agents?**
A **special agent** is typical of most of today's insurance agents. (p. 12.4)

**3. What specific actions by an insurer could bind the insurer to the unauthorized acts of an insurance agent?**
An insurer can **ratify the acts** of an insurance agent who lacks the authority to bind the insurer, and thus be bound. Ratification means the insurer accepts or endorses the unauthorized act after it occurs. (p. 12.5)

**4. Give an example of a broker acting as an insurer's agent.**
If an insurer sends a broker numerous blank copies of its automobile policies and receives from the broker numerous copies of policies the broker has issued, the broker would be the insurer's agent **by estoppel**. The insurer's conduct makes it appear the broker has authority. (p. 12.7)

**5. If a producer knows an insurance applicant is a bad risk, does the insurer-principal also know this?**
**YES.** If a producer knows an applicant for insurance is a bad risk, that knowledge is **imputed to the insurer-principal**. The law treats the insurer as having the same knowledge as its agent. (p. 12.10)

**6. In what two situations is an insurer not bound by the producer's knowledge?**
1. When **no actual agency relationship exists** between the producer and insurer
2. When **the agent has supplied false information** (colluding with the applicant against the insurer's interests) (p. 12.10)

**7. Explain why oral insurance contracts are valid.**
Oral insurance contracts are valid because the **need for immediate insurance coverage arises frequently**, and the lengthy underwriting process needed for a written contract is not time-efficient. The Statute of Frauds generally does not require insurance contracts to be in writing. (p. 12.12)

**8. Can an agent delegate any agency duties to another person?**
The general rule is that an insurance agent **cannot delegate** duties to another when the duties involve the producer's **individual care, skill, and judgment**. However, **other duties** (routine, clerical) can be delegated to subagents. (p. 12.13)

**9. How does an insurance agency relationship terminate?**
An insurance agency relationship usually terminates under **circumstances specified in the contract**. The relationship can also terminate by **an act of one of the parties** that the other might reasonably construe as showing the intent to terminate. (pp. 12.14-12.15)

**10. What standard of care and skill is required of insurance producers?**
An insurance producer must adhere to a **standard of reasonable care and skill** in relation to customers, who may have little or no knowledge of insurance matters. This is judged by what a reasonably competent insurance professional would do. (p. 12.16)

**11. If an insurance agent promises a customer a policy that the insurer later denies, can the customer sue the agent?**
To sue the agent, the customer must prove that the producer **agreed or undertook** to obtain insurance coverage. An agent who assumes the obligation to procure insurance could be liable for not doing so. HOWEVER, an agent who promised a policy, contacted the insurer on the customer's behalf, and then **advised the customer of the insurer's decision not to provide coverage** would have **no liability** -- they fulfilled their obligation. (p. 12.18)

**12. Explain the extent of an agent's duty to determine an insurer's solvency.**
The producer should make **reasonable attempts** to inquire into prospective insurers' solvency and should disclose to the customer any information revealing a weak financial condition. This is not an absolute guarantee but a duty of reasonable inquiry. (pp. 12.20-12.21)

**13. Under what circumstances does an agent have a duty to advise a client about the client's insurance needs?**
An agent has a duty to advise a client about insurance needs when the client has **inadequate or no insurance coverage** for an unanticipated event, or when the insured incorrectly believed something was covered. (pp. 12.21-12.22)

**14. Explain why an insured's failure to read the policy is not always an adequate insurer defense against liability.**
Some state courts **do not allow** this defense, reasoning that the customer has the **right to rely on the producer's expertise**, and that **policy language is often difficult to understand**. The producer's professional duty may override the customer's duty to read. (pp. 12.26-12.27)

**15. What right of action, if any, does an injured motorist have against a negligent motorist's insurance producer for failure to acquire liability insurance for the motorist?**
**No right of action.** A third party who intends to sue an insurance customer **cannot sue a producer** who wrongfully failed to assist the customer in obtaining insurance coverage for the event that is the subject of the suit. The duty runs to the customer, not to third parties. (p. 12.27)

**16. What is the extent of a producer's liability to an insurer for failure to comply with the insurer's instructions to cancel a policy?**
A producer who fails to comply with specific directions is liable to the insurer for the **full amount of the loss** the insurer must cover. (p. 12.31)

**17. Is an insurer ever liable for a producer's errors and omissions? Explain.**
**Yes.** Sometimes the insurer becomes liable for the producer's acts or omissions, such as when a customer sues the insurer in a lawsuit **alleging that the producer acted on the insurer's behalf**, and the insurer is therefore responsible for the producer's conduct. (p. 12.32)

**18. Would a self-insured company most likely work with a staff adjuster or an independent adjuster?**
A self-insured company most likely would work with an **independent adjuster**. (Self-insured companies typically do not have their own claims department staff.) (p. 12.34)

**19. What types of authority does an adjuster have to settle a claim?**
An adjuster can settle a claim under **implied authority** when the adjuster reasonably believes the insurer wants the adjuster to take a certain action despite the lack of specific instructions. An adjuster can also settle by **express authority** when following instructions in an employment agreement, a company manual, an insurer's memorandum, or a supervisor's verbal instructions. (pp. 12.35-12.37)

**20. A claim adjuster tells an insured motorist involved in an accident, "We'll handle your claim." Does this statement bind the insurer to pay the claim?**
**No.** A claim adjuster's statement "We'll handle your claim" does **not**, by itself, bind the insurer to pay the claim. It is merely an acknowledgment that the claim will be processed, not a guarantee of payment. (p. 12.37)

**21. An insurer gives an adjuster a manual that sets appropriate procedures for claim management. To which adjuster's duty does such a manual relate?**
An insurer's manual that sets appropriate procedures for claim management relates to the adjuster's **duty to follow instructions**. (p. 12.38)

**22. Who typically has authority to enforce state unfair claim settlement practices acts?**
Typically, the **state department of insurance** enforces a state's unfair claim settlement practices act. (p. 12.40)

**23. What damages can be recovered in a first-party bad faith lawsuit?**
Damages for **emotional distress** and for **attorney's fees**, as well as **compensatory damages**, can be recovered in a first-party bad faith lawsuit. Sometimes **punitive damages** are also imposed. (p. 12.40)

**24. Why do most jurisdictions not allow personal lawsuits against claim representatives for alleged unfair claim settlement practices?**
The unfair claim settlement practices acts regulate **insurers' conduct**, and redress should be against the insurers. Only the insurer and the insured have a relationship under the insurance policy that is the subject of the claim, and the **claim representative is not a party to that agreement**. Exception: personal liability may apply if actions were willful, intentional, or reckless. (p. 12.44)

## Application Questions & Answers

### Question 1: Angela the Soliciting Agent

**Scenario:** Angela is a soliciting agent in an insurance agency. Her boss, Helen (the general agent), steps out for about an hour. A worried customer calls saying her husband just had a bad auto accident, and their premium was "a few days late." Angela reassures the customer, "Oh, I'm sure it's OK. Helen isn't here, but I'm sure everything will be all right." The customer, relieved, goes to the hospital.

**a. Did Angela bind the insurer?**
Angela, as a soliciting agent, **did not have the power** to do anything beyond soliciting applications, forwarding them to the insurer, and performing other related activities. However, as to the worried customer, it may not matter because Angela has **apparent authority** to affirm that coverage is in effect. Although the customer probably would not keep her husband out of the hospital if Angela had replied negatively about coverage, she has **relied on Angela's apparent authority**. This question would not arise unless the insurer denied the claim. No evidence appeared that the insurer has given any required notice of policy cancellation, and the payment was only "a few days late."

**b. What is Angela's liability, if any?**
If there is a real problem with coverage in this case, and the insurer places liability on Helen, then the customer could sue Angela. Angela gave an unauthorized assurance about coverage that she had no authority to give.

**c. What is Helen's liability, if any?**
Helen is liable for Angela's exercise of **apparent authority**. As the general agent who left Angela in charge, Helen bears responsibility for the actions Angela took while Helen was absent. Helen's leaving Angela to handle calls created the appearance that Angela could speak on the agency's behalf.

### Question 2: Sally and Albert's Conspiracy

**Scenario:** Sally, a life insurance soliciting agent, is informed by Albert (an applicant) of facts that would cause the insurer to either reject Albert or increase his premium rate. Sally tells Albert about the problem, and they agree NOT to inform the insurer.

**Is the insurer on notice of those facts, on the theory that an agent's knowledge is imputed to the principal?**
**No.** Sally and Albert have conspired to misrepresent information to the insurer. In so doing, Sally has **abandoned her role as an agent** and has committed fraud against the insurer, in league with Albert, and in violation of her **duty of loyalty** to the insurer. The information Sally and Albert withheld is **NOT imputed to the insurer**. The insurer can void the policy.

### Question 3: Delegating Cancellation Notice

**Scenario:** An insurer directed its agent to notify the insured under a particular policy that the insurer was canceling the policy. The insurance agent instructed the agency secretary to notify the insured. Later, an allegation arose that this notice was not effective to cancel the policy because an insurer's agent had not given it.

**Was this correct?**
**This is a gray area.** If the notice is considered a ministerial/clerical task (simply delivering a pre-written notice), then delegation to the secretary may be acceptable. However, if providing the cancellation notice is considered part of the agent's professional duty requiring care and judgment, then the delegation would be improper and the notice might indeed be ineffective. The key is whether this duty involved the agent's individual care, skill, and judgment.

### Question 4: Fred's Error and Karl's Failure to Read

**Scenario:** Karl asked Fred, an insurance agent, to obtain specific liability coverage. Because of an error, the written policy did not contain certain requested coverages. Fred was unaware of the error and delivered the policy to Karl, giving full assurance that the policy was written as requested. Karl did not read the policy. A loss occurred that the insurer would not cover.

**What, if any, is the liability of Fred and Karl?**
- **Fred** is liable for his error. He had a duty to ensure the policy matched Karl's request and to deliver the correct coverage. His assurance that the policy was "written as requested" when it was not makes him liable for the uncovered loss.
- **Karl's failure to read the policy** is NOT necessarily a valid defense for Fred. Many courts hold that the customer has the right to rely on the producer's expertise and that policy language is often difficult to understand. Fred cannot escape liability by saying "Karl should have read it."

### Question 5: Jose the Independent Adjuster

**Scenario:** Jose is an independent adjuster who adjusts claims sent to him by several insurers. For what activities toward settling the following claims can Jose incur reasonable expenses that will probably get insurer approval?

**a. An auto collision in which two people suffered serious injuries:**
Jose can incur expenses for investigating the accident scene, obtaining police reports, interviewing witnesses, getting medical records, hiring accident reconstruction experts, and taking recorded statements. Serious injury auto collisions require thorough investigation, and insurers will approve reasonable expenses for these activities.

**b. Destruction of a dwelling by a fire:**
Jose can incur expenses for investigating the fire scene (possibly hiring a fire investigator/origin-and-cause expert), documenting the damage with photographs, obtaining repair/replacement estimates, reviewing the insured's inventory of damaged property, and verifying the policy terms. A total dwelling loss is significant and warrants thorough investigation expenses.

## Exam Tips: Common Traps and What to Memorize

### TRAP 1: Agent vs. Broker -- Who Represents Whom?
- **Agent = represents the INSURER**
- **Broker = represents the CUSTOMER**
- But a broker CAN become the insurer's agent by estoppel if the insurer treats them like one

### TRAP 2: "We'll Handle Your Claim" Does NOT Mean "We'll Pay"
- A claim adjuster saying "we'll handle your claim" is NOT a promise to pay
- It is only an acknowledgment that the claim will be processed
- This is a frequently tested distinction

### TRAP 3: Imputation of Knowledge Has Exceptions
- Generally: Agent knows it = Insurer knows it
- Exception 1: No actual agency relationship
- Exception 2: Agent is colluding/committing fraud against the insurer
- Memorize both exceptions -- they love testing exception 2

### TRAP 4: Soliciting Agent's Authority is EXTREMELY Limited
- Soliciting agents can ONLY: solicit applications, forward them, and do related activities
- They CANNOT: bind coverage, settle claims, or affirm that coverage is in effect
- But APPARENT authority can still apply if the insurer creates the appearance of broader authority

### TRAP 5: Third Parties Cannot Sue the Agent
- If an injured third party wants to sue someone because the at-fault driver was uninsured, they CANNOT sue the driver's insurance agent for failing to procure coverage
- The agent's duty runs to the CUSTOMER, not to third parties

### TRAP 6: Producer's Liability for Ignoring Insurer Instructions
- If the insurer says "cancel this policy" and the agent does not, the agent is liable for the FULL AMOUNT of any subsequent loss
- Not partial liability -- FULL liability

### TRAP 7: Oral Insurance Contracts ARE Valid
- No writing requirement for insurance contracts (generally)
- Oral binders are enforceable
- The Statute of Frauds generally does not apply to insurance contracts

### TRAP 8: "Failure to Read the Policy" Defense
- Many courts do NOT accept this defense against the producer
- Customers can rely on the producer's expertise
- Policy language is often too complex for laypeople

### TRAP 9: Who Can Be Sued Under Unfair Claim Settlement Practices Acts?
- The INSURER can be sued, not the individual claim representative
- Exception: if the claim rep acted willfully, intentionally, or recklessly
- Enforced by the state department of insurance

### TRAP 10: First-Party Bad Faith Damages
- Can include: compensatory, emotional distress, attorney's fees
- Can SOMETIMES include PUNITIVE damages
- "First-party" = the insured suing their own insurer (not a third party)

### TRAP 11: Waiver vs. Estoppel -- Know the Difference
- **Waiver** = insurer VOLUNTARILY gives up a known right (e.g., investigating without reserving rights)
- **Estoppel** = insurer's CONDUCT causes the insured to RELY on a representation (e.g., "you're covered")
- **Protection**: Reservation of rights letter (unilateral) or nonwaiver agreement (signed by both parties)
- If the adjuster investigates without reserving rights, the insurer may LOSE the right to deny coverage

### What to Memorize for the Exam
- The three types of agents and their authority levels (general > special > soliciting)
- Agent vs. broker distinction
- Two exceptions to imputation of knowledge
- "We'll handle your claim" is NOT a promise to pay
- Third parties cannot sue the agent for failure to procure coverage
- Producer liability = full amount of loss for failure to follow insurer instructions
- Oral contracts are valid
- Types of claim representatives (staff, independent, public)
- First-party bad faith damages can include punitive damages
- Unfair claim settlement practices acts are enforced by state DOI against INSURERS
- Reservation of rights letter vs. nonwaiver agreement -- know when to use each
- Surplus lines brokers place coverage with non-admitted insurers

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Insurance Producer Types
- **General Agent** -- Broadest authority, can bind coverage, manage operations, appoint sub-agents
- **Special Agent** -- Limited authority per contract, MOST COMMON type today
- **Soliciting Agent** -- Most limited, can only solicit/forward applications, CANNOT bind
- **Broker** -- Represents the CUSTOMER, not the insurer (but can become insurer's agent by estoppel)
- **Surplus Lines Broker** -- Places coverage with non-admitted insurers when standard market unavailable

### Authority Types
- **Express Authority** -- Written in the contract/appointment
- **Implied Authority** -- Reasonably necessary to carry out express authority
- **Apparent Authority** -- Created by the PRINCIPAL'S actions making third parties believe agent has authority
- **Ratification** -- Insurer accepts unauthorized act after the fact, becoming bound

### Knowledge Imputation
- Agent's knowledge = Insurer's knowledge (general rule)
- NOT imputed when: (1) no actual agency relationship, (2) agent is committing fraud against insurer

### Producer Duties
- Reasonable care and skill
- Procure requested coverage
- Investigate insurer solvency (reasonable efforts)
- Advise about coverage gaps (in some circumstances)
- Follow insurer instructions (or face full liability for losses)
- Fiduciary duty to insurer

### Claim Representatives
- **Staff** = insurer's employee
- **Independent** = works for multiple insurers on contract
- **Public** = hired by the insured
- "We'll handle it" does NOT equal "we'll pay"
- Authority: implied (reasonable belief) + express (manuals, instructions, agreements)

### Waiver and Estoppel Protections
- **Reservation of Rights Letter** = unilateral notice preserving insurer's right to deny coverage
- **Nonwaiver Agreement** = signed by both parties, insured acknowledges investigation does not waive defenses
- Send IMMEDIATELY when a coverage question arises -- delay can result in waiver

### Liability Framework
- Producer liable to CUSTOMER for failure to procure coverage (if they undertook the obligation)
- Producer liable to INSURER for failure to follow instructions (full amount of loss)
- Third parties CANNOT sue producer for customer's lack of coverage
- Insurer liable for producer's acts when producer acted on insurer's behalf
- First-party bad faith: compensatory + emotional distress + attorney fees + possible punitive damages
- Unfair claim practices: enforced by state DOI against INSURERS, not individual adjusters (exception: willful/intentional/reckless conduct)

### E&O Coverage
- Professional liability insurance for producers
- Covers mistakes in professional duties
- Essential protection for all insurance professionals
