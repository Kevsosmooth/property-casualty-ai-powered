### SECTION 1: The U.S. Legal System

#### A. Civil-Law System

**What it is in plain English**: A legal system built on a comprehensive written code (a giant rulebook of statutes). Judges look up the answer in the code rather than relying on what previous judges decided. Think of it like a detailed employee handbook -- if a situation arises, you check the handbook.

**Where it's used**: Louisiana (the only U.S. state with a civil-law heritage, due to French influence). Most of continental Europe, Latin America, and parts of Asia use civil-law systems.

**Real-World Example**:
- **The Setup**: A business dispute arises in France over a broken contract.
- **What Happens**: The judge consults the French Civil Code -- a comprehensive written statute that covers contract obligations.
- **The Result**: The judge applies the code directly. There is no need to look at how previous judges ruled on similar cases. The written code IS the law.

#### B. Common-Law System

**What it is in plain English**: A legal system where the law comes primarily from **court decisions** (case law), not just from written statutes. Judges look at how previous courts ruled on similar cases and follow those rulings. The U.S. (except Louisiana) and England use this system.

**Key concept -- Doctrine of Stare Decisis**: Latin for "to stand by things decided." This means courts should follow precedent -- past decisions on similar cases. It is the backbone of the common-law system.

**In plain English**: If a court in your state decided 10 years ago that a dog owner is liable for bites, today's court should rule the same way in a similar case. Predictability is the goal.

##### 1. Judicial Influence on Common Law

Courts don't just blindly follow old decisions. They can:
- **Apply** precedent to new cases (most common)
- **Distinguish** a case from precedent (find meaningful differences)
- **Discard** precedent that has lost its usefulness
- **Make landmark decisions** that depart entirely from precedent

##### 2. The Evolution of Common Law

Common law is not frozen in time. It changes as courts examine precedent and decide whether old rules still make sense in modern society.

**Real-World Example**:
- **The Setup**: In 1852, a township court ruled that dog owners must pay for ALL bite damages, no exceptions.
- **What Happens**: In 1952, a local court created a "one free bite" exception for green-eyed dog owners only (dogs with no prior vicious behavior get one free bite).
- **The Result**: The 1952 decision modified the 1852 precedent. Each new case can add to, modify, or replace prior common law. But note: the "green-eyed only" distinction would raise Equal Protection issues (see Constitutional provisions below).

##### 3. Equity

**What it is in plain English**: When the strict letter of the law would produce an unfair or unjust result, courts of equity can step in with fair remedies. Think of equity as the legal system's "fairness safety valve."

**Key difference**: Courts of law give money damages. Courts of equity give orders (injunctions, specific performance) when money alone would not be fair.

**Example**: You contracted to buy a one-of-a-kind historic home. The seller refuses to complete the sale. Money damages would not make you whole because the home is unique. An equity court can order **specific performance** -- forcing the seller to complete the sale.

#### C. Classifications of U.S. Law

##### 1. Criminal Law vs. Civil Law

**Criminal Law**:
- Applies to acts **society** deems so harmful that the **government** prosecutes
- The state (prosecution) brings the case, not the victim
- Penalties: fines, imprisonment, probation
- Standard of proof: **beyond a reasonable doubt** (very high bar)

**Civil Law**:
- Everything that is NOT criminal law
- Protects individual rights and provides remedies for wrongs
- The injured party brings the case
- Penalties: money damages, injunctions
- Standard of proof: **preponderance of the evidence** (more likely than not -- a lower bar)

**Insurance example**: If an agent commits insurance fraud (filing fake claims), the state can prosecute criminally AND the insurer can sue civilly. Both can happen from the same act.

##### 2. Classification by Subject Matter

Law is organized into specialty areas: contract law, tort law, property law, constitutional law, etc. Insurance law draws from multiple subject areas.

##### 3. Substantive Law vs. Procedural Law

**Substantive law**: The actual rules that create rights, duties, and obligations.
- In plain English: the "WHAT" of the law -- what you can and cannot do
- Example: "It is illegal to drive over 65 mph" = substantive

**Procedural law**: The rules for HOW legal proceedings are conducted.
- In plain English: the "HOW" of the law -- how you file a lawsuit, how trials run, how appeals work
- Example: "You must file your complaint within 30 days" = procedural

**Why this matters for the exam**: In federal diversity cases, federal courts apply **state substantive law** but **federal procedural law**. This principle comes from the landmark case ***Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins* (1938)**. This is a heavily tested concept.

### SECTION 2: Sources of U.S. Law

#### A. Constitutions

**The Supremacy Clause** (Article VI): The federal Constitution, federal statutes, and treaties are the "supreme law of the land." When state and federal laws conflict, **federal law prevails**. This is the foundational principle for understanding when federal law overrides state insurance regulation.

**The U.S. Constitution** is the "supreme Law of the Land." Everything else must be consistent with it.

**State constitutions** are the supreme law of each state, but they are **subservient** to the U.S. Constitution. If there is a conflict, the federal Constitution ALWAYS wins.

**Key Constitutional Provisions for Insurance**:

**Congressional Powers**: The Constitution gives Congress express (specifically listed) powers and implied powers (whatever is "necessary and proper" to carry out the express powers).

**Commerce Clause** (Article I, Section 8): Gives Congress power to regulate interstate commerce. This is HUGE for insurance because it determines whether the federal government or state governments regulate insurance.

**Real-World Example**:
- **The Setup**: Congress wants to pass a federal law regulating all insurance companies.
- **What Happens**: Under the Commerce Clause, Congress technically HAS the power because insurance crosses state lines.
- **The Result**: But Congress chose NOT to. Through the McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945), Congress said "states, you regulate insurance." More on this below.

**Due Process Clause** (5th Amendment for federal, 14th Amendment for states): The government cannot deprive any person of **life, liberty, or property** without due process of law.

- In plain English: The government must follow fair procedures before it takes something from you. You get notice and a hearing.
- **States ARE required** to provide due process because the 14th Amendment extends 5th Amendment protections to state actions.

**Equal Protection Clause** (14th Amendment): No state shall deny any person **equal protection of the laws**.

- In plain English: The government cannot treat people differently without a rational basis. Laws must apply equally.

**Three Levels of Scrutiny** (courts apply different standards depending on the classification):
1. **Strict scrutiny** (for suspect classifications like race or national origin) -- the government must show a **compelling interest** and the law must be narrowly tailored. Very hard for the government to win.
2. **Intermediate scrutiny** (for gender and legitimacy) -- the law must serve an **important governmental objective** and be substantially related to achieving it.
3. **Rational basis** (for economic regulations, including insurance) -- the law just needs a **reasonable relationship** to a **legitimate government purpose**. Very easy for the government to win.

> **Exam Tip**: Insurance regulation almost always faces only **rational basis** review. This is why states have broad authority to regulate insurance -- they just need to show the regulation has some rational connection to a legitimate state interest.

**Guest statute example**: Some states used to have laws saying a passenger in a car could only sue the driver for injuries if the driver was grossly negligent (not just regular negligent). These laws treated car passengers differently from other injured people. Courts have struck down many guest statutes as Equal Protection violations. [2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Guest statutes are now essentially extinct -- virtually all states have repealed them. The legal principle they illustrate (Equal Protection analysis) remains valid.]

**Insurance example**: A state cannot pass a law that only protects homeowners with blue eyes from insurance discrimination. There must be a rational basis for any classification.

**Insurance-Specific Commerce Clause Example**:
- **The Setup**: A large national insurer wants uniform regulations across all 50 states instead of dealing with 50 different state insurance departments.
- **What Happens**: The insurer argues that since it sells policies across state lines, insurance is interstate commerce, so Congress should regulate it under the Commerce Clause.
- **The Result**: The insurer is technically correct that insurance IS interstate commerce (established in *United States v. South-Eastern Underwriters*, 1944). However, Congress passed the **McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945)**, which says state regulation of insurance is in the public interest and federal law will NOT preempt state insurance regulation UNLESS the federal law specifically relates to insurance. So despite the Commerce Clause power, **states remain the primary regulators of insurance** -- a deliberate Congressional choice, not a constitutional requirement.

#### B. Legislative Bodies

Federal and state legislatures create **statutory law** (written laws/statutes). Local governments create **ordinances**.

**Uniform laws**: Because each state can pass different laws, there can be confusion. Organizations like the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws draft model laws that states can voluntarily adopt, creating consistency. The **Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)** is the most famous example.

#### C. Courts

##### 1. Federal Court System

Three levels:
- **U.S. District Courts** (trial courts) -- where cases START
- **U.S. Courts of Appeals** (Circuit Courts) -- review District Court decisions
- **U.S. Supreme Court** -- the final word. Hears cases by **writ of certiorari** (the Court CHOOSES which cases to hear; it does not have to accept every appeal)

**Jurisdiction** means the power of a court to hear a case. Types:

- **Original jurisdiction**: Power to hear a case FIRST (trial level). Federal courts have original jurisdiction over admiralty/maritime cases, cases involving ambassadors, etc.

- **Appellate jurisdiction**: Power to review lower court decisions. Appellate courts do NOT conduct new trials. They decide whether the lower court **applied the law correctly**.

- **General jurisdiction**: Courts that hear many TYPES of cases (state trial courts are usually courts of general jurisdiction)

- **Diversity jurisdiction**: When the plaintiff and defendant are from DIFFERENT states AND the amount in controversy exceeds a minimum threshold. [2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The threshold was $75,000 when the book was published; confirm current amount.] This is why a car accident case between citizens of different states can go to federal court.

**Real-World Example**:
- **The Setup**: Peter lives in State Y. Don lives in State Z. Don hits Peter's car in State Z. Peter has $4,000 in property damage and $150,000 in personal injuries ($154,000 total).
- **What Happens**: Peter can sue in State Z courts (where the accident happened and Don lives). Peter can ALSO sue in federal court because diversity jurisdiction applies -- different states, and the amount ($154,000) exceeds the minimum.
- **The Result**: If Peter sues in federal court, the federal court applies the **substantive law** of the state where the court sits, but applies **federal procedural rules**.

##### 2. State Court Systems

Most states have a similar three-tier structure:
- Trial courts (courts of general jurisdiction)
- Intermediate appellate courts
- State Supreme Court (highest state court)

##### 3. Conflicts of Law

**What it is**: When a legal dispute involves more than one state, which state's law applies? This body of rules is called **conflicts of law** (or "choice of law").

**Forum state**: The state where the lawsuit is filed. Courts generally apply the forum state's procedural law but may apply another state's substantive law depending on where the events occurred.

**How parties avoid conflicts of law**: Include a **choice of law clause** in the contract that specifies which state's laws will govern any disputes.

**Real-World Example**:
- **The Setup**: A New Jersey company signs a contract with a Texas company. The contract says "Texas law governs this agreement."
- **What Happens**: A dispute arises and the NJ company sues in New Jersey.
- **The Result**: The New Jersey court will apply **Texas substantive law** (because the contract says so) but **New Jersey procedural law** (because that is the forum state).

#### D. Executive Branches

The president (federal) and governors (state) influence law by:
- **Recommending** legislation
- **Approving** (signing) or **vetoing** legislation
- **Appointing** heads of administrative agencies
- **Influencing** agencies' rules and regulations

##### 1. Administrative Agencies

Created by legislatures to handle specific areas (like insurance regulation). Examples: state departments of insurance, the SEC, the EPA.

**Administrative law**: The body of law created by administrative agencies through their rulemaking and adjudicatory powers.

### SECTION 3: Legal Procedures

#### A. Court Procedure

##### 1. Pretrial Procedure

**Step-by-step lawsuit process**:

1. **Complaint**: The **plaintiff** (person suing) files a complaint -- a document containing **allegations** (claims of fact), stating a **cause of action** (legal basis for the lawsuit), and requesting a remedy.

2. **Summons**: The court notifies the **defendant** (person being sued) with a summons, which includes a copy of the complaint and a deadline to respond.

3. **Answer**: The defendant files an answer explaining why the plaintiff should not win. The answer may include **counterclaims** (claims back against the plaintiff) or **crossclaims** (claims against co-defendants).

4. **Reply**: After receiving the defendant's answer, the plaintiff may file a reply.

These documents are collectively called **pleadings**.

**Pre-trial motions**:
- **Motion to dismiss**: Defendant asks the court to throw out the case (e.g., court lacks jurisdiction)
- **Motion for judgment on the pleadings / Motion for summary judgment**: One side asks the court to decide the case without a trial because there are no genuine disputes about the facts

**Discovery**: The process where both sides gather evidence BEFORE trial.

Tools of discovery:
- **Depositions**: Oral testimony under oath, recorded by a court reporter. A lawyer asks a witness questions face-to-face.
- **Interrogatories**: Written questions sent to the other party that must be answered in writing under oath.
- **Motions to produce**: Requests to the court ordering the other party to hand over documents (contracts, emails, records, etc.)

**Real-World Example (Joyce's slip-and-fall)**:
- **The Setup**: Joyce slips on an icy mall parking lot and wants to sue the mall owner.
- **What Happens**: Joyce's lawyer files a complaint alleging negligence. The mall owner files an answer. During discovery, Joyce's lawyer deposes the maintenance person who was supposed to salt the lot, sends interrogatories to mall management about their ice removal policy, and files a motion to produce safety inspection records for the parking lot.
- **The Result**: Both sides build their cases through discovery before ever going to trial.

##### 2. Trial Procedure

**Evidence rules** -- three bases for EXCLUDING evidence at trial:

- **Relevance**: Does the evidence relate to the issues? Jack's old parking tickets are NOT relevant to whether he backed into Mike's car.

- **Materiality**: Is the evidence important enough to matter? Would it influence the outcome?

- **Competence**: Is the witness qualified? Is the evidence reliable?

**Hearsay rule**: Out-of-court statements offered to prove the truth of the matter are generally NOT admissible. BUT there are many exceptions (e.g., excited utterances, business records, dying declarations).

**Real-World Example (Jack's truck accident)**:
- Jack's parking tickets: **Inadmissible** -- irrelevant and immaterial to this accident
- Jack's drug abuse history: **Potentially admissible** IF connected to this case (e.g., Jack was on drugs at the time of THIS accident)
- Wilma overheard Jack say "Where'd you come from?" at the scene: **Admissible** -- relevant to show Jack did not see Mike. A hearsay objection might be raised, but exceptions (like excited utterance) likely apply.

**Types of verdicts**:
- **General verdict**: Jury simply finds for plaintiff or defendant (and sets damages if applicable)
- **Special verdict**: Jury answers specific factual questions, and the judge applies the law to those answers

**Res judicata** and **Collateral estoppel**: Once a case is decided, it cannot be relitigated (res judicata), and specific facts decided in one case cannot be re-argued in a related case (collateral estoppel).

##### 3. Appeals

Key points:
- **Appellant**: The party who appeals (the loser at trial)
- **Appellee**: The party who defends the appeal (the winner at trial)
- **Appellate courts do NOT conduct new trials**. They only review whether the lower court applied the law correctly.
- **Writ of certiorari**: The U.S. Supreme Court's way of choosing cases. If the Court denies "cert," the lower court's decision stands.

#### B. Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

ADR includes ways to resolve disputes WITHOUT going through a full trial. It is faster and cheaper.

##### 1. Arbitration

A neutral third party (arbitrator) hears both sides and makes a decision. **Binding arbitration** means the parties agreed in advance to accept the arbitrator's decision -- no appeal.

Many insurance policies contain arbitration clauses.

##### 2. Mediation

A neutral mediator helps the parties negotiate a compromise. The mediator does NOT make a decision -- they facilitate discussion. Non-binding unless the parties reach an agreement.

##### 3. Negotiation

Direct discussions between the parties (or their lawyers) to settle the dispute. Most lawsuits settle through negotiation before trial.

**ADR also includes**: mini-trials and summary jury trials, which give parties a preview of what a real trial might look like, encouraging settlement.

**When is ADR binding?** When the parties have **agreed in advance** that it will be binding.

**Insurance-Specific ADR Example**:
- **The Setup**: Maria's home suffers $80,000 in water damage. Her insurer says the damage is only $45,000. Maria disagrees but does not want to spend years in court.
- **What Happens**: Maria's homeowners policy has an **appraisal clause** (a form of ADR built into insurance contracts). Each side selects an appraiser, and the two appraisers select an umpire. Any two of the three agreeing on the amount settles the dispute.
- **The Result**: The appraisal process resolves the $80,000 vs $45,000 dispute in weeks instead of years. The appraisers and umpire agree on $67,000. This is binding on both parties. Many insurance disputes use arbitration or appraisal clauses, making ADR especially important in insurance.

### SECTION 4: Administrative Agency Procedure

#### A. Agencies' Rulemaking Function

Administrative agencies create three types of rules:

1. **Legislative rules**: Have the full force of law (like statutes). Agencies create these when the legislature delegates that power.

2. **Interpretative rules**: The agency's interpretation of existing statutes. They explain what the agency THINKS the law means but do not have the same force as legislative rules.

3. **Procedural rules**: Rules about HOW the agency conducts its own business (internal processes, filing requirements, etc.)

**Rulemaking process**: After reviewing public comments about a proposed rule, an agency can:
- Adopt the originally proposed rule
- Make minimal or extensive changes to the rule
- Nullify (abandon) the proposed rule entirely

#### B. Agencies' Adjudicatory Function

Agencies can hold hearings and make decisions, similar to courts. Due process requirements apply.

**An appropriate notice must include**:
- Statement of the hearing time, place, and nature
- Statement of the hearing's legal authority and jurisdiction
- Reference to the statute or rule involved
- A short, clear statement of the matters at issue

#### C. Agencies' Investigative Powers

Agencies can investigate, but the Constitution limits them:
- **Fourth Amendment**: Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures
- **Fifth Amendment**: Protection against self-incrimination

A **subpoena** is a legal order compelling someone to testify or produce documents.

#### D. Judicial Review

Courts will only review administrative agency decisions when ALL three conditions are met:
1. The plaintiff must have **standing to sue** (they are directly and adversely affected)
2. The agency must have issued a **final order**
3. The plaintiff must have **exhausted all administrative remedies** (tried every available appeal within the agency first)

**Real-World Example (Tattoo parlor case)**:
- **The Setup**: The Board of Health bans tattooing by unlicensed persons, citing a connection between tattooing and hepatitis. Unlicensed tattoo parlor operators sue.
- **What Happens**: The operators must FIRST exhaust administrative remedies (challenge the rule before the Board of Health). They DO have standing because the rule directly and adversely affects them. They can allege the rule violates Due Process (property/earnings taken without fair process) and Equal Protection.
- **The Result**: If the Board of Health cannot show a rational basis for excluding unlicensed persons, the rule may be struck down as arbitrary and capricious.

#### E. Privacy and Freedom of Information Acts

**Privacy Act**: Limits how federal agencies can collect, maintain, and share personal information about individuals.

**Freedom of Information Act (FOIA)**: Gives the public the right to access federal agency records, with certain exemptions (national security, trade secrets, personal privacy, etc.).

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: State-level equivalents (open records laws) have expanded significantly. FOIA has been modernized with electronic records requirements.]

### SECTION 5: Insurance Regulation

#### A. Federal Regulation

The key law: **McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945)**

**What it says in plain English**: Congress decided that STATES, not the federal government, should regulate insurance. As long as a state is actively regulating insurance, federal law will not preempt (override) state insurance law.

**Background**: In *United States v. South-Eastern Underwriters Association* (1944), the Supreme Court ruled insurance WAS interstate commerce and COULD be regulated by Congress. The insurance industry panicked. Congress responded by passing McCarran-Ferguson, essentially saying "we COULD regulate insurance, but we choose to let states do it."

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act (2010) created the Federal Insurance Office (FIO) within the U.S. Treasury. While the FIO does not have direct regulatory authority over insurers, it monitors the insurance industry and can preempt state law in limited circumstances related to international agreements. The McCarran-Ferguson Act remains the foundation, but federal involvement has increased.]

#### B. State Insurance Regulation

Each state has a **Department of Insurance** (DOI) headed by an **Insurance Commissioner** (or Superintendent) who oversees:
- Licensing of insurers and producers
- Approval of policy forms and rates
- Market conduct examinations
- Consumer complaints
- Insurer solvency monitoring

#### C. National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC)

**What it is**: A voluntary organization of state insurance commissioners from all 50 states, D.C., and U.S. territories.

**What it does**: Develops **model laws** that promote **uniform insurance regulation** among the states. States can adopt these models, but they are NOT required to.

**Zone examination**: A coordinated financial examination of an insurer conducted by a team of examiners from multiple states (organized by NAIC zones). This prevents one insurer from being separately examined by every state it does business in.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The NAIC has significantly expanded its role since 2008, including the Insurance Data Security Model Law (2017), group capital calculations, innovation/InsurTech initiatives, and climate risk disclosure requirements.]

#### D. Regulation of Insurers

States regulate insurer solvency, rates, policy forms, market conduct, and claims practices. Key areas include:

##### Rate Regulation

States use different approaches to regulate insurance rates:
- **Prior approval**: Insurer must submit rates to the DOI and get approval BEFORE using them. The most restrictive approach.
- **File-and-use**: Insurer files rates with the DOI and can use them immediately, but the DOI can disapprove them later.
- **Use-and-file**: Insurer can use new rates immediately but must file them with the DOI within a specified period.
- **Open competition (no-file)**: No filing required. Rates are regulated by market competition. The least restrictive approach.

> **Exam Tip**: Rates must be adequate (enough to pay claims), not excessive (not too high), and not unfairly discriminatory (similar risks pay similar rates).

##### Solvency Regulation

Ensuring insurers can pay claims is the MOST IMPORTANT regulatory function:
- **Risk-Based Capital (RBC)**: A formula that calculates the minimum amount of capital an insurer must hold based on the riskiness of its operations. Riskier business = more capital required. If an insurer's capital falls below RBC thresholds, regulators can take progressively stronger action (company action level → regulatory action level → authorized control level → mandatory control level).
- **Financial examinations**: State examiners review insurer financial records periodically (typically every 3-5 years).
- **State guaranty funds**: If an insurer becomes insolvent, state guaranty funds step in to pay policyholders' covered claims (up to limits, typically $300,000-$500,000). Every licensed insurer must contribute to the guaranty fund. This is the safety net that protects policyholders.

> **Key Distinction**: Guaranty funds are NOT government money -- they are funded by assessments on other insurance companies operating in the state.

##### Federal Insurance Office (FIO)

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The **Federal Insurance Office (FIO)** was created by the **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)**. Key facts:
- The FIO monitors the insurance industry for **systemic risk**
- Advises the Treasury Secretary on insurance matters
- Represents the U.S. in international insurance negotiations
- The FIO does **NOT** directly regulate insurers -- that remains with the states
- The FIO can recommend that the federal government preempt state insurance laws in limited circumstances, but this power has rarely (if ever) been used

#### E. Insurance Producer Regulation

##### 1. Licensing and License Renewal

To qualify for an insurance producer's license, an applicant must:
- Be of at least minimum age
- Have an appointment from a licensed insurance company
- Have completed the requisite application forms
- Have satisfactorily completed the examination requirement

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Producer licensing has been significantly modernized. The NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act has been widely adopted. Many states now use NIPR (National Insurance Producer Registry) for electronic licensing. Continuing education requirements vary by state but are now universal.]

##### 2. Managing General Agents (MGAs)

**What they are**: MGAs are agents with expanded authority -- they can underwrite, bind coverage, and settle claims on behalf of insurers. They function almost like mini-insurance companies.

**Why regulators watch them closely**: Some MGAs have played roles in **major insurer insolvencies**. When an MGA has broad authority but inadequate oversight, they can write too much risky business and bankrupt the insurer.

##### 3. Surplus Lines Brokers

**What they are**: Brokers licensed to place insurance with non-admitted (surplus lines) insurers when coverage cannot be obtained from admitted (licensed) insurers in the state.

**Requirements under NAIC model act**: Must pass a DOI-approved exam AND post a bond of a specified amount.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The Nonadmitted and Reinsurance Reform Act (NRRA), part of Dodd-Frank (2010), streamlined surplus lines regulation. The "home state" of the insured now has sole authority to regulate surplus lines taxation and eligibility. This was a major change.]

## Key Terms Glossary

**Administrative law** -- The body of law created by administrative agencies through rulemaking and adjudication.

**Allegation** -- A claim of fact made in a legal pleading; what one party asserts to be true.

**Alternative dispute resolution (ADR)** -- Methods of resolving disputes outside of traditional court litigation, including arbitration, mediation, and negotiation.

**Answer** -- The defendant's formal written response to the plaintiff's complaint, stating why the plaintiff should not win. May include counterclaims.

**Appeal** -- A request to a higher court to review and reverse a lower court's decision.

**Appellant** -- The party who appeals a court decision (usually the losing party).

**Appellate court** -- A court that reviews decisions of lower courts for errors of law. Does NOT conduct new trials.

**Appellate jurisdiction** -- The power of a court to review cases decided by lower courts.

**Appellee** -- The party who defends against an appeal (usually the winning party from the lower court).

**Arbitration** -- A form of ADR where a neutral third party hears both sides and makes a decision. Can be binding or non-binding.

**Cause of action** -- The legal basis (theory) for a lawsuit; the reason you have a right to sue.

**Civil-law system** -- A legal system based on comprehensive written codes/statutes rather than court precedent. Used in Louisiana and most of continental Europe.

**Collateral estoppel** -- A legal principle preventing parties from re-litigating specific factual issues already decided in a previous case.

**Commerce Clause** -- Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution giving Congress power to regulate interstate commerce. Critical for determining federal vs. state regulation of insurance.

**Competence** (evidence) -- Whether a witness is qualified to testify or evidence is reliable enough to be admissible.

**Complaint** -- The initial document filed by a plaintiff to start a lawsuit, containing allegations, a cause of action, and a request for relief.

**Conflicts of law** -- Rules determining which state's (or country's) law applies when a dispute involves multiple jurisdictions.

**Criminal law** -- Law dealing with acts harmful enough to the public welfare that the government prosecutes them. Standard: beyond a reasonable doubt.

**Cross-examination** -- Questioning of a witness by the opposing party's attorney, testing the witness's testimony.

**Defendant** -- The person or entity being sued or charged.

**Deposition** -- Oral testimony given under oath outside of court, recorded by a court reporter. A key discovery tool.

**Direct examination** -- Questioning of a witness by the attorney who called that witness.

**Discovery** -- The pretrial process where both parties gather evidence from each other through depositions, interrogatories, and motions to produce.

**Diversity jurisdiction** -- Federal court jurisdiction based on the parties being citizens of different states and the amount in controversy exceeding the minimum threshold.

**Doctrine of Stare Decisis** -- "To stand by things decided." The principle that courts should follow precedent.

**Due Process Clause** -- Constitutional protection (5th Amendment for federal, 14th for states) requiring fair procedures before the government can deprive someone of life, liberty, or property.

**Equal Protection Clause** -- 14th Amendment provision requiring that no state deny any person equal protection of the laws. Laws must have a rational basis for treating people differently.

**Equity** -- A body of law providing fair remedies (injunctions, specific performance) when money damages alone would be insufficient or unjust.

**Exhaustion of administrative remedies** -- The requirement that a party must pursue all available appeals within an administrative agency before seeking judicial review.

**Final order** -- An agency's conclusive decision on a matter, which is a prerequisite to seeking judicial review.

**Forum state** -- The state where a lawsuit is filed. Its procedural rules typically apply.

**General jurisdiction** -- Courts that can hear a wide variety of case types.

**General verdict** -- A jury verdict that simply finds for one party or the other (and sets damages if applicable).

**Guest statute** -- A law limiting a car passenger's right to sue the driver to cases of gross negligence only. Many have been struck down as Equal Protection violations.

**Hearsay rule** -- The rule that out-of-court statements offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted are generally inadmissible, subject to many exceptions.

**Interrogatories** -- Written questions sent by one party to another during discovery, requiring written answers under oath.

**Jurisdiction** -- The power of a court to hear and decide a case.

**Legislative rule** -- An administrative agency rule that has the full force and effect of law.

**Interpretative rule** -- An agency's interpretation of existing statutes; explains what the agency believes the law means.

**Managing general agent (MGA)** -- An agent with expanded authority to underwrite, bind, and settle claims on behalf of an insurer. Subject to special regulatory attention due to insolvency risks.

**Materiality** (evidence) -- Whether evidence is significant enough to influence the outcome of a case.

**Mediation** -- A form of ADR where a neutral mediator helps parties negotiate but does NOT make a binding decision.

**Motion** -- A formal request to a court for a ruling or order.

**Motion for judgment on the pleadings / Motion for summary judgment** -- Requests to the court to decide a case without trial because there are no genuine factual disputes.

**Motion to dismiss** -- A request to throw out a case (e.g., for lack of jurisdiction).

**Motion to produce** -- A discovery request asking the court to order the other party to provide documents.

**National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC)** -- Voluntary organization of state insurance commissioners that develops model laws to promote uniform regulation.

**Original jurisdiction** -- The power of a court to hear a case first (at trial level).

**Plaintiff** -- The person or entity who files a lawsuit.

**Pleading** -- Formal documents filed in a lawsuit (complaint, answer, reply, counterclaims).

**Procedural law** -- Rules governing HOW legal proceedings are conducted (filing deadlines, trial procedures, evidence rules).

**Procedural rule** (agency) -- An agency rule governing its own internal processes and procedures.

**Relevance** (evidence) -- Whether evidence relates to the issues in the case.

**Res judicata** -- "The thing has been decided." Prevents re-litigation of a case that has been finally decided.

**Special verdict** -- A verdict where the jury answers specific factual questions, and the judge applies the law.

**Standing to sue** -- The requirement that a plaintiff be directly and adversely affected by the matter to have the right to bring a lawsuit.

**Subpoena** -- A legal order compelling a person to testify or produce documents.

**Substantive law** -- The actual rules that create, define, and regulate rights, duties, and obligations.

**Writ of certiorari** -- An order by the U.S. Supreme Court agreeing to hear a case. The Court has discretion to accept or deny "cert."

**Zone examination** -- A coordinated financial examination of an insurer by examiners from multiple states, organized through NAIC zones.

## Review Questions & Answers

**Q1: Describe the civil-law and common-law systems.**
A: A **civil-law system** has a comprehensive code of written laws or statutes. A **common-law system** is a body of law derived from court decisions as opposed to statutes or constitutions.

**Q2: Explain how the common law changes.**
A: Common law changes through court examination of precedent. A court may apply a precedent to new cases, discard it if it has lost its usefulness, or make landmark decisions that depart from precedent.

**Q3: Distinguish between civil and criminal law.**
A: **Criminal law** applies to acts society deems so harmful to the public welfare that government is responsible for prosecuting and punishing the perpetrators. **Civil law** applies to legal matters not governed by criminal law, protecting rights and providing remedies for breaches of duties owed to others.

**Q4: Describe the respective roles of state constitutions and the federal Constitution.**
A: The U.S. (federal) Constitution is the "supreme Law of the Land." Each state has its own constitution, which is the supreme law of that state, subservient only to the U.S. Constitution. The U.S. Constitution always prevails over a state constitution in case of a conflict.

**Q5: Explain how the U.S. Constitution grants congressional powers.**
A: The Constitution establishes the express powers of Congress and the implied powers to pass laws necessary to implement the express powers.

**Q6: Explain why state governments are required to provide due process.**
A: States are required to provide due process because the U.S. Constitution's **Fourteenth Amendment** extends Fifth Amendment Due Process protection to state government actions.

**Q7: Describe the purpose of uniform laws.**
A: Uniform laws minimize confusion and conflict resulting from variations in state and local laws.

**Q8: Give one example of a legal matter over which federal courts have original jurisdiction.**
A: An admiralty or maritime case.

**Q9: What are courts of general jurisdiction?**
A: Courts of general jurisdiction are courts that hear a variety of types of cases.

**Q10: How can parties to a contract avoid conflicts of law?**
A: Parties can include in the contract an agreement about which state's laws will apply if a dispute arises (a choice of law clause).

**Q11: Explain how the executive branches influence legislation.**
A: An executive branch can recommend, approve, or veto laws. The executive can appoint heads of administrative agencies to assist in enforcing laws and can influence agencies' rules and regulations.

**Q12: Describe the types and order of pleadings in pretrial procedure.**
A: The plaintiff files a **complaint** setting out allegations, stating a cause of action, and requesting a remedy. The court issues a **summons** notifying the defendant, including a copy of the complaint and a timeframe for the defendant to answer. The defendant files an **answer** stating why the plaintiff should not win (may include counterclaims). Alternatively, the defendant may file an entry of appearance. After receiving the defendant's answer, the plaintiff files a **reply**.

**Q13: Identify three bases for excluding evidence at trial.**
A: Evidence can be excluded if it lacks **relevance**, **materiality**, or **competence**.

**Q14: Discuss whether appellate courts conduct new trials.**
A: Appellate courts do NOT conduct new trials. They decide whether the lower court has applied the law appropriately to a case.

**Q15: When is ADR binding on the parties?**
A: ADR can be binding on parties when they have **agreed in advance** that it will be binding.

**Q16: List the three types of administrative agency rules.**
A: (1) Legislative rules, (2) Interpretative rules, (3) Procedural rules

**Q17: What actions can an agency take after reviewing comments about a proposed rule?**
A: An agency can: adopt the originally proposed rule, make minimal or extensive changes to the rule, or nullify the proposed rule.

**Q18: What must an appropriate notice include in an administrative agency context?**
A: A statement of the hearing time, place, and nature; a statement of the hearing's legal authority and jurisdiction; reference to the statute or rule involved; and a short, clear statement of the matters at issue.

**Q19: Describe U.S. Constitutional limitations on agency investigations.**
A: Fourth Amendment protection against unreasonable searches and seizures, and Fifth Amendment protection against self-incrimination.

**Q20: What is required before courts will review administrative agency decisions?**
A: Three things: (1) the plaintiff must have standing to sue, (2) the agency must have issued a final order, and (3) the plaintiff must have exhausted all administrative remedies.

**Q21: Briefly summarize the McCarran-Ferguson Act.**
A: The McCarran-Ferguson Act permits each state to regulate the business of insurance conducted within its borders.

**Q22: What do NAIC model laws try to accomplish?**
A: NAIC model laws attempt to promote uniform insurance regulation among the states.

**Q23: What must an applicant do to qualify for an insurance producer's license?**
A: Be of at least minimum age, have an appointment from a licensed insurance company, have completed the requisite application forms, and have satisfactorily completed the examination requirement.

**Q24: Explain why MGAs are subject to regulatory attention.**
A: Regulatory attention to MGAs evidences increasing concern about the role that some MGAs have played in major insurer insolvencies.

**Q25: What must a producer do to qualify for a surplus lines broker license under the NAIC model act?**
A: A producer must pass a DOI-approved exam and post a bond of a specified amount.

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Question 1: The Dog Bite Case

**Facts**: In 1852, a court ruled dog owners must pay for ALL bite damages. In 1875, a statute added liability for damage caused by dogs running free. In 1952, a court created a "one free bite" rule for green-eyed dog owners only. Mary (brown eyes) owns a large dog Sam that just bit her neighbor.

**Q1a: How does common law apply?**
A: The 1852 case was the original law -- dog owners pay for all bites. The 1875 statute added to it (damage from dogs running free). The 1952 case carved out an exception ONLY for green-eyed owners. Each law supplemented the others without overruling them. Since Mary has brown eyes, the 1952 exception does not apply to her, so she is liable under the original 1852 rule.

**Q1b: What constitutional issue applies?**
A: This is an **Equal Protection Clause** violation (Fourteenth Amendment). The 1952 law discriminates unfairly and arbitrarily based on eye color. There is no rational basis for treating green-eyed dog owners differently from other dog owners.

### Application Question 2: Peter vs. Don (Car Accident Across State Lines)

**Facts**: Peter (State Y) is hit by Don (State Z) while driving in State Z. $154,000 in damages.

**Q2a: May Peter sue Don in State Z courts?**
A: Yes. State Z is where Don lives AND where the accident occurred. There is no reason Peter cannot sue there.

**Q2b: May Peter sue in federal court?**
A: Yes. Peter and Don are citizens of different states (diversity), and the $154,000 amount in controversy meets the minimum threshold for federal jurisdiction.

**Q2c: What substantive law will the federal court apply?**
A: The federal court will apply the **substantive law of the state in which the federal court is located**. If Peter sues in the federal court in State Y, that state's substantive law applies. If in State Z's federal court, State Z substantive law applies.

**Q2d: What procedural law will the federal court apply?**
A: The federal court will apply its own **federal procedural rules** regardless of which state the court sits in.

### Application Question 3: Joyce's Slip-and-Fall

**Q3a: What pleadings must Joyce file?**
A: Joyce must file a **complaint** to commence suit. If the defendant files an answer, Joyce may file a **reply**.

**Q3b: What discovery tools can be used?**
A: **Depositions** -- each party may depose witnesses (e.g., Joyce's lawyer questions the person responsible for parking lot safety). **Interrogatories** -- written questions to the other party requiring written answers (e.g., questions about mall safety policies). **Motions to produce** -- requests for documents (e.g., safety inspection records, maintenance logs for the parking lot).

### Application Question 4: Jack's Truck Accident (Evidence Admissibility)

**Q4a: Jack's parking tickets?**
A: Probably **inadmissible** -- irrelevant and immaterial to whether Jack backed into Mike.

**Q4b: Jack's drug abuse record?**
A: **Potentially admissible** IF connected to this case -- such as an allegation that Jack was under the influence at the time of this specific accident.

**Q4c: Wilma's testimony about Jack's first words ("Where'd you come from?")?**
A: **Admissible** -- relevant and material to show Jack did not see Mike. A hearsay objection might be raised, but one of the many exceptions to the hearsay rule (such as the excited utterance exception) may apply.

### Application Question 5: Jane's Product Injury (ADR)

**Q5: How could each form of ADR help?**
A: Any form of ADR could speed up the process since litigation is lengthy and expensive. **Arbitration** -- parties agree in advance to abide by the arbitrator's decision. **Mediation** -- helps Jane and the manufacturer analyze the dispute and devise a compromise. **Negotiation** -- provides a direct route to settlement. A **mini-trial or summary jury trial** could show what evidence and arguments would be presented at trial, encouraging settlement.

### Application Question 6: The Tattoo Parlor Regulation Case

**Facts**: Board of Health banned tattooing by unlicensed persons. Unlicensed operators sue.

**Q6a: Must operators seek further administrative action first?**
A: Yes. They must first **exhaust all administrative remedies** -- challenge the rule before the Board of Health under administrative procedures before any court will hear the case.

**Q6b: Do operators have standing to sue?**
A: Yes. The new rule applies **directly to** and **adversely affects** unlicensed persons who operate tattoo parlors. These complainants are exactly those people, so they have standing.

**Q6c: Were constitutional rights affected?**
A: Yes. The operators can allege their **property** (earnings, shops) was taken without **due process of law**. They can also allege an **Equal Protection** violation -- it is not clear who CAN tattoo if only lay persons cannot. There must be some rational basis for the distinction.

**Q6d: Was the rule arbitrary and capricious?**
A: This argument has merit. The facts as given do not clearly establish WHY unlicensed persons should be excluded from tattooing. If the Board of Health can show a rational connection between lack of licensing and hepatitis risk, the rule may stand. If not, it may be struck down as arbitrary and capricious.

## Exam Tips: Common Traps and Memorization Points

### Trap 1: Substantive vs. Procedural Law in Federal Court
The exam LOVES this. In federal diversity cases: **state SUBSTANTIVE law** + **federal PROCEDURAL law**. Do not mix them up. The state law tells you the rules; the federal law tells you the process.

### Trap 2: Appellate Courts Do NOT Retry Cases
An appellate court never hears witnesses or looks at new evidence. They only review whether the trial court correctly applied the law. If you see an answer choice saying an appellate court "conducts a new trial," it is WRONG.

### Trap 3: McCarran-Ferguson Does NOT Ban Federal Regulation
McCarran-Ferguson says states regulate insurance. It does NOT say the federal government CANNOT regulate insurance. It says the federal government CHOOSES to let states do it, as long as states are actively regulating.

### Trap 4: NAIC Model Laws Are NOT Mandatory
The NAIC proposes model laws, but states are **not required** to adopt them. Each state decides independently. The NAIC is a voluntary organization with no direct regulatory power.

### Trap 5: Standing to Sue Requires Direct Adverse Effect
You cannot sue an agency just because you disagree with their rule. You must be **directly and adversely affected** by it. Plus: final order + exhaustion of remedies.

### Trap 6: Due Process Applies to STATES Too
The Fifth Amendment Due Process Clause originally applied only to the federal government. The Fourteenth Amendment extended it to states. Both federal AND state governments must provide due process.

### Trap 7: Equal Protection Needs a "Rational Basis"
A law can treat people differently, but only if there is a **rational basis** for the distinction. Eye color, random physical characteristics, or arbitrary categories will fail this test.

### Trap 8: Discovery Tools -- Know All Three
Depositions (oral, in person), Interrogatories (written questions), Motions to produce (documents). The exam may describe a scenario and ask which tool applies.

### Trap 9: Hearsay Has MANY Exceptions
Do not automatically exclude testimony as hearsay. Excited utterances, business records, dying declarations, and many other exceptions exist. The exam may try to trick you into thinking all out-of-court statements are inadmissible.

### Trap 10: Conflicts of Law -- Choice of Law Clause is the Easy Fix
When a contract involves parties from different states, the simplest way to avoid conflicts of law is to include a choice of law provision in the contract specifying which state's law governs.

### Key Numbers to Memorize
- **3 types of agency rules**: Legislative, Interpretative, Procedural
- **3 requirements for judicial review of agency decisions**: Standing, Final order, Exhaustion of remedies
- **3 bases for excluding evidence**: Relevance, Materiality, Competence
- **3 forms of ADR**: Arbitration, Mediation, Negotiation
- **4 requirements for producer licensing**: Minimum age, Company appointment, Application forms, Examination
- **4 things due process notice must include**: Time/place/nature, Legal authority/jurisdiction, Statute or rule reference, Statement of matters at issue
- **2 Constitutional limits on agency investigations**: 4th Amendment (searches/seizures), 5th Amendment (self-incrimination)

## Quick-Reference Summary

**U.S. Legal System**: Civil-law (written codes, Louisiana) vs. Common-law (court precedent, rest of U.S.). Stare decisis = follow precedent.

**Classifications**: Criminal (government prosecutes, beyond reasonable doubt) vs. Civil (private parties, preponderance). Substantive (the "what") vs. Procedural (the "how").

**Sources of Law**: Constitutions (supreme law) > Statutes (legislatures) > Case law (courts) > Agency rules (executive/admin). Federal Constitution always trumps state.

**Constitutional Provisions**: Commerce Clause (interstate commerce power), Due Process (fair procedures required -- applies to states via 14th Amendment), Equal Protection (no arbitrary discrimination).

**Courts**: Federal (District > Circuit > Supreme) and State (Trial > Appellate > Supreme). Diversity jurisdiction = different states + minimum dollar amount. Writ of certiorari = Supreme Court chooses cases.

**Conflicts of Law**: Which state's law applies? Forum state procedural law + substantive law depends on circumstances. Solved by choice of law clauses.

**Pretrial**: Complaint > Summons > Answer > Reply. Discovery = Depositions + Interrogatories + Motions to Produce.

**Trial**: Evidence must be relevant, material, and competent. Hearsay generally excluded (many exceptions). General vs. Special verdicts.

**Appeals**: No new trial. Review law only. Appellant (loser) vs. Appellee (winner).

**ADR**: Arbitration (decision by third party), Mediation (facilitated negotiation), Negotiation (direct). Binding when parties agree in advance.

**Agency Rules**: Legislative (force of law), Interpretative (agency's reading), Procedural (internal process). Judicial review requires: standing + final order + exhaustion.

**Insurance Regulation**: McCarran-Ferguson (1945) = states regulate insurance. NAIC = voluntary body, model laws, zone examinations. Producer licensing requires: age, appointment, application, exam. MGAs get extra scrutiny (insolvency risk). Surplus lines brokers need exam + bond.
