## Section-by-Section Breakdown

### 1. Products Liability

#### What It Is in Plain English
**Products liability** is the area of law that holds manufacturers, sellers, and distributors responsible when a defective product injures someone. Over time, the law has moved from requiring a direct contract between buyer and seller (privity) to holding anyone in the chain of distribution liable, even without proof of negligence.

#### Historical Development - Common-Law Exceptions

Before modern products liability, you could only sue someone you had a direct contract with. The law carved out exceptions over time:

- **Exception 1: Inherently dangerous products** - If you marketed something inherently dangerous (like poison) that was also defective, you were liable to anyone injured, not just the buyer
- **Exception 2: Not inherently dangerous but defective** - A manufacturer who made, assembled, or sold a product that was not inherently dangerous but *could be* inherently dangerous if defective, was liable to anyone injured while using the product in a reasonable manner
- **Exception 3: Negligent concealment** - A manufacturer who was not only negligent in making the product but also *knowingly concealed the defect* could be liable for both negligence AND fraud

**The MacPherson Doctrine** (landmark case) - Eliminated the requirement for "privity of contract" (a direct buyer-seller relationship) in negligence suits. In plain English: you don't have to be the person who bought the product to sue the manufacturer.

> **Setup**: MacPherson buys a car from a dealer. A wheel was defective (made by a separate manufacturer). MacPherson is injured when the wheel collapses.
> **What Happens**: MacPherson sues the car manufacturer (Buick), not the dealer he bought from or the wheel maker.
> **Result**: Court rules MacPherson can sue Buick even though he didn't buy directly from them. The manufacturer has a duty to anyone who foreseeably uses the product.

#### Strict Liability (Absolute Liability)

**Strict liability** means a seller/manufacturer is liable for injuries from a defective product *regardless of whether they were negligent*. You don't have to prove the manufacturer did anything wrong -- you just have to prove the product was defective and it hurt you.

In plain English: "It doesn't matter if you tried your best. If the product was defective and someone got hurt, you pay."

#### Five Elements of a Strict Liability Products Claim

To win a products liability lawsuit based on strict liability, the plaintiff must prove ALL five elements:

1. **The seller was in the business of selling products** - Casual/occasional sellers are not covered (your neighbor's garage sale doesn't count)
2. **The product had a defect** that made it unreasonably dangerous
3. **The product was dangerously defective when it left** the manufacturer's or seller's custody or control - The defect existed at the time of sale, not something that happened later
4. **The defect was the proximate cause** of the plaintiff's injury - The defect actually caused the harm (not some unrelated event)
5. **The product was expected to and did reach the consumer** without substantial change in condition - The product arrived in essentially the same condition it left the factory

> **Setup**: Maria buys a new blender from a department store. The blender has a cracked blade housing from the factory.
> **What Happens**: While blending soup on the first use, the housing cracks open and hot liquid sprays on Maria, causing burns.
> **Result**: Maria wins under strict liability. (1) The store sells blenders as a business, (2) the cracked housing was a defect, (3) it existed when it left the factory, (4) the defect caused her burns, and (5) she used it without modifying it.

#### Three Ways a Product Can Be Defective

1. **Defect in Manufacture or Assembly** - The product does not match the original design. Something went wrong on the assembly line.
   - *Example*: A car tire is supposed to have 4 layers of reinforcement but one unit only got 3 due to a machine error

2. **Defect in Design** - The product matches the design perfectly, but *the design itself is flawed*. Every unit produced has the same problem.
   - *Example*: A space heater is designed without an automatic shut-off, and tips over easily, causing fires

3. **Failure to Warn** - The product is not defective in design or manufacture, but it has inherent dangers that the manufacturer failed to warn about.
   - *Example*: A prescription drug causes severe side effects when taken with common over-the-counter medications, but the label says nothing about this interaction

#### Parties Who Can Be Strictly Liable

Any entity in the chain of distribution that is **in the business of selling products** can be strictly liable:

- Manufacturers
- Wholesalers
- Distributors
- Retailers
- Bailors (those who lease/rent products)
- Lessors
- Builders
- Contractors

#### Who Can Sue? (Beyond the Buyer)

The buyer is NOT the only party who can sue. Protection extends to:

- **The ultimate user or consumer** (who might be different from the buyer)
- **Bystanders** (someone who didn't buy or use the product but was nearby when it malfunctioned)
- Any person who might be **foreseeably affected** by the defective product

> **Setup**: Greg buys a power mower. His neighbor's child, Amy, is playing in an adjacent yard.
> **What Happens**: The mower's blade guard breaks off and a rock shoots out, hitting Amy.
> **Result**: Amy (a bystander) can sue the manufacturer under strict liability even though she never bought or used the product.

#### Defenses to Products Liability Claims

Defendants have several possible defenses:

1. **State-of-the-art defense** - The product was safe by the standards that existed when it was made
   - "We used the best technology available at the time"

2. **Compliance with statutes and regulations** - The product met all applicable government standards when made
   - IMPORTANT: This is **NOT a complete defense** -- you can still be liable even if you followed all the rules

3. **Compliance with product specifications** - Manufacturer built to someone else's specs
   - Generally not a defense unless the defect is *sufficiently obvious* to alert the manufacturer

4. **Open and obvious danger** - The manufacturer has no duty to warn of dangers that are plainly obvious
   - A knife doesn't need a warning label saying "this is sharp"

5. **Plaintiff's knowledge defense** - The user had knowledge of the product's danger equal to the manufacturer's knowledge

6. **Assumption of risk** - The person using the product knowingly accepted the risk of loss, injury, or damage

7. **Misuse of product defense** - The plaintiff didn't prove the product was used in an appropriate and foreseeable manner

8. **Alteration of product** - The product was modified after it was sold

9. **Post-accident remedial measures defense** - Prohibits a plaintiff from proving negligence by showing the defendant improved the product after the accident
   - In plain English: "The fact that we made it safer after the accident can't be used against us"

10. **Written disclaimers** - Manufacturers use these to disclaim strict liability in some UCC breach-of-warranty situations
    - IMPORTANT: Most courts have **rejected** the validity of such disclaimers

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Many states have enacted additional tort reform measures since 2008, including caps on damages, modified comparative fault standards, and expanded assumption-of-risk defenses.

### 2. Toxic Torts and Environmental Liability

#### Toxic Torts

A **toxic tort** is a tort involving injury from exposure to toxic or hazardous substances. These cases are uniquely challenging because:

- Harm may not appear for years or decades after exposure
- Difficult to prove causation (what specifically caused the illness?)
- May involve thousands of plaintiffs (mass tort)

#### Environmental Law and CERCLA (Superfund)

**CERCLA** = Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (commonly called "Superfund")

In plain English: CERCLA is a federal law that forces cleanup of hazardous waste sites and makes the responsible parties pay for it.

**Primary purpose of CERCLA**: To require the cleanup of hazardous waste disposal sites.

#### Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs)

Under CERCLA, the following types of parties can be held liable:

1. **Current owners and operators** of hazardous waste facilities
2. **Past owners or operators** of hazardous waste facilities
3. **Generators** of hazardous waste (companies that created the waste)
4. **Transporters** of hazardous waste (companies that moved it)

CRITICAL POINT: Liability under CERCLA is **strict** -- you don't need to prove negligence. If you fall into one of these categories, you're potentially on the hook for cleanup costs even if you did nothing wrong.

> **Setup**: Jack buys a gas station. He discovers the underground storage tank has been leaking gasoline into the ground for years -- long before he bought the property.
> **What Happens**: The EPA identifies the site as contaminated.
> **Result**: Jack must correct the situation because he is the current owner, even though he didn't cause the contamination. He may be able to sue previous owners who were responsible.

#### CGL Policy Issues

**Commercial General Liability (CGL)** policies and environmental claims create tricky coverage questions:

- **Occurrence-based CGL policies** cover occurrences during the policy period
- For waste dumped years ago but discovered recently, the question is: *when did the "occurrence" happen?*
- Many CGL policies only cover "sudden and accidental" discharges, not gradual pollution
- [2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The "pollution exclusion" in CGL policies has been heavily litigated since 2008. Many modern CGL policies contain absolute pollution exclusions.

### 3. Damages in Tort Actions

#### Compensatory Damages

Compensatory damages are meant to make the plaintiff "whole" -- to put them back (financially) where they were before the tort. They come in two types:

**Special Damages** (Economic Damages) - Specific, quantifiable monetary losses:
- **Medical expenses** (past and future)
- **Loss of wages and earnings** (past and future)
- **Property damage** repair or replacement costs
- Other out-of-pocket costs

**General Damages** (Non-Economic Damages) - Losses that are real but hard to put an exact dollar amount on:
- **Pain and suffering** - Physical pain endured
- **Emotional distress** - Mental anguish, anxiety, depression
- **Loss of consortium** - Loss of companionship/relationship
- **Disfigurement** - Scarring, visible injuries
- **Loss of enjoyment of life**

#### Nominal Damages

**Nominal damages** are a token amount (like $1) awarded when a legal right has been violated but no actual loss occurred. They establish that the defendant was wrong, even if no real harm resulted.

> **Setup**: Your neighbor walks across your property every day without permission (technical trespass).
> **What Happens**: You sue for trespass.
> **Result**: Court awards $1 in nominal damages. You proved the trespass happened but suffered no real harm. It establishes the legal principle that your neighbor was wrong.

#### Punitive Damages (Bad-Faith Damages)

**Punitive damages** go beyond compensation -- they are meant to *punish* the defendant and *deter* similar behavior.

A court may award punitive damages when the defendant:
- Acted **oppressively**
- Acted **maliciously**
- Acted **fraudulently**
- Intended to cause harm

In plain English: "You didn't just make a mistake. You acted so badly that we need to punish you to send a message."

> **Setup**: An auto manufacturer discovers that its gas tanks explode in rear-end collisions. An internal memo shows it would cost $11 per car to fix but the company decides not to because it would hurt profits.
> **What Happens**: A driver dies in a rear-end collision when the tank explodes.
> **Result**: Jury awards compensatory damages PLUS millions in punitive damages because the manufacturer knowingly chose profits over safety.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Since 2008, the U.S. Supreme Court has issued additional rulings limiting punitive damages (generally holding that a single-digit ratio to compensatory damages is the most that due process allows). Many states have also enacted statutory caps.

### 4. Survival Statutes and Wrongful Death

**Survival statute** - A law that allows a deceased person's legal claims to survive their death and be pursued by their estate.

In plain English: If someone was injured by a tort and dies (either from the injury or from something else), their estate can still pursue the lawsuit the person would have had if they'd lived.

**What can be recovered under survival statutes:**
- Medical expenses incurred before death
- Pain and suffering the decedent experienced before death
- Lost wages between injury and death
- Property damage claims the decedent had

> **Survival Statute Example:**
> **Setup:** Tom is seriously injured when a defective elevator crushes his leg in an office building. He incurs $200,000 in medical bills and suffers months of pain. Six months later, Tom dies of an unrelated heart attack.
> **What Happens:** Tom's estate wants to continue his personal injury lawsuit against the elevator manufacturer.
> **Result:** Under the survival statute, Tom's estate CAN continue the lawsuit. The estate can recover Tom's medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering from the injury through the date of death. The claim "survives" Tom's death.

**Wrongful death actions** are fundamentally different -- the family members sue for *their own losses* resulting from the death. This is a NEW cause of action that did not exist at common law; it was created by statute.

**What can be recovered under wrongful death:**
- Loss of the deceased's future earnings/financial support
- Loss of companionship, consortium, parental guidance
- Funeral and burial expenses
- Survivors' emotional suffering (in some states)

> **Wrongful Death Example:**
> **Setup:** Sarah, a 35-year-old mother of two and primary breadwinner (earning $80,000/year), is killed by a drunk driver.
> **What Happens:** Sarah's husband and children file a wrongful death action against the drunk driver.
> **Result:** The family can recover for THEIR losses: the lost income Sarah would have earned over her remaining working life, loss of companionship and parental guidance for the children, funeral costs, and the husband's loss of consortium. These are the FAMILY'S damages, not Sarah's.

**KEY DISTINCTION for the Exam:**
- **Survival** = The dead person's OWN claim continues (what THEY suffered before dying)
- **Wrongful death** = A NEW claim by SURVIVORS for THEIR losses (what THEY lost because of the death)
- Both can be filed in the same case but they compensate different people for different losses

#### Animal Liability

Liability for injuries caused by animals depends on whether the animal is **domestic** or **wild**:

**Domestic animals** (dogs, cats, horses, cattle):
- Owner is liable only if the owner **knew or should have known** the animal was dangerous (the "one-bite rule" in many states)
- Exception: Some states have strict liability dog-bite statutes -- the owner is liable regardless of prior knowledge

**Wild animals** (bears, lions, monkeys, exotic pets):
- Owner is **strictly liable** for any injuries -- no need to prove negligence or prior knowledge of danger
- Keeping a wild animal is considered an inherently dangerous activity

> **Example:** A neighbor keeps a pet monkey. The monkey escapes and bites a mail carrier. The neighbor is strictly liable because monkeys are wild animals -- it doesn't matter that the monkey never bit anyone before.

### 5. Joint Tortfeasors and Contribution

#### Joint Tortfeasors

**Joint tortfeasors** are two or more people whose combined actions cause a single injury to the plaintiff.

#### Common Law: Joint and Several Liability

Under common law, each joint tortfeasor is **jointly and severally liable** for the FULL amount of damages.

In plain English: If three people cause you harm, you can sue ANY ONE of them for the entire amount. You don't have to sue all of them.

**Contribution** = The right of a tortfeasor who has paid more than their fair share to collect the excess from the other tortfeasors.

Key common-law rule: At common law, a release of one joint tortfeasor **released ALL joint tortfeasors**, even if the release specifically said otherwise. This was a harsh rule.

#### UCAJTFA (Uniform Contribution Among Joint Tortfeasors Act)

The **UCAJTFA** modernized the rules:

- A plaintiff **CAN release one or more defendants** without releasing the others
- A tortfeasor who pays more than their **pro rata share** has a right of contribution from the others
- Contribution is divided **equally** among tortfeasors (not prorated by fault in the original UCAJTFA)

> **Setup**: Jack, Carol, and Joe assault Tom in the dark. Tom suffers a fractured skull and $35,000 in damages (medical bills of $10,000 + $25,000 pain and suffering). Tom doesn't know which of the three struck the blow that fractured his skull.
> **What Happens**: Tom sues only Jack (maybe Carol moved away and Joe is hard to find).
> **Result**:
> - Tom CAN sue only Jack -- plaintiffs can choose which tortfeasors to sue
> - Jack can bring Carol and Joe into the suit, or sue them separately for their shares
> - **At common law**: Jack could be responsible for the entire $35,000 (joint and several liability)
> - **Under UCAJTFA**: Jack is liable for his $10,000 share (1/3), and can seek contribution for the remaining $25,000 to be split equally between Carol and Joe

#### Enterprise Liability / Industry-Wide Liability

When multiple companies in an industry produced the same defective product and the plaintiff can't identify which specific company made the one that hurt them.

#### Alternative Liability

When two or more defendants acted negligently but only one caused the harm, and it's impossible to tell which one, the burden shifts to the DEFENDANTS to prove they didn't cause it.

#### Market-Share Liability

Each manufacturer is liable based on its **percentage share of the market** for the product that caused injury. Used when you can't identify which specific manufacturer's product caused the harm.

> **Setup**: A woman took a drug (DES) during pregnancy decades ago that later caused cancer in her daughter. The drug was made by dozens of manufacturers with identical formulas.
> **What Happens**: The daughter can't identify which manufacturer made the specific pills her mother took.
> **Result**: Each manufacturer pays damages proportional to its market share. If Company A had 30% of the DES market, it pays 30% of damages.

#### Concert of Action

When multiple parties act together in a common plan or scheme that results in harm, all participants are liable.

#### Conspiracy

An agreement between two or more parties to commit an unlawful act. All conspirators are liable for the resulting harm.

### 6. Vicarious Liability

**Vicarious liability** = Holding one person liable for the torts of another, based on their relationship.

Three relationships that create vicarious liability:

1. **Principal and Agent** - A principal can be liable for their agent's torts committed within the scope of the agency

2. **Employer and Employee** - Under **respondeat superior** ("let the master answer"), employers are liable for employees' torts committed within the scope of employment

3. **Parent and Child** - Under the **family purpose doctrine**, a parent who provides a car for family use can be liable for a family member's negligent driving

> **Setup**: A pizza delivery driver (employee) runs a red light while delivering pizzas and hits a pedestrian.
> **What Happens**: The pedestrian is injured and sues.
> **Result**: The pedestrian can sue BOTH the driver AND the pizza company. The company is vicariously liable under respondeat superior because the driver was acting within the scope of employment.

### 7. Good Samaritan Statutes

**Good Samaritan statutes** protect from liability any person who gives emergency assistance to someone in need.

- In most states, these statutes protect **any person** who provides emergency aid
- In some states, protection is limited to **medical personnel only**
- The protection generally applies only if the helper acts in **good faith** and without **gross negligence**

Purpose: To encourage people to help in emergencies without fear of being sued.

> **Good Samaritan Example:**
> **Setup:** A nurse is eating at a restaurant when another customer collapses from an apparent heart attack. The nurse rushes over and performs CPR, accidentally breaking two of the customer's ribs in the process.
> **What Happens:** The customer survives but sues the nurse for the broken ribs.
> **Result:** The Good Samaritan statute protects the nurse from liability. She acted in good faith, provided emergency aid, and used reasonable care (broken ribs are a known risk of CPR). Without this protection, bystanders might hesitate to help, leading to more deaths.

### 8. Mass Tort Litigation

**Mass tort litigation** involves cases where a large number of plaintiffs are injured by the same product or event. Examples: asbestos exposure, pharmaceutical drugs with widespread side effects, environmental contamination.

Challenges include:
- Thousands or millions of potential plaintiffs
- Proof of causation across different exposures/time periods
- Coordinating litigation across multiple jurisdictions
- [2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Multidistrict litigation (MDL) procedures have become the dominant mechanism for mass tort cases since 2008.

### 9. Statutes of Limitations and Repose

#### Statute of Limitations

A **statute of limitations** sets a deadline for filing a lawsuit. If you don't file within the time limit, you lose your right to sue forever.

**When does the clock start (cause of action accrues)?**

A cause of action **accrues** when all the elements of the basis for a right to sue exist. In simpler terms, the clock starts when:
- The wrongful act occurred, AND
- The plaintiff knew or should have known about the injury

This is important because some injuries (like exposure to toxic chemicals) may not become apparent for years.

#### Statute of Repose

A **statute of repose** is like a statute of limitations but with a key difference: it sets an **absolute outer deadline** measured from a specific event (like when a product was sold or a building was completed), regardless of when the injury occurred or was discovered.

In plain English: A statute of limitations says "sue within X years of discovering the injury." A statute of repose says "sue within X years of the product being sold, period -- even if you didn't know about the injury yet."

#### Tolling for Infants/Minors

A statute of limitations **begins to run for an infant from the date the infant comes of age** (reaches the age of majority). This means minors get extra time -- the clock doesn't start until they're adults.

## Review Questions & Answers

### Q1: What were the common-law exceptions to the rule that a contractual relationship was necessary to sue for negligence relating to a product defect? (p. 10.3)

**Answer:** The three common-law exceptions were:

- **Inherently dangerous defective products**: If you marketed an inherently dangerous product that was also defective, you were liable to anyone injured because of the defect
- **Not inherently dangerous but could be if defective**: A manufacturer who made, assembled, or sold a product not inherently dangerous, but that could be inherently dangerous if defective, was liable to any person injured while using the product in a reasonable manner
- **Negligent concealment + fraud**: A manufacturer who was not only negligent in making the product but also knowingly concealed the defect could be liable for both negligence and fraud

### Q2: Explain the common-law liability of a person using or storing explosives. (pp. 10.4-10.5)

**Answer:** At common law, a person using or storing explosives is **strictly liable** for all damages caused by the explosives. This means they are liable regardless of how careful they were.

The distinction is between common law (strict liability for all damages) and situations involving domestic vs. wild animals (which have different rules).

### Q3: Distinguish between common-law liability for domestic animals and wild animals. (pp. 10.5-10.6)

**Answer:**

- **Domestic animals**: Owners are strictly liable for damages caused by their animals' **trespass**, but are strictly liable for *injuries* caused by the animal only if they **knew of its dangerous propensity** (e.g., the dog had bitten before)
- **Wild animals**: Owners are strictly liable for **all acts** and **all damages** caused by wild animals, period. No need to prove the owner knew the animal was dangerous -- wild animals are presumed dangerous.

### Q4: Identify the five elements for a products liability lawsuit based on strict liability. (p. 10.7)

**Answer:**

1. The seller was **in the business of selling products**
2. The product had a **defect** that made it unreasonably dangerous
3. The product was **dangerously defective when it left** the manufacturer's or seller's custody or control
4. The **defect was the proximate cause** of the plaintiff's injury
5. The product was **expected to and did reach the consumer** without substantial change in condition

### Q5: Describe the three major ways in which a product may be defective. (p. 10.8)

**Answer:**

1. **Defect in manufacture or assembly** - The product does not correspond to the original design (assembly line error)
2. **Defect in design** - The product corresponds to the design, and the manufacturer built it exactly as intended, but the design itself is faulty
3. **Failure to warn** - The product is defective in neither design nor manufacture, but it has some inherent danger about which the manufacturer has failed to give proper warning

### Q6: What parties in products liability cases can be strictly liable? (pp. 10.10-10.11)

**Answer:** Parties that can be strictly liable are entities that engage in the **business of selling products**: distributors, wholesalers, retailers, bailors, lessors, builders, and contractors.

### Q7: Is the buyer the only party who can sue in strict liability? Explain. (p. 10.11)

**Answer:** No. Protection can extend to the **ultimate user or consumer**, who might be different from the ultimate buyer, so the buyer is not the only party who can sue in strict liability. Bystanders and others foreseeably affected are also protected.

### Q8: Describe the defenses to a products liability claim. (pp. 10.12-10.13)

**Answer:**

1. **State-of-the-art defense** - Product was safe according to the state of the art at the time it was made
2. **Compliance with statutes and regulations** - Product met standards when made (NOT a complete defense)
3. **Compliance with product specifications** - Manufacturer built to another's specs (generally not liable unless defect is sufficiently obvious)
4. **Open and obvious danger** - No duty to warn of obvious dangers
5. **Plaintiff's knowledge** - User had knowledge equal to the manufacturer's
6. **Assumption of risk** - User knowingly accepted the risk
7. **Misuse of product** - Product was not used in an appropriate and foreseeable manner
8. **Alteration of product** - Product was modified after sale
9. **Post-accident remedial measures** - Prohibits using subsequent safety improvements as evidence of negligence
10. **Written disclaimers** - Attempts to disclaim strict liability (most courts have rejected these)

### Q9: What is the primary purpose of CERCLA? (p. 10.15)

**Answer:** The primary purpose of CERCLA is to **require the cleanup of hazardous waste disposal sites**.

### Q10: Identify the types of parties who might be liable under CERCLA. (p. 10.15)

**Answer:**

- **Current owners and operators** of hazardous waste facilities
- **Past owners or operators** of hazardous waste facilities
- **Generators** of hazardous waste
- **Transporters** of hazardous waste

### Q11: What types of damages do compensatory damages include? (p. 10.19)

**Answer:** Compensatory damages include both **special damages** (specific monetary losses like medical bills, lost wages, property damage) and **general damages** (non-quantifiable losses like pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of consortium).

### Q12: Describe the situations in which a plaintiff can recover punitive damages. (p. 10.20)

**Answer:** A court may award punitive damages when the defendant intended to cause harm or when the defendant acted **oppressively**, **maliciously**, or **fraudulently**.

### Q13: Contrast common law with UCAJTFA joint tortfeasor liability. (pp. 10.22-10.23)

**Answer:**

- **Common law**: A release of one joint tortfeasor released ALL joint tortfeasors, even if the release specifically stated otherwise. Releasing one defendant meant losing your claim against all of them.
- **UCAJTFA**: A plaintiff **CAN release one or more defendants** without releasing the others. This is a major improvement -- you can settle with one defendant and still pursue the others.

### Q14: Identify three relationships that can be the basis of vicarious liability. (pp. 10.26-10.27)

**Answer:**

1. **Principal and agent**
2. **Employer and employee**
3. **Parent and child** (family purpose doctrine)

### Q15: What protection do Good Samaritan statutes provide? (p. 10.27)

**Answer:** In most states, Good Samaritan statutes protect from liability **any person** who gives emergency assistance. Some states' Good Samaritan laws protect only medical personnel.

### Q16: When does a cause of action accrue for purposes of determining when a statute of limitations starts to run? (pp. 10.29-10.30)

**Answer:** A cause of action accrues when **all the elements of a basis for a right to sue occur** -- in other words, when the injury happens and the plaintiff knows or should know about it.

### Q17: When does a statute of limitations begin to run for an infant who is the victim of a tort? (pp. 10.30-10.31)

**Answer:** A statute of limitations begins to run for an infant from **the date the infant comes of age** (reaches the age of majority). The clock does not start ticking while they are a minor.

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Q1: Weed Chopper Products Liability

**Scenario**: Marla purchased a Weed Chopper, a powerful automatic weed cutter advertised as the "safest device on the market" for cutting weeds.

**(a) Whom does the law protect with regard to safety problems with the Weed Chopper?**

**Answer:** The law protects the buyer of a defective product, as well as the ultimate user and **anyone else injured** because of the use of the product. Most courts also allow suits by nonusers, such as **bystanders** and other strangers.

**(b) The Weed Chopper hits a rock and causes it to fly into the air, injuring Marla's neighbor's son, Denny. Can Denny sue the manufacturer? Can he sue the store where Marla bought it?**

**Answer:** In most states, Denny, a **bystander**, would be protected by law. Denny can sue **both** the store and the manufacturer, and a court would determine the relative liability (if any) of each.

**(c) For what might the Weed Chopper manufacturer be strictly liable, if anything?**

**Answer:** The Weed Chopper manufacturer can be strictly liable for **product defects** (faulty manufacture, assembly, or design) or for **failure to warn** of possible danger.

### Application Q2: Gas Station Environmental Liability

**Scenario**: Jack recently bought a gas station. He discovers the underground storage tank has been leaking gasoline into the ground for years.

**(a) Who must correct this situation?**

**Answer:** **Jack** must correct this situation because he faces **potential liability as the current owner** even though he did not know about the underground tank when he purchased the gas station. As a current owner under CERCLA, he is a potentially responsible party (PRP).

**(b) Can Jack sue anyone?**

**Answer:** Yes. Jack can sue the people from whom he bought the station and/or **any previous owners** who might be responsible for the leaking tank.

### Application Q3: Hazardous Waste on Sue's Ranch

**Scenario**: Sue discovered a hazardous waste dumping site on her cattle ranch, purchased only six months ago. She has no idea who dumped the medical wastes. Her CGL policy has been in effect for only six months and is occurrence-based.

**(a) What are the legal issues Sue now faces, particularly with her CGL policy?**

**Answer:** Sue's occurrence-based CGL policy would cover **occurrences** that have happened during the six months she has had the policy, but would **not cover** wastes dumped there years ago. Even if the dumping had occurred within the policy period, an issue might arise as to whether the wastes were **sudden and accidental discharges** -- the only type of covered pollutants under many CGL policies.

**(b) Sue's guest, Ronny, believes he contracted a serious disease from accidental contact with the medical wastes. What kinds of damages might he claim?**

**Answer:** Ronny might claim:
- **Compensatory damages**: Special damages (loss of wages and earnings) and general damages (pain and suffering, permanent injuries, emotional distress)
- **Punitive damages**: Because hazardous waste dumping is involved
- **Liability sources**: CERCLA imposes liability potentially on Sue as owner of the dump site, on past owners, on the generators of the polluting substances, and on the transporters (if anyone can determine who they are)

### Application Q4: Manufacturing Plant Pollution

**Scenario**: A manufacturing plant discharges polluted water into a river. Pauline and Sarah each own land along the river. Pauline's factory machinery is damaged. Sarah's property sustains lesser damages.

**(a) Does Pauline have a right of action? Explain.**

**Answer:** Yes. Pauline can sue for:
- **Nuisance** (pollution precludes her enjoyment of her property)
- **Trespass** (pollutants constitute an unlawful entry onto her property)
- **Negligence** (negligent discharge of pollutants causing damage to her machinery and business)
- **Strict liability** (if she claims the pollutants are hazardous substances)

**(b) Does Sarah have a right of action? Explain.**

**Answer:** Yes. Sarah can sue on the **same grounds** as Pauline: nuisance, trespass, negligence, and strict liability if the pollutants are hazardous substances.

### Application Q5: Joint Tortfeasors - Jack, Carol, and Joe Assault Tom

**Scenario**: Jack, Carol, and Joe assault Tom in the dark. Tom's fractured skull results in $10,000 medical bills plus $25,000 pain and suffering ($35,000 total). Tom can't tell which of the three struck the blow.

**(a) Can Tom sue Jack only? Explain.**

**Answer:** Yes. Tom can sue only Jack if he wishes. He can **choose** to sue all the tortfeasors or any one of them. (Plaintiffs often have good reasons for choosing specific defendants -- others may have moved, be hard to find, or lack assets.)

**(b) Assuming Tom sues only Jack, can Jack involve Carol and Joe in the suit?**

**Answer:** Yes. Jack can bring Carol and Joe into the suit, or he can **sue them separately** on his own to obtain their proportionate shares of the damages. Jack does not have to pay the entire judgment against him (unless he was totally at fault).

**(c) If Tom recovers the full $35,000 from Jack, what proportion can Jack recover from Carol and Joe under common law? Under the UCAJTFA?**

**Answer:**
- **At common law**: Jack could be totally responsible for the damages if he is the only one Tom sues. Common law held each tortfeasor was jointly and severally liable for the full amount.
- **Under the UCAJTFA**: Jack would have a **right of contribution** from Carol and Joe. If Jack pays more than his pro rata share of the common liability, he can collect the excess equally from Carol and Joe (not prorated between them). For example, if Jack is liable for $10,000 of the $35,000 (his 1/3 share), the remaining **$25,000 would be split equally** between Carol and Joe as their liabilities.

## Exam Tips

### Critical Concepts to Memorize

1. **Five elements of strict liability products claim** -- Know all five cold. This is a guaranteed exam question.

2. **Three ways a product can be defective**: Manufacture/assembly, Design, Failure to warn. The exam loves testing whether you can classify a defect into the right category.

3. **Four types of PRPs under CERCLA**: Current owners/operators, Past owners/operators, Generators, Transporters. Memorize this list.

4. **Three relationships for vicarious liability**: Principal-Agent, Employer-Employee, Parent-Child. Don't confuse with other relationships.

5. **Common law vs. UCAJTFA on release of joint tortfeasors**: Common law = release one, release all. UCAJTFA = can release one without releasing others.

### Common Exam Traps

- **TRAP 1**: "Compliance with regulations" is a **complete defense** to products liability. WRONG -- it is NOT a complete defense. You can still be liable even if you followed all the rules.

- **TRAP 2**: Only the buyer can sue under strict liability. WRONG -- users, consumers, and bystanders can also sue.

- **TRAP 3**: Confusing **statute of limitations** with **statute of repose**. Limitations = clock starts when injury is discovered. Repose = clock starts when product is sold (absolute deadline).

- **TRAP 4**: Thinking CERCLA requires proof of negligence. WRONG -- CERCLA imposes **strict liability**. Current owners are liable even if they didn't cause the contamination.

- **TRAP 5**: Confusing **nominal damages** with **punitive damages**. Nominal = tiny amount proving a right was violated. Punitive = large amount meant to punish bad behavior.

- **TRAP 6**: Thinking written disclaimers effectively eliminate strict liability. Most courts have **rejected** these disclaimers.

- **TRAP 7**: Under common law joint tortfeasor rules, releasing one defendant means releasing ALL. Under UCAJTFA, this is no longer the case.

- **TRAP 8**: **Post-accident remedial measures** (making a product safer after someone is hurt) CANNOT be used as evidence of negligence. The exam tests this.

- **TRAP 9**: Good Samaritan statutes protect everyone in most states, but some states limit protection to medical personnel only. Watch for jurisdiction-specific questions.

- **TRAP 10**: The statute of limitations for a minor/infant does NOT start running until they reach the **age of majority**.

### High-Priority Study Areas

The exam tends to focus on **products liability** (the five elements and defenses), **CERCLA/environmental liability** (who pays), and **joint tortfeasor contribution** (common law vs. UCAJTFA). These are the heaviest-tested sections.

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Products Liability at a Glance
- **MacPherson Doctrine**: No privity required to sue manufacturer
- **Strict Liability**: No need to prove negligence -- just prove the defect and that it caused injury
- **5 Elements**: (1) Seller in business, (2) Defect exists, (3) Defect existed when product left seller, (4) Proximate cause, (5) No substantial change in condition
- **3 Defect Types**: Manufacturing, Design, Failure to warn
- **Who can be liable**: Any entity in the chain of distribution (manufacturers through retailers)
- **Who can sue**: Buyers, users, consumers, AND bystanders

### Environmental Liability at a Glance
- **CERCLA/Superfund**: Requires cleanup of hazardous waste sites
- **PRPs**: Current owners, past owners, generators, transporters
- **Liability is STRICT** -- no negligence required
- **CGL policies**: May not cover gradual pollution; "sudden and accidental" limitation

### Damages at a Glance
- **Compensatory**: Special (economic) + General (non-economic)
- **Nominal**: Token amount ($1) when right violated but no real harm
- **Punitive**: Punishment for oppressive, malicious, or fraudulent conduct

### Joint Tortfeasors at a Glance
- **Common law**: Joint and several liability; release one = release all
- **UCAJTFA**: Can release individually; right of contribution; equal shares
- **Special theories**: Enterprise liability, alternative liability, market-share liability, concert of action, conspiracy

### Vicarious Liability at a Glance
- **3 Relationships**: Principal-Agent, Employer-Employee, Parent-Child
- **Family Purpose Doctrine**: Parent liable for family member's negligent driving

### Time Limits at a Glance
- **Statute of limitations**: Deadline from discovery of injury
- **Statute of repose**: Absolute deadline from date of sale
- **Minors**: Clock starts at age of majority
