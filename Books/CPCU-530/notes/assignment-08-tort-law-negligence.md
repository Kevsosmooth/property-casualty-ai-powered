### I. DEFINITION AND CLASSIFICATION OF TORTS

**What is a tort?** A tort is a wrongful act or omission (other than a crime or a breach of contract) that injures another person and for which the law allows a civil remedy, usually money damages. Think of it as a "civil wrong" -- someone does something (or fails to do something) that hurts you, and you can sue them for it.

**Plain English**: If someone wrongs you and it's not a crime or a broken contract, it's probably a tort. Torts are the basis of most personal injury lawsuits.

**Tortfeasor**: The person who commits a tort. (Plain English: the wrongdoer.)

**Three Classifications of Torts**:

1. **Negligence** -- Unintentional harm caused by failure to exercise reasonable care (the focus of THIS chapter)
2. **Intentional torts** -- Deliberate wrongful acts (covered in Assignment 9)
3. **Strict liability** -- Liability without fault for abnormally dangerous activities or defective products (covered in Assignment 10)

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A driver is texting while driving through a residential neighborhood
- **What Happens**: The driver runs a stop sign and hits a pedestrian, breaking the pedestrian's leg
- **The Result**: This is a **negligence** tort. The driver did not INTEND to hit anyone (not intentional), but failed to exercise reasonable care (was texting instead of watching the road). The pedestrian can sue the driver for negligence to recover damages for medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

---

### II. APPLICABLE LAW

When a tort case involves parties from different states, the court must decide which state's law to apply. This matters because different states have very different tort rules (some use contributory negligence, others use comparative negligence, etc.).

**Three Approaches to Choosing Applicable Law**:

#### A. Lex Loci (Law of the Place)

**Definition (Plain English)**: The traditional rule -- apply the law of the state where the wrong occurred (where the tort happened). The Latin phrase *lex loci delicti* means "law of the place of the wrong."

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Alice lives in New Jersey and is driving through Pennsylvania
- **What Happens**: She gets into a car accident in Pennsylvania with Bob, a Pennsylvania resident
- **The Result**: Under *lex loci*, Pennsylvania law applies because the accident happened in Pennsylvania. If Alice sues in a New Jersey court (the **forum** = the court where the case is heard), that court would still apply Pennsylvania law because the **situs** (place of the wrong) is Pennsylvania.

#### B. Interest Analysis

**Definition (Plain English)**: A more modern approach where the court looks at which state has a greater interest in protecting its citizens and applies THAT state's law. The court weighs the competing interests of each state.

#### C. Rule of Significant Contacts

**Definition (Plain English)**: The court looks at which state has the MOST connections to the case -- where the parties live, where the harm happened, where the conduct occurred -- and applies the law of the state with the most significant contacts.

**KEY DISTINCTION FOR EXAM**: These three rules can lead to DIFFERENT state laws being applied to the SAME case. This is a frequent test point.

**Forum** = The court or state where the case is being tried
**Situs** = The place where the tort occurred

### III. NEGLIGENCE -- THE FOUR ELEMENTS

**Negligence (Plain English)**: The failure to exercise the degree of care that a reasonably careful person would exercise under similar circumstances, resulting in harm to another person.

To win a negligence case, the plaintiff must prove ALL FOUR elements. If any one element is missing, there is NO negligence.

**The Four Elements** (memorize this -- it WILL be on the exam):

1. **Legal Duty** -- The defendant owed a duty of care to the plaintiff
2. **Breach of Duty** -- The defendant failed to meet that duty
3. **Proximate Cause** -- The defendant's breach actually caused the plaintiff's harm
4. **Actual Injury or Damage** -- The plaintiff suffered real, measurable harm

**Memory Trick**: Think **D-B-C-I** (Duty, Breach, Cause, Injury) -- "Don't Break Cars Intentionally"

---

#### A. Element 1: Legal Duty

**Definition (Plain English)**: A legal duty arises when the law recognizes a relationship between two parties that requires one party to exercise a certain standard of care toward the other. Not everyone owes a duty to everyone else -- there must be a legally recognized relationship.

**How Legal Duty Arises**:
- By **statute** (a law says you must act a certain way -- e.g., traffic laws require you to stop at red lights)
- By **contract** (you agreed to do something -- e.g., a doctor agrees to treat a patient)
- By **relationship** (the law creates duties based on your relationship to others -- e.g., property owner to visitors)

**Standard of Care Depends on WHO You're Dealing With**:

The duty owed varies depending on the status of the person:

1. **Trespasser** (someone on your property without permission) -- You owe only the duty to **refrain from willful or wanton injury**. You can't set traps, but you don't have to make the property safe for them.

2. **Licensee** (someone on your property with permission but for THEIR own benefit -- e.g., a friend visiting, a door-to-door salesperson) -- You owe the duty to **refrain from willful/wanton injury AND warn of hidden dangers** that you know about.

3. **Invitee** (someone on your property for YOUR mutual benefit or by public invitation -- e.g., a customer in your store, a patient in your office) -- You owe the HIGHEST duty: **reasonable care to keep the premises safe AND warn of concealed dangers**. You must actively inspect for hazards.

**CRITICAL EXAM DISTINCTION**: Trespasser < Licensee < Invitee (duty increases)

**The Reasonable Person Test**: The standard used to measure whether someone met their legal duty. Would a **reasonable person** (an imaginary careful, prudent person) have acted the same way under the same circumstances? If not, there's a breach.

**Special Standards of Care**:

- **Persons with Disabilities**: The standard is based on how a reasonable person WITH that same disability would act. A blind person is held to the standard of a reasonable blind person, not a sighted person.

- **Professionals** (doctors, lawyers, insurance agents): Held to a HIGHER standard -- the skill and knowledge of an **average member of that profession** in the same community, applied with reasonable care.

- **Children**: Generally held to the standard of a child of similar age, intelligence, and experience. EXCEPTION: A minor over 14 is often presumed capable of negligence. **A minor who engages in an adult activity (like driving a car) is held to the ADULT standard of care.**

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Dr. Smith, a general practitioner, performs a routine physical exam on a patient
- **What Happens**: Dr. Smith fails to notice a very obvious lump that any competent doctor would have detected, and the patient's cancer goes undiagnosed for a year
- **The Result**: Dr. Smith breached his legal duty. As a professional, he is held to the standard of an average doctor in his field. Missing an obvious lump that any competent GP would have found is a breach of that professional standard of care.

---

#### B. Element 2: Breach of Duty

**Definition (Plain English)**: The defendant failed to meet the standard of care that was owed. They didn't do what a reasonable person would have done (or did something a reasonable person would NOT have done).

**Breach vs. Reckless Misconduct** -- There's an important distinction:

**Reckless Misconduct** (Plain English): Acting with conscious disregard for a known, serious risk. It's worse than ordinary negligence but falls short of intentional harm. Think: "I know this is dangerous but I don't care."

**Why the Distinction Matters** (4 Key Consequences):

1. **Defense limitations**: Contributory negligence may NOT be a sufficient defense against reckless misconduct (it IS a defense against ordinary negligence)
2. **Punitive damages**: Courts can award punitive damages for reckless misconduct (not typically for ordinary negligence)
3. **Trespasser liability**: Landowners can be liable for reckless misconduct toward trespassers, but not usually for ordinary negligence
4. **Auto guest statutes**: Some state statutes do not permit recovery for ordinary negligence in guest-passenger cases, but DO permit recovery for reckless misconduct

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A delivery driver is running very late and decides to drive 90 mph through a school zone during dismissal time
- **What Happens**: The driver hits a child crossing the street
- **The Result**: This goes beyond ordinary negligence into **reckless misconduct**. The driver consciously disregarded a known, serious risk (speeding through a school zone when children are present). The child's family can seek both compensatory AND punitive damages.

---

#### C. Element 3: Proximate Cause

**Definition (Plain English)**: There must be a close enough causal connection between the defendant's breach and the plaintiff's injury. The defendant's action must have CAUSED the harm in a legally meaningful way. The injury can't be too remote or unforeseeable.

**Four Rules for Determining Proximate Cause**:

**1. "But For" Rule** -- The most basic test. Ask: "BUT FOR the defendant's action, would the injury have occurred?" If the answer is no (the injury would NOT have occurred without the defendant's action), then causation exists.

- **Example**: But for the driver running the red light, the pedestrian would not have been hit. Causation established.

**2. Substantial Factor Rule** -- Used when MULTIPLE causes contribute to an injury. Was the defendant's conduct a "substantial factor" in producing the harm? Even if other factors contributed, the defendant can be liable if their actions were a substantial factor.

- **Example**: Two factories both dump chemicals into a river. A downstream landowner's crops are destroyed. Even though both factories contributed, each factory's dumping was a "substantial factor" in the damage.

**3. Proof of Defendant's Responsibility** -- The plaintiff must present proof connecting the defendant's specific actions to the harm. You can't just say "someone was negligent" -- you have to show THIS defendant was responsible.

**4. Foreseeability Rule** -- The defendant is only liable for consequences that were **reasonably foreseeable**. If the harm was completely bizarre and unforeseeable, there's no proximate cause even if the defendant was negligent.

- **Example**: A driver negligently rear-ends a car. The car turns out to be carrying dynamite (unknown to anyone) and explodes, damaging buildings a block away. The driver may not be liable for the explosion damage because carrying dynamite in a regular car was not foreseeable.

**Important Causation Concepts**:

- **Intervening Act**: A new, independent act that occurs AFTER the defendant's negligence and contributes to the harm. If the intervening act was foreseeable, the defendant is still liable. If it was completely unforeseeable (a "superseding cause"), it may break the chain of causation.

- **Concurrent Causation**: When two or more causes combine at the SAME TIME to produce harm. Both parties can be held liable.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Contractor negligently leaves a gas line exposed during construction work at a building
- **What Happens**: A month later, an unrelated electrician accidentally sparks near the exposed gas line, causing an explosion
- **The Result**: The contractor's negligence is still a proximate cause because an accidental ignition of an exposed gas line is a **foreseeable** consequence of leaving it exposed. The electrician's spark is a foreseeable **intervening act**, not a superseding cause. Both the contractor AND the electrician may be liable.

---

#### D. Element 4: Actual Injury or Damage

**Definition (Plain English)**: The plaintiff must have suffered REAL, measurable harm -- physical injury, property damage, financial loss, etc. Without actual damages, there is no negligence claim, even if the defendant was careless.

**KEY POINT**: Unlike some intentional torts (like battery, where even offensive touching without injury counts), negligence REQUIRES actual damage. You can't sue for negligence just because someone COULD have hurt you.

---

### IV. PROOF OF NEGLIGENCE

Sometimes it's hard for a plaintiff to directly prove negligence. The law provides two special doctrines to help:

#### A. Negligence Per Se

**Definition (Plain English)**: "Negligence in itself." When a defendant violates a statute (law) that was designed to protect the kind of person the plaintiff is, from the kind of harm that occurred, the violation IS the proof of negligence. The plaintiff doesn't need to separately prove the defendant was careless -- the law violation proves it automatically.

**Requirements**:
1. The defendant must have violated a specific **statute or regulation**
2. The statute must have been designed to protect people in the **plaintiff's situation**
3. The harm must be the kind the statute was designed to **prevent**

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A state law requires all construction sites to have guardrails on scaffolding above 10 feet
- **What Happens**: A contractor skips the guardrails on 15-foot scaffolding, and a worker falls and breaks both legs
- **The Result**: This is **negligence per se**. The contractor violated a safety statute that was specifically designed to protect workers (the plaintiff's class) from falling (the type of harm). The violation itself proves negligence -- the worker doesn't need to separately show the contractor was careless.

#### B. Res Ipsa Loquitur

**Definition (Plain English)**: Latin for "the thing speaks for itself." This doctrine applies when the plaintiff can't prove exactly what the defendant did wrong, but the circumstances strongly suggest negligence. It shifts the burden -- instead of the plaintiff having to prove negligence, the defendant has to explain why they WEREN'T negligent.

**Two Requirements** (both must be met):

1. **Probability of negligence**: The event is of a type that ordinarily does NOT happen without someone being negligent
2. **Exclusive control**: The defendant had **exclusive control** of the instrumentality AND had **superior knowledge** of the circumstances, giving them the duty to explain what happened

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A patient goes under general anesthesia for knee surgery
- **What Happens**: After surgery, the patient discovers severe burns on their arm that have nothing to do with the knee operation
- **The Result**: **Res ipsa loquitur** applies. Burns on an arm during knee surgery don't happen without someone's negligence. The surgical team had exclusive control over the patient while under anesthesia and has superior knowledge of what happened. The burden shifts to the medical team to explain the burns.

**EXAM TIP**: Res ipsa loquitur does NOT automatically win the case for the plaintiff -- it just creates an inference of negligence that the defendant can rebut with evidence.

---

### V. IMPUTED NEGLIGENCE

**Definition (Plain English)**: Holding one person legally responsible for the negligence of ANOTHER person, even though the first person didn't personally do anything wrong. The negligence of Person A is "imputed" (attributed) to Person B because of their relationship.

#### A. Imputed Contributory Negligence

**Definition (Plain English)**: When the plaintiff's own claim is weakened because someone else's negligence is attributed TO THE PLAINTIFF. If the negligence of a person closely related to the plaintiff contributed to the injury, that negligence may be imputed to the plaintiff and reduce or bar their recovery.

**Common Situations**:
- **Auto accident passengers**: If a passenger is injured in a car accident and the driver of their car was also negligent, the driver's negligence may be imputed to the passenger (assumed the passenger knew about the driver's skills/lack thereof)
- **Spousal lawsuits**: If one spouse sues a third party for personal injury, and the OTHER spouse's negligence contributed to the injury, that spouse's negligence may be imputed to the injured spouse

#### B. Vicarious Liability

**Definition (Plain English)**: When one person is held liable for another person's negligent acts because of their relationship. The most common example: an employer is vicariously liable for the negligent acts of employees committed within the scope of employment.

**Key Doctrines of Vicarious Liability**:

**1. Negligent Entrustment** -- Entrusting a dangerous instrument (car, weapon, tool) to someone you know or should know is incompetent, inexperienced, or reckless. If that person causes harm, YOU are liable for negligent entrustment.

- **Example**: Letting a friend who you know has seizures borrow your car. If they have a seizure while driving and hit someone, you're liable for negligent entrustment.

**2. Dangerous Instrumentality Doctrine** -- In some states, the OWNER of a dangerous instrument (like a car) is automatically liable for injuries caused by anyone operating it with the owner's permission, regardless of whether the owner was negligent in lending it.

**3. Negligent Supervision** -- Failing to properly supervise someone you have a duty to supervise (like a parent supervising a minor child, or an employer supervising an employee).

**CRITICAL EXAM SCENARIO**: When a mother gives the keys to her automobile to her 15-year-old son, there are grounds for BOTH negligent entrustment AND negligent supervision if the son injures a person or damages property while driving. The mother negligently entrusted a dangerous instrument (car) to someone she knew was incompetent (no license, inexperienced), AND failed in her supervisory duty.

### VI. DEFENSES TO NEGLIGENCE ACTIONS

Even when a plaintiff proves all four elements of negligence, the defendant may raise defenses that reduce or eliminate the plaintiff's recovery.

---

#### D1. Contributory Negligence

**Definition (Plain English)**: The old, harsh common-law rule. If the PLAINTIFF was negligent AT ALL -- even 1% at fault -- the plaintiff gets NOTHING. Total bar to recovery.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A pedestrian jaywalks across a busy street at night wearing dark clothing
- **What Happens**: A driver who is texting hits the pedestrian
- **The Result**: In a **contributory negligence** state, the pedestrian gets NOTHING. Even though the driver was texting (clearly negligent), the pedestrian was also negligent by jaywalking in dark clothing. Under pure contributory negligence, ANY fault by the plaintiff = zero recovery.

**IMPORTANT**: Only a few states still follow pure contributory negligence (Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, and D.C. as of the textbook). Most states have moved to comparative negligence because contributory negligence is considered too harsh.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Some of these states may have modified their contributory negligence rules since 2008.

---

#### D2. Comparative Negligence

**Definition (Plain English)**: A fairer system that replaced contributory negligence in most states. Instead of barring all recovery, the plaintiff's damages are REDUCED by the percentage that is the plaintiff's own fault. There are several variations:

**a. Pure Comparative Negligence Rule**

The plaintiff can recover damages reduced by their own percentage of fault, NO MATTER HOW MUCH at fault the plaintiff is.

- Example: Plaintiff is 90% at fault, defendant is 10% at fault. Damages are $100,000. Plaintiff recovers $10,000 (10% of damages). Even though the plaintiff was overwhelmingly at fault, they still recover something.

**Criticism of Pure Comparative**: It doesn't base recovery on the relative amount of fault -- a party whose negligence was the MAJOR factor in causing the injury can still recover damages. Many people consider this unfair.

**b. 50 Percent Comparative Negligence Rule**

The plaintiff can recover reduced damages ONLY if the plaintiff's negligence is **NOT GREATER THAN** (i.e., 50% or less of) the defendant's negligence. If the plaintiff is 51% or more at fault, they get nothing.

- Example: Plaintiff is 50% at fault, defendant is 50% at fault. Damages are $100,000. Plaintiff recovers $50,000 (reduced by their 50% fault). But if plaintiff were 51% at fault, they'd recover nothing.

**c. 49 Percent Comparative Negligence Rule**

The plaintiff can recover reduced damages ONLY if the plaintiff's negligence is **LESS THAN** (i.e., strictly under 50% of) the defendant's negligence. If plaintiff is 50% or more at fault, they get nothing.

- Example: Plaintiff is 49% at fault, defendant is 51% at fault. Damages are $100,000. Plaintiff recovers $51,000. But if plaintiff were 50% at fault -- even though equally at fault -- they'd recover nothing.

**d. Slight Versus Gross Rule**

The plaintiff can recover only if their negligence was "slight" compared to the defendant's "gross" negligence. This is the narrowest form and rarely used.

**EXAM TIP -- Know the Differences**:
- **Pure**: Plaintiff ALWAYS recovers (even at 99% fault)
- **50%**: Plaintiff recovers if their fault is 50% OR LESS
- **49%**: Plaintiff recovers if their fault is LESS THAN 50% (strictly under)
- **Slight vs. Gross**: Plaintiff recovers only if their fault was "slight"

---

#### D3. Last Clear Chance Doctrine

**Definition (Plain English)**: A doctrine designed to soften the harsh results of contributory negligence. Even if the plaintiff was negligent, if the DEFENDANT had the "last clear chance" to avoid the injury and didn't take it, the defendant is still liable.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A pedestrian negligently crosses the street without looking
- **What Happens**: A driver sees the pedestrian from 200 feet away but is looking at their phone and doesn't brake. The driver hits the pedestrian.
- **The Result**: Under the **last clear chance** doctrine, even though the pedestrian was negligent (crossing without looking), the driver had the LAST clear opportunity to avoid the accident (saw the pedestrian from 200 feet away) and failed to act. The driver is liable despite the pedestrian's own negligence.

**KEY POINT**: This doctrine is mainly used in **contributory negligence** states to avoid the harsh "total bar" result. In comparative negligence states, it's less important because fault is already shared.

---

#### D4. Assumption of Risk

**Definition (Plain English)**: The plaintiff voluntarily chose to face a known danger. If the plaintiff knew about the risk and voluntarily took it on, they can't complain when the risk materializes.

**Two Requirements** (BOTH must be proven by the defendant):

1. The plaintiff had **full knowledge** of the risk
2. The plaintiff **voluntarily chose** to incur the risk

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A skier reads and signs a waiver at a ski resort stating that skiing involves risks of injury including falls, collisions, and changing snow conditions
- **What Happens**: The skier hits an icy patch on an intermediate trail and breaks their leg
- **The Result**: The skier **assumed the risk**. They had full knowledge of the dangers (signed the waiver, and icy conditions are a well-known skiing hazard) and voluntarily chose to ski anyway. The resort can use the assumption of risk defense.

**However**: If the injury was caused by the resort's SEPARATE negligence (e.g., a broken chairlift, an unmarked cliff), assumption of risk might NOT apply because the skier did not assume THAT specific risk.

---

#### D5. Release of Liability

**Definition (Plain English)**: A contractual agreement where one party gives up (releases) their right to sue another party for negligence. Common examples include waivers signed before sporting events, gym memberships, or adventure activities.

**KEY POINT**: A release can bar recovery even if the defendant was negligent. However, courts will scrutinize releases carefully and may refuse to enforce them if they are unconscionable, against public policy, or involve gross negligence/recklessness.

---

#### D6. Immunity From Liability

Certain parties have legal immunity that protects them from some or all negligence lawsuits:

**a. Sovereign (Governmental) Immunity**

**Definition (Plain English)**: The traditional rule that "the king can do no wrong" -- governments and their agencies can't be sued without their consent. Most states have partially waived this immunity, allowing some lawsuits against the government.

**KEY DISTINCTION -- Governmental vs. Proprietary Functions**:

- **Governmental function**: Activities that can ONLY be performed by the government (police, fire protection, courts, legislation). These are often STILL protected by sovereign immunity.

- **Proprietary function**: Activities that COULD be performed by private entities (running a bus system, operating a swimming pool, managing a parking garage). These are typically NOT protected by sovereign immunity.

**Another Important Distinction**:

- **Administrative/Discretionary acts**: Decisions involving judgment and policy-making (e.g., deciding where to build a road, how to allocate police resources). Generally immune.

- **Ministerial acts**: Routine tasks that don't require judgment (e.g., filling a pothole once it's been reported, maintaining a traffic signal). Generally NOT immune.

**Objective Reasonableness Standard**: Applied to government officials -- asks whether a reasonable official in the same position would have known their conduct violated the law. If reasonable officials could disagree, the official is immune.

**Related Theories**:
- **Quasi-governmental theory**: Private organizations performing government-like functions may also have some immunity
- **Trust fund theory**: Charitable organizations shouldn't be sued because their funds are held in trust for charitable purposes (largely abandoned in most states)

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Many states have further modified their sovereign immunity laws since 2008, and the scope of government liability has generally expanded.

**b. Interspousal Immunity**

**Definition (Plain English)**: The old rule that spouses could NOT sue each other for torts. Based on the legal fiction that husband and wife were "one person."

**Current Status**: **All states have abolished interspousal immunity in whole or in part.** This is a frequently tested point. Spouses CAN now sue each other.

**c. Parent-Child Immunity**

**Definition (Plain English)**: The rule that minor children could not sue their parents for negligence. Most states have modified or limited this immunity, but some retain it for injuries arising from the exercise of parental authority and discretion.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The trend toward abolishing parent-child immunity has continued since 2008, with many more states allowing such suits.

---

#### D7. Independent Contractors

**General Rule**: A person who hires an independent contractor is generally NOT liable for the contractor's torts. This is because the hiring party does not control HOW the independent contractor does the work (unlike an employer-employee relationship).

**THREE EXCEPTIONS** (when you ARE liable for an independent contractor's torts):

1. **Negligent employment**: You negligently hired the contractor (knew or should have known they were incompetent)
2. **Nondelegable duties**: You delegated a duty that the law says you CANNOT delegate (certain safety obligations)
3. **Inherently dangerous work**: You hired the contractor to do work that is inherently dangerous (demolition, blasting, handling hazardous materials)

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A general contractor hires a subcontractor to do demolition work on an old building
- **What Happens**: The subcontractor uses too much explosive, damaging neighboring buildings
- **The Result**: The general contractor IS liable even though the subcontractor is an independent contractor. Demolition with explosives is **inherently dangerous work**, which is one of the three exceptions. You can't avoid liability by delegating inherently dangerous work to an independent contractor.

---

#### D8. Contractual Liability

Three types of contractual agreements affect liability:

**a. Liquidated Damages Agreement**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A clause in a contract that specifies a predetermined amount of money to be paid if one party breaches the contract. Instead of arguing in court about damages, the parties agree upfront on a fixed amount.

- Enforceable when damages would be difficult to calculate and the amount is reasonable
- NOT enforceable if the amount is so high it's actually a "penalty" rather than a reasonable estimate of damages

**b. Hold-Harmless (Indemnity) Agreement**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A contractual provision where one party agrees to "hold harmless" (protect/indemnify) the other party from liability for certain losses. In other words, Party A agrees to cover any losses that Party B might incur.

**Example**: A construction contract where the subcontractor agrees to hold the general contractor harmless for any injuries caused by the subcontractor's work.

**Three Types**:
- **Limited form**: The indemnitor (party giving protection) only covers losses caused by their OWN negligence
- **Intermediate form**: Covers losses caused by BOTH parties' negligence (but not losses caused solely by the indemnitee's negligence)
- **Broad form**: Covers ALL losses, even those caused entirely by the indemnitee's (the protected party's) negligence

**c. Exculpatory Agreement**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A contract clause where one party agrees in advance NOT to hold the other party liable for future negligence. Think: "I agree not to sue you even if you're negligent."

**Three Conditions for Validity** (ALL must be met):

1. The exculpatory clause is **not adverse to a public interest** and not against public policy
2. The party excused from liability is **not under a duty to perform** (not a common carrier, utility, etc.)
3. The contract does not grow out of **unequal bargaining power** and is not otherwise unconscionable

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A skydiving company has all customers sign an exculpatory agreement waiving the right to sue for negligence
- **What Happens**: An experienced skydiver signs the waiver, then is injured due to the company's failure to properly maintain equipment
- **The Result**: The exculpatory agreement MAY be valid because: (1) skydiving is a voluntary recreational activity, not a public necessity; (2) the company is not under a public duty to perform; and (3) the customer had a genuine choice to skydive or not (no unequal bargaining power). However, courts vary on enforcing these, especially if the negligence was gross or reckless.

### VII. LIABILITY OF LANDOWNERS AND OCCUPIERS FOR NEGLIGENCE

This section deals with the special rules governing liability when someone is injured on another person's property.

---

#### A. Natural Conditions

**General Rule**: Landowners generally have **NO duty** to protect others from natural conditions on their land (fallen trees, naturally occurring ice, animal burrows). Nature is nature.

**EXCEPTION for Urban Areas**: In urban/developed areas, a landowner MAY have a duty to protect passersby from natural hazards that the landowner knows about and that pose a danger to people on adjacent public sidewalks or roads.

- **Example**: If a dead tree on your property in the city is leaning dangerously over a public sidewalk, you may have a duty to remove it.

---

#### B. Artificial Conditions

Artificial conditions (things built or placed by humans) create greater liability:

**1. Hidden Dangers**

If a landowner creates or maintains hidden artificial dangers that could injure someone, they may be liable. The key is whether the danger was **concealed** -- visible dangers generally don't create liability because the injured person should have seen and avoided them.

**2. Attractive Nuisance Doctrine**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A special rule for protecting children. If a landowner has an artificial condition on their land that is likely to attract children who are too young to understand the danger, the landowner may be liable if a child is injured, even if the child was technically a trespasser.

**Classic Examples**: Unfenced swimming pools, construction sites, abandoned machinery, trampolines

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A homeowner has an unfenced swimming pool in their backyard, which borders a neighborhood with many young children
- **What Happens**: A 5-year-old from the neighborhood wanders into the yard and falls into the pool, nearly drowning
- **The Result**: Despite the child being a trespasser, the homeowner may be liable under the **attractive nuisance** doctrine. The pool is an artificial condition that is certain to attract children, and a 5-year-old can't appreciate the danger. The homeowner had a duty to take reasonable precautions (like installing a fence).

**3. Sidewalks and Streets**

Landowners adjacent to public sidewalks and streets generally have a duty to keep them safe from hazards created by artificial conditions on their property (e.g., not allowing ice from their sprinklers to form on the sidewalk, not letting their construction debris spill onto the road).

---

#### C. Licenses -- Duties to Different Visitors

**1. Licensee**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A person who enters land with the owner's consent, but for the visitor's OWN purpose/benefit. Examples: social guests, door-to-door salespeople, people taking shortcuts with permission.

**Duty Owed**: Refrain from **willful and wanton injury** AND **warn of hidden dangers** that the landowner KNOWS about.

- **Express license**: Specific verbal or written permission ("Sure, you can walk through my yard")
- **Implied license**: Permission implied by circumstances (a well-worn path through property that the owner doesn't object to)

**2. Invitee**

**Definition (Plain English)**: A person who enters land at the owner's express or implied invitation, usually for a purpose connected to the owner's business or for public purposes. Examples: customers in a store, patients in a doctor's office, library patrons.

**Two Types**:
- **Public invitee**: Invited as a member of the public (e.g., someone visiting a public park or museum)
- **Business invitee**: Invited for business purposes (e.g., a customer in a store)

**Duty Owed**: **Reasonable care to keep the premises safe** AND **warn of concealed dangerous conditions**. This is the HIGHEST duty -- the landowner must actively INSPECT for hazards, not just warn of those they happen to know about.

**CRITICAL EXAM DISTINCTION**: Licensee = warn of KNOWN hidden dangers. Invitee = actively inspect AND keep premises safe. The invitee standard is significantly higher.

---

#### D. Hotel Operators

Hotel operators owe a **high duty of care** to guests (who are invitees). They must exercise reasonable care to keep the premises safe. Hotels are NOT insurers of guest safety (not strictly liable for every injury), but they must actively maintain safe conditions.

---

#### E. Landlords

**General Rule**: A landlord has a duty to maintain the common areas of a rental property (hallways, stairways, parking lots) in a safe condition. For the individual units, the duty generally shifts to the tenant once they take possession, UNLESS:

- The landlord knew of a hidden defect at the time of the lease and failed to disclose it
- The landlord agreed in the lease to make repairs and failed to do so
- The landlord negligently made repairs

**Liability to Third Parties**: Landlords are liable for injuries to third parties (not just tenants) if the injury is the result of a landlord's negligent acts or latent (hidden) defects on the premises that the landlord knew about.

## Key Terms Glossary

**Tort** (p. 8.3) -- A wrongful act or omission (other than a crime or breach of contract) that injures another person and gives rise to a civil action for damages.

**Intentional tort** (p. 8.4) -- A tort in which the wrongdoer intended to commit the act (though not necessarily to cause harm). Covered in detail in Assignment 9.

**Tortfeasor** (p. 8.4) -- The person who commits a tort; the wrongdoer.

**Forum** (p. 8.5) -- The court or state where a lawsuit is being tried.

**Situs** (p. 8.5) -- The place where a tort occurred; the location of the wrongful act.

**Rule of significant contacts** (p. 8.5) -- A method for choosing applicable law by looking at which state has the most connections to the case (where parties live, where the harm occurred, where the conduct occurred).

**Negligence** (p. 8.6) -- The failure to exercise the degree of care that a reasonably careful person would exercise under similar circumstances, resulting in harm to another.

**Legal duty** (p. 8.6) -- An obligation recognized by law requiring one party to exercise a certain standard of care toward another.

**Trespasser** (p. 8.7) -- A person who enters another's property without permission. Owed only the duty not to be willfully or wantonly injured.

**Licensee** (p. 8.8) -- A person on another's property with permission but for their own purpose. Owed duty to refrain from willful/wanton injury and warn of known hidden dangers.

**Invitee** (p. 8.8) -- A person on another's property at the owner's express or implied invitation, usually for mutual benefit or by public invitation. Owed the highest duty -- reasonable care to keep premises safe.

**Reasonable person test** (p. 8.8) -- The objective standard used to measure whether someone met their legal duty; asks what a hypothetical reasonable, prudent person would have done under the same circumstances.

**Reckless misconduct** (p. 8.10) -- Conduct demonstrating a conscious disregard for a known, serious risk of harm. More culpable than ordinary negligence but less than intentional wrongdoing.

**Proximate cause** (p. 8.11) -- A sufficiently close causal connection between the defendant's conduct and the plaintiff's harm to justify imposing liability.

**"But for" rule** (p. 8.12) -- A proximate cause test: but for the defendant's act, the harm would not have occurred.

**Substantial factor rule** (p. 8.12) -- A proximate cause test used when multiple causes contribute: asks whether the defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in producing the harm.

**Foreseeability rule** (p. 8.12) -- A proximate cause limitation: the defendant is only liable for consequences that were reasonably foreseeable.

**Intervening act** (p. 8.13) -- A new, independent act that occurs after the defendant's negligence and contributes to the harm. If foreseeable, doesn't break the causal chain.

**Concurrent causation** (p. 8.13) -- When two or more causes combine at the same time to produce harm; both parties can be held liable.

**Negligence per se** (p. 8.13) -- "Negligence in itself." Violation of a statute designed to protect the plaintiff's class from the type of harm that occurred, which automatically establishes negligence.

**Res ipsa loquitur** (p. 8.14) -- "The thing speaks for itself." A doctrine allowing an inference of negligence when: (1) the event probably wouldn't happen without negligence, and (2) the defendant had exclusive control and superior knowledge.

**Exclusive control** (p. 8.14) -- A requirement for res ipsa loquitur; the defendant must have had sole control over the instrumentality that caused the harm.

**Imputed negligence** (p. 8.15) -- Holding one person liable for another's negligence because of their relationship.

**Imputed contributory negligence** (p. 8.15) -- When the negligence of a person closely related to the plaintiff is attributed to the plaintiff, reducing or barring the plaintiff's recovery.

**Vicarious liability** (p. 8.15) -- Liability imposed on one person for the negligent acts of another because of their relationship (e.g., employer-employee).

**Negligent entrustment** (p. 8.16) -- Entrusting a dangerous instrument to someone known to be incompetent or reckless, creating liability if that person causes harm.

**Dangerous instrumentality doctrine** (p. 8.16) -- In some states, the owner of a dangerous instrument (like a car) is automatically liable for injuries caused by anyone using it with the owner's permission.

**Negligent supervision** (p. 8.16) -- Failing to properly supervise someone you have a duty to supervise (parent/child, employer/employee).

**Contributory negligence** (p. 8.17) -- A defense where ANY negligence by the plaintiff completely bars recovery. The traditional common-law rule, now used in only a few states.

**Comparative negligence** (p. 8.17) -- A system where the plaintiff's damages are reduced by their percentage of fault, rather than being completely barred. Several variations exist.

**Pure comparative negligence rule** (p. 8.18) -- The plaintiff can recover damages reduced by their fault percentage, no matter how much at fault they are (even 99%).

**50 percent comparative negligence rule** (p. 8.18) -- The plaintiff can recover reduced damages only if their negligence is NOT GREATER THAN the defendant's (50% or less at fault).

**49 percent comparative negligence rule** (p. 8.18) -- The plaintiff can recover reduced damages only if their negligence is LESS THAN the defendant's (strictly under 50% at fault).

**Slight versus gross rule** (p. 8.18) -- The plaintiff can recover only if their negligence was "slight" compared to the defendant's "gross" negligence. Rarely used.

**Last clear chance doctrine** (p. 8.19) -- A doctrine that holds the defendant liable even if the plaintiff was negligent, if the defendant had the last clear opportunity to avoid the injury and failed to act.

**Assumption-of-risk defense** (p. 8.20) -- A defense where the plaintiff voluntarily assumed a known risk; requires proof that the plaintiff had full knowledge of the risk and voluntarily chose to incur it.

**Sovereign, or governmental, immunity** (p. 8.21) -- The doctrine that the government cannot be sued without its consent. Most states have partially waived this for certain activities.

**Proprietary function** (p. 8.22) -- A government activity that could also be performed by private entities (e.g., running a bus system). NOT protected by sovereign immunity.

**Governmental function** (p. 8.22) -- A government activity that can ONLY be performed by the government (e.g., police, fire protection). Generally STILL protected by sovereign immunity.

**Objective reasonableness standard** (p. 8.22) -- The standard applied to government officials asking whether a reasonable official in the same position would have known their conduct violated the law.

**Administrative, or discretionary, act** (p. 8.23) -- A government act involving judgment and policy-making. Generally immune from suit.

**Ministerial act** (p. 8.23) -- A routine government task that doesn't require judgment. Generally NOT immune from suit.

**Quasi-governmental theory** (p. 8.23) -- The theory that private organizations performing government-like functions may enjoy some governmental immunity.

**Trust fund theory** (p. 8.23) -- The theory that charitable organizations shouldn't be sued because their funds are held in trust for charitable purposes. Largely abandoned.

**Interspousal immunity** (p. 8.23) -- The old rule that spouses could not sue each other for torts. **All states have abolished this in whole or in part.**

**Parent-child immunity** (p. 8.24) -- The rule that minor children cannot sue their parents for negligence. Modified or limited in most states.

**Hold-harmless, or indemnity, agreement** (p. 8.25) -- A contractual provision where one party agrees to protect the other from liability for certain losses.

**Nuisance** (p. 8.27) -- An unreasonable interference with another person's use and enjoyment of their property.

**Attractive nuisance doctrine** (p. 8.28) -- A rule that holds landowners liable for injuries to trespassing children caused by artificial conditions on land that are certain to attract children who cannot appreciate the danger.

**Express license** (p. 8.29) -- Specific, stated permission to enter property.

**Implied license** (p. 8.29) -- Permission to enter property implied by circumstances or custom.

**Public invitee** (p. 8.29) -- A person invited onto property as a member of the public (e.g., park visitor, museum patron).

**Business invitee** (p. 8.29) -- A person invited onto property for business purposes (e.g., customer in a store).

## Review Questions & Answers (from Course Guide pp. 8.17-8.19)

### Q1: Explain why the lex loci, interest analysis, and rule of significant contacts rules might result in different laws applying to the same case.

Under *lex loci*, the court applies the law of the state where the wrong occurred. Under interest analysis, the court applies the law of the state that has a greater interest in protecting its citizens. Under the rule of significant contacts, the court applies the law of the state with the most connections to the case. Since these tests look at DIFFERENT factors, they can easily point to different states' laws. For example, a New Jersey driver injured in a Pennsylvania accident by a New York driver: *lex loci* = Pennsylvania law; interest analysis might = New Jersey law (protecting its citizen); significant contacts might = any of the three depending on the connections.

### Q2: What are the four elements of negligence?

1. The defendant's **legal duty of care** to the plaintiff
2. The defendant's **breach of the duty**
3. **Proximate cause** connecting the breach to the harm
4. **Actual injury or damage** suffered by the plaintiff

### Q3: Describe the ways in which a legal duty can arise.

Legal duty arises when the parties are in such a relationship that the law imposes on one party a responsibility for the exercise of care toward the other. It can arise by statute, by contract, or by the nature of the parties' relationship (landowner-visitor, doctor-patient, driver-pedestrian, etc.).

### Q4: Explain the standard of care applicable to (a) people with disabilities and (b) professionals.

**a. People with disabilities**: The standard of care is based on how a reasonable person WITH that same disability would act under the circumstances. A blind person is judged against a reasonable blind person, not a sighted person.

**b. Professionals**: The standard is based on the skill and knowledge of average members of that profession in the same community, when applied with reasonable care. A doctor is measured against what a competent doctor would do, not what an average layperson would do.

### Q5: What important legal consequences depend on the distinction between negligence and reckless misconduct?

1. **Contributory negligence defense**: May NOT be sufficient against reckless misconduct (it IS sufficient against ordinary negligence)
2. **Punitive damages**: Courts can award punitive damages for reckless misconduct
3. **Trespasser liability**: Landowners can be liable for reckless misconduct toward trespassers
4. **Auto guest statutes**: Do not permit recovery for ordinary negligence but DO for reckless misconduct

### Q6: Explain the rules for determining proximate cause.

Four rules: (1) **"But for" rule** -- But for the defendant's act, the injury wouldn't have occurred. (2) **Substantial factor rule** -- The defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm (used for multiple causes). (3) **Proof of defendant's responsibility** -- Plaintiff must show THIS defendant was responsible. (4) **Foreseeability rule** -- The defendant is only liable for reasonably foreseeable consequences.

### Q7: Explain under what circumstances the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur applies.

Res ipsa loquitur applies when: (1) The probability exists that the defendant was negligent (the event is the type that doesn't ordinarily happen without negligence), AND (2) The defendant, as the party who had exclusive control of the instrumentality and superior knowledge of the causative circumstances, has the duty to explain the event in question.

### Q8: Describe situations in which imputed negligence can arise.

Imputed negligence can arise when: (1) Passengers injured in auto accidents were assumed to be aware of the driver's skills or lack thereof, making them considered negligent and unable to recover damages. (2) In a lawsuit where one spouse sues another party for personal injury, and the other spouse's negligence contributed to the first spouse's injury through negligence, that negligence is imputed to the injured spouse, barring recovery.

### Q9: When a mother gives the keys of her automobile to her 15-year-old son, do negligent entrustment or negligent supervision (or both) apply?

**BOTH** apply. The mother is liable for negligent entrustment because she entrusted a dangerous instrument (automobile) to someone she knew was incompetent to use it (a 15-year-old without a license). She is also liable for negligent supervision because she failed in her parental duty to supervise her minor child's activities. If the son injures a person or damages property while driving, there are grounds for both claims.

### Q10: What is the effect of contributory negligence at common law?

At common law, a finding of contributory negligence on the plaintiff's part **bars the plaintiff from recovering in a lawsuit**. Even if the plaintiff was only 1% at fault, they get nothing.

### Q11: What criticism has been made of the pure comparative negligence rule?

The pure comparative negligence rule is criticized because it does not base recovery on the relative amount of fault. Under this rule, a party whose negligence was a major factor in causing the incident can still recover damages from a party who was less at fault. For example, a plaintiff who was 90% at fault can still recover 10% of damages from a defendant who was only 10% at fault, which many consider unfair.

### Q12: What is the purpose of the doctrine of last clear chance?

The purpose is to **alleviate the harsh results of contributory negligence**. Under pure contributory negligence, any fault by the plaintiff bars all recovery. The last clear chance doctrine provides an exception: if the defendant had the last opportunity to avoid the injury and failed to take it, the plaintiff can still recover despite being partially at fault.

### Q13: What two circumstances establish the assumption of risk defense?

1. The plaintiff had **full knowledge of the risk**
2. The plaintiff **voluntarily chose to incur it**

Both must be proven. The plaintiff must have actually known about the specific risk (not just risks in general) and freely chose to face it.

### Q14: What types of activities performed by a government are NOT covered by sovereign immunity?

**Proprietary functions** -- activities that can be performed by entities other than governments. Examples include running a bus system, operating a swimming pool, managing a parking garage. When the government acts like a private business, it loses sovereign immunity protection.

### Q15: What is the current status of the defense of interspousal immunity?

**All states have abolished interspousal immunity in whole or in part.** Spouses can now sue each other for tortious conduct. The old common-law doctrine treated husband and wife as "one person" and barred suits between them, but this has been universally rejected as outdated.

### Q16: Under what circumstances might a person be liable for the torts of an independent contractor?

A person can be liable for an independent contractor's torts if the person:
1. **Negligently employs** the contractor (knew or should have known of incompetence)
2. **Delegates nondelegable duties** to the contractor (duties the law says must be performed by the hiring party personally)
3. **Employs the contractor to do inherently dangerous work** (demolition, blasting, hazardous materials)

### Q17: Under what circumstances is an exculpatory agreement valid?

An exculpatory agreement is valid when ALL three conditions are met:
1. The exculpatory clause is **not adverse to a public interest** and not against public policy
2. The party excused from liability is **not under a duty to perform** (not a common carrier, public utility, etc.)
3. The contract does not grow out of the parties' **unequal bargaining power** and is not otherwise unconscionable

### Q18: Explain what constitutes an attractive nuisance.

An attractive nuisance is something **artificial** on the land that is **certain to attract children** who are too young to appreciate the danger. Classic examples include unfenced swimming pools, construction sites, and abandoned machinery. The landowner may be liable for injuries to child trespassers if they fail to take reasonable steps to protect against the foreseeable risk to children.

### Q19: Distinguish between the duties a landowner owes to a licensee and those owed to an invitee.

**Licensee**: The landowner owes a duty to refrain from willful and wanton injury and to warn of hidden defects that the landowner KNOWS about. No duty to actively inspect.

**Invitee**: The landowner owes a duty of reasonable care to keep the premises reasonably safe AND to warn of concealed dangerous conditions. This INCLUDES a duty to actively inspect for hazards. Much higher standard than for licensees.

## Application Questions & Answers (from Course Guide pp. 8.18-8.20)

### Q1: Mitch gives the keys to his car to his son Steve, age 16, who does not have a driver's license. Steve drives negligently, causing an accident in which Peter is injured.

**a. Steve's potential liability to Peter:**
Steve is liable for his own negligence. Minors over fourteen are presumed capable of negligence. Many states hold that minors should be held to the same standard of care as other minors similar in age, experience, capacity, and development under similar circumstances. HOWEVER, a minor who engages in an activity usually undertaken only by adults (like driving a car) is held to the **adult standard of care**. Therefore, Steve is held to the same standard as an adult driver.

**b. Mitch's potential liability to Peter:**
Mitch is liable for **negligent entrustment**. He negligently allowed Steve to use an auto, a dangerous instrument that could cause harm. He knew Steve did not have a license and, in fact, was a poor driver. The accident was the natural result of such negligent entrustment.

### Q2: Joanna, the owner of a canoe rental service, rented a canoe to Alan knowing he was intoxicated. Alan upset the canoe and screamed for help. Joanna went out in her canoe, and its bow hit Alan in the head. Alan drowned.

**a. Joanna's duty to Alan:**
Joanna may be liable to Alan because she did not use due care in renting the canoe to an intoxicated customer. She had a duty to exercise reasonable care, and renting to someone she knew was intoxicated violated that duty.

**b. Joanna's breach of duty:**
Joanna breached her duty when she rented the canoe to Alan knowing he was intoxicated and unfit to use it.

**c. Joanna's potential liability to Alan's estate and family:**
Joanna is potentially liable for negligence and negligent entrustment for the following reasons:
- She was **negligent** in that she had a legal duty, she failed to conform to the appropriate standard of care, there was a causal connection between Joanna's negligence and Alan's death, and Alan suffered a loss of life
- She is liable for **negligent entrustment** in that she carelessly allowed an incompetent person (intoxicated Alan) to use a potentially dangerous instrument, and his incompetence was the proximate cause of his death
- Joanna's rental of a canoe to an intoxicated person might be considered **reckless misconduct**, for which Joanna may be subject to **punitive damages**

### Q3: Tim and Mary collide at an intersection. Tim sues Mary. Mary counterclaims. The jury finds Tim 49% negligent and Mary 51% negligent. What would the result be if:

**a. The state applies contributory negligence?**
In a contributory negligence state, Tim would collect nothing because he contributed to his own injury. Mary would also collect nothing on her counterclaim because she also contributed to her own injury.

**b. The state had a pure comparative negligence rule?**
Under pure comparative negligence, Tim would recover damages diminished by his proportion of the total negligence. Tim would recover 51% of the damages he claims (since Mary was 51% at fault). Mary would recover 49% of her damages on the counterclaim.

**c. The state had a 49 percent comparative negligence rule?**
Under the 49% rule, Tim can recover reduced damages so long as his negligence is LESS THAN the other party's. Here Tim's negligence was 49% (less than Mary's 51%), so he can recover damages reduced by his 49% proportion. Mary CANNOT recover on her counterclaim because her negligence (51%) is greater than Tim's (49%).

**d. The jury also found that Tim had a chance to avoid the accident but did not do so?**
Under the **last clear chance** doctrine, Tim could lose the case. This doctrine holds that the party who has the last clear chance to avoid the injury and fails to do so is solely responsible. If Tim saw the collision coming and could have avoided it but didn't, he bears responsibility regardless of Mary's negligence.

### Q4: Jane, while soldering a pipe joint in the process of installing plumbing in a new house, negligently set fire to the structure. The owner sues Jane, Brad (the plumbing contractor and Jane's employer), and Lyle (the general contractor).

**a. On what theory might Brad be liable for Jane's acts?**
Brad is Jane's employer and could be **vicariously liable** for her work. An employer is generally liable for the negligent acts of employees committed within the scope of employment. Unless a court determines that Jane is actually an independent contractor, Brad (as her employer) is liable for her on-the-job negligence.

**b. On what theory might Lyle be liable for Jane's acts?**
Generally, the employer of an independent contractor (Lyle hired Brad's plumbing company) is NOT liable for an independent contractor's torts. However, one exception is for **inherently dangerous work**. The question is whether soldering is inherently dangerous. A court would decide this based on prior case law (precedents) defining inherently dangerous work.

**c. Is Jane also liable?**
Yes. If Jane is an independent contractor (a professional plumber), she is also personally liable for her own negligence.

**d. What potential defenses might Brad and Lyle have?**
Brad might argue that he had no control over Jane's work and that she is, in reality, a professional and independent contractor responsible for her own negligence. Lyle might argue that only Brad is responsible for the torts of his own employee, Jane, and that Lyle as the general contractor had no control over how the plumbing work was done.

### Q5: Prospect invited Producer to his home to discuss insurance. Producer arrived late. When she left, it was dark, and Prospect's thirty-foot walk was lit dimly by a forty-watt yellow porch-light bulb. Producer failed to see eight concrete steps outside the gate, fell down the steps, and broke her arm.

**a. What was Producer's status on Prospect's property?**
Producer is a **licensee**. She was neither a public nor a business invitee -- she was on the property at Prospect's invitation for a discussion but does not fall into the special categories of invitees. Even though she was there to conduct business, she was essentially a social/business visitor without the mutual benefit relationship that typically defines an invitee.

**b. What duties, if any, did Prospect owe to Producer?**
Prospect owed Producer the duty to **refrain from willful and wanton injury** and to **warn of hidden defects**. Otherwise, Producer took the property in the condition in which it existed.

**c. Was Prospect liable to Producer in this situation?**
**Prospect was not liable** because the facts reveal no violation of duty. There was no willful or wanton injury, and the steps were not a "hidden defect" -- they were a standard feature of the property. The dim lighting created a condition that Producer should have exercised reasonable care to navigate. Prospect fulfilled the duties owed to a licensee.

## Exam Tips

### Most Likely Test Points

1. **Four Elements of Negligence** -- This will DEFINITELY be tested. Memorize: Legal Duty, Breach of Duty, Proximate Cause, Actual Injury/Damage. ALL FOUR must be present.

2. **Trespasser vs. Licensee vs. Invitee** -- Know the three categories and the escalating duty owed to each. This is a very common exam scenario question.

3. **Contributory vs. Comparative Negligence** -- Know ALL the variations: pure contributory (total bar), pure comparative (always recover), 50% rule (recover at 50% or less), 49% rule (recover at under 50%). Expect calculation questions.

4. **Res Ipsa Loquitur** -- Two requirements: probability of negligence + exclusive control with superior knowledge. Know when it applies and what it does (shifts the burden, doesn't automatically win).

5. **Negligent Entrustment vs. Negligent Supervision** -- The mother/15-year-old-son scenario is a classic exam question. Know that BOTH apply simultaneously.

6. **Independent Contractor Exceptions** -- Three situations where you ARE liable: negligent employment, nondelegable duties, inherently dangerous work.

7. **Exculpatory Agreement Requirements** -- Three conditions for validity: not against public interest, no duty to perform, no unequal bargaining power.

8. **Attractive Nuisance** -- Artificial condition + certain to attract children + children can't appreciate the danger.

9. **Last Clear Chance** -- Purpose: alleviates harsh results of contributory negligence. The party with the last opportunity to avoid harm is responsible.

10. **Interspousal Immunity** -- All states have abolished it in whole or in part. This is a simple "know or don't know" test point.

### Common Exam Traps

- **Trap**: Confusing the 50% and 49% comparative negligence rules. At EXACTLY 50% fault: the plaintiff RECOVERS under the 50% rule but gets NOTHING under the 49% rule. This one-percentage-point difference is a favorite exam trick.

- **Trap**: Thinking negligence per se applies to ANY law violation. It only applies when the statute was designed to protect the plaintiff's class from the type of harm that occurred.

- **Trap**: Assuming res ipsa loquitur means automatic victory for the plaintiff. It only creates an INFERENCE of negligence that the defendant can rebut.

- **Trap**: Forgetting that a minor driving a car is held to the ADULT standard of care, not the child standard.

- **Trap**: Thinking contributory negligence is still the majority rule. Most states have moved to comparative negligence. Only a few states still use pure contributory negligence.

- **Trap**: Confusing licensees and invitees. A door-to-door salesperson is a LICENSEE (there for their own purpose), not an invitee. A customer in a store is an INVITEE (mutual benefit).

- **Trap**: Thinking a person is NEVER liable for an independent contractor's torts. There are three clear exceptions (negligent employment, nondelegable duties, inherently dangerous work).

- **Trap**: Forgetting that assumption of risk requires BOTH knowledge AND voluntary choice. If the plaintiff didn't know about the specific risk, the defense fails even if they voluntarily participated.

- **Trap**: Thinking natural conditions on land always create no duty. In urban areas, there CAN be a duty to protect passersby from natural hazards the owner knows about.

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Four Elements of Negligence (ALL Required)
- **Legal Duty** = Defendant owed a duty of care to plaintiff
- **Breach of Duty** = Defendant failed to meet that duty
- **Proximate Cause** = Breach caused the harm (but for, substantial factor, foreseeability)
- **Actual Injury** = Plaintiff suffered real, measurable harm

### Standard of Care by Visitor Type
- **Trespasser** = Refrain from willful/wanton injury only
- **Licensee** = No willful/wanton injury + warn of KNOWN hidden dangers
- **Invitee** = Reasonable care to keep premises safe + actively inspect for hazards

### Proof of Negligence Doctrines
- **Negligence per se** = Statute violation = automatic proof of negligence (if statute protects this class from this harm)
- **Res ipsa loquitur** = "Thing speaks for itself" -- inference of negligence when defendant had exclusive control

### Imputed Negligence
- **Imputed contributory negligence** = Other person's negligence attributed to plaintiff
- **Vicarious liability** = Liability for another's acts due to relationship (employer-employee)
- **Negligent entrustment** = Lending dangerous instrument to known incompetent
- **Dangerous instrumentality** = Owner liable for anyone using their dangerous instrument with permission
- **Negligent supervision** = Failing to supervise someone you must supervise

### Defenses to Negligence
- **Contributory negligence** = ANY plaintiff fault = total bar (few states)
- **Pure comparative** = Plaintiff always recovers, reduced by fault % (even at 99%)
- **50% comparative** = Plaintiff recovers if fault is 50% or less
- **49% comparative** = Plaintiff recovers if fault is strictly under 50%
- **Slight vs. gross** = Plaintiff recovers only if fault was "slight" (rare)
- **Last clear chance** = Party with last opportunity to avoid harm is liable
- **Assumption of risk** = Full knowledge + voluntary choice = no recovery
- **Release of liability** = Contractual waiver of right to sue

### Immunity Types
- **Sovereign immunity** = Government immune for governmental functions; NOT immune for proprietary functions
- **Interspousal** = ABOLISHED in all states (in whole or in part)
- **Parent-child** = Modified/limited in most states

### Independent Contractor Liability (3 Exceptions)
- Negligent employment of the contractor
- Delegation of nondelegable duties
- Inherently dangerous work

### Contractual Liability Agreements
- **Liquidated damages** = Pre-set damages amount in contract
- **Hold-harmless** = One party covers the other's losses (3 forms: limited, intermediate, broad)
- **Exculpatory** = Agreement not to sue for negligence (valid if: not against public interest + no duty to perform + no unequal bargaining power)

### Landowner Liability
- **Natural conditions** = Generally no duty (exception: urban areas, known hazards near public ways)
- **Artificial conditions** = Greater liability for hidden dangers
- **Attractive nuisance** = Artificial + attracts children + children can't appreciate danger
- **Licensee** = Warn of known hidden defects
- **Invitee** = Keep premises safe + inspect for hazards
- **Hotel operators** = High duty of care to guests (invitees)
- **Landlords** = Liable for common areas + hidden defects known at lease + negligent repairs
