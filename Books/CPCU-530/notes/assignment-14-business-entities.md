### PART 1: CORPORATIONS

#### A. Advantage of Incorporation

**What is a Corporation?**
A **corporation** is a legal entity created under state law that exists separately from its owners (stockholders). Think of it as a "legal person" -- it can own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, all on its own.

**The Big Advantage: Limited Liability**
The #1 reason people form corporations is **limited liability**. Stockholders can only lose what they invested. If the corporation gets sued for $10 million but you only put in $5,000, the most you can lose is your $5,000.

> **Setup**: Sarah invests $10,000 to buy stock in ABC Corp. ABC Corp gets sued and owes $2 million in damages.
> **What Happens**: The corporation's assets are used to pay the judgment. Sarah's personal bank account, house, and car are protected.
> **Result**: Sarah loses her $10,000 investment (stock becomes worthless), but her personal assets are safe.

**Pierce the Corporate Veil** -- When Limited Liability FAILS

In plain English: "Piercing the corporate veil" means a court ignores the corporation's separate identity and holds stockholders personally liable. This happens when people abuse the corporate form.

**Exceptions to limited liability (EXAM FAVORITE)**:
- **Unpaid wages and benefits**: Several states hold stockholders liable for employees' wages and unemployment benefits earned but unpaid before a corporation's insolvency
- **Wrongful acts**: Courts hold stockholders liable when a corporation forms for an illegal purpose
- **Ignoring corporate identity**: If stockholders treat the corporation as their personal piggy bank (mixing personal and corporate funds, not keeping proper records), courts will "pierce the veil"
- **Inadequate capitalization**: If a corporation is set up with laughably little money relative to its business risks, courts may hold stockholders personally liable

> **Setup**: Bob forms "Bob's Construction LLC" with only $500 in capital. He uses the company bank account to pay his personal mortgage and car payments. He never holds board meetings or keeps minutes.
> **What Happens**: A worker is injured on the job and sues. The corporation has no real assets.
> **Result**: The court "pierces the corporate veil" because Bob treated the corporation as his alter ego. Bob is personally liable for the worker's injuries.

**Types of Corporations**:
- **Publicly held corporation**: Stock is traded on public exchanges (think NYSE, NASDAQ). Many stockholders. Subject to SEC regulations.
- **Privately held (close) corporation**: Stock is NOT publicly traded. Usually a small number of owners. Think of a family business that incorporated.
- **Foreign corporation**: A corporation doing business in a state OTHER than where it was incorporated. (NOT a foreign country -- that is an "alien corporation")
- **Alien corporation**: A corporation formed in another COUNTRY
- **Professional corporation (PC)**: Formed by professionals like doctors, lawyers, or accountants. Members are personally liable for their own malpractice but protected from other members' malpractice.

> **Exam Trap**: "Foreign corporation" does NOT mean from another country! A company incorporated in Delaware but doing business in New Jersey is a "foreign corporation" in NJ. A company from Canada doing business in the US is an "alien corporation."

**Runaway corporation statute**: A state law designed to prevent corporations from incorporating in one state just to avoid the stricter regulations of the state where they actually do business.

#### B. Federal and State Laws and Regulations

Corporations are primarily creatures of **state law**. Each state has its own corporation statute. However, **federal law** also plays a role:

- **State law**: Governs formation, internal governance, dissolution
- **Federal law**: Securities regulation (SEC), antitrust, tax (IRS), employment law

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: Corporate governance regulations have evolved significantly since 2008, particularly with the **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)** adding executive compensation rules, whistleblower protections, and enhanced corporate governance requirements.

#### C. Corporate Powers

**Corporate charter (articles of incorporation)**: The foundational document filed with the state to create a corporation. This is the corporation's "birth certificate."

Articles of incorporation typically contain:
- **Corporate name**
- **Duration** (can be perpetual)
- **Purpose** -- Most states allow a simple statement: "for any lawful purpose"
- **Number, classes, and par value of shares**
- **Provisions for stockholders' preemptive rights** (right to buy new shares proportionally)
- **Provisions restricting stock transfers**
- **Registered office and registered agent**
- **Number, names, and addresses of initial board of directors**
- **Names and addresses of incorporators**

**Ultra vires**: Latin for "beyond the powers." This describes acts a corporation takes that go beyond what its charter or state law allows. In plain English: the corporation did something it wasn't authorized to do.

> **Setup**: A corporation's charter says its purpose is "to manufacture and sell furniture." The board decides to invest heavily in cryptocurrency speculation.
> **What Happens**: A stockholder sues, claiming this activity is ultra vires -- outside the scope of the corporation's stated purpose.
> **Result**: If the charter had said "any lawful purpose," the investment would be fine. But with a narrow purpose clause, the court may block the activity.

#### D. Liability for Torts and Crimes

**Corporate tort liability**: A corporation is liable for torts committed by its employees acting within the scope of employment (respondeat superior -- same as any employer).

**Corporate criminal liability**: A corporation CAN be held criminally liable. Under statutes that impose **strict liability** (no intent required), an officer can be criminally responsible for failure to ensure that subordinates comply with the law -- even if the officer did not personally commit the violation.

> **Setup**: Carol is president of X Corporation, a food business. The FDA finds rodent infestation at a warehouse and issues an order to fix it. Carol instructs subordinates to comply. Two months later, the FDA finds the violation persists. After a third inspection with the same result, the FDA files criminal charges against both the corporation AND Carol.
> **What Happens**: Even though Carol gave instructions to fix the problem, she failed to ensure compliance.
> **Result**: Both Carol and the corporation are criminally liable. Officers are personally responsible for crimes they commit AND for failing to effect subordinate compliance.

#### E. Corporation Formation

**Three stages of forming a corporation:**

**1. Promoters**
A **promoter** is someone who takes the initial steps to form a corporation -- finding investors, securing office space, negotiating contracts, etc.

- Promoters owe a **fiduciary duty** (utmost good faith) to the corporation being formed, its creditors, and fellow promoters
- The corporation is NOT automatically bound by promoter contracts -- it must adopt them after formation
- Promoters are personally liable on pre-incorporation contracts unless the other party agreed to look solely to the corporation

**2. Incorporation**
- An **incorporator** is the person who actually signs and files the articles of incorporation with the state
- The state issues a **certificate of incorporation** upon approval
- **De jure corporation** ("in law"): A corporation formed in full compliance with all legal requirements. Its validity cannot be challenged.
- **De facto corporation** ("in fact"): A corporation with minor defects in its formation process. Only the **state** (not private parties) can challenge its validity.

> **Exam Trap**: Only the STATE can attack the validity of a de facto corporation. A private party who dealt with the de facto corporation cannot later claim it was not properly formed to escape their obligations.

**3. Organizational Meeting**
After incorporation, the first meeting is held to:
- Adopt bylaws
- Elect officers
- Issue stock
- Open bank accounts
- Handle initial business

#### F. Corporate Ownership

**Debt security (bond)**: A loan to the corporation. The bondholder is a creditor, NOT an owner. They get fixed interest payments and their money back at maturity.

**Equity security (stock)**: An ownership share in the corporation. Stockholders ARE owners.

**Two main types of stock:**

- **Common stock**: Basic ownership. Voting rights. Dividends not guaranteed. Last in line if the company dissolves.
- **Preferred stock**: Priority over common stock for dividends and capital distribution upon dissolution. Often no voting rights. Think of it as a hybrid between a bond and common stock.

**Par value**: The minimum price at which shares can be issued. This is a legal concept, NOT market value.

**Key stock-related terms:**
- **Stated capital**: Total par value of all issued shares. This is a "floor" that protects creditors -- the corporation cannot distribute this to shareholders.
- **Capital surplus**: Money received from stock sales ABOVE par value.
- **Stock right**: A short-term privilege to buy stock at a set price (given to existing shareholders).
- **Stock warrant**: A longer-term right to buy stock at a set price.
- **Stock option**: The right to buy or sell stock at a specific price within a specific time (commonly used as employee compensation).
- **Preemptive right**: The right of existing stockholders to buy a proportionate share of any NEW stock issued, preserving their ownership percentage.
- **Redemption**: The corporation buys back its own stock from shareholders.

> **Setup**: You own 10% of XYZ Corp (100 out of 1,000 shares). XYZ decides to issue 500 new shares.
> **What Happens**: If you have preemptive rights, you get the chance to buy 50 of the new shares (10% of 500) before they are offered to others.
> **Result**: You maintain your 10% ownership stake. Without preemptive rights, your ownership could be diluted to 6.67% (100/1,500).

#### G. Stockholders' Powers and Duties

**Stockholders' rights and powers:**
- Elect board of directors and remove them without cause (in many states)
- Approve changes to articles of incorporation
- Make or amend bylaws
- Approve loans to directors, officers, or agents
- Ratify board actions
- Sue directors for mismanagement (**shareholder derivative suit** -- a suit brought on behalf of the corporation)
- Right to inspect corporate financial statements and books
- Right to attend stockholders' meetings

**Important terms:**
- **Proxy**: A written authorization for someone else to vote your shares at a stockholders' meeting.
- **Record date**: The date set to determine which stockholders are entitled to vote or receive dividends.
- **Quorum**: The minimum number of shares that must be represented at a meeting for it to be valid (typically a majority).
- **Cumulative voting**: A method allowing minority stockholders a better chance of electing at least one director. You can pool all your votes on one candidate. (Example: If electing 5 directors and you own 100 shares, you get 500 votes to distribute however you like.)
- **Voting trust**: Stockholders transfer their shares to a trustee who votes them. Used to consolidate control.

**When can stockholders be denied access to corporate books?**
Stockholders can be denied the right to inspect books and records if they:
- Hold less than a specified percentage of outstanding shares, OR
- Have held stock for less than a specified period

---

#### H. Stockholders' Actions

- **Direct action**: A suit by a stockholder for damage to the stockholder's own individual rights.
- **Shareholder derivative suit**: A suit brought by a stockholder on BEHALF of the corporation against third parties (or directors/officers) who harmed the corporation.

> **Exam Trap**: In a derivative suit, any recovery goes to the CORPORATION, not directly to the stockholder who brought the suit.

---

#### I. Board of Directors

**Inside director**: A director who is also an officer or employee of the corporation (e.g., the CEO who also sits on the board).

**Outside director**: A director who is NOT an employee or officer. They bring independent perspective.

**Why outside directors are valuable:**
- Provide independent perspectives on corporate activities
- Less likely to rubber-stamp management decisions
- Bring expertise from other industries
- More credible in oversight roles

**Board responsibilities:**
- Set corporate policy and strategy
- Hire and fire officers
- Declare dividends
- Approve major transactions

**Number and qualifications**: Set by bylaws. Must meet periodically. Need a quorum to conduct business.

**Committees**: Boards can delegate to committees (audit committee, compensation committee, etc.)

---

#### J. Duties of Directors and Officers

**Two fundamental duties:**

**1. Duty of Care**: Directors must act with the care that a reasonably prudent person would use in similar circumstances. They must be informed and make decisions carefully.

**2. Duty of Loyalty**: Directors must put the corporation's interests above their own. No self-dealing, no competing with the corporation, no taking corporate opportunities for personal gain.

**Transactions with the corporation**: A director CAN enter into a contract with the corporation IF any of the following is true:
- The material facts were disclosed to and approved by the **board**
- The material facts were disclosed to and approved by the **stockholders**
- The contract was **fair** to the corporation

**Appropriation of corporate business opportunity**: A director cannot take a business opportunity that rightfully belongs to the corporation for personal gain.

**Abuse of minority stockholders**: Majority stockholders and directors cannot use their power to oppress minority stockholders.

**Insider information and insider trading**: Directors and officers with access to material non-public information CANNOT use it to buy or sell the corporation's stock.

**Penalties for insider trading:**
- Damages to the stockholders from whom shares were purchased
- Corporate seizure of profits
- The U.S. Treasury can take up to **three times** the profit (treble damages)

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)** significantly strengthened insider trading enforcement and added whistleblower bounties. The SEC has become far more aggressive in enforcement since 2008.

**Interlocking directors**: When the same person serves on the boards of two or more corporations. This can create conflicts of interest and may violate antitrust laws.

**Liability and Indemnification**:
- **Indemnification**: The corporation reimburses directors/officers for legal expenses incurred in lawsuits arising from their corporate duties.
- Directors who manage employee benefit plans under **ERISA** (Employment Retirement Income Security Act of 1974) must:
  - Act solely in the plan participants' interest
  - Exercise reasonable care and skill
  - Diversify investments unless clearly unreasonable
  - Act in accordance with plan documents

---

#### K. Dividends

**Dividend**: A distribution of corporate earnings to stockholders. Key types:

- **Property dividend**: Distribution of corporate property (not cash)
- **Stock dividend**: Distribution of additional shares of stock (no cash changes hands -- everyone's percentage stays the same but they own more shares)
- **Liquidating dividend**: A return of capital (NOT from earnings). Usually happens when the corporation is winding down.
- **Capital gains**: Profit from selling stock (this is the stockholder's gain, NOT a corporate dividend)

**Treasury stock**: Stock that was issued and then repurchased by the corporation. It exists but has no voting rights and receives no dividends.

**Stock split**: Dividing existing shares into more shares (e.g., a 2-for-1 split doubles the number of shares but halves the price per share).

> **Exam Trap**: Distribution of assets is NOT a dividend because dividends come from earnings, not assets. If the corporation returns invested capital to stockholders, that is a liquidating distribution, not a true dividend.

---

#### L. Merger and Share Exchange

**Corporate merger**: Two or more corporations combine into one. One corporation survives; the others cease to exist.

**Share exchange**: One corporation acquires all the shares of another corporation. The acquired corporation may continue to exist as a subsidiary.

**Takeover**: One corporation acquires control of another, sometimes against the target's wishes (hostile takeover).

**Tender offer**: A public offer to buy shares directly from stockholders, usually at a premium above market price. This is how hostile takeovers typically work -- the acquirer goes directly to stockholders, bypassing the target's board.

**Insurance Company Mergers**: In the insurance industry, mergers require approval from state insurance regulators (Department of Insurance) in addition to normal corporate requirements.

---

#### M. Dissolution and Reorganization

**Dissolution**: The legal termination of a corporation's existence.

**Two types:**
1. **Voluntary dissolution**: The corporation's board and stockholders decide to end the business.
2. **Involuntary dissolution**: Forced by the state or by court order.

**Involuntary dissolution occurs when:**
- Directors are deadlocked and cannot break it, causing irreparable injury
- Directors' or officers' acts are illegal, oppressive, or fraudulent
- Stockholders are deadlocked in voting power and have failed to elect directors for two successive meetings
- Corporate assets are being wasted or misapplied

**Insolvency**: When a corporation cannot pay its debts as they come due, or its liabilities exceed its assets. This can lead to reorganization (Chapter 11 bankruptcy) or liquidation (Chapter 7 bankruptcy).

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: Bankruptcy law has been updated by the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 and subsequent amendments. The Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 added Subchapter V to Chapter 11.

---

#### N. Foreign Corporations

**Licensing to do business**: A foreign corporation (incorporated in another state) must obtain a license/certificate of authority to do business in a new state. This subjects it to local taxes, regulations, and court jurisdiction.

**Long-arm statute**: A state law that gives courts jurisdiction over out-of-state parties (including foreign corporations) that have "minimum contacts" with the state. This allows you to sue a foreign corporation in your home state if they did something affecting you there.

**Service of process**: The formal delivery of legal documents (lawsuit papers) to the defendant. Foreign corporations must designate a registered agent in each state where they do business to accept service.

**Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]:
Enacted in 2002 after Enron/WorldCom scandals. Key provisions:
- CEO and CFO must personally certify financial statements
- Criminal penalties for securities fraud
- Enhanced auditor independence
- Requires internal controls over financial reporting
- Whistleblower protections

> Since 2008, SOX has been supplemented by Dodd-Frank (2010) provisions on corporate governance, executive compensation clawbacks, and enhanced whistleblower protections.

### PART 2: PARTNERSHIPS

#### A. Partnership Formation

**Partnership**: An association of two or more persons to carry on as co-owners of a business for profit. NO filing required with the state (unlike corporations). A partnership can be formed by a simple handshake agreement.

**Key characteristics:**
- Partners share profits AND losses
- Each partner is an agent of the partnership
- Partners have unlimited personal liability for partnership debts

**Joint ventures**: A partnership-like arrangement for a SINGLE business transaction or project, not an ongoing business. Same rules as partnerships but limited in scope.

**Partnership by estoppel**: When someone represents themselves (or allows themselves to be represented) as a partner and a third party relies on that representation to their detriment, the person may be treated as a partner even if no formal partnership exists.

**Common name statute**: A law requiring partnerships doing business under a trade name to register that name with the state.

---

#### B. Partnership Liability

**Tort liability**: Every partner is jointly and severally liable for torts committed by any partner acting in the ordinary course of partnership business. In plain English: if your partner injures someone while doing partnership work, YOU can be personally sued for the full amount.

**Criminal liability**: Partners are generally NOT liable for crimes committed by other partners unless they participated in or authorized the crime.

> **Setup**: Alice and Bob are partners in a delivery business. While making a delivery, Bob runs a red light and injures a pedestrian.
> **What Happens**: The injured person sues. Both the partnership AND Alice personally can be held liable.
> **Result**: Even though Alice was not driving and did nothing wrong, she is personally liable for Bob's negligence because it occurred in the ordinary course of partnership business. This is the HUGE risk of general partnerships.

---

#### C. Partners' Relationships to One Another

**Financial relationship**: Each partner shares equally in the enterprise's profits and in any surplus remaining on dissolution after all liabilities are paid.

**Fiduciary relationship**: Every partner owes fiduciary duties to the other partners AND the partnership:
- **Mutual trust**
- **Loyalty** (no competing with the partnership)
- **Good faith** (similar to duties of an agent or trustee)

**Partnership books and property**: The partnership must maintain books. All partners have the right to inspect them.

**Assignment of partner's interest**: A partner can assign (transfer) their financial interest in the partnership (right to receive profits), but CANNOT transfer their right to participate in management without the other partners' consent.

**Types of decisions and consent required:**
- **Ordinary business matters**: Majority of partners decides
- **Extraordinary matters** (changing the nature of the business, admitting new partners): Usually requires unanimous consent

---

#### D. Relationship of Partners to Third Parties

**Apparent authority (estoppel)**: If a partner appears to have authority to act and a third party reasonably relies on that appearance, the partnership is bound -- even if the partner actually lacked authority.

**Acts outside the usual scope of business**: A partner acting outside the ordinary scope of partnership business generally does NOT bind the partnership unless the other partners authorized the act.

**Conveying real property**: Under the Uniform Partnership Act (UPA):
- If property is held in the partnership's name, any partner can transfer real property by signing a deed
- The partnership IS bound unless the purchaser knew the partner lacked actual authority to sell

**Spouses' rights**: The UPA does not give a partner's spouse any direct rights in partnership property.

---

#### E. Dissolution, Winding Up, and Termination

**Rightful dissolution**: Partners agree to dissolve, term expires, or a partner gives notice.

**Wrongful dissolution**: A partner dissolves the partnership in violation of the partnership agreement. The wrongfully dissolving partner may be liable for damages.

**Winding up**: The process of settling partnership affairs after dissolution -- collecting debts, paying obligations, distributing remaining assets.

**Distribution of assets upon dissolution** (ORDER MATTERS -- memorize this):
1. First: Pay outside creditors
2. Second: Pay partners for loans they made to the partnership
3. Third: Return partners' capital contributions
4. Fourth: Distribute any remaining surplus equally among partners

> If the partnership is **solvent**: Partners share surplus.
> If the partnership is **insolvent**: Partners share losses.

**Effect of dissolution on third parties**: Until third parties receive notice of dissolution, they can still hold the partnership liable for new transactions entered into by the partners.

### PART 3: LIMITED PARTNERSHIPS AND LIMITED LIABILITY PARTNERSHIPS

#### F. Limited Partnerships and Limited Liability Partnerships

**Limited partnership**: Has at least one **general partner** (unlimited liability, runs the business) and one or more **limited partners** (liability limited to their investment, passive investors).

- Best suited for **temporary enterprises requiring large amounts of capital** with general partners who are good managers
- Limited partners who participate in management may LOSE their limited liability protection
- Must file a certificate with the state (unlike general partnerships)

**Limited liability partnership (LLP)**: A general partnership where partners have limited liability for the wrongful acts of OTHER partners. Popular with professional firms (law firms, accounting firms).

> **Setup**: Three accountants form an LLP. One partner commits malpractice.
> **What Happens**: Under the LLP structure, only the partner who committed malpractice is personally liable.
> **Result**: The other two partners' personal assets are protected from the malpractice claim (though partnership assets are still at risk).

### PART 4: LIMITED LIABILITY COMPANIES (LLCs)

#### G. Limited Liability Companies

**LLC (Limited Liability Company)** = A hybrid business entity that combines the best features of corporations and partnerships. Think of it as a "choose your own adventure" entity -- you get corporate-style liability protection with partnership-style tax flexibility, plus enormous freedom in how you structure the business.

**Plain English:** An LLC protects your personal assets (house, car, savings) from business debts and lawsuits, while letting you avoid the "double taxation" problem that corporations face. This is why LLCs have become the #1 choice for new businesses.

#### Formation Requirements

To form an LLC, you must:
1. **File Articles of Organization** (also called a Certificate of Formation) with the state
2. **Create an Operating Agreement** -- the internal rulebook that governs how the LLC runs (member rights, profit sharing, management structure, what happens if a member leaves)
3. **Pay state filing fees** (vary by state)

**Key difference from corporations**: LLCs have far fewer formalities. No required annual meetings, no board of directors, no corporate minutes. This simplicity is a major advantage.

#### Member-Managed vs. Manager-Managed

**Member-managed LLC** = ALL members participate in running the business. Each member has authority to bind the LLC in ordinary business transactions.
- Best for: Small businesses where all owners are actively involved
- Example: Three friends open a restaurant as an LLC. All three make decisions together.

**Manager-managed LLC** = Members appoint one or more managers (who may or may not be members) to run the business. Non-manager members are passive investors.
- Best for: Larger LLCs or those with passive investors
- Example: A real estate LLC with 10 investor-members appoints one member as manager to handle day-to-day operations.

#### Single-Member LLCs

A **single-member LLC** has only one owner. It provides liability protection while being treated as a "disregarded entity" for federal tax purposes (reported on the owner's personal tax return, Schedule C).

**Key consideration**: Courts are more willing to "pierce the veil" of single-member LLCs if the owner doesn't maintain separation between personal and business finances. Commingling funds is the #1 risk.

#### Tax Treatment

LLCs have flexible tax classification:
- **Default (1 member)**: Disregarded entity -- taxed as sole proprietorship
- **Default (2+ members)**: Taxed as partnership -- profits/losses pass through to members
- **Elective**: Can choose to be taxed as S-Corp or C-Corp by filing IRS Form 8832

This flexibility is unique to LLCs -- no other entity type offers this range of tax options.

#### LLC Liability Protection -- Real-World Example

> **Setup:** Maria forms a single-member LLC called "Maria's Bakery LLC" and properly maintains it (separate bank account, operating agreement, adequate capitalization). A customer slips on a wet floor in the bakery and suffers a broken hip.
> **What Happens:** The customer sues and wins a $500,000 judgment. Maria's Bakery LLC only has $100,000 in assets.
> **Result:** The customer can collect the $100,000 from the LLC's assets, but Maria's personal assets (her home, car, personal savings) are **protected** by the LLC structure. The customer cannot go after Maria personally -- UNLESS they can prove Maria failed to maintain the LLC properly (commingling funds, fraud, or inadequate capitalization).

#### Comparing LLCs to Other Entities

- **vs. Corporations**: LLCs provide the same limited liability but with fewer formalities, more flexible management, and (by default) no double taxation
- **vs. General Partnerships**: LLCs provide limited liability for ALL members (partnerships expose general partners to unlimited liability)
- **vs. Limited Partnerships**: LLCs allow ALL members to participate in management without losing liability protection (limited partners who manage risk becoming general partners)
- **vs. Sole Proprietorship**: LLCs provide liability protection (sole proprietors have none)

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: LLCs have become the dominant business entity form since this 2008 textbook was written. Key developments include: the Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (RULLCA) adopted by many states, Series LLCs (allowing separate series of members/assets within one LLC -- available in about 20 states), Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) reporting requirements under the Corporate Transparency Act (2024), Professional LLCs (PLLCs) for licensed professionals, and increasing judicial guidance on piercing the LLC veil. Every state now has comprehensive LLC statutes.

### PART 5: UNINCORPORATED ASSOCIATIONS

#### A-E. Unincorporated Associations

**Unincorporated association**: A group of people joined together for a common purpose other than making a profit. They do NOT have a separate legal identity from their members (unlike corporations).

**Six types of unincorporated associations:**
1. **Trade associations** (industry groups)
2. **Labor unions**
3. **Benevolent and fraternal associations** (Elks, Rotary, etc.)
4. **Religious organizations**
5. **Clubs** (social, country clubs)
6. **Condominium owners' associations**

**State regulation**: Varies by state. Some states treat them like partnerships (members personally liable). Others give them more corporate-like protections.

**Formation**: Usually governed by articles of association and bylaws.

**Liability**: Members may be personally liable for the association's debts and obligations, depending on state law. This is a KEY difference from corporations and LLCs.

## Key Terms Glossary

**Corporation** (p. 14.4): A legal entity created under state law that exists separately from its owners, providing limited liability to stockholders.

**Pierce the corporate veil** (p. 14.4): When a court ignores the corporation's separate identity and holds stockholders personally liable, typically due to fraud, inadequate capitalization, or failure to observe corporate formalities.

**Publicly held corporation** (p. 14.5): A corporation whose stock is traded on public stock exchanges and available for purchase by the general public.

**Privately held (close) corporation** (p. 14.5): A corporation whose stock is NOT publicly traded, typically owned by a small group of individuals.

**Foreign corporation** (p. 14.6): A corporation doing business in a state other than the state where it was incorporated. (NOT from another country.)

**Alien corporation** (p. 14.6): A corporation formed in another country.

**Runaway corporation statute** (p. 14.6): A state law designed to prevent corporations from incorporating in a lenient state to avoid regulations in the state where they actually operate.

**Professional corporation (PC)** (p. 14.7): A corporation formed by licensed professionals (doctors, lawyers, accountants). Members are liable for their own malpractice but shielded from others' malpractice.

**Corporate charter / Articles of incorporation** (p. 14.7): The foundational legal document filed with the state to create a corporation, specifying its name, purpose, stock structure, and governance.

**Ultra vires** (p. 14.8): Latin for "beyond the powers." Acts that exceed the corporation's authority as defined in its charter or by law.

**Promoter** (p. 14.9): A person who takes the initial steps to organize and form a corporation, including finding investors and negotiating preliminary contracts.

**Incorporator** (p. 14.10): The person who signs and files the articles of incorporation with the state.

**Certificate of incorporation** (p. 14.10): The document issued by the state officially creating the corporation.

**De jure corporation** (p. 14.10): A corporation formed in full compliance with all legal requirements. Its existence cannot be challenged.

**De facto corporation** (p. 14.10): A corporation with minor formation defects. Only the STATE can challenge its validity.

**Organizational meeting** (p. 14.10): The first meeting after incorporation where bylaws are adopted, officers elected, and initial business conducted.

**Debt security / Bond** (p. 14.11): A loan to the corporation. The bondholder is a creditor, not an owner.

**Equity security / Stock** (p. 14.11): An ownership share in the corporation.

**Common stock** (p. 14.11): Basic ownership shares with voting rights but no priority for dividends.

**Preferred stock** (p. 14.11): Stock with priority over common stock for dividends and capital distribution upon dissolution.

**Par value** (p. 14.11): The minimum legal price at which shares can initially be issued.

**Dissolution** (p. 14.12): The legal termination of a corporation's existence.

**Stated capital** (p. 14.12): The total par value of all issued shares; acts as a protected floor for creditor protection.

**Capital surplus** (p. 14.12): Amount received from stock sales above par value.

**Stock right** (p. 14.12): A short-term privilege to buy stock at a set price.

**Stock warrant** (p. 14.12): A longer-term right to buy stock at a set price.

**Stock option** (p. 14.12): The right to buy or sell stock at a specific price within a specific time period.

**Preemptive right** (p. 14.12): An existing stockholder's right to purchase a proportionate share of any new stock issued, preserving their ownership percentage.

**Redemption** (p. 14.13): The corporation buys back its own stock.

**Shareholder derivative suit** (p. 14.14): A lawsuit brought by a stockholder on behalf of the corporation. Recovery goes to the corporation, not the individual stockholder.

**Proxy** (p. 14.15): A written authorization allowing someone else to vote your shares at a stockholders' meeting.

**Record date** (p. 14.15): The date that determines which stockholders can vote or receive dividends.

**Quorum** (p. 14.15): The minimum number of shares that must be represented for a stockholders' meeting to be valid.

**Cumulative voting** (p. 14.16): A voting method allowing stockholders to pool all votes on fewer candidates, helping minority stockholders elect at least one director.

**Voting trust** (p. 14.16): An arrangement where stockholders transfer shares to a trustee who votes them, used to consolidate voting control.

**Direct action** (p. 14.17): A lawsuit by a stockholder for harm to their individual rights (vs. derivative suit which is on behalf of the corporation).

**Inside director** (p. 14.18): A director who is also an officer or employee of the corporation.

**Outside director** (p. 14.18): A director who is NOT an officer or employee, providing independent perspective.

**Chief executive officer (CEO)** (p. 14.19): The highest-ranking corporate officer, responsible for overall management.

**Chief operating officer (COO)** (p. 14.20): Officer responsible for day-to-day operations.

**President** (p. 14.20): Corporate officer often responsible for running the company (role varies by corporation).

**Vice president** (p. 14.20): Officer who assists the president and may assume presidential duties.

**Treasurer** (p. 14.20): Officer responsible for managing corporate finances.

**Secretary** (p. 14.20): Officer responsible for corporate records, meeting minutes, and official filings.

**Interlocking directors** (p. 14.21): When the same person serves on the boards of two or more corporations, creating potential conflicts of interest.

**Insider information** (p. 14.21): Material non-public information about a corporation that could affect its stock price.

**Indemnification** (p. 14.23): The corporation's reimbursement of directors/officers for legal expenses arising from their corporate duties.

**Dividend** (p. 14.24): Distribution of corporate earnings to stockholders.

**Property dividend** (p. 14.24): Distribution of corporate property (not cash) to stockholders.

**Stock dividend** (p. 14.24): Distribution of additional shares (no cash leaves the corporation).

**Liquidating dividend** (p. 14.24): Return of invested capital to stockholders, not from earnings.

**Capital gains** (p. 14.24): Profit realized when stock is sold for more than its purchase price.

**Treasury stock** (p. 14.24): Stock issued then repurchased by the corporation. No voting rights, no dividends.

**Stock split** (p. 14.25): Dividing existing shares into more shares (e.g., 2-for-1 split).

**Corporate merger** (p. 14.25): Two corporations combine into one surviving entity.

**Share exchange** (p. 14.25): One corporation acquires all shares of another.

**Takeover** (p. 14.26): Acquisition of control of one corporation by another.

**Tender offer** (p. 14.26): A public offer to buy shares directly from stockholders, usually at a premium, to accomplish a takeover.

**Insolvency** (p. 14.27): The inability to pay debts as they come due, or having liabilities exceed assets.

**Long-arm statute** (p. 14.28): A state law allowing courts to exercise jurisdiction over out-of-state parties who have "minimum contacts" with the state.

**Service of process** (p. 14.29): Formal delivery of legal documents to a defendant.

**Partnership** (p. 14.29): An association of two or more persons carrying on as co-owners of a business for profit.

**Joint venture** (p. 14.30): A partnership-like arrangement for a single business transaction or project.

**Common name statute** (p. 14.31): A law requiring partnerships to register trade names with the state.

**Limited partnership** (p. 14.41): A partnership with at least one general partner (unlimited liability) and one or more limited partners (liability limited to investment).

**Limited liability partnership (LLP)** (p. 14.41): A partnership where partners have limited liability for other partners' wrongful acts.

**Unincorporated association** (p. 14.42): A group organized for a common purpose without formal incorporation, lacking separate legal identity.

## Review Questions & Answers

**1. Identify and explain the exceptions to the limited liability of corporate stockholders. (pp. 14.4-14.5)**

**Answer**: Several states hold stockholders liable for employees' wages and unemployment benefits earned but unpaid before a corporation's insolvency. Courts also make exceptions by holding stockholders liable for a corporation's wrongful acts, usually when a corporation forms for an illegal purpose. If stockholders ignore the corporation's separate identity, so will courts. Finally, inadequate capitalization can defeat a corporation's limited liability.

**2. Identify what articles of incorporation typically contain. (p. 14.7)**

**Answer**: Articles of incorporation typically contain:
- Corporate name
- Duration
- Purpose
- Number, classes, and par value of shares
- Provisions for stockholders' right to purchase a proportionate share of newly authorized stock
- Provisions restricting transferability of shares
- Registered office and registered agent
- Number, names, and addresses of initial board of directors
- Names and addresses of incorporators

**3. How can a corporation's purpose be simply stated? (p. 14.7)**

**Answer**: Most states permit a corporate charter to describe its purpose as simply "for any lawful purpose."

**4. Is the officer of a corporation responsible for an employee's job-related criminal activity? (p. 14.9)**

**Answer**: Under statutes that impose criminal liability for unintentional acts, an officer can be criminally responsible for failure to ensure that subordinates comply with the statute.

**5. What duties does a promoter owe to the corporation, creditors, and fellow promoters? (p. 14.9)**

**Answer**: Promoters have a fiduciary duty of the utmost good faith in dealing with one another and with the corporations they are organizing.

**6. Who can attack the validity of a de facto corporation? (p. 14.10)**

**Answer**: Only the state can legally challenge the existence of a de facto corporation.

**7. Describe four typical bylaw provisions. (p. 14.11)**

**Answer**:
1. **Stockholders' meetings**: date, place, proxies, conduct of elections, order of business
2. **Directors**: term of office, compensation, meetings, loans, and authority to elect officers
3. **Officers**: names and functions, appointment, removal, authority to sign checks and enter contracts
4. **Indemnification**: provides for corporate reimbursement for individuals' liability (directors, officers, and agents)

**8. What preferences over common stocks do preferred stocks generally enjoy? (p. 14.11)**

**Answer**: Preferred stock has priority over common stock, usually regarding dividends and capital distribution if the corporation ends its existence.

**9. Describe stockholders' rights and powers. (p. 14.14)**

**Answer**: Stockholders have the power to elect board members, remove them without cause in many states, approve changes in articles of incorporation, make or amend bylaws, and approve loans to directors/officers/agents. They can ratify board actions, sue directors for mismanagement, access financial statements, and attend stockholders' meetings.

**10. Under what circumstances can stockholders be denied the right to inspect books and records? (pp. 14.14-14.15)**

**Answer**: Stockholders can be denied the right to inspect their corporation's books and records if they hold less than a specified percentage of outstanding shares or have held stock for less than a specified period.

**11. What type of decisions made by a board of directors must stockholders approve? (p. 14.17)**

**Answer**: Amendments to the articles of incorporation, such as changes in the corporation's capital structure and form, require stockholder approval, as do decisions to merge, consolidate, or dissolve the corporation.

**12. In what ways are outside directors valuable? (p. 14.18)**

**Answer**: Outside directors can provide perspectives on the corporation's activities that those involved in day-to-day management might not have.

**13. What are corporate officers' functions? (p. 14.19)**

**Answer**: Corporate officers generally have one or both of the following functions:
1. Managing the corporation's internal affairs
2. Dealing with persons outside the corporation

**14. Under what circumstances can a director contract with a corporation? (p. 14.21)**

**Answer**: A director can enter into a contract with a corporation if any of the following is true:
- The material facts and relationship or interest were known or disclosed to the board, and the board approved it
- The material facts and relationship or interest were known or disclosed to the stockholders, and the stockholders consented
- The contract was fair to the corporation

**15. What are the penalties for insider trading? (p. 14.22)**

**Answer**: If officers or directors engage in insider trading, a court can award damages to the stockholder from whom shares were purchased, the corporation can take the profit, or the U.S. Treasury can take three times the profit.

**16. What are the duties of directors and officers who manage an employee benefit plan under ERISA? (p. 14.23)**

**Answer**: Directors and officers who manage an employee benefit plan under ERISA have an obligation to act solely in the plan participants' interest, to exercise reasonable care and skill, to diversify investments unless it is clearly unreasonable to do so, and to act in accordance with the plan documents.

**17. Why is distribution of assets not a dividend? (p. 14.24)**

**Answer**: Distribution of assets is not a dividend because dividends come out of earnings, not assets.

**18. How do tender offers accomplish hostile takeovers? (p. 14.26)**

**Answer**: In a tender offer, the acquiring corporation offers to purchase shares directly from the target company's stockholders.

**19. Under what circumstances might an involuntary dissolution occur? (p. 14.27)**

**Answer**: State involuntary dissolutions occur in cases of gross abuse. Stockholders request involuntary dissolutions when:
- Directors are deadlocked, cannot break the deadlock, and irreparable injury has occurred or might occur
- Directors' and officers' acts are illegal, oppressive, or fraudulent
- Stockholders are deadlocked in voting power and have failed to elect directors for two successive meetings
- Corporate assets are being wasted or misapplied

**20. What is the purpose of long-arm statutes? (p. 14.28)**

**Answer**: Long-arm statutes enable states' citizens to sue in their own state courts people or entities who are not physically present in the state but who have had minimum contacts there.

**21. In what name or names can a partnership be sued? (p. 14.31)**

**Answer**: A partnership can be sued as a partnership in name or as the names of each partner individually.

**22. Describe the financial and fiduciary relationships among partners. (pp. 14.32-14.33)**

**Answer**: The financial relationship provides that each partner shares equally in the enterprise's profits and in any surplus remaining on dissolution and after satisfaction of all liabilities. Every partner has a fiduciary relationship with the other partners and with the firm, requiring duties of mutual trust, loyalty, and good faith similar to those of an agent or trustee.

**23. What is the managing partner's role? (p. 14.35)**

**Answer**: Managing partners are appointed to make all long-range policy decisions.

**24. Explain the extent of the power of a partner to convey real property to a third person under the UPA. (p. 14.37)**

**Answer**: If the property is held under the partnership's name, any partner can transfer real property in the firm's name by signing a deed, and the partnership is bound unless the partner lacked actual authority to sell the property and the purchaser knew this fact.

**25. How are partnership assets distributed upon dissolution? (p. 14.39)**

**Answer**: If the partnership is solvent at the time of dissolution, the parties share in any surplus remaining after payment of debts and partners' equity. If it is insolvent, they share the losses.

**26. What is the purpose of a limited partnership? (p. 14.41)**

**Answer**: A limited partnership is best suited to a temporary enterprise requiring a large amount of capital and having general partners who are good managers.

**27. Compare the limited liability company with (a) corporations and (b) partnerships. (p. 14.42)**

**Answer**:
- **(a) vs. Corporations**: LLCs provide the limited liability of a corporation to all owners or members of the business.
- **(b) vs. Partnerships**: LLCs offer the tax advantages of a partnership (pass-through taxation).

**28. List six types of unincorporated associations. (pp. 14.43-14.44)**

**Answer**:
1. Trade associations
2. Labor unions
3. Benevolent and fraternal associations
4. Religious organizations
5. Clubs
6. Condominium owners' associations

## Application Questions & Answers

### Question 1: Eric and APB Corporation

**Facts**: Eric, an engineer, was introduced to Douglas, a director of APB Corporation. Douglas offered Eric a position, which Eric accepted, resigning from his current job. When Eric showed up to work, the board had voted not to approve Douglas's contract.

**a. What rights does Eric have against APB Corporation?**

**Answer**: Eric can sue APB for breach of contract. Eric relied on the contract to his detriment (resigned from his job). The corporation might dispute the director's authority to hire and to contract with Eric, but Eric's recourse is against the corporation.

**b. Would the answer change if Eric had contracted with Paul, the CEO, who had no express authority from the board?**

**Answer**: Paul, as CEO, would have the authority to hire personnel and to represent APB in this process. He is an agent for the corporation and acted as such in hiring Eric. As CEO, Paul would not have needed express authority from the board to hire Eric. In either instance (a) or (b), Eric's recourse is against the corporation.

### Question 2: Carol and FDA Violations

**Facts**: Carol is president of X Corporation (food business). FDA found rodent infestation, issued orders to fix it. Carol instructed subordinates to comply, but they didn't. After three inspections with the same violation, FDA filed criminal charges against both Carol and the corporation.

**Answer**: Carol gave specific instructions but her subordinates disobeyed her and the law. This is a form of strict liability -- lack of intent is immaterial. Even though the subordinates acted independently, disobeying Carol as the company representative, the company is criminally liable. A corporation may be responsible for an employee's criminal activity if it could have uncovered the activity through reasonably diligent supervision, which is probably the case here. Officers and employees who commit crimes are personally responsible. Carol, as well as the corporation, would be criminally liable because she failed to ensure her subordinates' compliance.

### Question 3: J.P. and Insider Trading

**Facts**: J.P. is president, CEO, and owns 11% of a corporation's stock. He knows about a secret project that will boost share prices. He wants to buy 1,000 additional shares.

**a. What legal issues arise if J.P. buys the shares?**

**Answer**: J.P. might be in violation of his legal duties to the corporation. He cannot use material information obtained through his duties to decide whether to buy or sell the corporation's stock. Shareholders can sue him for use of insider information.

**b. If J.P. bought shares on January 15 and sold at a profit on March 15, what are the consequences?**

**Answer**: He is guilty of illegal use of insider information under the Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984 and faces three liabilities:
1. Damages to the stockholder(s) from whom the shares were purchased
2. Corporate seizure of his profits
3. Forfeiture of up to three times the profit to the U.S. Treasury

### Question 4: Ricardo's Partnership Compensation

**Facts**: Ricardo and his three daughters formed a general partnership to manage real estate. Ricardo was the only active manager, taking $1,000/month as compensation. The daughters sued, claiming he was not entitled to compensation.

**Answer**: The daughters might prevail if they can prove they did not authorize the father's compensation. Under the UPA, no partner is entitled to remuneration for services unless the partners agree to it.

## Exam Tips

### Top 10 Things Most Likely to Be Tested

1. **Pierce the corporate veil** -- Know ALL the exceptions to limited liability (wages, wrongful acts, ignoring identity, inadequate capitalization)

2. **De jure vs. de facto corporation** -- De jure = fully compliant, unchallengeable. De facto = minor defects, only the STATE can challenge. Private parties CANNOT.

3. **Insider trading penalties** -- Three consequences: damages to stockholders, profit seizure, up to 3x profit to Treasury

4. **Director contracts with the corporation** -- Three ways it can be valid: board approved, stockholders consented, or the contract was fair

5. **Involuntary dissolution** -- Four grounds: deadlocked directors, illegal/oppressive acts, deadlocked stockholders, wasted assets

6. **Partnership asset distribution order** -- Outside creditors > Partner loans > Capital contributions > Surplus

7. **LLC advantages** -- Limited liability of a corporation + tax advantages of a partnership

8. **Foreign vs. alien corporation** -- Foreign = different state. Alien = different country.

9. **Preemptive rights** -- Existing stockholders can buy proportionate share of new stock to maintain ownership percentage

10. **No partner compensation without agreement** -- UPA default: partners are NOT paid for their services unless all partners agree

### Common Exam Traps

- "Foreign corporation" does NOT mean from a foreign country
- In a derivative suit, recovery goes to the CORPORATION, not the suing stockholder
- Distribution of assets is NOT a dividend (dividends come from earnings)
- De facto corporation can only be challenged by the STATE
- Promoters are personally liable on pre-incorporation contracts unless third party agreed otherwise
- Limited partners who participate in management may LOSE limited liability protection
- Officers CAN be criminally liable for subordinates' violations (strict liability statutes)
- A partnership can form WITHOUT any written agreement or filing
- In a tender offer, shares are purchased from STOCKHOLDERS, not the corporation itself

### Memorization Aids

**WIPE** -- Exceptions to limited liability (Wages unpaid, Illegal purpose, Personal identity ignored, Equalization/inadequate capitalization)

**DIFC** -- Director contracts with corporation are valid if: Disclosed to board, Informed stockholders consented, Fair to corporation

**OLDE** -- Involuntary dissolution: Oppressive/illegal acts, Locked directors, Deadlocked stockholders, Exhausted/wasted assets

**Partnership dissolution payout order**: "**C**reditors **L**oans **C**apital **S**urplus" (think: "CLiCkS" -- Creditors, Loans, Capital, Surplus)

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Business Entity Comparison

**Sole Proprietorship**
- One owner, unlimited liability, simplest form

**General Partnership**
- 2+ owners, unlimited personal liability for ALL partners, no filing required, pass-through taxation

**Limited Partnership**
- General partner(s) with unlimited liability + limited partner(s) with liability limited to investment. Must file with state.

**Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)**
- Like general partnership but partners shielded from other partners' malpractice. Popular with professional firms.

**Corporation**
- Separate legal entity, limited liability for stockholders, double taxation (corporate + dividend), most formal structure

**Limited Liability Company (LLC)**
- Limited liability for ALL members + pass-through taxation. Best of both worlds.

**Unincorporated Association**
- No separate legal entity, members may be personally liable, not-for-profit purpose

### Corporate Formation Steps
1. Promoter does preliminary work (fiduciary duty)
2. Incorporator files articles of incorporation with state
3. State issues certificate of incorporation
4. Organizational meeting (bylaws, officers, stock)

### Key Distinctions
- **De jure** = legally perfect | **De facto** = minor defects, only state can challenge
- **Common stock** = voting, last for dividends | **Preferred stock** = no voting, first for dividends
- **Inside director** = also an employee | **Outside director** = independent
- **Direct action** = stockholder sues for own injury | **Derivative suit** = stockholder sues on behalf of corporation
- **Merger** = two become one | **Share exchange** = one acquires all shares of another
- **Foreign** = different state | **Alien** = different country
- **Bond** = debt (creditor) | **Stock** = equity (owner)
