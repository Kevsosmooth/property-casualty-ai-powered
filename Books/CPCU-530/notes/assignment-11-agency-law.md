### 1. AGENCY CREATION

**Agency** is one of the most fundamental legal relationships in business. In plain English, an agency is when one person (the **agent**) is authorized to act on behalf of another person (the **principal**).

#### Key Definitions

- **Agency** -- A relationship in which one party (the agent) is authorized to act on behalf of another party (the principal). In plain English: "I'm giving you permission to make deals for me."

- **Principal** -- The person who gives authority to another to act on their behalf. Think of the principal as "the boss" in this relationship.

- **Agent** -- The person authorized to act for the principal. The agent represents and acts for the principal in dealings with others.

- **Power of Attorney** -- A written document that formally grants an agent authority to act for the principal. This is a specific, legal instrument that spells out exactly what the agent can and cannot do.

---

#### A. Agency by Appointment (The Normal Way)

**What it means in plain English:** The principal directly says to the agent, "You are authorized to act for me." This is the most common way agencies are created.

**Key rule:** An agency contract must be **in writing** if it is to last **beyond one year** (Statute of Frauds).

**The usual method** for creating an agency relationship is by **express appointment** -- the principal explicitly tells the agent what they can do.

> **Setup:** Sarah owns a real estate company. She hires Jake and says, "You are authorized to list and sell properties on behalf of my company."
> **What Happens:** Jake is now Sarah's agent by express appointment.
> **Result:** Any contracts Jake enters within his authorized scope are binding on Sarah.

---

#### B. Agency by Estoppel

**What it means in plain English:** Even though the principal never officially appointed someone as their agent, the principal's words or behavior made a third party *reasonably believe* an agency existed. The principal is then "estopped" (legally blocked) from denying the agency.

**Critical concept:** The principal does NOT have to have done anything to deliberately create the agency. Their conduct or words just need to give a third party a reasonable basis to believe the agency exists.

> **Setup:** Bob, the owner of a car dealership, lets his friend Mike sit at a sales desk, wear a company polo, and talk to customers -- even though Mike is not actually an employee.
> **What Happens:** A customer walks in, sees Mike at the desk in company gear, and reasonably believes Mike works there. Mike sells the customer a car.
> **Result:** Bob is "estopped" from denying Mike was his agent. The sale is binding on Bob because his conduct created the reasonable appearance of an agency. Bob cannot later say "Mike wasn't my agent" because Bob's own behavior created that impression.

---

#### C. Agency by Ratification

**What it means in plain English:** Someone acts as your agent *without your authorization*, and after the fact you say "OK, I approve what they did." By approving (ratifying) the unauthorized act, you retroactively create an agency.

**The 4 conditions for ratification of an unauthorized contract:**

1. **The agent must have purported to act for the principal** -- the person must have claimed to be acting on behalf of the principal (not for themselves)
2. **The principal must ratify the entire transaction** -- you can't cherry-pick; it's all or nothing
3. **The principal must ratify before the third party withdraws** -- timing matters; if the third party backs out first, it's too late
4. **The principal must have all material facts available** -- you can't approve something you don't fully understand

> **Setup:** Carlos tells a supplier, "I'm ordering 500 widgets on behalf of Acme Corp," even though Acme never authorized Carlos to do this.
> **What Happens:** Acme's CEO finds out about the order and says, "Actually, we do need those widgets. I approve the order."
> **Result:** By ratifying the order, Acme retroactively creates an agency. The contract is now binding on Acme as if Carlos had been authorized all along.

### 2. AGENT'S AUTHORITY

This section answers: "What exactly is an agent allowed to do?" This is critical because if an agent acts *outside* their authority, the principal may not be bound.

---

#### A. Scope of Authority

##### 1. Actual Authority

**Actual authority** is the authority the principal has genuinely given to the agent. It comes in two flavors:

- **Express authority** -- Authority the principal has specifically and explicitly given. Plain English: "I told you that you can do X."

- **Implied authority** -- Authority that is not explicitly stated but is reasonably necessary to carry out the express authority, OR that arises from business custom. Plain English: "I didn't specifically say you could do X, but it's obviously part of doing the job I gave you."

> **Setup:** Ed owns a men's clothing store. He tells his agent Al, "Go purchase merchandise for the store." Ed never specifically mentions perfumes.
> **What Happens:** Al buys men's clothing (which Ed expressly authorized) and also buys a line of perfumes (believing customers might buy gifts).
> **Result:** Al has **express authority** to buy men's clothing. He likely has **implied authority** to buy perfumes because a reasonable agent in a men's clothing store might stock gift items. BUT if Ed specifically told Al "never buy denim," Al has **no authority** (express or implied) to purchase denim.

##### 2. Apparent Authority

**Apparent authority** -- Authority that a third party *reasonably believes* the agent has, based on the principal's words or conduct, even if the agent does NOT actually have that authority.

**Critical distinction:** Actual authority is between the principal and agent. Apparent authority is about what a **third party** reasonably believes.

**A principal does NOT grant apparent authority.** It arises because of a third party's reasonable belief that the agent has authority.

> **Setup:** Ed (from the example above) told Al never to buy denim. But Ethel, a denim supplier, has no idea about this restriction. She sees Al purchasing all kinds of clothing for Ed's store.
> **What Happens:** Al buys denim from Ethel.
> **Result:** Ethel had no reason to think Al couldn't buy denim -- it's a clothing store! Al has **apparent authority** from Ethel's perspective, so Ed is bound by the purchase. Ed's remedy is to sue Al for disobeying instructions, but Ed still owes Ethel for the denim.

---

#### B. Duty to Ascertain Scope of Authority

**Key rule:** A third party must communicate directly with the principal to confirm the agent's authority **if the agent acts in a way adverse to the principal's best interests.**

In plain English: If something smells fishy, the third party has a duty to check with the principal before relying on the agent.

---

#### C. Delegation of Authority

**General rule:** An agent **cannot delegate duties to others** (subagents).

**Three exceptions** (these are called **ministerial duties** -- routine tasks that don't require special judgment):

1. **Ministerial duties** -- Routine, mechanical tasks (like filing paperwork)
2. **Customary appointments** -- Situations where delegation is common practice in the industry
3. **Emergency appointments** -- When an emergency requires immediate delegation

**Ministerial duties** -- Routine tasks that don't require the agent's personal care, skill, or judgment. These CAN be delegated. In plain English: If it's just "fill out this form" type work, someone else can do it.

### 3. DUTIES AND REMEDIES

This is one of the most exam-heavy sections. It covers what agents owe principals, what principals owe agents, and what happens when either side messes up.

---

#### A. Agent's Duties to Principal (5 Key Duties)

##### 1. Loyalty

**What it means:** The agent must act solely in the principal's best interest. This is a **fiduciary duty** -- the highest standard of trust in the law.

**Key rule:** An agent **must not** undertake any business venture that competes with or interferes with the principal's business. This is because loyalty is one of the agent's implied fiduciary duties.

> **Setup:** Janet is a sales agent for ABC Insurance Company. She secretly starts her own insurance agency on the side, targeting the same customers.
> **What Happens:** Janet is competing with her principal.
> **Result:** Janet has breached her duty of loyalty. ABC can terminate her and sue for damages.

##### 2. Obedience

**What it means:** The agent must follow the principal's reasonable instructions. If the principal says "don't do X," the agent must not do X.

##### 3. Reasonable Care

**What it means:** The agent must act with the level of care, skill, and diligence that a reasonable person in that position would use.

**Important note:** **Unpaid agents do NOT owe a lesser standard of care** to their principals than paid agents do, although unpaid agents cannot be compelled to perform duties.

##### 4. Accounting

**What it means:** The agent must account for **all** of the principal's property and money that comes into the agent's possession. In plain English: "Keep track of every penny of the principal's money and property."

##### 5. Information

**What it means:** The agent owes a duty to keep the principal informed of **all facts relating to the agency**. If the agent learns something important, they must tell the principal.

---

#### B. Principal's Remedies (Against an Agent)

When an agent breaches their duties, the principal can:

- **Sue for breach of the agency contract** or in **tort** for harm done
- Seek **transfer of improperly held property** or damages
- If the agent is **insolvent**, the remedy may be transfer of property or value of any benefit the agent received
- Obtain an **injunction** prohibiting the agent from:
  - Revealing trade secrets obtained during employment
  - Competing with the principal after termination of employment (if there's a non-compete agreement)

---

#### C. Principal's Duties to Agent (4 Key Duties)

##### 1. Agreed-on Period of Employment
The principal must honor the agreed employment term.

##### 2. Compensation
The principal must pay the agent as agreed.

##### 3. Reimbursement for Expenses
**Duty of reimbursement** means a principal must reimburse an agent for any expenses necessarily incurred for the discharge of agency duties.

##### 4. Indemnity for Losses
**Duty of indemnity** means a principal must reimburse an agent for any losses or damages the agent has suffered arising because of the agency and incurred through no fault of the agent.

**Key distinction (exam favorite):**
- **Reimbursement** = paying back expenses the agent spent doing their job (like travel costs)
- **Indemnity** = making the agent whole for losses they suffered because of the agency (like being sued by a third party while doing the principal's work)

---

#### D. Agent's Remedies (Against a Principal)

The text references agent remedies including:
- **General lien** -- The right to retain possession of ALL of the principal's property in the agent's hands until the principal pays what is owed
- **Special lien** -- The right to retain only specific property of the principal related to the particular transaction

---

### 4. AGENCY TERMINATION

An agency relationship can end in several ways. This is important for the exam because **termination affects the agent's authority** and what third parties can rely on.

---

#### A. Just Cause
Either party can terminate if the other breaches the agreement.

#### B. Lapse of Time
The agency ends when the specified time period expires.

#### C. Accomplishment of Purpose
The agency ends when its purpose is achieved (e.g., "Sell my house" -- once sold, the agency ends).

#### D. Revocation
The principal cancels the agent's authority.

#### E. Renunciation
The agent quits.

#### F. Death or Incapacity
**General rule:** The agency terminates when either party dies or becomes incapacitated.

**Key exception:** In banking, a bank has authority to pay checks drawn on a depositor's account until it receives **actual notice of death**. No notice to third parties is required when the principal dies.

**Another exception to the "notice not necessary" rule:** When a **government bond** or similar obligation exists -- notice of the principal's death must be given to third parties.

#### G. Changed Circumstances
Significant changes (like bankruptcy) can terminate the agency.

**Does an agent's bankruptcy terminate the agency?** An agent's bankruptcy **usually terminates** the agency relationship.

### 5. THIRD-PARTY LIABILITIES AND RIGHTS

This is the most complex section and heavily tested. It deals with the three-way relationship between principal, agent, and third parties.

---

#### A. Agency Contract Liability

The liability of the principal depends on whether the principal is **disclosed**, **partially disclosed**, or **undisclosed**.

- **Disclosed principal** -- The third party knows BOTH that the agent is acting for a principal AND knows the principal's identity. ("I'm buying this for General Motors.")

- **Partially disclosed principal** -- The third party knows the agent is acting for a principal but does NOT know the principal's identity. ("I'm buying this for my client, whose name I can't reveal.")

- **Undisclosed principal** -- The third party has NO IDEA the agent is acting for anyone other than themselves. The third party thinks the agent is the actual party to the deal.

##### 1. Third Party's Rights Against a Principal

- **Disclosed principal:** Third party can sue the principal directly.
- **Partially disclosed principal:** Third party can sue both the agent AND the principal.
- **Undisclosed principal:** When the third party discovers the principal's identity, they can elect to hold either the agent or the principal liable. But once they make a final election, they're bound by it.

**Key rule:** When the third party learns of the principal's existence and identity and elects to hold the agent liable, the third party's rights against the principal are **discharged**.

##### 2. Principal's Rights Against a Third Party

The principal can enforce contracts made by the agent on the principal's behalf.

##### 3. Agent's Liability to a Third Party

**The bases for holding an agent liable to a third party:**

- **Breach of warranty of authority** -- An agent acts for a principal even with no actual authority to do so. If the principal ratifies the contract, the agent's liability for breach of warranty terminates.

- **Incompetent principal** -- The agent is personally liable for breach of warranty of authority when acting on behalf of a minor or mentally incompetent person, if the third party was not aware of the principal's incapacity.

- **Undisclosed and partially disclosed principals** -- If a third party intended to contract with the agent personally (not for a principal), the agent must disclose both the existence and identity of the principal.

- **Agent's personal liability** -- An agent who voluntarily assumes responsibility for performing the agreement is liable for the principal's nonperformance.

- **Agent's liability to account** -- If an agent can receive money from a third party and does not provide it to the principal, the principal can recover it in a suit against the agent.

- **Agent's liability for torts and crimes** -- An agent is liable for fraudulent or malicious acts that harm a third party.

##### 4. Agent's Rights Against a Third Party

An agent can sue a third party if the third party breaches a contract that the agent entered into on the principal's behalf, under certain circumstances.

**Why can't the agent of an undisclosed principal have apparent authority?**
By definition, an undisclosed agent is unknown to the third party, who does not know the agent is acting as an agent. Because apparent authority is based on the third party's belief that an agent has authority, it cannot arise if the third party is not aware of the agency.

**When does failure to disclose the principal's identity constitute fraud?**
An agent's failure to disclose the principal's identity to a third party constitutes fraud when the agent has represented that the contract is on the agent's behalf alone or that the agent represents someone other than the real principal.

---

#### B. Tort Liability -- Employment

##### 1. Respondeat Superior

***Respondeat superior*** (Latin: "let the master answer") -- A legal doctrine that makes an **employer liable for the torts of an employee** committed within the scope of employment.

In plain English: If your employee hurts someone while doing their job, YOU (the employer) are on the hook.

**The 3 circumstances that must be present for respondeat superior to apply:**

1. **The employee has committed a wrong** for which he or she can be liable
2. **The employer has retained the right to control** the employee's physical conduct
3. **The employee has committed the wrong within the scope of employment**

> **Setup:** Smith works as a delivery driver for XYZ Baking Company. While driving in his assigned territory during a usual work day, Smith negligently strikes and injures a pedestrian.
> **What Happens:** The pedestrian wants to sue.
> **Result:** The pedestrian can sue Smith individually (he was negligent). BUT can the pedestrian also sue XYZ Baking Company? That depends on whether Smith was an **employee** or **independent contractor** (see next section).

##### 2. Employees vs. Independent Contractors

This distinction is CRITICAL because respondeat superior generally only applies to employees, NOT independent contractors.

- **Employee** -- A person whose physical conduct in performing services is controlled or subject to the right of control by the employer. The employer tells them HOW to do the job.

- **Independent contractor** -- A person who contracts with another to do something but who is NOT controlled by the other party as to physical conduct in performing services. The independent contractor controls HOW they do the job.

- **Master-servant relationship** -- The older legal term for the employer-employee relationship. The "master" (employer) has the right to control the physical conduct of the "servant" (employee).

**The 7 tests generally used to determine whether an employment relationship exists:**

1. **What extent of control** the parties' agreement specifies
2. **Whether the worker is engaged in a distinct occupation** (independent contractors usually are)
3. **What skill the occupation requires** (highly specialized = more likely independent contractor)
4. **Who supplies the instrumentalities, tools, and place of work** for the worker
5. **What length of time** the worker is employed
6. **What method of payment** applies (hourly/salary = employee; by the job = contractor)
7. **Whether the work is part of the employer's regular business**

> **Setup (from the textbook):** Smith has a distribution agreement with XYZ Baking Company. Smith owns and operates his own truck (with XYZ's name painted on it, followed by "Owned and Operated by Smith"). Smith sells only XYZ products, establishes his own customer base, sets his own schedule, receives commissions (not salary), and controls his own comings and goings.
> **What Happens:** Smith negligently injures a pedestrian while delivering.
> **Result:** Despite wearing XYZ's uniform and having XYZ's name on the truck, Smith is most likely an **independent contractor**, not an employee. XYZ has little physical control over Smith. Smith owns his truck, controls his routes, builds his own customer base, and is paid by commission. Therefore, XYZ is probably NOT vicariously liable for Smith's negligence under respondeat superior.

**3 Exceptions where an employer IS liable for an independent contractor's torts:**

1. **Employer's negligence** -- The employer was negligent in selecting, instructing, or supervising the contractor
2. **Nondelegable duty** -- The employer had a legal duty that cannot be delegated (e.g., keeping premises safe)
3. **Inherently dangerous activities** -- The work itself is inherently dangerous

##### 3. Scope of Employment

Even if a worker IS an employee, the employer is only liable under respondeat superior if the tort occurred **within the scope of employment**.

**Factors for determining whether a tort occurred within the scope of employment:**

- Did the employer **authorize** the act, or was the act incidental to an act the employer did authorize?
- Was the act one that **such employees usually perform**?
- **When and where** did the act occur, and what was its purpose?
- To what extent did the parties intend to **advance the employer's interests** by performing the act?
- Did the employer **furnish the instrumentality** (tools, vehicle, etc.) by which the employee committed the tort?
- Did the employer authorize the **extent of departure** from the usual method of accomplishing the act?
- Did the act involve the **commission of a serious crime**?

**Employer liability for negligent vs. intentional torts:**
- **Negligent torts:** Employer is responsible if the act was within the scope of employment
- **Intentional torts:** Employer is liable **whether or not the employee intended to benefit the employer** by the act, as long as it was within the scope of employment

> **Setup:** Burt owns a bar. He hires Phyllis as a bartender and instructs her NOT to serve alcohol to intoxicated persons (because it's a crime under state law).
> **What Happens:** While Burt is absent, Phyllis serves an obviously intoxicated person. That person later causes a car accident.
> **Result:** Even though Phyllis disobeyed Burt's instructions, Burt is probably liable under the state statute that imposes criminal liability on the employer for illegally serving alcohol. The fact that Phyllis disobeyed instructions does not necessarily shield Burt.

## Review Questions & Answers

**1. When must an agency contract be in writing to be enforceable? (p. 11.3)**

Agency contracts must be in writing if they are to last beyond a year (under the Statute of Frauds).

---

**2. What is the usual method for creating an agency relationship? (p. 11.3)**

The usual method for creating an agency relationship is by **express appointment** -- the principal explicitly authorizes the agent.

---

**3. Explain how an agency by estoppel can be created even though the principal has done nothing to create an agency? (p. 11.4)**

An agency by estoppel can be created if the principal's words or conduct cause a third person to reasonably believe that an agency exists. The principal doesn't have to have done anything deliberately to create the agency -- their behavior just has to give a reasonable basis for the third party's belief.

---

**4. State the four conditions for ratification of an unauthorized contract. (p. 11.5)**

1. The agent must have purported to act for the principal
2. The principal must ratify the entire transaction
3. The principal must ratify before the third party elects to withdraw from the agreement
4. The principal must have all material facts available before the ratification is binding

---

**5. How may business custom relate to an agent's scope of authority? Give an example. (pp. 11.6-11.7)**

An agent may have implied authority based on business custom. For example, even though an agent may not have express authority to sell specific goods for a specific price, if the agent has customarily sold the same kind of goods for the same price, then the agent has implied authority to sell the most recently delivered goods for that price.

---

**6. How does an agent's apparent authority arise? (p. 11.6)**

A principal does not grant apparent authority. Apparent authority arises because of a **third party's reasonable belief** that the agent has authority.

---

A third party must communicate directly with the principal to confirm the agent's authority if the **agent acts in a way adverse to the principal's best interests**.

**20. Describe the bases for holding an agent liable to a third party. (pp. 11.18-11.19)**

- **Breach of warranty of authority** -- Agent acts without actual authority (liability ends if principal ratifies)
- **Incompetent principal** -- Agent is personally liable when acting for a minor or incompetent person if third party didn't know
- **Undisclosed and partially disclosed principals** -- Agent must disclose if third party intended to contract personally with agent
- **Agent's personal liability** -- Agent who voluntarily assumes responsibility is liable for principal's nonperformance
- **Agent's liability to account** -- Agent who receives money from third party and doesn't give it to principal
- **Agent's liability for torts and crimes** -- Agent is liable for their own fraudulent or malicious acts

---

**21. What three circumstances must be present for the application of the doctrine of respondeat superior? (p. 11.21)**

1. The employee has committed a wrong for which he or she can be liable
2. The employer has retained the right to control the employee's physical conduct
3. The employee has committed the wrong within the scope of the employment

---

**22. Identify the seven tests generally used to determine whether an employment relationship exists. (p. 11.22)**

1. What extent of control the parties' agreement specifies
2. Whether the worker is engaged in a distinct occupation
3. What skill the occupation requires
4. Who supplies the instrumentalities, tools, and place of work for the worker
5. What length of time the worker is employed
6. What method of payment applies
7. Whether the work is part of the employer's regular business

---

**23. List three exceptions to the rule that one who engages an independent contractor is not vicariously liable for the latter's torts. (p. 11.24)**

1. **Employer's negligence** (in selecting, instructing, or supervising the contractor)
2. **Nondelegable duty** (employer has a duty that can't be delegated)
3. **Inherently dangerous activities**

---

**24. What factors are important to determining whether a tort has occurred within the scope of employment? (p. 11.24)**

- Did the employer authorize the act, or was the act incidental to an act the employer did authorize?
- Was the act one that such employees usually perform?
- When and where did the act occur, and what was its purpose?
- To what extent did the parties intend to advance the employer's interests by performing the act?
- Did the employer furnish the instrumentality by which the employee committed the tort?
- Did the employer authorize the extent of departure from the usual method of accomplishing the act?
- Did the act involve the commission of a serious crime?

---

**25. Describe an employer's liability for an employee's negligent and intentional torts. (p. 11.26)**

For **both** intentional torts and negligence, the employer is responsible if the act was within the scope of employment. If the employee's tort is **intentional**, the employer is liable **whether or not** the employee intended to benefit the employer by the act.

## Application Questions & Answers

### Question 1: April's Power of Attorney

**Scenario:** April has a power of attorney authorizing her to sell and convey Paul's building for $100,000. April enters into a contract with June, the buyer. The contract provides that the price is $100,000 and that Paul will take back a $60,000 mortgage. Is the contract binding on the principal?

**Answer:** April has a binding power of attorney, which gives her the power to sell and convey Paul's building. Whether she can require Paul to take back a $60,000 mortgage depends on **custom and usage** involved in such real property sales. If she had **implied authority** to agree to such a mortgage, then the contract is binding on Paul.

---

### Question 2: Ed's Clothing Store (Authority Types)

**Scenario:** Ed owns a men's clothing store. Al is Ed's purchasing agent. Ed has personal ideas about merchandise. Ed included perfumes, abhors denim, and instructed Al not to buy denim.

**a. What authority does Al have to purchase perfumes, regular men's clothing, or denim?**

Al may have **express authority** to purchase perfumes and regular men's clothing if Ed specifically instructed him. If not, Al at least has **implied authority** to purchase these items because they are regularly stocked in the store. He has **no authority** (express or implied) to purchase denim clothing.

**b. Al purchases denim from Ethel, who doesn't know about Ed's denim ban. Is Ed liable?**

Ethel had no reason to believe Al was doing anything inappropriate for a clothing store. Ethel would be reasonable in assuming Al had authority to buy denim, so Al's authority is **apparent**. Ed could hold Ed liable for the purchase price. (Ed's remedy is to fire Al and possibly sue him for disobeying instructions.)

---

### Question 3: Annie, Pat, and Tom (Disclosed vs. Undisclosed Principal)

**Scenario:** Annie (agent) and Pat (principal) enter a written agreement to purchase goods from Tom. Annie did not disclose Pat's existence or identity. She signed as an individual. The contract was within Annie's actual authority. Goods were stolen; Pat never received them.

**a. If Tom sues Annie for the agreed price of the goods, will Tom recover?**

Pat was an **undisclosed principal** to Tom. Tom can sue Annie. If Tom wins the lawsuit, Annie can attempt to collect from Pat the amount of damages she was required to pay to Tom.

**b. If Annie told Tom before entering the contract that she was an agent for Pat, will Tom recover?**

Tom can recover from Pat because Pat is, in this scenario, a **disclosed principal**. Tom's primary action would be against Pat.

---

### Question 4: Smith and XYZ Baking Company (Employee vs. Independent Contractor)

**Scenario:** Smith has a distribution agreement with XYZ Baking Company. Smith owns his truck (with XYZ's name + "Owned and Operated by Smith"), sells only XYZ products, establishes his own customer base, sells at XYZ prices, wears a company uniform, receives commissions, and cannot transfer the agreement. Smith negligently injures a pedestrian.

**a. What are the pedestrian's rights against Smith?**

Smith was negligent in injuring the pedestrian, and the pedestrian can sue Smith individually, regardless of Smith's relationship to XYZ.

**b. What are the pedestrian's rights against XYZ Baking Company?**

The pedestrian is more likely to be successful in a suit against Smith because XYZ has **little physical control** over Smith. Using the seven tests, one could argue either way, but it is **more likely** that Smith would be considered an **independent contractor**. Smith owned and operated his own truck, controlled his own comings and goings, had his own customer base, and had responsibility for establishing his own territory. Even though XYZ gave him a territory, prices, and a uniform, Smith controlled how he did his work. If Smith is an independent contractor, XYZ is **NOT vicariously liable** under respondeat superior.

---

### Question 5: Burt's Bar (Criminal Liability for Employee's Acts)

**Scenario:** Burt owns a bar. He hires Phyllis as a bartender and instructs her NOT to serve alcohol to intoxicated persons (a crime under state law). While Burt is absent, Phyllis serves an obviously intoxicated person who later causes a car accident.

**Answer:** Generally, no one is responsible for another's criminal act. Burt had instructed Phyllis not to serve intoxicated persons. Under common law, Burt would NOT be liable for Phyllis's criminal act. **However**, the state statute changes the analysis -- if the statute imposes **criminal liability on the employer** for the employee's illegal service of alcohol, Burt would probably be liable under the statute. The specific statutory provisions determine the outcome.

## Exam Tips

### Top 10 Things to Memorize

1. **3 ways to create an agency:** Appointment, Estoppel, Ratification
2. **4 conditions for ratification:** Agent purported to act for principal; entire transaction ratified; ratified before third party withdraws; principal has all material facts
3. **Actual vs. Apparent authority:** Actual = what principal really gave agent; Apparent = what third party reasonably believes
4. **5 agent duties:** Loyalty, Obedience, Reasonable Care, Accounting, Information
5. **Reimbursement vs. Indemnity:** Reimbursement = expenses; Indemnity = losses/damages
6. **3 types of principals:** Disclosed, Partially disclosed, Undisclosed
7. **3 requirements for respondeat superior:** Employee committed a wrong; employer has right to control; wrong was within scope of employment
8. **7 tests for employee vs. independent contractor** (listed above)
9. **3 exceptions for independent contractor liability:** Employer negligence, nondelegable duty, inherently dangerous activities
10. **Unpaid agents** do NOT owe a lesser standard of care

### Common Exam Traps

- **TRAP: "Apparent authority comes from the principal."** WRONG. Apparent authority arises from the THIRD PARTY's reasonable belief, NOT from anything the principal intentionally grants.

- **TRAP: "An undisclosed principal's agent can have apparent authority."** WRONG. By definition, apparent authority requires the third party to know an agency exists. If the principal is undisclosed, the third party doesn't know there IS an agent, so apparent authority is impossible.

- **TRAP: "Unpaid agents owe less care."** WRONG. Unpaid agents owe the SAME standard of care, but they can't be forced to perform.

- **TRAP: "The principal can ratify part of a transaction."** WRONG. Ratification must cover the ENTIRE transaction -- it's all or nothing.

- **TRAP: "Independent contractors always shield the employer from liability."** WRONG. Three exceptions exist: employer negligence, nondelegable duty, inherently dangerous activities.

- **TRAP: "An agent who follows instructions can't be liable."** WRONG. An agent can still be personally liable for torts and crimes they commit, even if instructed by the principal.

- **TRAP: Confusing employee vs. independent contractor.** The key test is **physical control**. Who controls HOW the work is done? If the employer controls the method, it's an employee. If the worker controls the method, it's an independent contractor.

- **TRAP: "Estoppel requires the principal to intend to create an agency."** WRONG. Estoppel can arise even when the principal did NOTHING to deliberately create an agency -- it's about the third party's reasonable belief based on the principal's conduct.

### [2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Flags

- This textbook is from **2008**. Agency law fundamentals have not changed dramatically, but some areas to verify against current law:
  - **Gig economy workers** (Uber, DoorDash, etc.) have created massive new case law on the employee vs. independent contractor distinction. The "ABC test" adopted by California (AB5, 2019) and other states presumes workers are employees unless the hiring entity proves otherwise. Federal DOL rules have also been updated (2024 final rule using economic reality test).
  - **Non-compete agreements** are under significant regulatory scrutiny. The FTC issued a final rule in 2024 attempting to ban most non-competes (currently being litigated). Many states have restricted or banned non-competes since 2008.
  - **Respondeat superior** principles remain largely unchanged, but scope-of-employment analysis has evolved with remote work becoming common since 2020.
  - The 7-factor employee test is supplemented in many jurisdictions by the **economic reality test** and **ABC test** for determining worker classification.

## Quick-Reference Summary

**AGENCY CREATION (3 Methods)**
- Appointment = Principal directly authorizes agent (most common; must be written if >1 year)
- Estoppel = Principal's conduct creates reasonable belief in third party
- Ratification = Principal approves unauthorized act after the fact (4 conditions)

**AUTHORITY TYPES**
- Express = Principal explicitly grants
- Implied = Reasonably necessary or by business custom
- Apparent = Third party's reasonable belief (NOT granted by principal)

**AGENT'S 5 DUTIES TO PRINCIPAL**
- Loyalty (fiduciary; no competing)
- Obedience (follow instructions)
- Reasonable Care (same for paid and unpaid)
- Accounting (track all property/money)
- Information (keep principal informed)

**PRINCIPAL'S 4 DUTIES TO AGENT**
- Honor employment period
- Pay compensation
- Reimburse expenses
- Indemnify for losses (not agent's fault)

**AGENCY TERMINATION (7 Ways)**
- Just cause, Lapse of time, Purpose accomplished, Revocation, Renunciation, Death/incapacity, Changed circumstances

**THREE TYPES OF PRINCIPALS**
- Disclosed = Third party knows identity
- Partially disclosed = Third party knows principal exists but not identity
- Undisclosed = Third party has no idea

**RESPONDEAT SUPERIOR (3 Requirements)**
- Employee committed a wrong
- Employer has right to control physical conduct
- Wrong was within scope of employment

**EMPLOYEE vs. INDEPENDENT CONTRACTOR (7 Tests)**
- Control, Distinct occupation, Skill required, Who supplies tools, Length of time, Payment method, Part of regular business

**3 EXCEPTIONS (IC Liability)**
- Employer negligence, Nondelegable duty, Inherently dangerous activities

**SCOPE OF EMPLOYMENT FACTORS (7)**
- Employer authorize act? Usual employee conduct? Time/place/purpose? Advance employer interests? Employer furnish tools? Departure from usual method? Serious crime?
