### SECTION 1: Statute of Frauds

#### What Is It (Plain English)?

The **statute of frauds** is a law designed to prevent fraud and perjury by requiring that certain types of contracts be put in writing and signed by the party responsible for performing the contract. Without a writing, these contracts are generally unenforceable.

> **Plain English**: Think of it as the law saying, "Some deals are too important to rely on a handshake. Write it down or it won't hold up in court."

#### Six Types of Contracts That MUST Be in Writing

1. **Contracts for the sale of land or any interest in land**
   - Includes not just buying property, but leases, easements, mortgages -- any "interest" in land
   - **Important exception**: When a purchaser of real property has *taken possession* AND *made substantial improvements* in reliance on an oral contract to sell, most courts will enforce the oral contract

   > **Setup**: Tom orally agrees to sell his farm to Sarah for $200,000.
   > **What Happens**: Sarah moves onto the property, builds a barn, and plants crops.
   > **Result**: Even though there is no written contract, most courts will enforce the oral agreement because Sarah took possession and made substantial improvements in reliance on the deal.

2. **Contracts that cannot be performed within one year**
   - Courts have NOT favored this requirement and generally hold the provision inapplicable if it is *possible* to perform the contract within one year
   - The key is possibility, not probability

   > **Setup**: An employer orally agrees to hire someone for a 14-month project.
   > **What Happens**: The employee argues the contract should be enforceable.
   > **Result**: Because the contract by its terms CANNOT be performed within one year (14 months), it needed to be in writing. However, if the contract was for an indefinite period (say, "until the project is done"), it could theoretically be completed within a year, so a writing might not be required.

3. **Promises to pay another's debt** (suretyship/guaranty)
   - If you promise to pay someone else's debt, that promise must be in writing
   - **Key exception**: If the primary purpose of the promise is to serve the promisor's own business interest, it may NOT need to be in writing

   > **Setup**: Frank owes a creditor money. Charles, who is also Frank's creditor, wants to guarantee Frank's debt to protect his own business interests.
   > **What Happens**: Charles orally agrees to pay Frank's debt so Frank's business stays open and can pay Charles back.
   > **Result**: Because Charles's primary purpose is to serve his OWN business interest (collecting his own debt), the agreement may not need to be in writing.

4. **Promises in consideration of marriage**
   - Prenuptial agreements must be in writing

5. **Promises by executors to pay estate debts from executor's own funds**
   - An **executor** is the person who manages an estate left by a will
   - An **administrator** is the person who manages the estate of a deceased person who has NOT written a will
   - If either promises to pay estate debts from their personal funds, it must be in writing

6. **Contracts for sale of personal property for $500 or more** (under the UCC)
   - The contract must contain terms relating to the **quantity** of goods for sale
   - Under the UCC, the contract writing need NOT set forth all material terms -- it just needs to be sufficient to show a contract was made
   - The only required term in the basic written agreement is **quantity**
   - A written note or memorandum is sufficient

#### What Form of Writing Is Required?

- A **written note or memorandum** is sufficient
- Does NOT need to be a formal contract
- Must be signed by the party to be charged (the person against whom enforcement is sought)

#### Insurance Contracts and the Statute of Frauds

Of the six categories, courts have considered only **two** as applying to insurance contracts:

1. **Contracts that cannot be performed within one year** -- Policies with terms exceeding one year
2. **Promises to answer for another's debt** -- Such as surety bonds

> **EXAM TRAP**: Most property-casualty insurance policies have terms of one year or less, so the statute of frauds generally does NOT apply to them. Life insurance policies can theoretically be performed within one year (the insured could die within a year), so many courts hold the one-year rule does not apply to life insurance either.

### SECTION 2: Parol Evidence Rule

#### What Is It (Plain English)?

The **parol evidence rule** limits the terms of a contract to those expressed in writing. It has three purposes:

1. **Carry out the parties' presumed intentions** -- if they wrote it down, that's what they meant
2. **Achieve certainty and finality** about the parties' rights and duties
3. **Exclude fraudulent and perjured claims** -- prevent someone from fabricating terms later

> **"Parol"** means "oral" or "spoken." The rule basically says: once you've written down your deal, you can't introduce outside oral or written evidence to contradict or add to what's already in the written contract.

#### Four Exceptions to the Parol Evidence Rule

Parol evidence IS allowed in these circumstances:

1. **Incomplete contracts** -- to prove terms of contracts that are not complete on their face
2. **Ambiguity** -- to clarify ambiguous terms in a contract
3. **Fraud, accident, illegality, or mistake** -- to support allegations of these issues relating to the contract
4. **Condition precedent** -- to prove that a condition precedent (something that had to happen first) failed to occur

> **Setup**: Barry buys Sam's house for $200,000. The written contract only mentions the property. Barry produces a letter from Sam saying the patio furniture goes with the house.
> **What Happens**: Barry tries to introduce the letter and an oral conversation about a microwave.
> **Result**: Under the parol evidence rule, this evidence is generally excluded because the written contract governs. HOWEVER, if Barry can prove the contract was *incomplete* or *ambiguous*, or that there was *fraud or mistake*, the evidence might be admitted.

#### Parol Evidence and the UCC

The UCC is more flexible than common law:
- Under the UCC, the contract writing need not set forth ALL material terms
- A letter showing that the written contract was not the entire agreement IS allowable evidence
- The UCC allows evidence of **prior course of dealings** between the parties
- The UCC allows evidence of **trade usage** (common practices in the industry)
- The only REQUIRED term in the basic written agreement is the **quantity**

### SECTION 3: Contract Interpretation

#### A. Plain Meaning

Courts first look at the **plain, ordinary meaning** of the words in a contract. If the language is clear and unambiguous, courts will enforce it as written.

#### B. Effectuation of Intent

The court's goal in interpreting a contract is to **determine the intentions of the parties**. Courts try to give effect to every provision and avoid interpretations that make any part of the contract meaningless.

#### C. Entire and Divisible Contracts

- **Entire contract**: All parts are interdependent; failure of one part can affect the whole
- **Divisible contract**: Can be broken into separate, independent parts
- The effect of treating contracts as **divisible** is that performance of a portion entitles the performing party to **immediate payment** for that portion

> **Setup**: A contractor agrees to build a 3-phase project. Phase 1 is the foundation, Phase 2 the structure, Phase 3 the finishing work, with separate payment for each.
> **What Happens**: The contractor completes Phase 1 but disputes arise about Phase 2.
> **Result**: If the contract is divisible, the contractor gets paid for Phase 1 regardless of the Phase 2 dispute.

#### D. Clerical Errors and Omissions

Courts can correct obvious clerical errors in contracts. Minor mistakes in writing do not destroy the contract.

#### E. Contradictory Terms

When terms within a contract contradict each other, courts apply a **priority system**:

1. **Handwriting prevails over typewriting** -- Handwritten changes show the most recent intent
2. **Typewriting prevails over printing** -- Typed additions are more specific than pre-printed form language
3. **Words prevail over figures** -- Written-out numbers trump numerical figures

> **Memory Trick**: Think "most specific and recent wins" -- Handwriting > Typewriting > Printing; Words > Figures

#### F. Ambiguity

- If a provision has more than one reasonable meaning, courts adopt the interpretation **least favorable to the party who put the provision in the contract** and most favorable to the party who assented to it
- If a provision is SO ambiguous that its meaning cannot be determined with usual tools, the court can **admit evidence from outside the contract**
- This is especially important in insurance because the insurer drafts the policy, so ambiguities are construed **against the insurer**

> **EXAM TRAP**: The "contra proferentem" rule (interpret against the drafter) is CRITICAL in insurance law. Since the insurer writes the policy, any genuinely ambiguous language will be interpreted in favor of the insured.

#### G. Parties' Own Interpretation

If both parties understood a term to have a specific meaning, courts will honor that shared understanding.

#### H. Legal and Fair Interpretations

Courts prefer interpretations that make the contract legal and fair rather than illegal or unfair.

#### I. Trade Usage, Course of Dealings, and Course of Performance

The **UCC reconciles conflicts** by establishing the following priority:

1. **Express terms** of the contract (highest priority)
2. **Course of performance** (how the parties have acted under THIS contract)
3. **Course of dealing** (how the parties have acted in PREVIOUS contracts)
4. **Trade usage** (common practices in the industry) (lowest priority)

> **Setup**: A furniture oil contract calls for "300 barrels of furniture oil." Buyer claims specifications were required. Seller says no specs were mentioned.
> **What Happens**: Both sides want to introduce outside evidence about what "furniture oil" means in the trade and what their prior dealings looked like.
> **Result**: The UCC allows prior course of dealings and trade usage as evidence, even when the parol evidence rule might otherwise exclude such evidence. Express terms still take top priority.

#### UCC and Missing Price Terms

If the parties fail to state a price for goods, a court will assume the contract implies a **reasonable price**.

### SECTION 4: Third-Party Rights

#### A. Contract Assignments

An **assignment** is the transfer of rights or property from one party (the **assignor**) to another (the **assignee**).

##### Rights That ARE Assignable
- Most contract rights can be assigned
- Under the UCC, the right to receive payment is almost always assignable

##### Rights That Are NOT Assignable (6 Situations)
1. **Legal restrictions** -- Statutes prohibiting assignment
2. **Contract restrictions** -- The contract itself says "no assignment allowed"
3. **Personal contracts** -- Services that depend on the specific person (e.g., a portrait artist)
4. **Alteration of performance** -- Assignment would significantly change what the other party must do
5. **Personal satisfaction contracts** -- Where performance depends on one party's personal taste
6. **Damages for personal injury** -- You generally cannot assign your right to personal injury damages

##### Forms of Assignment
- Can be oral or written
- No specific form required
- No consideration required (can be a gift)

##### Key Rules About Assignments

- **Assignee's Rights**: The assignee gets whatever rights the assignor had -- no more, no less
- **Assignee's Duties**: The assignee takes on the duties that go with the assigned rights
- **Notice of Assignment**: The assignee should **immediately notify the obligor** (the person who owes performance) of the assignment to ensure the obligor pays the assignee rather than the assignor, thus defeating the original assignor's right to demand payment or performance

> **Setup**: Alice has a contract with Bob where Bob owes her $10,000. Alice assigns her right to that payment to Carol.
> **What Happens**: Carol doesn't notify Bob of the assignment. Bob pays Alice directly.
> **Result**: If Carol didn't notify Bob, Bob's payment to Alice may discharge Bob's obligation. Carol should have notified Bob immediately so Bob would know to pay Carol instead.

#### B. Third-Party Beneficiaries

A **third-party beneficiary contract** is a contract between two parties that benefits a third party.

##### Types of Beneficiaries

1. **Creditor beneficiary** -- A third party who is owed a debt that is to be satisfied by performance of the contract
   > Example: Dad owes $5,000 to the bank. Dad's employer agrees to pay the bank directly. The bank is a creditor beneficiary.

2. **Donee beneficiary** -- A third party who receives the benefits of the contract's performance as a gift from the promisor
   > Example: Mom buys a life insurance policy naming her son as beneficiary. The son is a donee beneficiary.

3. **Incidental beneficiary** -- A third party who benefits from a contract even though the contracting parties did not intend to benefit them. Incidental beneficiaries have **NO contractual rights** and **CANNOT sue** under the contract
   > Example: A city hires a contractor to repave a road. Businesses on that road benefit from increased traffic, but they are only incidental beneficiaries and cannot sue if the contractor fails to perform.

4. **Intended beneficiary** -- A third party whom the contracting parties actually intended to benefit. Intended beneficiaries generally CAN enforce the contract.

##### Characteristics of Beneficiary Contracts
- A binding contract must exist between the promisor and the promisee
- The parties to the contract must **intend** that the third party benefit from the contract
- The modern legal trend is to permit original parties to cut off beneficiaries' rights UNLESS the beneficiary can prove their position has **changed materially in reasonable reliance** on the contract

> **Setup**: Jerry contracts with X Bank for financing to build a building for Maggie. Jerry hires subcontractors. X Bank refuses to lend, so Jerry can't pay subcontractors.
> **What Happens**: Subcontractors sue X Bank as third-party beneficiaries.
> **Result**: This is arguable. Financing the project directly benefits subcontractors who must be paid via that financing, making them potentially creditor beneficiaries. However, the bank might argue it only owes performance to Jerry, and therefore only Jerry can sue.

### SECTION 5: Discharge of Contracts

"Discharge" means the contract obligations are ended. There are several ways this happens:

#### A. Performance

The most common way to discharge a contract is by **performing what you promised**.

1. **Payment** -- Paying what you owe under the contract
2. **Tender of Performance** -- An offer to perform one's duties under a contract. If one party properly tenders performance and the other refuses, the tendering party is discharged.
3. **Substantial Performance** -- Rather than requiring PERFECT performance, courts consider whether the performance actually given was **substantial** and whether the party performed in **good faith**
   - Allows the performing party to recover under the contract minus deductions for defects
   - The non-breaching party gets an **offset** (deduction) for any shortcomings

   > **Setup**: A builder constructs a house but installs the wrong brand of plumbing (equivalent quality).
   > **What Happens**: The homeowner refuses to pay.
   > **Result**: The builder has substantially performed. The homeowner must pay the contract price minus any cost difference for the plumbing substitution.

4. **Personal Satisfaction** -- Some contracts require performance to the personal satisfaction of one party. Courts interpret "satisfaction" objectively when possible (would a reasonable person be satisfied?).

5. **Agreed Time / "Time Is of the Essence"** -- If a contract requires performance by a certain date AND states that "time is of the essence," the court WILL enforce that deadline strictly.

#### B. Agreement of the Parties

Contractual obligations can be discharged by another agreement:
- **Accord and satisfaction** -- Parties agree to substitute different performance for what was originally required
- **Novation** -- An agreement to replace an original party to a contract with a new party

#### C. Substitution

Parties can agree to substitute a different performance for the one originally required.

#### D. Impossibility

Performance can be excused when it becomes truly impossible. Four causes of impossibility:

1. **Change in law** -- A new law makes performance illegal
2. **Death or incapacity** -- Of a party whose personal performance was required
3. **Destruction of subject matter** -- The thing the contract is about is destroyed
4. **Other party's act** -- The other party does something that makes performance impossible

> **Setup**: A venue contracts to host a wedding. A hurricane destroys the venue.
> **What Happens**: The venue cannot perform.
> **Result**: Performance is discharged by impossibility (destruction of subject matter). The deposit should be returned.

##### Temporary and Partial Impossibility
- Temporary impossibility only **suspends** the duty to perform; it does not discharge it permanently
- Partial impossibility excuses only the impossible part

##### Frustration of Purpose
- When an unexpected event destroys the PURPOSE of the contract (even though performance is still technically possible), the contract may be discharged
- The frustrated purpose must have been the **principal purpose** of the contract

##### Commercial Impracticability
- Increased cost can constitute commercial impracticability if the increase is **drastic**
- Even so, the seller must take **commercially reasonable precautions** to protect the source of supply
- A mere increase in cost is NOT enough -- it must be extreme and unforeseen

##### Statutes of Limitations
- Legal time limits for bringing lawsuits
- If the statute of limitations runs out, the obligation is effectively discharged because it can no longer be enforced

##### Fraudulent Alteration
- If a party fraudulently alters the terms of a written contract, it may discharge the other party's obligations

### SECTION 6: Conditions

A **condition** is an event that must occur (or not occur) before a duty to perform under a contract arises or is discharged.

#### Three Types of Conditions

1. **Condition Precedent** -- An event that MUST OCCUR BEFORE a duty of performance arises in a contract
   > **Example**: "I'll buy your house IF I can get a mortgage." Getting the mortgage is a condition precedent -- the buyer's duty to purchase only kicks in if the mortgage is approved.

2. **Condition Concurrent** -- An event that must occur AT THE SAME TIME as another condition in a contract
   > **Example**: In a home sale closing, the buyer pays the money and the seller delivers the deed simultaneously. Neither party has to go first.

3. **Condition Subsequent** -- An event that, IF IT OCCURS, discharges a duty of performance in a contract
   > **Example**: An insurance policy states coverage ends if the insured fails to pay premiums within 30 days. Failure to pay is a condition subsequent that terminates the insurer's duty to cover claims.

> **EXAM TRAP**: Know the difference between precedent (triggers a duty), concurrent (duties happen simultaneously), and subsequent (extinguishes a duty). These are heavily tested.

### SECTION 7: Breach of Contract

#### A. Types of Breach

1. **Repudiation** -- A party's outright refusal to meet obligations under a contract
   > "I'm not going to do what I promised." Flat-out refusal.

2. **Anticipatory Breach** -- A party's unequivocal indication BEFORE contract performance is due that he or she will not perform when performance IS due
   - Purpose: To avoid **"enforced idleness"** on the part of the aggrieved party
   - The aggrieved party does NOT have to wait until performance was due to sue
   - The aggrieved party does NOT need to tender their own performance to prove the breach

   > **Setup**: A caterer for a June wedding announces in March that they will not be providing services.
   > **What Happens**: The couple doesn't have to wait until June to sue.
   > **Result**: The anticipatory breach allows them to immediately sue and find a replacement caterer.

3. **Material (Major) Breach** -- A party fails to perform a substantial part of the contract
   - Gives the non-breaching party the right to consider the entire contract broken
   - The non-breaching party can sue for damages AND is discharged from their own obligations

4. **Minor Breach** -- A party fails to perform a small part of the contract
   - **Temporarily suspends** any duty of performance by the non-breaching party
   - Gives the aggrieved party a right to sue for damages (usually as an offset to the agreed price)
   - Does **NOT** give the right to treat the entire contract as broken

   > **EXAM TRAP**: Minor breach = offset to price, still must perform. Material breach = contract is over, full damages available.

#### B. Remedies for Breach

##### 1. Damages

- **Compensatory Damages** -- Payment awarded by a court to indemnify (reimburse) a victim for actual harm
  > Plain English: Money to make you whole -- to put you back where you'd be if the contract had been performed.

- **Consequential Damages** -- Payment to indemnify an injured party for losses that result *indirectly* from a breach, such as lost profits
  - Only awarded when the defendant **was aware** of the probable occurrence of the damages at the time of contracting

  > **Setup**: A supplier fails to deliver parts to a factory on time.
  > **What Happens**: The factory loses $50,000 in production because it can't operate without the parts.
  > **Result**: If the supplier KNEW the factory would lose production without timely delivery, consequential damages of $50,000 may be awarded. If the supplier had no idea, likely not.

- **Punitive (Exemplary) Damages** -- Payment to punish a defendant for a reckless, malicious, or deceitful act, or to deter similar conduct
  - Need NOT bear any relationship to the party's actual damages
  - Rarely awarded in pure contract disputes (more common in tort-like bad faith cases)

- **Extracontractual Damages** -- Payment awarded by a court that exceeds the usual contract damages
  - Against **insurers** specifically on two grounds:
    1. Breach of the insurer's duty of **good faith and fair dealing** in insurance contracts
    2. **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** on the insured by the insurer's extreme and outrageous conduct

- **Mitigation of Damages** -- A duty owed by the injured party to take reasonable action to minimize a loss after learning of a breach
  > You can't just sit back and let damages pile up. You have a duty to take reasonable steps to reduce your losses.

- **Liquidated Damages** -- A reasonable estimation of actual damages, agreed to by the contracting parties and included in the contract, to be paid in the event of a breach or for negligence
  - Must be a REASONABLE estimate, not a penalty
  - If unreasonable, courts will strike it down as a penalty clause

##### 2. Action for Price (UCC Remedy)

Courts order equitable remedies under the following circumstances:
- When the buyer has accepted the goods
- When a carrier has tendered goods to the buyer (if the contract requires seller to ship)
- When the seller has delivered goods to the carrier (if delivery is only to the carrier)
- When a third party holds goods for delivery without moving them
- When the buyer has received a document indicating the buyer's right to the goods
- When the third party acknowledges the buyer's right to possession

##### 3. Equitable Remedies

Used when money damages are **inappropriate or inadequate**:

- **Specific Performance** -- A court orders the breaching party to actually DO what they promised
  - Most common in real estate (every piece of land is unique)
  - Rare in personal service contracts (courts won't force someone to work for you)

  > **Setup**: Seller agrees to sell a unique historic property. Seller backs out.
  > **What Happens**: Buyer sues for specific performance.
  > **Result**: Court can order the seller to complete the sale because the property is unique and money damages won't adequately compensate the buyer.

- **Injunction** -- A court order requiring a party to act or refrain from acting
  > Example: A court orders a former employee to stop violating a non-compete agreement.

- **Reformation** -- An equitable remedy by which a court modifies a written contract to reflect the parties' actual intent
  > Example: A contract mistakenly lists the wrong property address. A court can reform (correct) the contract to reflect what the parties actually agreed to.

## Review Questions & Answers

### Q1. Identify the situations in which a contract must be in writing under the statute of frauds. (p. 4.4)

**Answer**: The six situations are:
- Contracts for the sale of land or any interest in land
- Contracts that cannot be performed within one year
- Promises to pay another's debt
- Promises in consideration of marriage
- Promises by executors of decedents' estates to pay estate debts from executors' own funds
- Contracts involving sale of personal property for $500 or more

### Q2. Under what circumstances, if any, will an oral executory contract to sell a house and land be legally enforceable? (pp. 4.4-4.5)

**Answer**: When the purchaser of real property has taken possession of the property AND made substantial improvements in reliance on an oral contract to sell, most courts will enforce the oral contract.

### Q3. How have courts interpreted the statute of frauds with regard to contracts that cannot be performed in a year? (pp. 4.5-4.6)

**Answer**: Courts have NOT favored the one-year requirement and generally hold the provision inapplicable if it is **possible** to perform the contract within one year. The test is whether performance within a year is possible, not whether it is probable.

### Q4. For sale of personal property under the UCC, what terms must a contract contain to satisfy the statute of frauds? (p. 4.8)

**Answer**: A contract for the sale of personal property must contain terms relating to the **quantity** of goods for sale to satisfy the statute of frauds. The quantity is the only required term.

### Q5. What type of writing is sufficient to satisfy the statute of frauds? (p. 4.9)

**Answer**: To satisfy the statute of frauds, a written note or memorandum is sufficient. It does not need to be a formal contract.

### Q6. What insurance contracts, if any, are subject to the statute of frauds? (p. 4.10)

**Answer**: Of the six situations to which the statute of frauds applies, courts have considered only TWO as applying to insurance contracts:
1. Policies that are contracts that cannot be performed within one year
2. Policies that are promises to answer for another's debt (such as surety bonds)

### Q7. What is the purpose and effect of the parol evidence rule? (p. 4.11)

**Answer**: The parol evidence rule limits the terms of a contract to those expressed in writing. It has three purposes:
1. To carry out the parties' presumed intentions
2. To achieve certainty and finality as to the parties' rights and duties
3. To exclude fraudulent and perjured claims

### Q8. Describe the exceptions to the parol evidence rule. (pp. 4.12-4.13)

**Answer**: Parol evidence is allowed under the following circumstances:
- To prove terms of incomplete contracts
- To clarify ambiguity in a contract
- To support an allegation of fraud, accident, illegality, or mistake relating to a contract
- To prove failure of a condition precedent

### Q9. What is a court's goal in interpreting a contract? (p. 4.14)

**Answer**: A court's goal in interpreting a contract is to **determine the intentions of the parties**.

### Q10. Describe the effect of treating contracts as divisible. (pp. 4.15-4.16)

**Answer**: The effect of treating contracts as divisible is that performance of a portion of the contract entitles the performing party to **immediate payment** for that portion.

### Q11. Under the UCC, how will a court enforce a contract for the sale of goods when the contract does not contain the price of goods sold? (p. 4.17)

**Answer**: If the parties fail to state a price for the goods, a court will assume that the contract implies a **reasonable price**.

### Q12. What guidelines do courts use to resolve contradictory terms? (pp. 4.17-4.18)

**Answer**: To resolve contradictory terms, courts apply a system of priorities:
- Handwriting prevails over typewriting
- Typewriting prevails over printing
- Words prevail over figures

### Q13. How do courts resolve ambiguous contractual terms? (p. 4.18)

**Answer**: If a provision can have more than one reasonable meaning, courts adopt the interpretation **least favorable to the party who put the provision into the contract** and most favorable to the party who assented to it. If a provision is so ambiguous that its meaning cannot be determined with usual tools, the court can admit evidence from outside the contract.

### Q14. How does the UCC reconcile conflicts among: (a) express terms, (b) trade usage, (c) course of dealing, (d) course of performance? (p. 4.20)

**Answer**: The UCC establishes the following priorities when these considerations are in conflict:
1. Express terms of the contract (highest)
2. Course of performance
3. Course of dealings
4. Trade usage (lowest)

### Q15. State the types of situations in which rights may not be assigned. (p. 4.21)

**Answer**: The most common situations in which contract rights are NOT assignable:
- Legal restrictions
- Contract restrictions
- Personal contracts
- Alteration of performance
- Personal satisfaction contracts
- Damages for personal injury

### Q16. Explain why an assignee of a debt should immediately notify the obligor of the assignment. (p. 4.24)

**Answer**: The assignee should notify the obligor of the assignment to ensure that the obligor pays the assignee rather than the assignor, thus defeating the original assignor's right to demand payment or performance.

### Q17. In the absence of a provision in the contract to the contrary, may the original parties to a contract cut off the rights of a third-party beneficiary without the consent of the beneficiary? (p. 4.26)

**Answer**: The modern legal trend is to permit the original parties to cut off the beneficiaries' rights UNLESS the beneficiaries can prove that their positions have **changed materially in reasonable reliance** on the contracts.

### Q18. Why do courts recognize substantial performance that falls short of actual performance? (pp. 4.28-4.29)

**Answer**: Rather than permitting the promisee to escape liability completely on the ground of nonperformance, courts consider whether the performance actually given was **substantial** and whether the party performed in **good faith**. This prevents unjust enrichment of the non-breaching party.

### Q19. What is the effect of a contract provision that requires performance by a certain date and states that "time is of the essence"? (p. 4.29)

**Answer**: If a contract provision requires performance by a certain date and states that "time is of the essence in this agreement," then a court will **strictly enforce** that provision (the deadline becomes a firm requirement, and missing it is a breach).

### Q20. Explain how one agreement can discharge contractual obligations under another agreement. (p. 4.31)

**Answer**: Contractual obligations can be discharged by another agreement by:
- **Accord and satisfaction** -- agreeing to substitute different performance
- **Novation** -- substituting a new party for an original party
- **Substitution** of a different performance for the performance required in a contract

### Q21. Identify four changes in circumstances that make performance of a contract impossible. (p. 4.32)

**Answer**: The four changes that make performance impossible are:
1. Change in law
2. Death or incapacity
3. Destruction of subject matter
4. Other party's act

### Q22. Discuss the relationship of increased cost to commercial impracticability. (p. 4.34)

**Answer**: Increased cost CAN constitute commercial impracticability if the increase is **drastic**. However, even in this case, the seller must take commercially reasonable precautions to protect the source of supply. A mere increase in cost is NOT sufficient -- it must be extreme and unforeseen.

### Q23. What is the purpose of the doctrine of anticipatory breach? (p. 4.37)

**Answer**: The concept of anticipatory breach developed to avoid **"enforced idleness"** on the part of the aggrieved party, who would otherwise have to wait until the time of performance to sue for breach, and to make it unnecessary for that party to tender performance at the time stated in the contract to prove the other party's breach.

### Q24. What are the effects of a minor breach of contract? (p. 4.37)

**Answer**: The effects of a "minor" breach include:
- It **temporarily suspends** any duty of performance by the nonbreaching party that would have arisen on proper performance
- It gives the aggrieved party a basis to sue for damages for the breach -- usually an offset to the agreed price -- but NOT remedies for breach of the entire contract

### Q25. When does a court award consequential damages for breach of contract? (p. 4.39)

**Answer**: Consequential damages are awarded to the plaintiff only when the **defendant was aware** of the probable occurrence of the damages at the time of contracting.

### Q26. When does a court award extracontractual damages against insurers? (pp. 4.39-4.40)

**Answer**: Courts award extracontractual damages against insurers on the following grounds:
- Breach of the insurer's duty of **good faith and fair dealing** in insurance contracts
- **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** on the insured by the insurer's extreme and outrageous conduct

### Q27. Under what circumstances do courts order equitable remedies? (pp. 4.40-4.41)

**Answer**: Courts order equitable remedies under the following circumstances:
- When the buyer has accepted the goods
- When a carrier has tendered the goods to the buyer if the contract requires the seller to ship them to the buyer
- When the seller has delivered the goods to the carrier and the contract provides only for delivery to the carrier and not to the destination
- When a third party holds the goods for delivery without moving them
- When the buyer has received a document indicating the buyer's right to the goods
- When the third party acknowledges the buyer's right to possession

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Question 1: Barry's House Purchase (Parol Evidence)

**Scenario**: Barry negotiated for over three months to buy Sam's house for $200,000. Sam's lawyer prepared an agreement stating only the property would be conveyed at that price within 90 days. At closing, Barry asks about patio furniture and a microwave. Sam says they were not included. Barry produces a letter from Sam (dated two months before the final agreement) saying the patio furniture goes with the house. Barry also claims Sam orally agreed the microwave would go with the house at the time of signing.

**Q1a. On what legal basis will the letter and conversation be excluded as evidence?**

**Answer**: A real estate contract is a formal agreement that must be in writing under the statute of frauds. The **parol evidence rule** limits the terms of a contract to those expressed in writing. It prevents the introduction of any oral or written evidence of an agreement, including letters and conversations. This rule would exclude the evidence in question.

**Q1b. What does Barry need to allege and prove to have such evidence admitted?**

**Answer**: Barry would have to prove that the contract was **incomplete**, **ambiguous**, or that it was the result of **fraud, accident, illegality, or mistake**; or that a **condition precedent** failed to occur.

### Application Question 2: Frank's Guarantee of Dianne's Debt

**Scenario**: Charles had a $5,000 judgment against Dianne. Frank, also a creditor of Dianne for a much greater amount, feared that if Charles attached Dianne's machinery, it would halt Dianne's business and she couldn't pay Frank. Frank wants Charles not to proceed with the attachment and is willing to guarantee the payment of Dianne's debt. Must the agreement between Frank and Charles be in writing?

**Answer**: Ordinarily, an agreement to pay another's debt must be in writing under the statute of frauds. HOWEVER, when the **primary purpose** of the promise is to achieve the promisor's **own business objective**, the agreement need NOT be in writing. Here, Frank has agreed to pay Dianne's debt to achieve his objective of collecting his OWN larger debt from her. Because the primary purpose serves Frank's own business interest, the agreement need not be in writing to be enforceable.

### Application Question 3: Eric's Employment with Beauty-Smart

**Scenario**: Eric quit his job at Cosmetics Corporation to work for Beauty-Smart. On Dec. 16, Beauty-Smart told Eric that if he started Jan. 1, and his work was satisfactory for 12 months, he'd be paid $60,000 the following Jan. 15. Eric agreed, quit, and started. After 9 months, he was unjustly dismissed.

**Q3a. Is Eric entitled to recover?**

**Answer**: Because this contract could be performed within one year (12 months from Jan. 1 to Jan. 1), a writing was NOT necessary under the statute of frauds. Eric performed his part of the agreement, but Beauty-Smart was in breach. As a matter of fairness, a court would allow Eric to recover any damages.

**Q3b. Assume the parties agreed orally that Eric was to have employment for life. Would the contract be enforceable?**

**Answer**: A contract for employment for life APPEARS to require a writing because it can involve performance beyond a year. However, courts do NOT favor the one-year requirement and generally hold the provision **inapplicable if it is possible to perform the agreement within one year**. Here, Eric's life could end within one year (he could die), and thus his employment could end within one year, taking this agreement out of the statute of frauds. Courts would likely hold this enforceable even without a writing.

### Application Question 4: Buyer vs. Seller -- Furniture Oil (UCC / Parol Evidence)

**Scenario**: Buyer sues Seller for breach of contract for 300 barrels of furniture oil, claiming the oil did not meet specifications. The written contract merely called for "300 barrels of furniture oil." Buyer wants to introduce evidence of prior contracts (with specifications), a letter of inquiry about "another 300 barrels of the special furniture oil," and trade usage. Seller objects to all evidence and wants to introduce the generally accepted meaning of "furniture oil" in the trade.

**Answer**: The UCC provides that the contract writing need not set forth ALL material terms of the contract. The letter is allowable evidence to show the written contract was not the entire agreement and that the letter further explains the agreement. The only required term in the basic written agreement is **quantity**, which need not be stated accurately. The UCC recognizes the parol evidence rule but allows evidence of:
- A **prior course of dealings** between the parties (Buyer's evidence would be admitted)
- **Trade usage** (Seller's evidence would also be admitted)

Both Buyer's and Seller's evidence should be admissible under the UCC's more permissive approach.

### Application Question 5: Subcontractors as Third-Party Beneficiaries

**Scenario**: Jerry contracted with X Bank for financing to build a building for Maggie. Jerry hired subcontractors. X Bank refused to honor its lending agreement, so Jerry couldn't pay subcontractors. Subcontractors sue X Bank as third-party beneficiaries.

**Answer**: This could have several answers. A third-party beneficiary contract is a contract between two parties that benefits a third party, either directly or indirectly. In this situation:
- Jerry and X Bank are the two contracting parties
- The subcontractors are potentially beneficiaries of that contract
- Because financing a project through a bank directly benefits any subcontractors who must be paid via that financing, the subcontractors are potentially **direct beneficiaries** (creditor beneficiaries)
- Financing the project would allow Jerry to discharge obligations owed to subcontractors, so they could be **creditor beneficiaries**
- HOWEVER, the bank might argue that it owes performance only to Jerry, and that therefore only Jerry can sue
- Notable characteristics: A binding contract must exist between promisor and promisee; the parties must intend for the third party to benefit; and performance must be due to the subcontractors

## Exam Tips: Common Traps and What to Memorize

### Critical Items to Memorize

1. **Six contracts requiring writing under statute of frauds**: Land, >1 year, another's debt, marriage consideration, executor's personal funds, personal property $500+ (UCC)

2. **Four exceptions to parol evidence rule**: Incomplete contracts, ambiguity, fraud/accident/illegality/mistake, condition precedent failure

3. **Priority for contradictory terms**: Handwriting > Typewriting > Printing; Words > Figures

4. **UCC conflict resolution priority**: Express terms > Course of performance > Course of dealing > Trade usage

5. **Six non-assignable rights**: Legal restrictions, contract restrictions, personal contracts, alteration of performance, personal satisfaction, personal injury damages

6. **Four causes of impossibility**: Change in law, death/incapacity, destruction of subject matter, other party's act

7. **Three types of conditions**: Precedent (before), concurrent (at the same time), subsequent (after, discharges)

8. **Four types of beneficiaries**: Creditor, donee, incidental (NO rights), intended

### Common Exam Traps

- **Trap 1**: The one-year rule under the statute of frauds -- the test is whether it is POSSIBLE to perform within a year, not whether it is LIKELY. A lifetime employment contract might be performable within a year (person could die).

- **Trap 2**: Confusing "parol" with "parole" (prison release). "Parol" means oral/spoken evidence. The parol evidence rule is about limiting outside evidence.

- **Trap 3**: Incidental beneficiaries have NO rights. Just because you happen to benefit from someone else's contract does NOT give you the right to sue under it. Only intended/creditor/donee beneficiaries have rights.

- **Trap 4**: Substantial performance does NOT mean perfect performance. The builder who installs equivalent-quality plumbing has substantially performed. The other party must still pay, minus an offset.

- **Trap 5**: Anticipatory breach lets the aggrieved party sue IMMEDIATELY -- they do NOT have to wait until performance was due.

- **Trap 6**: Minor breach vs. material breach -- Minor = offset/suspension; Material = full damages and discharge.

- **Trap 7**: Consequential damages require the defendant's AWARENESS of probable damages at contracting time. No awareness = no consequential damages.

- **Trap 8**: Specific performance is mostly for unique items (land, rare art). Courts will NOT order specific performance for personal services.

- **Trap 9**: The assignee gets EXACTLY the rights the assignor had -- no more, no less. And the assignee MUST notify the obligor promptly.

- **Trap 10**: Under UCC, the ONLY required term in a written contract for goods is QUANTITY. Price, delivery terms, etc. can be implied.

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Statute of Frauds
- 6 types of contracts must be in writing
- Writing = note or memo, signed by party to be charged
- Insurance: Only 2 categories apply (>1 year, another's debt)
- Exception for land: possession + substantial improvements = oral enforceable

### Parol Evidence Rule
- Limits contract to written terms
- 4 exceptions: incomplete, ambiguous, fraud/mistake, condition precedent
- UCC is more flexible -- allows course of dealing and trade usage

### Contract Interpretation
- Goal: determine parties' intent
- Contradictory terms: Handwriting > Typewriting > Printing; Words > Figures
- Ambiguity: construe against drafter (critical in insurance)
- UCC priority: Express > Course of performance > Course of dealing > Trade usage
- Missing price in UCC: court implies reasonable price
- Divisible contracts: partial performance = partial payment

### Third-Party Rights
- Assignment: transfer of rights (most rights assignable; 6 exceptions)
- Assignee must notify obligor immediately
- Beneficiaries: Creditor, Donee, Incidental (no rights), Intended
- Modern trend: parties can cut off beneficiary rights unless material reliance

### Discharge of Contracts
- Performance (payment, tender, substantial performance)
- Agreement (accord and satisfaction, novation, substitution)
- Impossibility (change in law, death, destruction, other party's act)
- Commercial impracticability (drastic cost increase)
- Time is of the essence = strict deadline enforcement

### Conditions
- Precedent: must happen BEFORE duty arises
- Concurrent: must happen at SAME TIME
- Subsequent: if happens, DISCHARGES duty

### Breach of Contract
- Repudiation: flat refusal
- Anticipatory: advance notice of non-performance (can sue immediately)
- Material: substantial failure (discharge + full damages)
- Minor: small failure (offset only, no discharge)

### Remedies
- Compensatory: actual harm
- Consequential: indirect losses (defendant must have been aware)
- Punitive: punishment for bad acts
- Extracontractual: beyond normal contract damages (bad faith by insurers)
- Liquidated: pre-agreed damage amount (must be reasonable)
- Specific performance: court orders actual performance (unique items)
- Injunction: court orders action/inaction
- Reformation: court rewrites contract to match intent
- Mitigation: injured party must minimize losses
