### I. INTENTIONAL PHYSICAL TORTS AGAINST PERSONS

These are torts where someone deliberately does something that causes physical harm or the fear of physical harm to another person. Unlike negligence (where harm is accidental), these torts require **intent** -- the person meant to do the act (even if they didn't intend all the consequences).

---

#### A. Battery

**Definition (Plain English)**: Battery is the intentional, unauthorized touching of another person in a harmful or offensive way. You do NOT need to actually injure someone -- just touching them without permission in an offensive manner counts.

**Key Points**:
- The touching must be **intentional** (not accidental)
- The touching must be **unauthorized** (no consent)
- It must be **harmful or offensive** to a reasonable person
- **Actual physical injury is NOT required** -- offensive touching is enough
- Touching someone's clothing or something closely connected to them counts

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Dave is angry at his coworker Maria during a meeting
- **What Happens**: Dave grabs Maria's arm and shoves her away from the conference table. Maria is not injured but is offended and frightened.
- **The Result**: This is battery. Even though Maria wasn't physically hurt, Dave intentionally touched her in an offensive, unauthorized way. Maria can sue for battery and recover damages for the offensive contact.

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION**: You CAN have a battery without an assault, and an assault without a battery.
- Someone touches you from behind without warning = **battery but no assault** (you had no apprehension beforehand)
- Someone swings at you and misses = **assault but no battery** (you feared being hit, but no contact occurred)

**Defenses to Battery**:
- **Consent**: The plaintiff agreed to the touching (e.g., contact sports, medical procedures with informed consent)
- **Self-defense**: The defendant was protecting themselves
- **Defense of others**: The defendant was protecting someone else
- **Physical discipline with reasonable force**: Parent disciplining a child (within reason)
- **Good faith**: Acting in good faith under certain circumstances

---

#### B. Assault

**Definition (Plain English)**: Assault is an intentional act that causes another person to **fear** or **apprehend** an immediate harmful or offensive touching. Think of it as the "threat" of battery. No actual touching is required -- just making someone believe they are about to be touched.

**Key Points**:
- The victim must have **reasonable apprehension** (fear) of immediate harmful contact
- The threat must be **imminent** -- future threats don't count
- **Words alone are usually not enough** -- there must be some physical gesture or action
- The defendant must have the **apparent ability** to carry out the threat

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Tom raises his fist right in front of Karen's face
- **What Happens**: Tom swings at Karen but misses. Karen flinches and ducks.
- **The Result**: This is assault. Karen had reasonable fear of immediate harmful contact. Even though Tom never touched her (no battery), the assault is complete because Karen feared the contact.

**Another Example -- Assault WITHOUT Battery**:
- Swinging a fist close to someone's face without touching them = assault
- Pointing an unloaded gun at someone (if they don't know it's unloaded) = assault

**Defenses to Assault**: Same as battery -- consent, self-defense, defense of others

---

#### C. False Imprisonment and False Arrest

**False Imprisonment -- Definition (Plain English)**: The intentional and unlawful detention (confinement) of one person by another. You're holding someone against their will when you have no legal right to do so.

**Key Points**:
- The detention must be **intentional** (not accidental)
- The person must be **actually confined** -- they can't reasonably leave
- Confinement can be physical (locked room), by threat of force, or by assertion of legal authority
- There must be **no lawful justification** for the detention

**False Arrest -- Definition (Plain English)**: A specific type of false imprisonment where someone is detained under the false claim of legal authority. This applies to both police officers making unlawful arrests and private citizens making improper "citizen's arrests."

**Rules for Citizen's Arrests**:
- **For felonies**: A private citizen can arrest someone if they have **reasonable grounds to believe** the person committed a felony
- **For misdemeanors**: A private citizen can ONLY make an arrest for a **breach of the peace** committed in their presence

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A department store security guard suspects a customer of shoplifting
- **What Happens**: The guard detains the customer in a back room for 3 hours, even though the customer has receipts for all items
- **The Result**: This could be false imprisonment. While many state statutes allow brief, reasonable detention of suspected shoplifters (usually an hour or less), holding someone for 3 hours is likely unreasonable. The customer can sue for false imprisonment.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Shopkeeper's privilege laws vary significantly by state and have been updated since 2008. Some states have expanded or restricted merchant detention rights.

**Defenses**:
- Lawful authority to detain
- Reasonable detention for a reasonable time (shopkeeper's privilege, where it applies)
- Consent

---

#### D. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

**Definition (Plain English)**: When someone deliberately engages in extreme and outrageous conduct that causes severe emotional distress to another person. This is for truly shocking behavior -- not just being rude or insensitive.

**Key Points**:
- The conduct must be **extreme and outrageous** -- beyond all bounds of decency
- The defendant must have **intended** to cause distress OR acted with **reckless disregard**
- The plaintiff must suffer **severe emotional distress**
- **In many courts**: The plaintiff can succeed even without physical injury, IF there is a physical manifestation (e.g., anxiety attacks, insomnia, ulcers) of the emotional distress

**Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress** (related but different):
- Does NOT require intent -- just careless behavior causing emotional harm
- Usually requires some physical impact or manifestation

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance company knows a claim is valid
- **What Happens**: The insurer deliberately delays payment for months, sends threatening letters, and tells the insured they will "destroy" them financially if they complain
- **The Result**: This could be intentional infliction of emotional distress. The insurer's conduct is extreme and outrageous, going far beyond normal claims handling. The insured can sue for emotional distress damages.

### II. DEFAMATION (Libel and Slander)

**Defamation -- Definition (Plain English)**: Making a false statement about someone to a third party that damages their reputation. There are two types: **slander** (spoken) and **libel** (written/published).

**Key Elements of Any Defamation Claim**:
1. A **false statement** of fact (not opinion)
2. **Publication** to at least one third party (meaning someone other than the plaintiff heard/read it)
3. The statement was **about the plaintiff** (identifiable)
4. The statement **damaged** the plaintiff's reputation

---

#### A. Slander (Spoken Defamation)

**Definition (Plain English)**: False spoken statements that harm someone's reputation. Generally, the plaintiff must prove they suffered **actual damages** (lost money, lost business, etc.) UNLESS the slander falls into a "per se" category.

**Slander Per Se** -- Categories where damages are **presumed** (you don't have to prove specific financial loss):
- Accusing someone of committing a **serious crime**
- Saying someone has a **loathsome disease**
- Statements that harm someone's **business, trade, or profession**
- Accusing a woman of being **unchaste** (historically -- outdated in many jurisdictions)

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance agent, Jim, is competing with another agent, Sarah, for a large commercial account
- **What Happens**: Jim tells the prospective client: "Don't use Sarah -- she's been investigated for fraud and embezzlement." This is completely false.
- **The Result**: This is slander per se (imputing a serious crime and harming someone's profession). Sarah does NOT need to prove specific financial loss. The statement was false, published to a third party, and falls into a per se category.

---

#### B. Libel (Written/Published Defamation)

**Definition (Plain English)**: False written, printed, or published statements that harm someone's reputation. Libel is generally considered MORE serious than slander because written statements are more permanent and have wider distribution.

**Libel Per Se**: Written statements that are **defamatory on their face** -- meaning any reasonable reader would understand them to be harmful to reputation. When libel per se is found, the plaintiff does NOT need to prove specific damages.

**Key Distinction for News Media**:
The landmark case **Sullivan v. New York Times** (1964) created special rules for defamation of **public officials and public figures**:
- A public figure suing for defamation must prove **actual malice**
- **Actual malice** means the defendant KNEW the statement was false OR acted with **reckless disregard** for its truth or falsity
- This is a MUCH harder standard to meet than for private individuals

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Social media defamation law has evolved significantly since 2008. Online posts, tweets, and reviews can constitute libel. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (which shields platforms from liability for user content) has been subject to ongoing legal challenges and potential reform.

**Commercial Speech**: Product advertising and commercial statements can also be subject to defamation-like claims through **product disparagement** (also called **trade libel**) -- making false statements about a competitor's products.

---

#### Defenses to Defamation (Both Libel and Slander)

1. **Truth**: The statement is actually true. This is an **absolute defense** -- if it's true, it's not defamation, period.
2. **Retraction**: Publicly taking back the statement. This doesn't eliminate liability but can **reduce damages**.
3. **Absolute Privilege**: Statements made in certain protected settings (e.g., testimony in court, statements by legislators during legislative proceedings) are completely immune from defamation claims, even if false.
4. **Conditional (Qualified) Privilege**: Statements made in good faith in certain contexts (e.g., employer references, credit reports) are protected unless made with malice. The privilege can be lost if the person acts with malice or exceeds the scope of the privilege.

### III. INVASION OF THE RIGHT OF PRIVACY

**Definition (Plain English)**: A group of torts that protect your right to be left alone and to control personal information about yourself. There are SIX recognized types.

#### The Six Types of Invasion of Privacy:

**1. Intrusion on Solitude or Seclusion**
- Physically or electronically intruding into someone's private space
- Example: Wiretapping, secretly filming someone in their home, peeping toms

**2. Physical Invasion**
- Physically entering someone's private space
- Example: Breaking into someone's home to take photographs

**3. Public Disclosure of Private Facts**
- Publishing true but private information that would be highly offensive to a reasonable person
- Example: Publishing someone's medical records or sexual history without consent

**4. Publicity Placing Plaintiff in a False Light**
- Publishing information that creates a false, highly offensive impression of someone
- Example: Using someone's photo alongside a story about drug dealers, implying they are one

**5. Unauthorized Release of Confidential Information**
- Releasing private records or data without authorization
- Example: A doctor sharing patient records with an employer, a bank sharing financial information

**6. Appropriation of Plaintiff's Name or Likeness**
- Using someone's name, photo, or identity for commercial purposes without consent
- Example: Using a celebrity's photo in an advertisement without permission

#### Seven Defenses to Invasion of Privacy Claims:

1. The plaintiff **previously published** the information themselves
2. The plaintiff **consented** to the publication
3. The plaintiff is a **public figure**, or the information is **public knowledge**
4. The information was part of a **news event**
5. The publication would **not offend** a person of ordinary sensibility
6. The matters were **disclosed in judicial proceedings**
7. The information is of **public interest**

### IV. FRAUD

**Definition (Plain English)**: Fraud is intentional deception. Someone deliberately lies to you (or hides important information) to get you to do something, and you suffer harm because you believed them.

**The Six Elements of Fraud** (plaintiff must prove ALL six):

1. **False representation** -- A statement of fact that is untrue (or concealing a fact that should be disclosed)
2. **Material fact** -- The false statement is about something important (not trivial)
3. **Knowingly made** -- The person KNEW the statement was false (or was reckless about whether it was true)
4. **Intent to influence or deceive** -- The liar intended for you to rely on the false information
5. **Reasonable reliance** -- You actually relied on the false statement, and a reasonable person in your position would have too
6. **Detriment** -- You suffered actual harm (financial loss, etc.) as a result

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance agent is selling a life insurance policy to Mr. Henderson
- **What Happens**: The agent tells Mr. Henderson: "This policy will pay double your investment in 5 years, guaranteed." The agent knows this is false -- the policy has no such guarantee.
- **The Result**: Mr. Henderson buys the policy based on this statement. When the policy doesn't perform as promised, he has suffered a detriment. All six elements of fraud are met: false representation, material fact, knowingly made, intent to deceive, reasonable reliance, and detriment.

**Key Insurance Connection**: Fraud claims are extremely relevant in insurance -- both fraud by insureds (inflating claims) and fraud by agents/insurers (misrepresenting policy terms).

### V. BAD FAITH, OR OUTRAGE

**Definition (Plain English)**: In the insurance context, bad faith is when an insurer fails to act fairly and in good faith in handling claims or performing its duties under an insurance contract. The insurer has an **implied duty** to deal with its insured honestly and in good faith.

**When Can an Insured Sue for Bad Faith?**
- The insurer has failed in its **implied duty to act fairly** in discharging its duties under the insurance contract
- This includes: unreasonable claim denials, failure to investigate, failure to settle within policy limits, deliberate delays

**Damages Available**:
- Compensatory damages (the actual financial loss)
- **Punitive damages** (extra punishment money) -- available in bad faith cases because the conduct is intentional/willful
- Emotional distress damages

**Insurance Cases Involving Bad Faith**:
- Denying a clearly valid claim without investigation
- Refusing to defend an insured against a lawsuit when the policy requires defense
- Low-balling settlement offers in bad faith
- Unreasonable delays in processing claims

**Defenses to Bad Faith**:
- The insurer acted reasonably based on the information available
- There was a genuine dispute about coverage
- The claim was legitimately questionable

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Bad faith law has expanded significantly since 2008. Many states have enacted specific bad faith statutes with additional penalties. First-party bad faith (insurer vs. its own insured) is now recognized in most jurisdictions. Some states allow statutory penalties, attorney's fees, and treble damages.

### VI. INTERFERENCE WITH RELATIONSHIPS BETWEEN OTHERS

This covers several types of intentional torts where someone deliberately disrupts other people's relationships -- business, employment, or personal.

---

#### A. Injurious Falsehood (Product Disparagement / Trade Libel)

**Definition (Plain English)**: Making false statements about someone's business, products, or services that cause them to lose money. Different from defamation because it targets the **product/business** rather than the person's reputation.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Company A and Company B both sell commercial insurance
- **What Happens**: Company A publishes an article falsely claiming Company B's policies have a hidden exclusion that makes them worthless
- **The Result**: Company B loses customers who believe the false article. Company B can sue for injurious falsehood/trade libel.

---

#### B. Malicious Interference With Prospective Economic Advantage

**Definition (Plain English)**: Intentionally and wrongfully interfering with someone's expected business relationships or opportunities. You deliberately mess up a deal someone was about to close.

**Common-Law Elements**:
- A valid business relationship or expectancy existed
- The defendant knew about it
- The defendant intentionally interfered
- The interference was improper/malicious
- The plaintiff suffered economic loss

**Defense**: Legitimate competition. You ARE allowed to compete fairly -- the interference must be **improper** or **malicious**, not just competitive.

---

#### C. Unfair Competition

**Definition (Plain English)**: Broadly covers dishonest business practices that give an unfair advantage over competitors, including trademark infringement, trade secret theft, false advertising, and deceptive practices.

**Common Forms of Unfair Competition**:
- **Trademark infringement** -- Using a competitor's name, logo, or branding to confuse customers
- **Trade secret theft** -- Stealing proprietary formulas, customer lists, or business methods
- **False advertising** -- Making deceptive claims about your own product OR a competitor's
- **Passing off / palming off** -- Selling your goods as if they were someone else's (better-known) brand

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Acme Insurance Agency has been the dominant auto insurer in town for 20 years with a well-known green shield logo.
- **What Happens**: A new agency opens across the street called "Acne Insurance Agency" using an almost-identical green shield logo and similar font. Customers walk in thinking it is Acme.
- **The Result**: This is unfair competition through trademark infringement and passing off. Acme can sue for an injunction (forcing the competitor to change its name/logo) and damages for lost business.

---

#### D. Interference With Employment

**Definition (Plain English)**: When a third party wrongfully causes someone to lose their job or prevents them from getting a job.

**This tort arises when**:
- A third party **procures an employee's discharge** (gets them fired)
- A third party **prevents someone from getting employment**
- A third party **violates implied contract provisions** of employment
- An employer **breaches an express or implied contract** of employment

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Jerry was laid off from an insurance agency
- **What Happens**: Jerry's former supervisor tells prospective employers that Jerry was "ineffective" and "had alcohol problems" -- statements that are false
- **The Result**: Jerry can sue for interference with employment. The supervisor is actively preventing Jerry from gaining employment through false statements. Jerry may also have claims for defamation/slander.

---

#### E. Interference With Copyright, Patent, or Trademark

Using someone else's copyrighted material, patented inventions, or trademarks without authorization.

**Three Categories**:
- **Copyright**: Protects original creative works (books, software, music, marketing materials). Lasts for the author's life + 70 years.
- **Patent**: Protects inventions and processes. Lasts 20 years from filing date. Gives the inventor exclusive right to make, use, and sell the invention.
- **Trademark**: Protects brand identifiers (names, logos, slogans). Can last indefinitely if renewed and used in commerce.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance tech startup develops proprietary risk-scoring software (patented algorithm) and a unique claims-processing manual (copyrighted).
- **What Happens**: A competitor hires away three of the startup's engineers, who bring copies of the source code and the claims manual to their new employer. The competitor launches a nearly identical product within months.
- **The Result**: The startup can sue for both patent infringement (the algorithm) and copyright infringement (the manual). The competitor could face injunctions stopping them from selling the product, plus substantial damages. The departed engineers could also face trade secret claims.

---

#### F. Interference With Right to Use One's Own Name in Business

Preventing someone from using their own name in legitimate business activities.

---

#### G. Interference With Family Relationships

**Spousal Rights Against Third Parties**:
- A spouse can sue a third party for **personal injury** to the other spouse
- Claims for **alienation of affection** and **loss of consortium** (loss of companionship, support, and marital relations)
- **Wrongful-life actions**: Lawsuits related to negligence in genetic counseling or prenatal testing
- **Wrongful-pregnancy / wrongful-conception actions**: Lawsuits for failed sterilization procedures

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Many states have abolished alienation of affection claims since 2008. Only a handful of states still recognize this cause of action. Wrongful-birth and wrongful-life actions have also evolved significantly.

### VII. MISUSE OF LEGAL PROCESS

Two related but distinct torts involving abuse of the legal system.

---

#### A. Malicious Prosecution

**Definition (Plain English)**: Starting a criminal or civil legal proceeding against someone **without probable cause** and **with malice** (bad intent). The proceeding must eventually end in favor of the person who was wrongfully prosecuted.

**Elements**:
1. Institution of criminal or civil proceedings
2. **Without probable cause** (no reasonable basis for the charges)
3. **With malice** (done for improper purposes, not to seek justice)
4. The proceedings **terminated in favor of the defendant** (charges dropped, acquitted, etc.)

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: An insurer suspects fraud on a claim
- **What Happens**: Without any real evidence, the insurer files criminal fraud charges against the insured purely to pressure them into dropping a legitimate claim
- **The Result**: The criminal charges are eventually dismissed. The insured can sue for malicious prosecution because the insurer had no probable cause and acted with malice.

**Defense**: **Probable cause** -- if the person initiating the proceedings had a reasonable basis for believing the claims were valid, there is no malicious prosecution.

---

#### B. Malicious Abuse of Process

**Definition (Plain English)**: Using legal procedures (that may have been properly started) for a **purpose for which they were not designed**. The legal process itself may be legitimate, but it's being twisted to achieve an improper goal.

**Key Distinction from Malicious Prosecution**:
- **Malicious prosecution** = starting a case without probable cause
- **Malicious abuse of process** = using legal procedures for an improper purpose, even if the case itself was properly filed

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Jeff wants to take control of Scott's business assets
- **What Happens**: Jeff files a complaint to have Scott declared legally incompetent, not because Scott is actually incompetent, but to gain control over Scott's assets
- **The Result**: This is malicious abuse of process. Jeff is using a legitimate legal procedure (competency hearing) for an improper purpose (seizing assets). Scott can sue Jeff for malicious abuse of process.

### VIII. INTENTIONAL TORTS AGAINST PROPERTY

---

#### A. Trespass

**Definition (Plain English)**: Unauthorized entry onto or interference with someone else's property. There are two types:

**1. Trespass to Real Property (Land)**
- Intentionally entering someone else's land without permission
- Remaining on land after permission has been revoked
- Placing objects on someone's land without permission
- Even entering the airspace above or ground below someone's property

**2. Trespass to Personal Property (Chattels)**
- Intentionally interfering with someone's personal property (not land)
- Taking, using, or damaging someone's belongings
- The same elements of proof apply as for trespass to real property

**Key Point**: The same rules apply to both -- the trespasser must have intentionally entered/interfered with the property. You don't need to prove actual damage for trespass to real property (the unauthorized entry itself is the wrong).

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example (Real Property)**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance adjuster needs to inspect a property for a claim.
- **What Happens**: The homeowner says "Do not come on my property." The adjuster enters the backyard anyway to take photos without consent.
- **The Result**: This is trespass to real property. Even though the adjuster meant no harm and caused no damage, the unauthorized entry itself is the tort. The homeowner can sue, and the insurer could face liability for the adjuster's actions.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example (Personal Property)**:
- **The Setup**: After a car accident, a tow truck driver hauls the vehicle to the repair shop.
- **What Happens**: The tow driver opens the trunk, takes out the owner's golf clubs, and uses them for a week before returning them undamaged.
- **The Result**: This is trespass to personal property (chattels). The tow driver intentionally used someone else's belongings without permission. Even though the clubs were returned undamaged, the unauthorized use itself is actionable.

---

#### B. Conversion

**Definition (Plain English)**: The intentional and unlawful exercise of dominion (control) over someone else's personal property, to the detriment of the property's owner. Think of it as "treating someone else's stuff as if it were your own."

**Chattel** = Legal term for personal property (movable things, NOT land)

**Four Circumstances That Constitute Conversion**:

1. **Acquisition of possession** -- Taking someone's property (e.g., stealing a car)
2. **Unauthorized transfer of chattel** -- Selling or giving away someone's property without their permission
3. **Unreasonable withholding of chattel** -- Refusing to return someone's property when asked
4. **Damaging, altering, or misusing chattel** -- Damaging or fundamentally changing someone's property

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: You lend your car to a friend for one day
- **What Happens**: Your friend keeps the car for three months, repaints it a different color, and refuses to return it
- **The Result**: This is conversion. Your friend is exercising unauthorized dominion over your property (unreasonable withholding + altering the chattel). You can sue for the value of the car.

**Defenses to Conversion**:
- The defendant had lawful authority to possess the property
- The plaintiff consented to the defendant's use
- The defendant had a legitimate claim of ownership

---

#### C. Nuisance

**Definition (Plain English)**: An unreasonable interference with someone's use and enjoyment of their property. This is about activities that annoy, disturb, or harm your ability to use your own property.

**Two Types**:

**1. Private Nuisance**
- Interferes with a **specific person's** use of their property
- Only the affected property owner can sue
- Example: Your neighbor runs a noisy machine shop 24/7 that shakes your house

**2. Public Nuisance**
- Interferes with the rights of the **general public**
- Usually addressed by government action
- Example: A factory polluting a public river

**Nuisance Per Se**: An activity that is **always** a nuisance, regardless of circumstances (e.g., operating an illegal dump in a residential area)

**Intentional Nuisance**: When the person creating the nuisance knows their activity interferes with others' property and continues doing it anyway

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Jack and his neighbor share a property line
- **What Happens**: Jack regularly throws his garbage and rubbish over the fence into his neighbor's yard
- **The Result**: This is a private nuisance. Jack's actions interfere with his neighbor's enjoyment of their property. The neighbor can sue to stop the behavior (injunction) and for damages.

**Another Example -- Is Loud Music a Nuisance?**:
- Joe plays very loud music late at night, keeping neighbors awake
- This IS a nuisance because it inflicts discomfort on his neighbors -- UNLESS Joe can prove his music was not unreasonably loud (a tough argument if neighbors are losing sleep)

## Key Terms Glossary

**Battery** (p. 9.3) -- Intentional, unauthorized, harmful or offensive touching of another person. Physical injury is not required.

**Assault** (p. 9.4) -- An intentional act that creates reasonable apprehension (fear) of immediate harmful or offensive contact. No actual touching is required.

**False Imprisonment** (p. 9.5) -- Intentional and unlawful confinement of a person against their will by another person.

**False Arrest** (p. 9.5) -- A specific type of false imprisonment where someone is detained under a false claim of legal authority.

**Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress** (p. 9.7) -- Extreme and outrageous conduct intentionally or recklessly causing severe emotional distress to another.

**Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress** (p. 9.7) -- Careless (not intentional) conduct causing emotional distress, usually requiring physical manifestation.

**Defamation** (p. 9.8) -- A false statement published to a third party that damages someone's reputation. Umbrella term covering both slander and libel.

**Slander** (p. 9.8) -- Spoken defamation -- false oral statements harming reputation.

**Publication** (p. 9.8) -- In defamation law, "publication" means communicating the false statement to at least one person other than the plaintiff.

**Slander Per Se** (p. 9.9) -- Categories of slander where damages are presumed without proof of specific loss (accusing of crime, loathsome disease, harm to profession, unchastity).

**Libel** (p. 9.9) -- Written or published defamation -- false statements in print, broadcast, or other permanent form.

**Libel Per Se** (p. 9.10) -- Written statements that are defamatory on their face; no proof of specific damages required.

**Product Disparagement, or Trade Libel** (p. 9.14) -- False statements about another's business products or services that cause economic loss.

**Invasion of the Right of Privacy (Invasion of Privacy)** (p. 9.14) -- A group of torts protecting a person's right to be left alone and to control personal information. Six recognized types.

**Bad Faith, or Outrage** (p. 9.18) -- In insurance, the insurer's failure to act fairly and in good faith in handling claims. Can give rise to compensatory and punitive damages.

**Injurious Falsehood** (p. 9.20) -- Making false statements about someone's business, products, or property rights that cause financial harm.

**Malicious Interference With Prospective Economic Advantage** (p. 9.21) -- Intentionally and improperly interfering with someone's expected business relationships or opportunities.

**Malice** (p. 9.22) -- In tort law, acting with evil intent, ill will, or improper purpose. In defamation of public figures, knowing falsity or reckless disregard for truth.

**Unfair Competition** (p. 9.22) -- Dishonest business practices giving an unfair advantage, including trademark infringement, false advertising, and trade secret theft.

**Interference With Employment** (p. 9.22) -- When a third party wrongfully causes someone to lose or be unable to obtain employment.

**Wrongful-Life Action** (p. 9.26) -- A lawsuit (typically by or on behalf of a child born with defects) alleging negligence in genetic counseling or prenatal testing.

**Wrongful-Pregnancy Action (Wrongful-Conception Action)** (p. 9.26) -- A lawsuit for failed sterilization or contraception procedures resulting in unwanted pregnancy.

**Malicious Prosecution** (p. 9.26) -- Instituting criminal or civil proceedings against someone without probable cause and with malice, which terminate in the defendant's favor.

**Probable Cause** (p. 9.27) -- Reasonable grounds for believing that a claim or charge is well-founded. A defense to malicious prosecution.

**Malicious Abuse of Process** (p. 9.28) -- Using legal procedures for a purpose for which they were not designed (improper use of properly issued process).

**Conversion** (p. 9.30) -- Intentional and unlawful exercise of dominion or control over another's personal property (chattel) to the owner's detriment.

**Chattel** (p. 9.30) -- Legal term for personal property -- movable things (not real estate/land).

**Private Nuisance** (p. 9.32) -- Unreasonable interference with a specific person's use and enjoyment of their property.

**Intentional Nuisance** (p. 9.32) -- A nuisance where the person knows their activity unreasonably interferes with others' property rights and continues anyway.

**Nuisance Per Se** (p. 9.32) -- An activity that is always considered a nuisance regardless of circumstances (e.g., illegal activity in a residential area).

**Public Nuisance** (p. 9.32) -- An activity that unreasonably interferes with the rights of the general public, usually addressed by government action.

## Review Questions & Answers

### Q1: Is it possible to have a battery without an assault, or vice versa? Give examples.

**Answer**: Yes to both.
- **Battery without assault**: Touching someone else's clothing from behind without warning is battery (unauthorized offensive touching) but NOT assault (the person had no apprehension beforehand -- they didn't see it coming). Swinging a fist at someone who doesn't see it and hitting them = battery without prior assault.
- **Assault without battery**: Swinging a fist close to someone's face without touching them is assault (fear of imminent contact) but NOT battery (no actual touching occurred).

---

### Q2: What are the defenses to assault and battery?

**Answer**: The defenses are:
- The plaintiff **consented** to the touching (for battery)
- The defendant acted in **self-defense**
- The defendant acted in **defense of others**
- The defendant was using **physical discipline with reasonable force** (e.g., parent-child)
- The defendant acted in **good faith**

---

### Q3: Under what circumstances can a private citizen make a valid arrest with respect to (a) a felony and (b) a misdemeanor?

**Answer**:
- **(a) Felony**: Citizens can make arrests for felonies committed out of their presence, provided they have **reasonable grounds to believe** that those arrested committed the felonies. Private citizens can also make arrests for **felonies in progress**.
- **(b) Misdemeanor**: Private citizens can make arrests for misdemeanors **only when they constitute breaches of the peace** (disturbing public order).

---

### Q4: Can a plaintiff who suffers no physical injury succeed in a claim for infliction of emotional distress?

**Answer**: In many courts, yes. A plaintiff who suffers no physical injury can succeed in a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress **if a physical impact (manifestation) of the emotional distress results**. This means symptoms like anxiety attacks, insomnia, ulcers, or other physical symptoms caused by the emotional distress. Some jurisdictions are relaxing even this requirement.

---

### Q5: What is the effect of a finding of slander per se or libel per se?

**Answer**: The effect is that **the plaintiff is not required to prove injury or damage**. Damages are **presumed** by the law. The plaintiff does not need to show specific financial loss -- the defamatory statement itself is considered so harmful that the court will presume damages occurred.

---

### Q6: What protection did Sullivan v. New York Times give the media?

**Answer**: The Sullivan v. New York Times decision established that **public officials suing for defamation must prove actual malice**. This means the public figure must prove that:
1. The defendant's statement was **false**, AND
2. The defendant **knew it was false** OR made it with **reckless disregard** for its truth or falsity

This makes it significantly harder for public figures to win defamation suits against the media, protecting press freedom.

---

### Q7: List the defenses to defamation (libel or slander).

**Answer**: The four defenses are:
1. **Truth** -- An absolute defense; if the statement is true, it cannot be defamation
2. **Retraction** -- Publicly withdrawing the statement (reduces but does not eliminate damages)
3. **Absolute privilege** -- Statements made in certain protected settings (courtroom testimony, legislative proceedings)
4. **Conditional or qualified privilege** -- Statements made in good faith in certain contexts (employer references, credit reports); can be lost if made with malice

---

### Q8: Describe the (a) six types of invasion of privacy and (b) seven defenses for such claims.

**Answer**:

**(a) Six Types of Invasion of Privacy:**
1. Intrusion on solitude or seclusion
2. Physical invasion
3. Public disclosure of private facts
4. Publicity placing plaintiff in a false light
5. Unauthorized release of confidential information
6. Appropriation of plaintiff's name or likeness

**(b) Seven Defenses:**
1. The plaintiff previously published the information
2. The plaintiff consented to publication
3. The plaintiff is a public figure, or the information is public knowledge
4. The information was part of a news event
5. The publication would not offend a person of ordinary sensibility
6. Matters were disclosed in judicial proceedings
7. The information is of public interest

---

### Q9: What elements must a plaintiff prove to establish fraud?

**Answer**: A plaintiff must prove these **six elements**:
1. **False representation** -- A materially untrue statement
2. **Material fact** -- The falsehood concerns something important
3. **Knowingly made** -- The defendant knew it was false
4. **Intent to influence or deceive** -- Made to induce the plaintiff's reliance
5. **Reasonable reliance** -- The plaintiff actually relied on the statement, and a reasonable person would have too
6. **Detriment** -- The plaintiff suffered actual harm

---

### Q10: Explain under what circumstances an insured can sue an insurer for bad faith.

**Answer**: An insured can sue an insurer for bad faith when **the insurer has failed in its implied duty to act fairly and in good faith in discharging its duties under an insurance contract**. This includes situations where the insurer unreasonably denies claims, fails to investigate, refuses to settle within policy limits, or deliberately delays claim payments.

---

### Q11: Under what circumstances does interference with employment arise?

**Answer**: Interference with employment arises when a third party has:
- **Procured the employee's discharge** (gotten them fired)
- **Interfered with the employee's right to seek lawful employment** (preventing them from getting a new job)
- **Breached an express or implied contract of employment**
- The employer has violated implied or express contract provisions

---

### Q12: What rights do spouses have against third parties?

**Answer**: A spouse can sue a third party for:
- **Personal injury** to the other spouse
- **Alienation of affection** (a third party luring a spouse away)
- **Loss of consortium** (loss of companionship, support, and marital relations due to injury to the other spouse)

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Many states have abolished alienation of affection claims.

---

### Q13: Distinguish between malicious prosecution and malicious abuse of process.

**Answer**:
- **Malicious prosecution** is the institution of **criminal or civil proceedings without probable cause and with malice**, that is, maliciously causing process to issue. The key is that the case itself was brought without proper basis.
- **Malicious abuse of process** is the use of legal procedures **for a purpose for which they were not designed**, that is, improper use of process after it has issued. The case may have been properly filed, but the legal tools are being misused for an improper purpose.

---

### Q14: How do trespass to real property and trespass to personal property differ?

**Answer**: The **same elements of proof** apply to trespass to real property as to personal property. The difference is simply **what is being trespassed upon**:
- **Real property trespass** = unauthorized entry onto or interference with someone's land
- **Personal property trespass** = unauthorized interference with someone's movable property (chattels)

---

### Q15: Describe four different circumstances that can constitute conversions.

**Answer**: The four circumstances are:
1. **Acquisition of possession** -- Taking someone's property (e.g., theft)
2. **Unauthorized transfer of chattel** -- Selling or giving away someone's property
3. **Unreasonable withholding of chattel** -- Refusing to return property to its rightful owner
4. **Damaging, altering, or misusing chattel** -- Harming or fundamentally changing someone's property

---

### Q16: Is Jack throwing rubbish in his neighbor's yard a nuisance?

**Answer**: Yes. Jack's actions interfere with his neighbor's enjoyment of his or her property and constitute **private nuisance**. The neighbor can seek an injunction (court order to stop) and sue for damages.

---

### Q17: If Joe plays his music very loudly and keeps his neighbors awake, is Joe's act a nuisance?

**Answer**: Joe's act **is a nuisance** because it inflicts discomfort on his neighbors. This is private nuisance -- unreasonable interference with the neighbors' use and enjoyment of their property. UNLESS Joe can prove that his music was not unreasonably loud (which is difficult if neighbors are losing sleep). The key test is reasonableness -- persistent, loud music that prevents sleep would be considered unreasonable.

---

## Application Questions & Answers

### Q1: A child was kidnapped and later returned for a ransom. Aside from criminal charges, enumerate the grounds for a tort lawsuit the parents can file against the kidnappers.

**Answer**: The parents can file tort claims for:
- **Assault** -- The kidnapping usually involves a threat of bodily harm
- **Battery** -- The kidnapper had to physically touch the child to take them
- **False imprisonment** -- The child was unlawfully detained against their will
- **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** -- Upon both the parents AND the child
- **Trespass** -- If the kidnapper entered the parents' property wrongfully

---

### Q2: For each of the following, indicate whether a tort has occurred, identify it, and justify:

**a. A surgeon performs an unauthorized operation on an unconscious patient.**
- **Yes, this is battery.** The surgeon committed an unauthorized touching (the operation) without the patient's consent. The patient's unconsciousness does NOT justify the lack of permission -- consent should have been obtained beforehand or from a legal representative.

**b. A store manager prevents a suspected shoplifter from leaving the premises for three hours.**
- **This depends on state law.** False imprisonment is the intentional and unlawful detention of one person by another. People detained for suspected shoplifting have successfully sued for false imprisonment. However, many state statutes permit **reasonable detention for a reasonable time** (usually an hour or less). Three hours would likely be considered unreasonable in most jurisdictions, resulting in the tort of false imprisonment.

**c. A person steals a car and sells it.**
- **Yes, this is conversion.** The sale of a stolen item is the intentional and unlawful exercise of dominion or control over personal property to the detriment of the person entitled to control the property.

**d. A newspaper prints a story that says a well-known politician is suspected of bribery.**
- **The politician could sue for libel** but might not be successful because: the article says the politician is only "suspected" of bribery (not that they committed it), does not allege guilt of that crime, and appears to assert only truth. Furthermore, the politician is a **public figure** and therefore must prove the newspaper's **actual malice** (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth) -- a difficult burden of proof per Sullivan v. New York Times.

**e. Jeff files a complaint to have Scott declared incompetent to acquire control over Scott's assets.**
- **Yes, this is malicious abuse of process.** Jeff has committed the use of legal procedures for a purpose for which they were not designed. Jeff is attempting to use coercion (a competency hearing) to acquire control over Scott's assets -- an improper purpose.

---

### Q3: Jerry was laid off from an insurance agency. He has difficulty finding a job. His former supervisor has not been giving a good recommendation and has been saying he was "not effective" in his former position. She also accused him of alcohol abuse.

**a. Does Jerry have grounds to sue for defamation?**
- **Yes.** Jerry can sue the supervisor who has maligned him to others. The supervisor's assertions may be slander. However, the supervisor could use the defense that her assertions are **true**. If they're opinions about work performance, they may be protected under qualified privilege (former employer giving references), but specific factual allegations like alcohol abuse could be defamatory if false.

**b. Does Jerry have grounds to sue for interference with employment?**
- **Yes.** Jerry can sue, but his suit might not be successful if the supervisor's assertions were true. The supervisor is interfering with Jerry's ability to obtain employment by making negative (potentially false) statements to prospective employers.

**c. Does Jerry have grounds to sue for invasion of the right of privacy?**
- **Possibly**, with regard to the allegation of alcohol abuse. Jerry can sue for invasion of the right of privacy regarding private matters like alcohol use. His **work performance**, however, would not be a private matter. Therefore, if alcohol abuse affected his work performance and the supervisor can prove it, Jerry's privacy suit would likely fail. But if the alcohol allegation is unrelated to work and is a private matter, the privacy claim may succeed.

---

### Q4: Rob had a disfiguring scar removed by a plastic surgeon. Without Rob's permission, the surgeon used before-and-after photos to promote his services. On what grounds can Rob sue?

**Answer**: Rob can sue the surgeon for **invasion of privacy**, specifically:
- **Public disclosure of private facts** -- Medical information is especially private
- **Unauthorized release of confidential information** -- Medical records and images are subject to strict confidentiality rules
- **Appropriation of plaintiff's name or likeness** -- Using Rob's photos for commercial purposes without consent

Medical information is subject to particularly strict rules of confidentiality, making this a strong case.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) regulations, which have been strengthened since 2008, provide additional protections and penalties for unauthorized disclosure of medical information.

### Q5: Mikey and Jimmy (both seven years old) were trading comic books. Jimmy's father told Jimmy to try to get Mikey's "Captain Flyer" because he knew it was valuable. Neither child knew the true value. Mikey traded it for Jimmy's "Gray Lynx." Mikey's mother discovered the true value. Can she sue? On what grounds?

**Answer**: Mikey's mother could sue for **fraud** because Jimmy's father did the following:
- **Deliberately concealed (falsely represented)** -- Hid the true value of the comic book
- **Material information** -- The value of the comic was important to the transaction
- **Knowingly** -- Jimmy's father knew the true value
- **With intent to influence or deceive** -- He instructed Jimmy to get the comic specifically because of its value
- **Resulting in Mikey's and his parents' reasonable reliance** -- They relied on the apparent fairness of a child's trade
- **To their detriment** -- They lost a valuable comic book

**All six elements of fraud are present**, making this a viable lawsuit.

## Exam Tips

### Most Likely Test Points

1. **Battery vs. Assault Distinction** -- This will DEFINITELY be tested. Remember: battery = touching, assault = fear of touching. You can have one without the other.

2. **Slander Per Se Categories** -- Memorize the four categories where damages are presumed: (1) accusing of a serious crime, (2) loathsome disease, (3) harm to business/profession, (4) unchastity.

3. **Six Elements of Fraud** -- Know all six cold. A common exam question will present a scenario and ask if all elements are met. The mnemonic: **F-M-K-I-R-D** (False representation, Material fact, Knowingly made, Intent to deceive, Reasonable reliance, Detriment).

4. **Sullivan v. New York Times** -- Public figures must prove "actual malice" (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth). This is a higher standard than for private individuals.

5. **Malicious Prosecution vs. Malicious Abuse of Process** -- Know the difference. Prosecution = bad case brought without probable cause. Abuse of Process = good process used for a bad purpose.

6. **Six Types of Invasion of Privacy** -- Be able to identify which type applies in a scenario. Pay special attention to appropriation of likeness and unauthorized release of confidential information (insurance-relevant).

7. **Four Types of Conversion** -- Acquisition, unauthorized transfer, unreasonable withholding, damaging/altering.

8. **Private vs. Public Nuisance** -- Private affects a specific person, public affects the general community.

9. **Defenses to Defamation** -- Truth (absolute), retraction, absolute privilege, conditional/qualified privilege.

10. **Bad Faith in Insurance** -- Insurer's implied duty to act fairly; can result in punitive damages.

### Common Exam Traps

- **Trap**: Confusing battery with assault. Battery does NOT require the victim to be aware of the touching (e.g., someone hit from behind). Assault REQUIRES awareness/apprehension.

- **Trap**: Thinking defamation requires the statement to be published in a newspaper. "Publication" in legal terms just means communicating it to ONE third party.

- **Trap**: Forgetting that truth is an ABSOLUTE defense to defamation. If the statement is true, it cannot be defamation no matter how damaging.

- **Trap**: Confusing conversion with trespass to personal property. Conversion involves exercising DOMINION (control/ownership) over property. Trespass is a lesser interference.

- **Trap**: Thinking all nuisances must be intentional. Nuisances can also result from negligent or reckless conduct.

- **Trap**: Forgetting that fraud requires ALL SIX elements. If even one element is missing, there is no fraud.

- **Trap**: Thinking private citizens can arrest anyone for any crime. For misdemeanors, they can ONLY arrest for breaches of the peace.

## Quick-Reference Summary

### Intentional Physical Torts Against Persons
- **Battery** = Unauthorized harmful/offensive touching (no injury needed)
- **Assault** = Causing fear of immediate harmful contact (no touching needed)
- **False Imprisonment** = Unlawful confinement against someone's will
- **False Arrest** = Detention under false claim of legal authority
- **Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress** = Extreme/outrageous conduct causing severe emotional harm

### Defamation
- **Slander** = Spoken false statements harming reputation
- **Libel** = Written/published false statements harming reputation
- **Per Se** = Damages presumed (no proof of specific loss needed)
- **Sullivan v. NYT** = Public figures must prove actual malice
- **Defenses**: Truth, retraction, absolute privilege, conditional privilege

### Invasion of Privacy (6 Types)
- Intrusion on solitude/seclusion
- Physical invasion
- Public disclosure of private facts
- Publicity placing plaintiff in false light
- Unauthorized release of confidential info
- Appropriation of name/likeness

### Fraud (6 Elements -- ALL Required)
- False representation + Material fact + Knowingly made + Intent to deceive + Reasonable reliance + Detriment

### Bad Faith (Insurance)
- Insurer fails implied duty to act fairly
- Can result in compensatory AND punitive damages

### Interference Torts
- **Injurious falsehood/trade libel** = False statements about products/business
- **Malicious interference** = Deliberately disrupting business relationships
- **Interference with employment** = Getting someone fired or blocking hiring
- **Unfair competition** = Dishonest business practices

### Misuse of Legal Process
- **Malicious prosecution** = Bringing a case without probable cause + with malice
- **Malicious abuse of process** = Using legal procedures for improper purposes

### Torts Against Property
- **Trespass (real property)** = Unauthorized entry on land
- **Trespass (personal property)** = Unauthorized interference with chattels
- **Conversion** = Exercising dominion over another's property (4 types: acquire, transfer, withhold, damage)
- **Private nuisance** = Unreasonable interference with specific person's property use
- **Public nuisance** = Unreasonable interference with general public's rights
- **Nuisance per se** = Activity that is always a nuisance

### Key Defenses by Tort
- **Assault/Battery**: Consent, self-defense, defense of others, reasonable discipline, good faith
- **Defamation**: Truth (absolute), retraction, absolute privilege, qualified privilege
- **Privacy**: Prior publication, consent, public figure, news event, ordinary sensibility, judicial proceedings, public interest
- **Malicious Prosecution**: Probable cause
- **Bad Faith**: Insurer acted reasonably, genuine coverage dispute
