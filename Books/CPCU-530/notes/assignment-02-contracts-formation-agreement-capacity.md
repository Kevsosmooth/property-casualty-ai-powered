## Section-by-Section Breakdown

### 1. CONTRACT FORMATION - The Four Elements

A **contract** is a legally enforceable promise. For any contract to be valid and enforceable, it must have ALL four of these elements:

1. **Agreement** (offer + acceptance)
2. **Capacity to contract** (the parties must be legally qualified)
3. **Consideration** (something of value exchanged)
4. **Legal purpose** (the contract cannot be for something illegal)

**Plain English:** Think of these four elements as legs on a table. Remove any one leg, and the table (contract) falls. Every single enforceable contract -- whether it is an insurance policy, a car purchase, or a handshake deal -- must satisfy all four.

**Key parties in a contract:**
- **Promisor** - the party making the promise
- **Promisee** - the party to whom the promise is made
- **Third-party beneficiary** - someone who is NOT a party to the contract but has a legal right to enforce it if breached (example: your child named as beneficiary on your life insurance policy)

**Privity of contract** - the connection or relationship between the parties to a contract because they have mutual interests. Ordinarily, only parties in privity can sue for breach. The exception is a **third-party beneficiary**, who CAN sue even though they are not in privity.

**Breach of contract** - the failure of a party to a contract, without legal excuse, to perform all or part of the contract.

### 2. TYPES OF CONTRACTS

#### A. Bilateral vs. Unilateral Contracts

- **Bilateral contract** - A contract in which EACH party promises a performance. Both sides make promises to each other.
  - **Example:** You agree to buy a house, and the seller agrees to sell it to you. Both parties have made promises -- you promised to pay, they promised to deliver the deed.

- **Unilateral contract** - A contract in which ONLY ONE party makes a promise or undertakes the requested performance.
  - **Example:** "I will pay $500 to whoever finds my lost dog." Only you (the offeror) have made a promise. The person who finds the dog is not obligated to look -- but if they do find it, you must pay. Insurance policies are classic unilateral contracts: the insurer promises to pay if a covered loss occurs, but the insured does not promise that a loss will occur.

#### B. Executed vs. Executory Contracts

- **Executed contract** - A contract that has been COMPLETELY PERFORMED by both parties. Everything promised has been done.
  - **Example:** You paid the mechanic $200, and the mechanic already fixed your car. Both sides fully performed. Done.

- **Executory contract** - A contract that has NOT been completely performed by one or both parties. There is still something left to do.
  - **Example:** You signed a 12-month apartment lease and have only lived there 3 months. The contract is still executory because 9 months of performance remain.

**Key distinction:** A contract can be partly executed and partly executory. If you paid for a car but have not received it yet, the contract is executed on your side (you paid) but executory on the dealer's side (they have not delivered).

#### C. Express vs. Implied Contracts

- **Express contract** - A contract that contains BOTH the terms and the parties' intentions, either in writing or orally. The parties have clearly stated what they agree to.
  - **Example:** A signed, written insurance policy. Or even a verbal agreement: "I will paint your house for $3,000 by Friday."

- **Implied-in-fact contract** - A contract that is NOT express but that the parties presumably intended, either by tacit understanding or by the assumption that it existed. The words were never spoken or written, but the parties' behavior shows they had a deal.
  - **Example:** You sit down at a restaurant and order food. Nobody signs a contract, but it is understood you will pay for the meal. Your behavior (ordering) and the restaurant's behavior (serving) create an implied-in-fact contract.

- **Implied-in-law contract** (also called **quasi-contract**) - NOT an actual contract at all. It is an obligation imposed by law because of the parties' conduct, their special relationship, or because one of them would otherwise be unjustly enriched.
  - **Example:** An unconscious person is rushed to the ER. They never agreed to pay, but the law implies a contract so the hospital can recover the reasonable value of its services. Without this, the patient would be unjustly enriched (free medical care).

**Critical distinction for the exam:** An implied-in-FACT contract is still a real contract based on the parties' behavior. An implied-in-LAW contract is a legal fiction created by courts to prevent injustice -- there is no actual agreement.

#### D. Voidable vs. Void Contracts

- **Voidable contract** - A contract that one of the parties CAN reject (avoid) based on some circumstances surrounding its execution. Until it is avoided, it remains valid.
  - **Example:** A minor signs a contract to buy a car. The minor can choose to honor it OR disaffirm (cancel) it. The contract is voidable at the minor's option.

- **Void contract** - An agreement that, despite the parties' intentions, NEVER reaches contract status. It is not legally enforceable or binding. It was never a real contract.
  - **Example:** A contract to commit a crime (e.g., a "contract" to have someone robbed). This is void from the start -- a court will never enforce it.

**Exam trap:** Voidable means "valid UNLESS someone chooses to cancel it." Void means "was NEVER valid in the first place." A voidable contract can be ratified (confirmed) and become fully binding. A void contract can never become valid.

### 3. FIRST ELEMENT: AGREEMENT (Offer + Acceptance)

An **agreement** consists of one party's offer and another party's acceptance of that offer. These are the two building blocks.

#### A. Offer

An **offer** is a promise that requires some action by the intended recipient to make an agreement. It is the first step in forming a contract.

**Three requirements of a valid offer:**

**1. Intent to Contract**
- The offeror must have a genuine, objective intention to enter a contract.
- Courts use the **objective test**: Would a reasonable person believe this was a serious offer? It does not matter what the offeror secretly intended -- what matters is how a reasonable person would interpret the words and actions.
- **NOT offers:** Jokes, expressions of opinion, statements made in anger or excitement, preliminary negotiations, and advertisements (generally).
- **Advertisements are generally NOT offers** -- they are invitations to negotiate or to make offers. The store is inviting you to come in and make an offer to buy, which the store can then accept or reject.
  - **Exception:** An ad CAN be an offer if it is very specific and leaves nothing to negotiate (e.g., "First person to arrive at our store at 9am Saturday gets this fur coat for $1").

**2. Definite Terms**
- The offer must have **reasonably certain** terms so a court can determine what was agreed upon and provide a remedy if there is a breach.
- A court asks: Are the terms clear enough to provide a basis for a remedy if default occurs?
- Vague or unclear terms = no enforceable offer.

**3. Communication to Offeree**
- The offer must actually be communicated to the offeree. You cannot accept an offer you do not know about.
- **Example:** If you find someone's lost dog without knowing they offered a reward, you cannot claim the reward. The offer was never communicated to you.

**Duration and Termination of Offers -- Five Factors:**

An offer does not last forever. It can end in five ways:

1. **Lapse of time** - If the offeror sets a deadline, the offer expires when that deadline passes. If no deadline is set, the offer expires after a "reasonable time" (depends on the circumstances).

2. **Operation of law** - The offer terminates automatically if:
   - The subject matter is destroyed (the house you offered to sell burns down)
   - One of the parties dies or becomes insane
   - A new law makes the contract illegal

3. **Offeree's rejection** - Once the offeree says "no," the offer is dead. The offeree cannot come back later and say "Actually, I accept."

4. **Counteroffers** - A **counteroffer** is a proposal that varies in some material way from the original offer. It simultaneously REJECTS the original offer AND creates a new offer.
   - **Example:** "I will sell you my car for $10,000." "I will give you $8,000." This counteroffer killed the original $10,000 offer. The seller cannot now accept $10,000 from this buyer unless making a new offer.

5. **Offeror's revocation** - The offeror can generally revoke (take back) the offer at any time BEFORE acceptance. Exception: an **option contract**, which is an agreement to keep an offer open for a stated period, supported by consideration (the offeree pays something to keep the offer open).

**Option contract** - An agreement to keep an offer open for a stated period, supported by consideration. Once you have an option contract, the offeror CANNOT revoke during the option period.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example (Option Contract)**:
- **The Setup**: A commercial property owner wants to buy the vacant lot next door to expand her parking. The lot owner offers to sell for $200,000.
- **What Happens**: The property owner is not ready to commit but wants time to secure financing. She pays the lot owner $5,000 for a 90-day option contract -- the lot owner promises to keep the offer open for 90 days at $200,000.
- **The Result**: On day 45, the lot owner gets an offer of $250,000 from someone else. Too bad -- the option contract is binding. The lot owner CANNOT revoke the $200,000 offer during the 90-day period. The $5,000 is the consideration that makes this enforceable (unlike a regular offer, which can be revoked anytime).

#### B. Acceptance

**Acceptance** is the second step of an agreement, occurring when an offeree agrees to a proposal or does what the offeror proposed.

**Three requirements of a valid acceptance:**

**1. Acceptance by Offeree**
- Only the person to whom the offer was made (the offeree) can accept. If the offer is made to a specific person, only that person can accept. If it is made to the general public (like a reward), anyone who performs can accept.

**2. Unconditional and Unequivocal Acceptance**
- The acceptance must be a clear, unqualified "yes" -- it must match the offer exactly (the **mirror image rule**).
- If the offeree adds conditions or changes terms, that is a counteroffer, NOT an acceptance.
- **Substantial performance** - the performance of the primary, necessary terms of an agreement. In some cases, substantial (though not perfect) performance can satisfy the acceptance requirement.

**UCC Exception (Battle of the Forms)**: Under UCC 2-207, an acceptance that includes additional or different terms can STILL be a valid acceptance (not a counteroffer) for sales of goods. This is a major departure from the common law mirror image rule. Between merchants, additional terms become part of the contract unless they materially alter it, the offer expressly limits acceptance to its terms, or the offeror objects within a reasonable time.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example (Battle of the Forms)**:
- **The Setup**: An insurance company orders 500 office chairs from a furniture supplier. The purchase order says "Payment due in 30 days."
- **What Happens**: The supplier sends back a confirmation form that says "Payment due in 15 days" and adds "All disputes subject to arbitration in Chicago." These are additional/different terms.
- **The Result**: Under **common law**, the supplier's response would be a counteroffer (no contract yet). Under **UCC 2-207** (which applies because chairs are goods), the supplier's response IS a valid acceptance -- a contract exists. Since both parties are merchants, the additional terms (15-day payment, arbitration clause) become part of the contract UNLESS they materially alter the deal, the original offer limited acceptance to its exact terms, or the buyer objects within a reasonable time. The arbitration clause likely materially alters the contract and would NOT automatically become part of it.

**3. Offeree's Communication of Acceptance**
- The offeree must communicate acceptance to the offeror by appropriate word or act.
- **The Mailbox Rule:** When acceptance is sent by an authorized method (like mail, if the offer was mailed), the acceptance is effective when SENT (dropped in the mailbox), not when received. This is a huge exam point.
- **Silence is generally NOT acceptance.** An offeror cannot force someone into a contract by saying "If I don't hear from you by Friday, I will assume you accept." The offeree's silence does not create a contract.

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The traditional mailbox rule was designed for postal mail. Modern contract formation increasingly occurs electronically. The **E-SIGN Act (2000)** and **Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA)** validate electronic signatures and contracts. Click-wrap agreements (clicking "I agree") and browse-wrap agreements are now common forms of acceptance in e-commerce.

**Forbearance** - the act of giving up, or the promise to give up, a legal right. This can serve as consideration or as a form of acceptance in some contracts.

### 4. SECOND ELEMENT: CAPACITY TO CONTRACT

**Capacity to contract** is a legal qualification that determines one's ability to enter an enforceable contract.

A **competent party** is a party to a contract who has the basic or minimal ability to do something -- and the mental ability to understand problems and make decisions.

**Who might lack capacity?**
- Minors
- Insane persons
- Intoxicated persons
- Artificial entities (corporations) acting outside their authority

#### A. Minors' Contracts

- The law protects minors from disposing of their property while underage.
- A minor CAN assert minority as a defense against liability in contracts, **EXCEPT** for contracts involving the purchase of **necessaries** (food, clothing, shelter, medical care, basic transportation to work).
- Minor's contracts are **voidable** (not void) -- the minor can choose to honor OR disaffirm the contract.
- **Disaffirmance:** A minor can disaffirm a contract at any time during minority or within a reasonable time after reaching the age of majority.
- **Ratification:** After reaching majority age, the former minor can ratify (confirm) the contract, making it fully binding.
- **Necessaries exception:** Even if a minor disaffirms a contract for necessaries, the minor must still pay the **reasonable value** of the necessaries received (not necessarily the contract price).

**Parents' liability:** Parents are generally NOT liable for their minor children's contracts UNLESS:
- A parent cosigns the contract
- The child acted on the parent's behalf or at the parent's direction
- The parent neglected or refused to pay for necessaries for the minor

**Restitution** - the act of making an injured party whole again. When a minor disaffirms, restitution may be required (returning whatever was received under the contract).

#### B. Insane Persons' Contracts

- Any agreement entered by a person **adjudged by a court to be insane** is **VOID** (not just voidable) -- except for contracts for necessaries.
- A contract by a person who **claims to be insane but has NOT been adjudged insane by a court** is **VOIDABLE** under certain conditions.
- The distinction matters: Court-declared insanity = void. Self-claimed insanity without court declaration = voidable.

#### C. Intoxicated Persons' Contracts

- An intoxicated person can avoid a contract IF the person **did not know a contract was being formed** OR **did not understand the legal consequences** of the acts purporting to form the contract.
- The level of intoxication must be so severe that the person lacked understanding of what was happening.
- Mere impairment of judgment is generally NOT enough -- the person must have been essentially incapacitated.

**Example -- Intoxicated Person Avoids a Contract**:
- **The Setup**: Dave goes to a bar and gets extremely drunk. A car salesman at the next table gets Dave to sign a purchase agreement for a $50,000 car.
- **What Happens**: The next morning, Dave has no memory of signing anything.
- **The Result**: Dave can likely avoid the contract because he was so intoxicated he did not understand he was entering into a contract. However, if Dave had only had a couple of drinks and was just "feeling good," the contract would likely stand.

#### D. Artificial Entities' Contracts (Corporations)

- Corporations have the capacity to contract, but their capacity is limited by their **charter** (articles of incorporation) and applicable state laws.
- A corporation acting beyond the scope of its charter may be acting **ultra vires** (beyond its powers), which can affect the enforceability of the contract.

#### E. Insurance Contracts -- Special Capacity Rules

**1. Insurer's Capacity**
- An insurer must be **licensed** in the state where it does business.
- Key rule: Policies sold by insurers NOT licensed to do business in a state are **enforceable against the insurer** (the insurer cannot avoid the contract just because it was unlicensed). The insurer bears the consequences of operating without a license, not the policyholder.
- This rule protects consumers: the unlicensed insurer cannot collect premiums and then refuse to pay claims by saying "We were not licensed."

**2. Insurance Producer's Capacity**
- Producers (agents/brokers) must be properly licensed and authorized to bind coverage.
- An agent's authority to bind the insurer can be actual (explicitly granted), implied (reasonably necessary to carry out actual authority), or apparent (the insurer's actions led the insured to reasonably believe the agent had authority).

**3. Insured's Capacity**
- Generally follows the same rules as other contracts (minors, insane persons, intoxicated persons).
- Most states allow minors above a certain age (often 14 or 15) to enter insurance contracts that cannot be disaffirmed.

## Review Questions & Answers

**1. List the elements of a contract. (p. 2.3)**

The four elements of a contract are:
- Agreement (offer + acceptance)
- Capacity to contract
- Consideration
- Legal purpose

All four must be present for a contract to be legally enforceable.

---

**2. Can a party that is not in privity of contract ever sue for breach of the contract? (p. 2.4)**

Ordinarily, a party cannot sue for breach of contract without being in privity of contract. The **exception** is a **third-party beneficiary** -- a person who is not a party to the contract but who benefits from it and has a legal right to enforce it.

---

**3. Are implied contracts actual agreements between the parties? Explain. (p. 2.6)**

Yes, implied contracts are agreements that are intended by parties, even though the parties might not have expressed their agreements explicitly. In an **implied-in-fact** contract, the parties' behavior demonstrates their agreement (like ordering food at a restaurant). An **implied-in-law** contract is somewhat different -- it is not a true agreement but an obligation imposed by law to prevent unjust enrichment.

---

**4. List the elements of an offer. (p. 2.8)**

The three elements of a valid offer are:
- **Intent to contract** (objective test -- would a reasonable person believe this was serious?)
- **Definite terms** (clear enough for a court to determine what was agreed and provide a remedy)
- **Communication to offeree** (the offer must reach the intended recipient)

---

**5. Are advertisements offers? Explain. (p. 2.9)**

**No**, advertisements are generally NOT offers. They are invitations to negotiate or to make offers, expressing no present intent to contract. Exception: An ad with very specific terms leaving nothing to negotiate may constitute an offer.

---

**6. How does a court determine reasonable certainty to determine whether an offer exists? (p. 2.10)**

To determine reasonable certainty, a court may ask whether an offer's terms are clear enough to **provide a basis for a remedy if default occurs**. If the terms are so vague that a court cannot determine what was promised or what damages would result from a breach, there is no enforceable offer.

---

**7. List five factors regarding duration and termination that are key to determining whether an offer is binding. (pp. 2.11-2.12)**

The five factors are:
1. **Lapse of time** - offer expires after stated deadline or reasonable time
2. **Operation of law** - automatic termination (destruction of subject matter, death, illegality)
3. **Offeree's rejection** - once rejected, the offer is dead
4. **Counteroffers** - a counteroffer kills the original offer and creates a new one
5. **Offeror's revocation** - offeror can withdraw the offer before acceptance (unless option contract)

---

**8. List the elements of acceptance. (p. 2.14)**

The three elements of acceptance are:
- The acceptance must be made **by the offeree**
- The acceptance must be **unconditional and unequivocal**
- The offeree must **communicate the acceptance** to the offeror by appropriate word or act

---

**9. Can silence ever be acceptance? (p. 2.16)**

**No**, an offeree's silence cannot be acceptance. An offeror cannot force a contract on someone by stating that silence will be deemed acceptance.

---

**10. Identify parties that might be incompetent to contract. (p. 2.19)**

Parties that might be incompetent to contract include:
- **Minors**
- **Insane persons**
- **Intoxicated persons**
- **Artificial entities** (corporations acting outside their authority)

---

**11. What are minors' rights and duties under their contracts? (pp. 2.20-2.21)**

The law protects minors from disposing of their property while underage. Generally, a minor can assert minority as a defense against liability in contracts, **except** those involving the purchase of necessaries. A minor's contracts are **voidable** -- the minor can choose to disaffirm (cancel) OR ratify the contract. If a minor disaffirms a contract for necessaries, the minor must still pay the reasonable value of what was received.

---

**12. What are the rights and duties of parents under minors' contracts? (p. 2.22)**

Parents are generally **NOT liable** for minor children's contracts UNLESS:
- A parent **cosigns** the contract
- The child acted on the **parent's behalf** or at the parent's direction
- A parent has **neglected or refused to pay** for necessaries for the minor

---

**13. What are insane persons' rights and duties under contracts? (pp. 2.22-2.23)**

- A contract entered by a person **adjudged by a court** to be insane is **VOID** (except for necessaries).
- A contract by a person who **claims to be insane** but has NOT been adjudged so by a court is **VOIDABLE** under certain conditions.
- Either way, insane persons remain liable for the reasonable value of necessaries received.

---

**14. Under what circumstances will intoxication allow a party to avoid a contract? (p. 2.24)**

An intoxicated person can avoid a contract if the person:
- **Did not know** a contract was being formed, OR
- **Did not understand** the legal consequences of the acts purporting to form the contract

The intoxication must be severe enough to deprive the person of understanding what was happening, not merely impair judgment.

---

**15. Can an insurer avoid a contract entered into in a state in which the insurer is unlicensed? (pp. 2.25-2.26)**

**No.** Policies sold by insurers not licensed to do business in a state are **enforceable against the insurer**. The insurer cannot use its own failure to obtain a license as a defense to avoid paying claims. This protects policyholders who relied on the insurer in good faith.

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Question 1

**Facts:** Alex was a young high school student whose parents died in an automobile accident. He was left penniless and without means of support. A local merchant invited Alex to live with him and his family, with room, board, clothing, and all other necessities provided. Alex worked around the house and also worked after school in the merchant's store. Two years later, after Alex's graduation from high school, the merchant died, and his family told Alex to move out. Alex then entered a claim against the merchant's estate for the reasonable value of services rendered.

**a. Express contract:** Nothing indicates an express contract existed. There were no oral or written declarations of terms.

**b. Implied-in-fact contract:** Most likely theory. An implied-in-fact contract arises when parties express assent through behavior, not words. A court could imply such a contract, though the estate could argue room and board was sufficient pay.

**c. Implied-in-law contract:** Possible but weaker. The law can impose contractual obligations based on relationships or unusual circumstances. However, since Alex did receive free room and board, equity may not require additional payment.

---

### Application Question 2

**Facts:** Daniel signed a contract to purchase real estate from Patricia and mailed it. Patricia received it January 15, signed it, and mailed it back. Later that same day, Patricia called Daniel to repudiate. Daniel received the signed contract January 17.

**Answer:** **Yes, valid and binding contract.** Under the **mailbox rule**, Patricia's acceptance was effective when she put it in the mail on January 15. Her phone repudiation came AFTER acceptance was already effective. Too late.

---

### Application Question 3

**Facts:** Oliver offered his car to Tom for $1,000, saying "call me tonight." Tom didn't call. Next day, a colleague offered $1,500 and Oliver accepted. That night Tom came to accept.

**Answer:** **Oliver is NOT bound to Tom.** The offer lapsed when Tom failed to respond by the implied deadline ("tonight"). Oliver was free to sell to his colleague.

---

### Application Question 4

**Facts:** Sue, a minor, bought a car from Fred for $1,200, misrepresenting her age. After reaching legal age, the car was destroyed. Sue wants to disaffirm. Fred wants parents to pay.

**Answer:** Sue's minority makes the contract **voidable** at her option. Even though she misrepresented her age, minority is generally still a valid defense. Parents are **not liable** unless they cosigned or directed the purchase. Even if the car was a necessity, the facts don't show parental neglect. Fred likely has no recourse.

## Exam Tips -- Common Traps and Memorization Points

### Trap 1: Void vs. Voidable -- Know the Difference Cold
- **Void** = never was a contract, cannot be ratified
- **Voidable** = IS a valid contract UNTIL the protected party cancels it
- Court-adjudged insane person's contract = VOID
- Minor's contract = VOIDABLE

### Trap 2: The Mailbox Rule
- Acceptance by authorized method is effective when SENT, not when received
- Revocation is effective when RECEIVED, not when sent
- This asymmetry is heavily tested

### Trap 3: Advertisements Are NOT Offers
- General ads are invitations to negotiate
- Exception: extremely specific terms with nothing left to negotiate

### Trap 4: Counteroffers Kill the Original Offer
- A counteroffer = REJECTION of original + NEW offer
- Once you counteroffer, you cannot go back and accept the original

### Trap 5: Silence is NOT Acceptance
- An offeror CANNOT impose a duty to respond

### Trap 6: Unlicensed Insurer Rule
- An unlicensed insurer CANNOT avoid its contracts
- Policies are enforceable AGAINST the insurer

### Trap 7: Minors and Necessaries
- Minors CAN disaffirm non-necessaries contracts
- Minors owe reasonable value for necessaries received
- Parents are NOT automatically liable

### Trap 8: Insanity -- Court-Adjudged vs. Claimed
- Court-adjudged = VOID
- Claims insanity without court ruling = VOIDABLE

### Key Numbers to Memorize
- **4** elements of a contract (Agreement, Capacity, Consideration, Legal Purpose)
- **3** elements of an offer (Intent, Definite Terms, Communication)
- **3** elements of acceptance (By offeree, Unconditional/Unequivocal, Communicated)
- **5** ways an offer terminates (Lapse, Operation of law, Rejection, Counteroffer, Revocation)
- **4** types of potentially incompetent parties (Minors, Insane, Intoxicated, Artificial entities)

## Quick-Reference Summary

**What is a contract?** A legally enforceable promise requiring: Agreement + Capacity + Consideration + Legal Purpose.

**Bilateral vs. Unilateral:** Bilateral = both sides promise. Unilateral = only one side promises (insurance policies are unilateral).

**Executed vs. Executory:** Executed = fully performed. Executory = still has obligations outstanding.

**Express vs. Implied:** Express = stated in words. Implied-in-fact = shown by conduct. Implied-in-law = imposed by courts to prevent unjust enrichment.

**Void vs. Voidable:** Void = never a contract. Voidable = valid unless the protected party cancels.

**Offer requirements:** Intent + Definite Terms + Communication to offeree.

**Offer dies by:** Lapse of time, operation of law, rejection, counteroffer, or revocation.

**Acceptance requirements:** By offeree + Unconditional + Communicated. Mailbox rule: effective when sent.

**Minors:** Contracts are voidable. Exception: necessaries (owe reasonable value). Parents generally not liable.

**Insane persons:** Court-adjudged = void. Not adjudged = voidable. Exception: necessaries.

**Intoxicated persons:** Voidable if person did not know or understand what was happening.

**Unlicensed insurers:** Cannot avoid their own contracts. Policies are enforceable against them.
