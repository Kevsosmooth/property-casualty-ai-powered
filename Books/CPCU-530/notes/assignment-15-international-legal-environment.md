### 1. International Business

#### A. Foreign Trade -- Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage

**Absolute advantage** means a country can produce a good or service more efficiently than other countries. Plain English: "We make it better and cheaper than anyone else."

**Comparative advantage** occurs when trading partners gain from trading with each other, even when they are themselves more efficient in production of the traded goods. Plain English: "Even if Country A is better at making BOTH cars and wine, it still makes sense for Country A to focus on cars and let Country B make the wine, because the relative efficiency gains benefit both."

> **Setup:** Japan is extremely efficient at making both electronics and rice. Thailand is less efficient at both, but Thailand is relatively much better at growing rice than making electronics.
> **What Happens:** Japan focuses on electronics, Thailand focuses on rice, and they trade.
> **Result:** Both countries benefit -- Japan gets cheaper rice, Thailand gets better electronics. This is comparative advantage in action.

**U.S. Trade in the World Context:** The textbook discusses the licensing of products between different countries, noting three reasons it occurs:
1. It is not economically viable to sell a product in the second country because of labor costs, transportation costs, or regulations
2. A company does not have the time or resources to produce the product in another country
3. A company might lack sufficient knowledge about the country's legal, political, social, and business environments

#### B. Foreign Contractual Relationships

**Product licensing** -- When a company in one country grants permission to a company in another country to make or sell its product. This happens when it is not practical for the original company to operate there directly.

**Franchising** -- When one company assigns to another the right to supply its products within a market. Think McDonald's opening in Moscow -- a local operator runs the restaurant using McDonald's brand, recipes, and systems.

> **Setup:** A U.S. insurance training company wants to expand to Brazil.
> **What Happens:** Rather than opening offices directly, it licenses a Brazilian education firm to use its curriculum and brand name.
> **Result:** The Brazilian firm handles local regulations, language translation, and marketing. The U.S. company earns licensing fees without the risk and expense of direct operations.

#### C. Foreign Direct Investments

**Foreign direct investment** -- When a company invests directly in facilities, operations, or assets in another country. This is the most committed (and riskiest) way to go international.

**Foreign portfolio investment** -- Buying stocks or bonds of foreign companies without taking a controlling interest. Less risk, less control.

**Subsidiary** -- A company owned or controlled by another company (the parent) in a foreign country. A **fully owned subsidiary** provides the highest level of control over operations but also presents the highest level of business risk, commitment of capital, and managerial control.

**Joint venture** -- Two or more companies team up to do business together in a foreign market. Shared ownership, shared control, shared risk.

> **Setup:** A U.S. insurer wants to enter the Indian market.
> **What Happens (Option A -- Subsidiary):** It creates a wholly-owned subsidiary in India. Full control, but must navigate Indian insurance regulations alone, invest heavily, and bear all the risk.
> **What Happens (Option B -- Joint Venture):** It partners with an established Indian insurer. Shared control and profits, but gains local expertise, regulatory knowledge, and existing distribution channels.
> **Result:** The subsidiary offers more control but higher risk. The joint venture offers local expertise but shared profits and potential conflicts with the partner.

#### Types of International Companies

**Resource seekers** -- Companies that enter a foreign market to access that country's resources (natural resources, cheap labor, etc.)

**Market seekers** -- Companies that seek new markets outside their own country's boundaries (selling products/services to new customers abroad)

**Market followers** -- Companies that follow their existing customers into foreign countries (e.g., an insurer that expands abroad because its major commercial clients are expanding there)

#### D. Challenges and Barriers

Companies engaging in international business face these challenges:

1. **Language barrier** -- Misunderstandings in contracts, regulations, negotiations
2. **Cultural differences** -- Business practices, negotiation styles, relationship-building norms vary widely
3. **Time differences** -- Coordinating across time zones creates delays
4. **Physical distance and space** -- Between countries creates logistical challenges
5. **Types of governments** -- Different political systems create different regulatory environments and risk levels

#### Types of Governments (Important for Risk Assessment)

- **Democratic rule** -- Government elected by the people
  - **Nonparty democracy** -- No formal political parties
  - **Parliamentary democracy** -- Legislature selects the executive (e.g., UK, Canada)
  - **Presidential democracy** -- Separate election of executive (e.g., U.S.)
  - **Multiparty democracy** -- Multiple political parties compete (most European countries)
- **Junta** -- Military government that has taken power by force
- **Martial law** -- Military rule imposed during emergencies
- **Monarchy** -- Rule by a king/queen
  - **Absolute monarchy** -- Monarch has unlimited power
  - **Constitutional monarchy** -- Monarch's power limited by constitution (e.g., UK, Japan)
- **Single-party government** -- One party controls everything (e.g., China, Cuba)
- **Theocratic government** -- Religious leaders control government (e.g., Iran)
- **Transitional government** -- Country in the process of changing from one system to another

> **Why This Matters for Insurance:** The type of government directly affects:
> - Whether foreign insurers can operate there at all
> - How contracts are enforced
> - Risk of expropriation (government seizure of assets)
> - Regulatory stability and predictability
> - Repatriation of earnings (can you get your profits out?)

### 2. Legal Systems

The two major legal systems in the majority of countries are the **civil-law system** and the **common-law system**.

#### A. Civil Law System

Civil law is based on comprehensive written legal codes (statutes). Judges apply the code to facts -- they do NOT create law through their decisions. There is no binding precedent from prior cases.

**Three stages of a typical civil-law case:**
1. **Preliminary stage** -- Filing of claims, initial review
2. **Evidence stage** -- Gathering and presenting evidence
3. **Decision stage** -- Judge applies the code to reach a decision

**Three major sub-types of civil law:**

- **Roman-French Law** -- Based on the Napoleonic Code. Used in France, Belgium, Spain, Latin America, parts of Africa. The most widespread civil law system.
- **German Law** -- Based on the German Civil Code (BGB). Used in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Japan (partly), South Korea, Taiwan. More systematic and academic than Roman-French.
- **Scandinavian (Nordic) Law** -- Used in Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, Sweden. A blend -- not purely Roman or German, with some customary law elements.

#### B. Common Law System

In the common-law system, a judge interprets the facts of a case, examines precedents, and makes a decision based on the facts in the current case. Prior court decisions (precedent) are binding on lower courts.

Used in: United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, India, and many former British colonies.

**Key difference from civil law:** In common law, judges effectively "make law" through their decisions. In civil law, judges only apply existing written codes.

> **Setup:** A contract dispute arises in France (civil law) and a similar dispute arises in England (common law).
> **What Happens in France:** The judge looks at the French Civil Code, finds the relevant articles about contracts, and applies them to the facts. Previous court decisions are persuasive but NOT binding.
> **What Happens in England:** The judge looks at prior court decisions involving similar contract disputes (precedent) and follows the reasoning of those prior decisions. If no precedent exists, the judge creates new law.
> **Result:** Same dispute could be resolved differently depending on the legal system.

#### C. Far Eastern Law

Emphasizes mediation and social harmony over adversarial litigation. Formal law exists but is seen as a last resort. Relationships and informal resolution are preferred. Used primarily in China, Japan (blended with German civil law), and Southeast Asia.

#### D. Hindu Law

Based on religious texts and traditions of Hinduism. Applies primarily to personal matters (marriage, inheritance, adoption) in India and Nepal. India's commercial law follows the common-law tradition from British colonial rule.

#### E. Islamic Law (Sharia)

Based on the Quran and other Islamic religious texts. Governs personal conduct, business transactions, and finance. Important for insurance because:
- Traditional insurance may be considered *haram* (forbidden) under strict Islamic law
- **Takaful** is the Islamic alternative to conventional insurance (cooperative risk-sharing)
- Islamic finance prohibits *riba* (interest), which affects investment and premium structures

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Islamic finance and Takaful have grown enormously since 2008. The global Takaful market is now worth tens of billions of dollars and is a major segment of the international insurance industry.]

#### F. Socialist-Communist Law

Based on Marxist-Leninist ideology. The state owns the means of production. Private insurance may be limited or nonexistent -- the state provides all coverage. Used in Cuba, North Korea, and (historically) the Soviet bloc.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Since 2008, China and Vietnam have significantly liberalized their insurance markets despite maintaining communist political systems. China is now one of the world's largest insurance markets.]

### 3. International Law

**International law** governs the relationships between nations and between individuals/corporations across national borders.

**Public international law** -- Concerns the interrelation of nation states. Governed by treaties and other international agreements. Deals with issues like sovereignty, territorial disputes, war, trade between nations.

**Private international law** -- Involves disputes between individuals or corporations in different countries. Also called "conflict of laws." Deals with questions like: Which country's law applies? Which country's courts have jurisdiction?

**Comity of nations** -- The courtesy by which one country recognizes, within its own territory or in its courts, another country's institutions. Plain English: "I'll respect your laws and court decisions in my country as a matter of courtesy and mutual respect, not because I'm legally required to."

> **Setup:** A French insurance company sues a German reinsurer in a French court and wins a judgment.
> **What Happens:** The French company needs to collect money from the German company's assets in Germany.
> **Result:** Under comity of nations, the German courts would generally recognize and enforce the French judgment, even though it was not made in Germany. This is courtesy between nations, not a legal requirement.

### 5. Financial Considerations in International Insurance

#### A. Currency and Foreign Exchange Markets

**Pegged currency** -- A currency whose value is fixed (pegged) to another currency, typically the U.S. dollar or the Euro. Plain English: "The government says 1 of our dollars always equals X of their dollars."

Currency risk is a major concern in international insurance because:
- Premiums may be collected in one currency
- Claims may be paid in another currency
- Exchange rate fluctuations can create unexpected gains or losses

> **Setup:** A U.S. insurer writes a policy for a UK company. The premium is 1 million British pounds.
> **What Happens:** When the policy was written, 1 GBP = $1.50, so the premium was worth $1.5 million. When a claim is paid six months later, 1 GBP = $1.30.
> **Result:** The insurer received $1.5 million worth of premium but the claims are now valued in cheaper pounds -- a gain for the insurer in this case. But it could easily go the other way, creating a loss.

#### B. Expropriation

**Expropriation** -- A government's lawful acquisition of property without the owner's consent, and often without compensation. The government acquires property rights and the owner loses them.

Contrast with **eminent domain** -- A government's power to confiscate private property for public use. The U.S. Constitution requires **just compensation** for property taken by eminent domain.

**Key distinction:**
- **Expropriation** = Government takes your stuff, often WITHOUT paying you (common in developing countries, political upheavals)
- **Eminent domain** = Government takes your stuff FOR PUBLIC USE and MUST pay fair value (U.S. constitutional requirement)

> **Setup:** A U.S. insurance company owns office buildings and a subsidiary in Country X.
> **What Happens:** A new government comes to power in Country X and nationalizes all foreign-owned financial services companies.
> **Result:** The U.S. insurer loses its buildings and subsidiary. Under expropriation, there may be no compensation. This is why political risk insurance exists -- to protect against exactly this scenario.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Political risk and expropriation concerns have evolved. Recent examples include Russia seizing foreign company assets after 2022 sanctions, and various countries in Latin America and Africa nationalizing mining and energy operations.]

#### C. Accounting Issues

International insurance companies must deal with different accounting standards across countries. Three major frameworks exist:

**SAP (Statutory Accounting Principles)** -- Used in the U.S. for regulatory reporting to state insurance departments. SAP is conservative -- it focuses on an insurer's ability to pay claims (solvency). Assets are valued at the lower of cost or market, and certain assets (like furniture, goodwill) are "non-admitted" and excluded.

**GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)** -- Used in the U.S. for financial reporting to investors and the public. GAAP provides a more complete picture of profitability and includes assets that SAP excludes.

**IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards)** -- Used in most countries outside the U.S. Set by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB). Required in the EU, UK, and many other jurisdictions.

**Why This Matters for Insurance:** The SAME insurer can appear profitable under GAAP but financially strained under SAP, or vice versa. An international insurer must prepare financial statements under multiple frameworks depending on the jurisdiction.

> **Example:** A global insurer headquartered in London with a U.S. subsidiary must prepare IFRS statements for UK regulators, SAP statements for U.S. state regulators, and GAAP statements for U.S. investors. The same $100M in premiums can produce different profit figures under each standard because of how they treat reserves, investments, and expenses.

> **[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]**: IFRS 17 (Insurance Contracts), effective January 2023, is the biggest change to international insurance accounting in decades. It replaced IFRS 4 and fundamentally changes how insurers recognize revenue, measure liabilities, and present financial results. Key features: the "building block approach" for measuring insurance contracts, the contractual service margin (CSM) concept that spreads profit recognition over the coverage period, and increased disclosure requirements. U.S. insurers using GAAP follow ASU 2018-12 (LDTI) which made similar changes. These convergence efforts have narrowed but not eliminated differences between GAAP and IFRS for insurance.

#### D. Taxation Issues

Five different approaches to taxing a corporation's earnings in international commerce:

1. **Territorial tax system** -- Only taxes income earned within the country's borders. If you earn it abroad, your home country does not tax it.

2. **Worldwide tax system** -- Taxes all income regardless of where it is earned. The U.S. traditionally used this system (with credits for foreign taxes paid).

3. **Border tax adjustment (BTA)** -- Taxes are applied to imports and removed from exports to level the playing field. Like a VAT (value-added tax) adjustment at the border.

4. **Earnings stripping** -- A tax avoidance strategy where a company reduces its taxable income in a high-tax country by making excessive interest payments to a related company in a low-tax country. Governments try to prevent this.

5. **Inversion (also called expatriation or reincorporation)** -- When a U.S. company reincorporates in a foreign country with lower taxes to reduce its tax burden. The company moves its legal home but often keeps its operations in the U.S.

**Transfer price** -- The price one subsidiary charges another subsidiary of the same parent company for goods or services. Companies can manipulate transfer prices to shift profits to low-tax countries.

**Tax haven** -- A country with very low or no taxes that attracts foreign businesses to incorporate there. Examples include Bermuda, Cayman Islands, Ireland, Luxembourg, Switzerland.

When establishing a subsidiary in a **tax haven**, a business should consider:
- The country's **tax structure**
- The country's level of enforcement of its **privacy laws**
- The country's **language**
- The type of **judicial system** the country has
- The country's **political stability**
- The country's **independence from the parent company's home country**
- The **costs** of establishing the new subsidiary

**Bearer share** -- A stock certificate where ownership belongs to whoever physically holds the certificate. No registered owner -- provides anonymity. Common in tax havens.

**Registered share** -- A stock certificate with the owner's name recorded in the company's books. The company knows who its owners are.

**Repatriation of earnings** -- The process by which a U.S. parent company moves earnings from its foreign-based affiliates back to the U.S. to the parent company or its stockholders. Some countries restrict or tax repatriation.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act fundamentally changed U.S. international taxation. The U.S. moved from a worldwide system to a modified territorial system. The OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework agreed to a global minimum tax of 15% (Pillar Two), effective 2024 in many countries. Corporate inversions have been largely curtailed by legislation. Bearer shares have been banned or restricted in most jurisdictions due to anti-money laundering rules.]

### 6. U.S. Law Affecting International Business

#### A. Tax Code (Internal Revenue Code -- IRC)

**Foreign sales corporation (FSC)** -- A special type of corporation that U.S. exporters could create in foreign countries to get tax benefits on export income. [2026 UPDATE NEEDED: FSCs were ruled illegal by the WTO and were replaced by the ETI Act, which was also struck down. Current U.S. export tax incentives operate under different structures.]

**Extraterritorial Income (ETI)** -- A tax exclusion that replaced FSCs, also later struck down by the WTO as an illegal export subsidy.

The IRC affects international insurance through rules on:
- How foreign income is taxed
- Tax credits for foreign taxes paid
- Transfer pricing rules
- Controlled foreign corporation (CFC) rules

#### B. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)

The FCPA prohibits U.S. companies and their agents from bribing foreign government officials to obtain or retain business. It has two main provisions:

1. **Anti-bribery provisions** -- Illegal to pay bribes to foreign officials
2. **Accounting provisions** -- Requires companies to keep accurate books and records and maintain internal accounting controls

This is critical for insurers operating internationally because bribes can be disguised as commissions, consulting fees, or "facilitation payments."

> **FCPA Insurance Example:**
> **Setup:** A U.S.-based insurer wants to enter the market in Country X. The local insurance regulator hints that the licensing process could take "years" unless the insurer hires the regulator's brother-in-law as a "consultant" for $200,000.
> **What Happens:** The insurer pays the "consulting fee." The license is approved within weeks.
> **Result:** This is an FCPA violation. The "consulting fee" is really a bribe to a foreign official disguised as a legitimate business expense. The insurer faces criminal prosecution, massive fines (potentially hundreds of millions), and reputational damage. Both the company AND the individuals involved can be prosecuted.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: FCPA enforcement has intensified dramatically since 2008. The UK Bribery Act 2010 is even broader than the FCPA. International anti-corruption enforcement is now coordinated across multiple jurisdictions.]

#### C. The Patriot Act

Five U.S. laws changed by the Patriot Act (any five of the following):
- **Wiretap Statute (Title III)**
- **Electronic Communications Privacy Act**
- **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act**
- **Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA)**
- **Family Education Rights and Privacy Act**
- **Pen Register and Trap and Trace Statutes**
- **Money Laundering Act**
- **Money Laundering Control Act**
- **Bank Secrecy Act**
- **Right to Financial Privacy Act**
- **Fair Credit Reporting Act**
- **Immigration and Nationality Act**

The Patriot Act affects insurance by:
- Requiring enhanced due diligence on international transactions
- Anti-money laundering compliance requirements
- Suspicious activity reporting
- Sanctions screening for policyholders and claimants

> **Patriot Act / AML Insurance Example:**
> **Setup:** A walk-in customer at an insurance office wants to purchase a $5 million single-premium whole life insurance policy and pay with a cashier's check from an offshore bank.
> **What Happens:** The insurer's compliance department flags this as potentially suspicious -- large single-premium life policies are a known money laundering vehicle (dirty money goes in as premium, clean money comes out as death benefit or cash value).
> **Result:** The insurer must file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with FinCEN, conduct enhanced due diligence on the customer (identity verification, source of funds), and screen the customer against OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list. If the customer appears on the SDN list, the insurer must block the transaction and report it. Failure to comply can result in severe penalties.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The Patriot Act provisions have been amended multiple times. The USA FREEDOM Act (2015) reformed some surveillance provisions. OFAC sanctions have become a major compliance area for international insurers, especially after Russian sanctions in 2022. The Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 (part of the NDAA) further strengthened AML requirements and created new beneficial ownership reporting obligations under the Corporate Transparency Act.]

### 7. Multinational Organizations and Agreements

#### A. United Nations (UN)

The UN's purposes are:
- To maintain **international peace and security**
- To develop **friendly relations** among nations
- To cooperate in solving international **economic, social, cultural, and humanitarian** problems
- To promote respect for **human rights and fundamental freedoms**
- To be a center for **harmonizing the actions of nations** in attaining these goals

#### B. World Trade Organization (WTO)

The three primary WTO agreements:
1. **GATT** -- General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (goods)
2. **GATS** -- General Agreement on Trade in Services (services, including insurance)
3. **TRIPS** -- Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights

**Dumping** -- Selling goods in a foreign country at below their cost of production or below their domestic price. The WTO has anti-dumping rules to prevent this unfair trade practice.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The WTO has faced significant challenges since 2008. The Doha Round of trade negotiations effectively collapsed. The WTO Appellate Body has been non-functional since 2019. Trade tensions between the U.S. and China have challenged the multilateral trading system.]

#### C. North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA)

NAFTA applies to **Canada, Mexico, and the United States**.

NAFTA eliminated nearly all tariffs between the U.S. and Canada by 1998 and was to eliminate nearly all tariffs between the U.S. and Mexico by 2008.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: NAFTA was replaced by the USMCA (United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement) in 2020. The USMCA includes updated provisions on digital trade, labor standards, environmental protections, and rules of origin for automobiles.]

#### D. European Union (EU)

The EU operates a **Common Market** -- an economic area with free movement of goods, services, capital, and people across member states.

The **Euro** is the common currency used by most EU member states (the "Eurozone").

The EU has significant implications for insurance because:
- EU insurance directives harmonize regulation across member states
- Solvency II (EU regulatory framework for insurance) sets capital requirements
- Freedom of services allows insurers licensed in one EU country to operate across the EU

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED: The UK left the EU (Brexit) in 2020, fundamentally changing the European insurance landscape. London-based insurers lost "passporting" rights to serve EU clients directly. Solvency II has been implemented and is being reformed. The EU now has 27 member states.]

#### E. ASEAN and ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA)

**ASEAN** (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) member states include: Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, Brunei Darussalam, Vietnam, Laos, Myanmar, and Cambodia.

ASEAN purposes:
- Accelerate **economic growth**, social progress, and cultural development of the region
- Promote **regional peace and stability** through respect for justice and the rule of law

#### F. Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC)

**APEC** attempts to facilitate economic growth, cooperation, trade, and investment in the Asia-Pacific region. It is the **only inter-governmental body** that operates on the basis of **nonbinding commitments**, open dialogue, and equal respect for the views of all participant countries.

## Review Questions & Answers

**1. Compare the concepts of absolute and comparative advantage. (p. 15.5)**

A country has **absolute advantage** when it specializes in goods or services it produces more efficiently and trades them for the goods and services it produces less efficiently. A **comparative advantage** occurs when trading partners gain from trading with each other even when they are themselves more efficient in production of the traded goods and services.

1. A company might decide that it is not economically viable to sell its product in the second country because of labor costs, transportation costs, or regulations

3. A company might lack sufficient knowledge about the country's legal, political, social, and business environments

Franchising occurs when one company assigns to another the right to supply its products within a market.

A **fully owned subsidiary** provides a company with the highest level of control over operations. The disadvantage is that it presents the highest level of business risk, commitment of capital, and managerial control.

## Application Questions & Answers

**Facts:** Farm Insurance Company (FIC), with ninety years of experience specializing in farm-related insurance coverages in the midwestern United States, has decided that the time has come to export its farm insurance products abroad by becoming a global competitor in the farm insurance business. FIC's plan includes going into developing countries that currently do not have any similarly specialized insurers.

---

**1. An international business consultant has advised FIC that its best choices for globalizing are (a) to own a subsidiary in each country or (b) to enter a joint venture in each country. Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of each option.**

**(a) Subsidiary:**
FIC would own or control any subsidiaries, which would issue stock. FIC could own 100 percent of the stock, giving it the highest level of control, or it could trade shares publicly in foreign markets. Some foreign markets might require FIC to form subsidiaries to bring their operations under local laws or incorporation. This option would provide a higher level of risk with higher returns. Use of subsidiaries is best for a company experienced in international operations, so FIC's lack of experience could be a disadvantage. The time required for implementing this option could vary by country, and development of subsidiaries from the ground up could take years, with large investments of resources and time.

**(b) Joint Venture:**
FIC's forming joint ventures would involve shared ownership and control, offering high earnings and growth potential and more options for entry into foreign markets. This option commonly would involve joining forces with a second company in each country. One disadvantage is that, because of its specialized business, FIC might not find suitable insurers (or any insurers at all) with which to form joint ventures in some countries, particularly those that have no similar insurers in existence. Although, like subsidiaries, joint ventures increase companies' business risk and commitment of resources, with substantial capital, the value of a joint venture can be greater than the sum of what the individual partners contribute. Choosing the right partners could be the most problematic issue.

---

**2. FIC is investigating doing business in Peru and Liberia. Identify the challenges FIC might face in each of these countries.**

**In Peru**, FIC could face the following challenges: **Language, culture, and distance, and space.** Time differences probably would not be a problem because Peru is in a similar time zone.

**In Liberia**, FIC could face the following challenges: **Distance and space, culture, and time.** The company should research what language problems might arise as well as the stability of the government.

---

**3. FIC has hired a consulting firm to explore taxation issues in various countries. What should the company research about these three countries -- Peru, Liberia, and Uzbekistan -- to determine if they might be tax havens?**

The company should research **how the countries tax foreign income** and **how businesses are taxed for income made from internal business as well as external business**.

## Exam Tips -- Common Traps and What to Memorize

### Must-Know Distinctions

1. **Absolute advantage vs. Comparative advantage** -- This is a classic exam question. Remember: comparative advantage means BOTH countries benefit from trade even if one is better at everything

2. **Expropriation vs. Eminent domain** -- Expropriation = often NO compensation. Eminent domain = MUST compensate (U.S. constitutional requirement). Do not confuse these.

3. **Public vs. Private international law** -- Public = between NATIONS (treaties). Private = between INDIVIDUALS or CORPORATIONS across borders.

4. **Civil law vs. Common law** -- Civil = written codes, no binding precedent. Common = precedent-based, judges "make law." Three stages of civil law case: preliminary, evidence, decision.

5. **Territorial vs. Worldwide tax system** -- Territorial = only taxes domestic income. Worldwide = taxes ALL income everywhere.

6. **Bearer shares vs. Registered shares** -- Bearer = anonymous ownership (whoever holds it). Registered = name on the books.

### Lists to Memorize

7. **Three reasons for product licensing**: (1) not economically viable, (2) lack time/resources, (3) lack knowledge of country

8. **Five challenges of international business**: Language, Culture, Time, Distance/Space, Government type

9. **Three WTO agreements**: GATT (goods), GATS (services), TRIPS (intellectual property)

10. **NAFTA countries**: Canada, Mexico, United States [now USMCA]

11. **UN purposes**: Peace/security, friendly relations, economic/social/cultural cooperation, human rights, harmonizing actions

12. **Tax haven considerations** (7 items): Tax structure, privacy law enforcement, language, judicial system, political stability, independence from parent's home country, costs

### Likely Exam Traps

13. **"A fully owned subsidiary provides the LOWEST risk"** -- WRONG. A fully owned subsidiary provides the HIGHEST control but also the HIGHEST risk. Joint ventures share risk.

14. **"Common law uses written codes as primary source"** -- WRONG. That is civil law. Common law uses precedent (prior court decisions).

15. **"Comity is required by international law"** -- WRONG. Comity is a courtesy, not a legal requirement. Countries choose to recognize each other's institutions as a matter of mutual respect.

16. **"APEC agreements are binding on member nations"** -- WRONG. APEC operates on NONBINDING commitments. This is its unique characteristic.

17. **"Eminent domain does not require compensation"** -- WRONG. The U.S. Constitution requires JUST COMPENSATION for eminent domain. EXPROPRIATION is what often lacks compensation.
