## PART 1: SALES CONTRACTS (UCC Article 2)

### What is UCC Article 2?

**UCC** stands for the **Uniform Commercial Code** -- a set of standardized laws adopted by all 50 states to govern commercial transactions. **Article 2** specifically covers the **sale of goods** (tangible, movable things like cars, furniture, electronics -- NOT services, real estate, or stocks).

> **Plain English**: Think of Article 2 as the "rulebook" for buying and selling physical stuff. It fills in the blanks when a sales contract is silent on certain terms.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The UCC has been revised multiple times since 2008. While the core principles remain, some provisions -- particularly around electronic transactions and digital goods -- have been updated. Check current UCC Article 2 revisions.

---

### A. General Contract Law Application

#### 1. Offer and Acceptance

Under UCC Article 2, the rules are **more flexible** than regular contract law:

- An **offer** can be accepted even if the acceptance contains **additional or different terms** (unlike the common law "mirror image" rule)
- Between **merchants** (professional business sellers), additional terms become part of the contract UNLESS they materially alter the deal, the offer limits acceptance to its terms, or the offeror objects within a reasonable time

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: MegaCorp sends a purchase order to SteelCo for 500 tons of steel at $200/ton
- **What Happens**: SteelCo sends back an acceptance that adds "Delivery within 60 days" -- a term not in the original order
- **The Result**: Under UCC Article 2, this IS a valid acceptance (not a counteroffer). Since both are merchants, the 60-day delivery term likely becomes part of the contract unless MegaCorp objects

#### 2. Consideration

Standard contract law consideration rules apply -- there must be a bargained-for exchange of value.

#### When Nonconforming Goods Are NOT a Breach

A shipment of **nonconforming goods** (goods that don't match the order) does NOT automatically constitute a breach in these situations:

- The **seller notifies the buyer** that the shipment is only an **accommodation** (meaning: "I'm trying to help, here's what I have, take it or leave it")
- The **buyer rejects goods as nonconforming** before the contractual performance time has expired -- the seller can then **notify the buyer** of intent to cure the nonconformity by delivering conforming goods

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A restaurant orders 100 cases of Coca-Cola from a distributor
- **What Happens**: The distributor is out of Coke and ships 100 cases of Pepsi instead, with a note saying "This is an accommodation -- we're out of Coke, sending Pepsi as a substitute"
- **The Result**: This is NOT a breach of contract. The restaurant can accept or reject the Pepsi. If rejected, the distributor owes nothing more

### B. Warranties

Warranties are promises (explicit or implied) about the quality and condition of goods.

#### 1. Express Warranties

**What it means**: Any statement of fact, description, or sample/model that becomes the **basis of the bargain** creates an express warranty.

- The seller does NOT need to use the word "warranty" or "guarantee"
- Mere **puffery** ("This is the best car on the market!") is NOT an express warranty -- it's just sales talk
- A specific claim ("This car gets 35 MPG") IS an express warranty

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A salesperson tells a buyer, "This industrial generator will run continuously for 10,000 hours without maintenance"
- **What Happens**: The generator breaks down after 2,000 hours
- **The Result**: The buyer can claim breach of express warranty. The specific claim about 10,000 hours became part of the deal

#### 2. Implied Warranties

These exist AUTOMATICALLY by operation of law -- nobody has to say anything.

**Implied Warranty of Merchantability** (p. 6.6):
- Only arises in sales **by a merchant** (someone who regularly deals in goods of that kind)
- Means the goods are fit for their **ordinary purpose**
- A toaster must toast. A car must drive. Shoes must be wearable.
- NOT implied in a sale by a **nonmerchant** (e.g., selling your old lawnmower at a garage sale)

> **EXAM TRAP**: The implied warranty of merchantability is implied in a sale by a MERCHANT but NOT in a sale by a nonmerchant. This is a commonly tested distinction!

**Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose** (p. 6.6):
- Arises when the **buyer relies on the seller's expertise** in selecting a product for a **specific** (not ordinary) purpose
- The seller must **know** the buyer's particular purpose AND know the buyer is relying on the seller's judgment

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A customer walks into a paint store and says, "I need paint that can withstand constant exposure to saltwater on my boat hull"
- **What Happens**: The paint store employee recommends a specific paint. The customer buys it, but it peels off after one month in saltwater
- **The Result**: Breach of the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose. The seller knew the particular purpose (boat hull/saltwater), the buyer relied on the seller's expertise, and the product failed

#### 3. Third-Party Beneficiaries of Warranties

A seller's warranty extends beyond just the buyer. It covers:
- The buyer's **family**
- Members of the buyer's **household**
- **Any guest** in the buyer's home who suffers injury from breach of warranty

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Tom buys a defective space heater from HomeStore
- **What Happens**: Tom's dinner guest, Sarah, is burned when the heater explodes
- **The Result**: Sarah can sue HomeStore for breach of warranty even though she didn't buy the heater. She's a guest in the buyer's home

---

### Disclaimer of Warranties

Sellers can limit or exclude warranties under UCC 2-316:

- To disclaim the **implied warranty of merchantability**, the disclaimer must mention the word "merchantability" and, if in writing, must be **conspicuous** (bold, capitalized, or otherwise noticeable)
- To disclaim the **implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose**, the disclaimer must be in writing and conspicuous
- The simplest way to disclaim ALL implied warranties is to sell goods **"as is"** or **"with all faults"**
- **Express warranties generally CANNOT be disclaimed** if they were part of the basis of the bargain

**Example**:
- **The Setup**: A used car dealer sells a car with a sign on the dashboard reading "SOLD AS-IS, NO WARRANTIES EXPRESS OR IMPLIED"
- **What Happens**: The transmission fails two days later
- **The Result**: The buyer likely has NO warranty claim. The "as-is" language effectively disclaimed all implied warranties. However, if the dealer verbally promised "this car will run great for years," that express warranty may survive.

### C. Types of Sales Contracts

Sales contracts can be categorized by their delivery/shipping terms. These determine WHO bears the risk of loss and WHEN.

---

### D. Delivery Terms, Inspection, and Time

#### 1. Inspection

The buyer generally has the right to inspect goods before accepting and paying. Exception: **COD** sales (see below).

#### 2. Time for Delivery

If no time is specified, delivery must occur within a **reasonable time**.

---

### Key Shipping Terms (MEMORIZE THESE!)

These terms define when **ownership (title)** and **risk of loss** shift from seller to buyer:

**FOB (Free on Board) Place of Shipment** (p. 6.8):
- The seller delivers goods to the **carrier** at the seller's location
- **Risk and expense shift to the buyer** once goods are given to the carrier
- Plain English: "Once I put it on the truck at my warehouse, it's YOUR problem"

**FOB (Free on Board) Place of Destination** (p. 6.8):
- Ownership passes from seller to buyer when the carrier **delivers goods to the buyer's location**
- Plain English: "It's MY problem until it arrives at YOUR door"

**FAS (Free Alongside) Vessel** (p. 6.8):
- Ownership passes from seller to buyer when the seller **delivers goods alongside a vessel** for loading
- Used in international/maritime shipping

**FOB (Free on Board) Vessel** (p. 6.8):
- Goods are loaded **on board the vessel** at the seller's risk and expense, THEN ownership passes to the buyer
- One step further than FAS -- seller pays for loading too

**CIF (Cost-Insurance-Freight)** (p. 6.8):
- The seller is obligated to pay for **insurance AND freight charges** for delivery to the buyer
- Seller bears more cost but risk shifts once goods are delivered to carrier

**CAF (Cost and Freight)** (p. 6.8):
- The seller pays **freight charges** but NOT insurance for delivery
- Same as CIF minus the insurance

**COD (Collect on Delivery)** (p. 6.8):
- The **buyer pays** when goods are delivered
- Buyer has **NO right to inspect** the goods before payment
- No right to inspect AND must accept -- this is a condition of the sale

> **EXAM TRAP**: In CIF sales, the seller pays insurance and freight. In FOB sales, the key is whether it's FOB *shipment* (seller's place) or FOB *destination* (buyer's place). This determines who bears risk of loss!

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: **Incoterms 2020** (International Commercial Terms, published by the International Chamber of Commerce) have modified several shipping terms. FOB is now restricted to maritime transport only under Incoterms. For multimodal transport, FCA (Free Carrier) has replaced FOB in international practice. The UCC still uses FOB for domestic transactions, but be aware that international trade uses the updated Incoterms framework.

---

### E. Title and Risk of Loss

**Risk of Loss Under the UCC**: While title and risk of loss are related, the UCC deliberately SEPARATES risk of loss from title in many situations. Under UCC 2-509 and 2-510, risk of loss depends on the **terms of the contract, shipping arrangements, and whether the seller is a merchant** -- not simply who holds title.

**Key Rules for Risk of Loss**:
- **(a) Carrier involved (shipment contract, e.g., FOB Shipping Point)**: Risk passes to buyer when goods are delivered to the carrier
- **(b) Goods held by a bailee**: Risk passes when buyer receives the document of title
- **(c) All other cases**:
  - If the **seller is a merchant**: Risk does NOT pass to the buyer until the buyer **receives** the goods
  - If the **seller is NOT a merchant**: Risk passes when the seller **tenders delivery** (makes goods available)

> **Key Distinction**: Do NOT simply say "risk follows title." The UCC intentionally moved away from that approach. Focus on the shipping terms and merchant status instead.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Wanda buys furniture from Selma's store. The furniture is marked with Wanda's name and set aside. Wanda is to pick it up within a week
- **What Happens**: A fire destroys Selma's store (NOT caused by Selma's negligence) and the furniture is destroyed before Wanda picks it up
- **The Result**: Title likely passed to Wanda (goods were identified and marked). BUT the question is risk of loss. Since Selma is a merchant, risk of loss doesn't pass until the buyer receives the goods. Wanda does NOT have to pay for the destroyed furniture

### F. Breach of Sales Contracts

#### 1. Revocation of Acceptance

A buyer can revoke acceptance of goods if there's a **substantial** nonconformity that impairs the value of the goods.

#### 2. Excuses for Nonperformance

Three legitimate excuses for not performing a sales contract:

1. **Loss of identified goods** -- The specific goods designated in the contract are destroyed (without fault of either party)
2. **Unavailability of agreed-on shipping** -- The agreed transportation method becomes unavailable
3. **Failure of a presupposed condition** -- A basic assumption underlying the contract turns out to be false

#### 3. Seller's Remedies (when buyer breaches)

- **Withhold delivery** of goods
- **Stop delivery** if goods are in transit and buyer is insolvent
- **Resell** the goods and recover damages
- **Recover the price** if goods can't be resold
- **Cancel** the contract

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A seller delivers goods to a carrier for shipment to a buyer
- **What Happens**: The seller discovers the buyer is **insolvent** (can't pay debts)
- **The Result**: The seller can **stop delivery** of the goods or stop their transit. The seller can withhold delivery and demand cash payment

#### 4. Buyer's Remedies (when seller breaches)

- **Reject nonconforming goods**
- **Revoke acceptance** for substantial nonconformity
- **Cover** -- buy substitute goods elsewhere and recover the price difference
- **Recover damages** for non-delivery
- **Specific performance** (force seller to deliver unique goods)
- **Deduct damages** from price still owed

## PART 2: NEGOTIABLE INSTRUMENTS (UCC Article 3)

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The revised UCC Article 3 (widely adopted) changed the official term from "commercial paper" to **"negotiable instruments."** The textbook may use the older "commercial paper" terminology, but current law uses "negotiable instruments." The core principles remain the same.

### What are Negotiable Instruments?

A negotiable instrument is a **written document** that promises or orders payment of money. Think: checks, promissory notes, drafts. The key feature is that they can be **transferred** from person to person, essentially functioning as a substitute for money.

---

### A. Types of Commercial Paper

**Draft, or Check** (p. 6.12):
- A type of commercial paper containing an **unconditional order** by the drawer (person making out the draft), requiring the drawee (usually a bank) to pay a certain sum to the payee
- Plain English: When you write a check, YOU are the drawer, your BANK is the drawee, and the person you're paying is the payee

**Certificate of Deposit (CD)** (p. 6.13):
- Issued by a **financial institution** acknowledging receipt of money and promising to repay it, with interest, at a specific time
- Plain English: You give a bank $10,000, they give you a paper saying "We'll pay you back $10,500 in one year"

**Promissory Note** (p. 6.13):
- A written **promise** to pay money on demand or at a definite future time
- Plain English: An "I owe you" document with legal teeth. Common in loans

**Trade Acceptance** (p. 6.13):
- A type of **party draft** used when a seller wants cash immediately but the buyer can't provide it until the goods are resold
- Plain English: "I'm shipping you goods now; sign this paper promising to pay me when you sell them"

---

### B. Transfer and Negotiation

#### 1. Primary and Secondary Liability

**Primary Liability** (p. 6.14):
- The **absolute** obligation to pay a negotiable instrument according to its terms
- The **maker** of a note has primary liability; the **drawee** of a draft has primary liability once they accept it

**Secondary Liability** (p. 6.14):
- The obligation to pay ONLY IF the primary party **refuses** to pay or accept
- **Drawers** and **endorsers** have secondary liability

#### 2. Endorsements

An **endorsement** is a signature on the back of a negotiable instrument that transfers it to another party. Types:

**Special Endorsement** (p. 6.14):
- Names a specific person to whom the instrument is payable
- Example: "Pay to the order of Howard Roe /s/ John Doe"
- Only Howard Roe can cash or further negotiate it

**Blank (General) Endorsement** (p. 6.15):
- Just a signature with NO specific payee named
- Example: "/s/ John Doe" on the back of a check
- Makes the instrument payable to **the bearer** -- ANYONE who holds it can cash it
- **DANGEROUS** -- like signing cash. If you lose it, anyone can use it

**Restrictive (Collection) Endorsement** (p. 6.15):
- Includes language that **limits** further negotiation
- Example: "For deposit /s/ John Doe"
- The check can ONLY be deposited into John Doe's account -- can't be cashed by someone else

**Qualified Endorsement** (p. 6.15):
- Includes words like "**Without recourse**"
- Example: "Without recourse /s/ John Doe"
- The endorser **avoids secondary liability** -- if the check bounces, John Doe is NOT on the hook
- The check remains negotiable, but John Doe won't be liable if it's dishonored

**Unqualified Endorsement** (p. 6.15):
- A regular endorsement WITHOUT the "without recourse" language
- The endorser IS secondarily liable if the instrument is dishonored

> **EXAM TRAP**: Know the difference between special vs. blank and qualified vs. unqualified endorsements. A "blank" endorsement is the MOST dangerous (makes it payable to bearer). A "restrictive" endorsement like "For deposit" is the SAFEST.

---

### C. Holders in Due Course

**Holder in Due Course (HDC)** (p. 6.15):
- Someone who possesses a negotiable instrument drawn to his/her order AND who took it under these conditions:
  1. **For value** (paid something for it)
  2. **In good faith** (honest transaction)
  3. **Without notice** that it's overdue, dishonored, or subject to any claims/defenses

**Why does this matter?** An HDC takes the instrument **free of personal defenses** (like fraud in the inducement, failure of consideration, etc.). Only **real defenses** (like forgery, fraud in the execution, minority, illegality) can defeat an HDC's claim.

**Personal Defense** (p. 6.15):
- Defenses that are good against ordinary holders but NOT against holders in due course
- Examples: breach of contract, fraud in the inducement, failure of consideration

**Real Defense** (p. 6.15):
- Defenses that work against EVERYONE, including holders in due course
- Examples: forgery, material alteration, fraud in the execution (the person didn't know they were signing a negotiable instrument), minority/infancy, illegality

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Ellen tricks Ken into signing a promissory note by substituting it for a receipt while distracting him. Ellen then sells the note to Joyce, a good-faith purchaser who pays full value
- **What Happens**: Joyce presents the note to Ken for payment. Ken says he was tricked
- **The Result**: Joyce is a holder in due course. Ken's defense depends on whether it's a **real defense** (fraud in the execution -- he didn't know he was signing a note) or a **personal defense** (fraud in the inducement). If Ken can prove he had NO idea he was signing a note (real defense), he might win. If he was merely careless in not reading what he signed, Joyce wins as HDC

---

### D. Checks

**Certified Check** (p. 6.16):
- A check that the bank has **stamped and guaranteed** -- the bank sets aside funds and promises to pay
- Essentially as good as cash

**Cashier's Check** (p. 6.16):
- A check drawn by the **bank on itself** -- the bank is both the drawer and the drawee
- Also essentially as good as cash

## PART 3: SECURED TRANSACTIONS (UCC Article 9)

### What is a Security Interest?

**Security Interest** (p. 6.17):
- A creditor's **legal claim** to specific property (collateral) that the debtor pledges as backup for a loan
- Plain English: "If you don't pay me back, I get to take your stuff"

**Key Players**:
- **Secured Party** (p. 6.17): The creditor who holds the security interest (the lender)
- **Collateral** (p. 6.17): The property pledged as security (what the lender can take)
- **Secured Transaction** (p. 6.17): The entire deal -- loan + security agreement + collateral

**Pledge** (p. 6.17):
- When the debtor physically hands over the collateral to the creditor
- Example: Pawning your watch at a pawn shop

**Surety** (p. 6.17):
- A third party who guarantees payment of another's debt
- **Principal** (p. 6.17): The debtor whose obligation the surety guarantees
- **Obligee** (p. 6.17): The creditor who benefits from the surety's guarantee

---

### A. Perfecting a Security Interest

To "perfect" a security interest means to **make it legally enforceable against third parties** (not just between the debtor and creditor). You perfect by:

1. **Filing a financing statement** with the appropriate government office (usually Secretary of State)
2. **Taking possession** of the collateral (pledge)

> **EXAM TRAP**: A financing statement should be filed BEFORE the security interest attaches to consumer goods. If the seller delays filing until after delivery, the security interest is unperfected, and another security interest might attach in the meantime.

---

### B. Rights of Perfected Security Interests

- A **perfected** security interest has **priority** over unperfected interests
- Generally, **first to file or perfect wins** among competing perfected interests
- Priority order: Perfected security interests > unperfected interests > general creditors

### C. Rights of Unperfected Security Interests

- An unperfected security interest is valid between the debtor and secured party
- BUT loses to perfected interests, lien creditors, and certain buyers

**Two exceptions** where a perfected security interest is NOT superior to a later perfected interest:
1. A **holder in due course** of a negotiable instrument takes it free of any perfected security interest
2. An **artisan's lien** (like a mechanic's lien for repairs) on the collateral takes priority over a perfected security interest in that collateral

---

### D. Default

When a debtor **defaults** (fails to pay), the secured party's options are:

- **Sue** on the underlying debt
- **Foreclose** on the collateral
- **Regain possession** of the collateral (can be self-help repossession if done without breach of peace)
- **Retain or sell** the collateral
- **Dispose** of the collateral as desired

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A manufacturer sells TVs on credit to George's TV shop. They sign a security agreement and file a financing statement (perfected interest). George sells a TV to Sidney, a consumer
- **What Happens**: George doesn't pay the manufacturer. The manufacturer repossesses the TV from Sidney
- **The Result**: The manufacturer had a perfected security interest BUT a consumer who buys goods for personal purposes from a retailer takes those goods **free of the seller's security interest**. Sidney, buying for personal use, gets to keep the TV. The manufacturer can only go after George

## PART 4: CONSUMER LAW

### A. Fair Trade Laws

#### 1. Federal Trade Commission Act

- The FTC Act prohibits **unfair methods of competition** and **unfair or deceptive acts or practices**
- Applies to most businesses, but...

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The FTC's enforcement powers and scope have evolved significantly since 2008. Recent rulemakings on non-compete clauses, data privacy, and AI-related deceptive practices may be relevant.

#### 2. State Unfair Trade Practices Acts

- States have their own consumer protection laws
- Insurance is generally **exempt from federal antitrust laws** under the **McCarran-Ferguson Act** because states regulate insurance
- HOWEVER, if a state does NOT have antitrust legislation applicable to insurance, federal laws (including the FTC Act and Deceptive Trade Practices Acts) can apply

> **EXAM TRAP**: Under McCarran-Ferguson, the federal government generally does NOT regulate the insurance business because states do. But if a state lacks applicable antitrust laws, federal antitrust laws WILL apply to insurance. Some states have adopted the **Model Insurance Fair Trade Practices Act**.

#### 3. Consumer Warranties: Magnuson-Moss

The **Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act** is a federal law governing consumer product warranties.

**Limited Warranty** (p. 6.21):
- A warranty that does NOT meet all the requirements of a full warranty
- Manufacturers often characterize warranties that are essentially full warranties as "limited" to avoid the stricter obligations of the full warranty designation

**Full Warranty** (p. 6.22):
- Must meet specific federal standards, including:
  - Remedy within a reasonable time at no charge
  - No unreasonable conditions on warranty coverage
  - If defect can't be fixed after reasonable attempts, consumer can choose refund or replacement

> **EXAM TRAP**: Why do manufacturers call warranties "limited" even when they're essentially full warranties? To avoid "lemon law" provisions that would apply to full warranties! If a product can't be fixed, a full warranty requires a refund or replacement.

---

### B. Consumer Credit Laws

#### 1. Truth in Lending Act (p. 6.22-6.23)

- Requires creditors to **disclose finance charges** clearly
- Applies to all persons or organizations that **regularly extend credit** or make finance charges in connection with installment purchases
- Must disclose: Annual Percentage Rate (APR), total finance charges, payment schedule, etc.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The Truth in Lending Act has been amended multiple times since 2008, including by Dodd-Frank (2010). TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosures (TRID) changed mortgage disclosure forms significantly.

#### 2. Fair Credit Reporting Act (p. 6.25)

A **consumer reporting agency** can issue a consumer report ONLY under these circumstances:
- In response to a **court order**
- Under **written instructions** of the subject of the report
- To a person who intends to use the information in connection with a **credit transaction**, **employment purposes**, **insurance underwriting**, eligibility for a **business license**, or for a **legitimate business need**

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The FCRA has been significantly expanded. Key changes include enhanced dispute rights, free annual credit reports (AnnualCreditReport.com), and stricter data breach notification requirements.

#### 3. Equal Credit Opportunity Act

- Prohibits credit discrimination based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, or receipt of public assistance

**Fair Debt Collection Practices Act** (p. 6.25):
Prohibits debt collectors from:
- Using **violent or criminal acts**
- Using **profane language**
- Publishing **lists of debtors**
- Calling debtors **repeatedly** or in the **middle of the night**
- Threatening **legal action with no intent** to follow through
- Contacting the debtor at **work** or at unusual times/places without consent

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The CFPB's **Regulation F** (effective November 2021) significantly updated FDCPA rules. Key changes: debt collectors may now contact consumers via email, text, and social media (with opt-out rights); phone call frequency is limited to **7 calls per account per 7-day period**; and collectors must provide written validation notices with more detail than previously required.

**Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT) System** (p. 6.24):
- The Electronic Fund Transfer Act governs EFT transactions (ATMs, debit cards, direct deposits)
- Provides consumer protections for unauthorized transfers

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] EFT law has expanded massively with digital payment systems (Venmo, Zelle, PayPal, cryptocurrency). The CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, created 2010) now oversees much of consumer financial protection.

## PART 5: BANKRUPTCY

### A. Bankruptcy Act

**Bankruptcy Law** (p. 6.26):
- Federal law that allows debtors to get a fresh start by either liquidating assets to pay creditors or reorganizing their finances

**Chapter 7** (p. 6.26):
- **Liquidation** -- Selling ALL the debtor's nonexempt assets and distributing proceeds to creditors
- The debtor's remaining debts are then **discharged** (forgiven)
- Plain English: "Sell everything, pay what you can, wipe the slate clean"

**Chapter 11** (p. 6.27):
- **Reorganization** -- The debtor (usually a business) keeps operating while restructuring its debts
- A plan is proposed to pay creditors over time
- Plain English: "Keep the business alive, but restructure the debt so it's manageable"

**Chapter 12** (p. 6.27):
- **Family farmers and fishermen** with regular income
- Similar to Chapter 13 but with higher debt limits

**Chapter 13** (p. 6.27):
- **Individual debt adjustment** -- For individuals with regular income who want to keep their property
- Debtor proposes a repayment plan (typically 3-5 years)
- Plain English: "I'll pay you back on a schedule, but I get to keep my house and car"

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA) significantly changed bankruptcy law AFTER this textbook's reference material was written. Key changes include means testing for Chapter 7, mandatory credit counseling, and higher barriers to filing. In 2024, debt limits for Chapter 13 were also revised.

---

### B. Liquidation Proceedings

**Liquidation** (p. 6.28):
- Involves selling all the debtor's assets and distributing proceeds to creditors

**Who can initiate involuntary bankruptcy?** (p. 6.27):
- Any creditors who believe a bankrupt person or organization favors other creditors
- Creditors who continue to dispute remaining assets of a bankrupt estate
- Can petition the federal bankruptcy court for involuntary proceedings

### Priority of Creditors in Liquidation

In a liquidation, creditors are paid in this order (**MEMORIZE THIS**):

1. **Administrative expenses** of the bankruptcy proceeding
2. **Unsecured business debts** (arising after involuntary petition filed)
3. **Limited amount of wage claims** (employee wages earned within 180 days before filing)
4. **Contributions to employee benefit plans**
5. **Claims of unsecured individuals** (consumer deposits, up to a limit)
6. **Unsecured claims of governmental units** (taxes)

> **EXAM TRAP**: Secured creditors get paid FIRST from their specific collateral, BEFORE this priority list even kicks in. This priority list is for UNSECURED claims only. Administrative expenses always come first among unsecured claims.

### Automatic Stay

The moment a bankruptcy petition is filed, an **automatic stay** goes into effect. This immediately STOPS all collection actions, lawsuits, foreclosures, garnishments, and creditor harassment. It gives the debtor breathing room to reorganize or liquidate. Violating the automatic stay can result in sanctions.

**Example**: A homeowner files for Chapter 13 bankruptcy. The bank was about to foreclose on the house next week. The automatic stay STOPS the foreclosure immediately. The bank cannot proceed until the bankruptcy court lifts the stay or the case is resolved.

### Non-Dischargeable Debts

Not all debts can be wiped out in bankruptcy. The following debts generally SURVIVE bankruptcy:
- **Student loans** (unless "undue hardship" is proven -- a very high bar)
- **Child support and alimony**
- **Most tax debts** (especially recent income taxes)
- **Debts obtained through fraud** (e.g., lying on a credit application)
- **Criminal fines and restitution**
- **Debts from willful or malicious injury**
- **DUI-related injury debts**

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: Student loan discharge rules may be evolving under recent Department of Education and court interpretations. Some bankruptcy courts have become more willing to discharge student loans under the "undue hardship" standard.

> **Exam Tip**: "Can this debt be discharged?" is a common exam question. Memorize the non-dischargeable categories -- especially student loans, child support, fraud, and taxes.

## KEY TERMS GLOSSARY

### Sales Contracts & Shipping
- **Implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose** -- Warranty arising when buyer relies on seller's expertise to select goods for a specific use
- **Implied warranty of title** -- Seller's implied promise that they have legal ownership and the right to sell
- **FOB place of shipment** -- Risk shifts to buyer when seller delivers goods to carrier at seller's location
- **FOB place of destination** -- Risk stays with seller until goods arrive at buyer's location
- **FAS vessel** -- Risk shifts when seller delivers goods alongside the ship
- **FOB vessel** -- Risk shifts when goods are loaded on board the ship
- **CIF** -- Seller pays cost, insurance, and freight for delivery
- **CAF** -- Seller pays cost and freight (no insurance)
- **COD** -- Buyer pays upon delivery; no right to inspect before payment

### Negotiable Instruments
- **Draft/Check** -- An unconditional order to pay, drawn on a bank
- **Certificate of Deposit (CD)** -- Bank's written promise to repay deposited money with interest
- **Promissory Note** -- Written promise to pay money on demand or at a definite time
- **Trade Acceptance** -- A draft used when seller needs cash but buyer can't pay until resale
- **Primary liability** -- Absolute obligation to pay (maker of note, acceptor of draft)
- **Secondary liability** -- Obligation to pay only if primary party refuses
- **Special endorsement** -- Names a specific payee ("Pay to the order of Jane Smith")
- **Blank/General endorsement** -- Just a signature; makes instrument payable to bearer
- **Restrictive endorsement** -- Limits further negotiation ("For deposit only")
- **Qualified endorsement** -- Includes "without recourse" to avoid secondary liability
- **Unqualified endorsement** -- Regular endorsement; endorser remains secondarily liable
- **Holder in due course** -- Holder who took instrument for value, in good faith, without notice of defects
- **Personal defense** -- Defense good against ordinary holders but NOT against HDC
- **Real defense** -- Defense good against EVERYONE including HDC (forgery, fraud in execution)
- **Certified check** -- Check guaranteed by the bank
- **Cashier's check** -- Check drawn by bank on itself

### Secured Transactions
- **Security interest** -- Creditor's legal claim to specific property pledged as loan collateral
- **Secured transaction** -- Transaction in which a security interest is created
- **Secured party** -- The creditor holding the security interest
- **Collateral** -- Property pledged as security for a debt
- **Pledge** -- Security interest created by physical delivery of collateral to creditor
- **Surety** -- Third party who guarantees another's debt
- **Principal (surety)** -- The debtor whose obligation the surety guarantees
- **Obligee** -- The creditor who benefits from the surety's guarantee

### Consumer Law
- **Limited warranty** -- Warranty that does not meet all requirements of a full warranty
- **Full warranty** -- Warranty meeting all federal (Magnuson-Moss) standards including free repair/replacement
- **Electronic funds transfer (EFT) system** -- Electronic payment systems (ATMs, debit cards, direct deposits)

### Bankruptcy
- **Bankruptcy law** -- Federal law allowing debtors a fresh financial start
- **Chapter 7** -- Liquidation bankruptcy; sell assets, discharge debts
- **Chapter 11** -- Reorganization bankruptcy; restructure debts while continuing business
- **Chapter 12** -- Family farmer/fisherman bankruptcy
- **Chapter 13** -- Individual repayment plan bankruptcy
- **Liquidation** -- Selling all debtor assets and distributing to creditors

## REVIEW QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**1. What type of transactions does UCC Article 2 govern?**
UCC Article 2 covers the law of **sales of goods** (tangible, movable property).

**2. Under what circumstances will a shipment of nonconforming goods NOT automatically constitute a breach of contract?**
- If the seller **notifies the buyer** that the shipment is only an **accommodation** to the buyer
- If a buyer rejects goods as nonconforming before the time for contractual performance has expired, the seller can then notify the buyer of its intention to **cure** the nonconformity by delivering conforming goods

**3. Name a warranty implied in a sale by a merchant that is NOT implied in a sale by a nonmerchant.**
The **implied warranty of merchantability**. It is implied in a sale by a merchant but NOT in a sale by a nonmerchant.

**4. What persons, if any, other than the buyer, are covered by the seller's warranties in a transaction for the sale of goods?**
A seller's warranty extends to any person in the buyer's **family or household**, or any **guest** in the buyer's home, who suffers injury resulting from breach of the warranty.

**5. What costs does a seller pay in CIF sales that are NOT paid in an FOB sale?**
A seller pays the cost of **insurance and freight** charges in a CIF sale, but NOT in an FOB sale.

**6. Under the UCC, when does risk of loss NOT follow title?**
Risk of loss does NOT follow title when identified goods are at the **seller's place of business or residence** and the buyer is to **pick them up**. If the seller is a merchant, risk of loss does NOT pass to the buyer until the buyer receives the goods.

**7. Describe three excuses for nonperformance of a sales contract.**
1. **Loss of identified goods** (destroyed without fault)
2. **Unavailability of agreed-on shipping** (transportation method becomes unavailable)
3. **Failure of a presupposed condition** (a basic assumption of the contract fails)

**8. What remedy might be available to a seller who, after delivering the goods to a carrier, discovers that the buyer is insolvent?**
The seller can **withhold delivery** of the goods or **stop their delivery** in transit. The seller discovers that the buyer is insolvent and can demand cash payment.

**9. Describe the elements of negotiability.**
A negotiable instrument must have the following elements:
- Be **signed** by the maker or drawer
- Contain an **unconditional promise or order** to pay a certain sum in **money**
- Be payable on **demand** or at a **definite time**
- Be payable to **order** or to **bearer**

**10. Why is salability an essential characteristic for negotiable instruments?**
Salability is essential so that the seller does NOT have to wait for payment once the goods are delivered to the buyer. Negotiable instruments allow the seller to transfer the right to payment immediately.

**11. How can the payee of a negotiable instrument negotiate it to another specific person?**
The payee can negotiate it by **special endorsement** -- signing the back and naming the specific person to whom payment should be made.

**12. What is the significance of being a holder in due course?**
A holder in due course possesses a negotiable instrument but is **free of personal defenses**. Only real defenses can be asserted against an HDC.

**13. Identify the characteristics of a holder in due course.**
A holder in due course has taken the instrument:
- **For value**
- **In good faith**
- **Without notice** that it is overdue, has been dishonored, or is subject to any other person's defenses or claims

**14. Why should a financing statement be filed before the security interest attaches to consumer goods?**
If the seller delays filing the financing statement until after delivery of the goods to the buyer, the security interest is **unperfected**, and another security interest might **attach** to the property in the meantime.

**15. Describe two instances in which a perfected security interest is NOT superior to a later perfected security interest.**
1. A **holder in due course** takes a negotiable instrument free of any perfected security interest in that instrument
2. An **artisan's lien** for services or materials with respect to the collateral takes priority over a perfected security interest in that collateral

**16. Describe a secured party's options upon a debtor's default.**
- Right to **sue** on the underlying debt
- Right to **foreclose**
- Right to **regain possession**
- Right to **retain or sell** the collateral
- Right to **dispose** of the collateral as desired

**17. What, if any, fair trade laws is the insurance business subject to?**
Under the **McCarran-Ferguson Act**, the federal government generally does NOT regulate the insurance business because it is subject to state regulations. States usually apply their own antitrust laws to insurance. However, if a state does NOT have antitrust legislation applicable to insurance, federal antitrust laws apply. State laws, including Deceptive Trade Practices Acts, may apply to insurers. Some states have adopted the **Model Insurance Fair Trade Practices Act**.

**18. Why do manufacturers characterize as "limited" warranties that are essentially full warranties?**
To avoid **"lemon law" provisions**. If a product can't be fixed after reasonable attempts under a full warranty, the consumer can demand a refund or replacement. By labeling the warranty "limited," manufacturers avoid these stricter obligations.

**19. What are the requirements of the Truth in Lending Act?**
The Truth in Lending Act requires creditors to **disclose finance charges** and applies to all persons or organizations that regularly extend credit or make finance charges in connection with installment purchases.

**20. Identify the practices that violate the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act.**
- Using **violent or criminal acts**
- Using **profane language**
- Publishing **lists of debtors**
- Calling debtors **repeatedly** or in the **middle of the night**
- Threatening **legal action with no intent** to follow through
- Contacting the debtor at **work** or at any unusual time or place, except with the debtor's consent

**21. Under what circumstances might a reporting agency issue a consumer report?**
- In response to a **court order**
- Under **written instructions** of the subject of the report
- To a person who intends to use the information in connection with a **credit transaction**, for **employment purposes**, in connection with **insurance underwriting**, for determination of eligibility for a **business license** if financial status is relevant, or for a **legitimate business need**

**22. Distinguish liquidation from reorganization as bankruptcy remedies.**
- **Liquidation** (Chapter 7): Selling ALL the debtor's assets and distributing proceeds to creditors. Remaining debts are discharged.
- **Reorganization** (Chapter 11): The debtor's affairs are set free of claims of creditors, and partial or full repayment of debts occurs during the reorganization process. The business continues operating.

**23. Who can initiate involuntary bankruptcy proceedings?**
Any creditors who believe that a bankrupt person or organization **favors other creditors** or who continue to **dispute the remaining assets** of a bankrupt estate can petition the federal bankruptcy court for involuntary proceedings.

**24. What are the priorities among creditors in a liquidation proceeding?**
In order of priority:
1. **Administrative expenses** of the bankruptcy proceeding
2. **Unsecured business debts**
3. **Limited amount of wage claims**
4. **Contributions to employee benefit plans**
5. **Claims of unsecured individuals**
6. **Unsecured claims of governmental units**

## APPLICATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Question 1: Herman's Stainless Steel

**The Setup**: Herman, a wholesaler, bought "fully nonmagnetic stainless steel" from a manufacturer who mistakenly shipped steel that was NOT fully nonmagnetic. Herman sold the steel to a buyer as fully nonmagnetic. The buyer found it was the wrong type and offered to return the steel, demanding his money back. Herman claimed it wasn't his fault.

**Is Herman liable to the buyer?**

**Answer**: YES, Herman is liable. Herman is a **merchant**, and the buyer had the right to rely on Herman's representations as to the **fitness** of the steel for the purpose intended. The implied warranty of merchantability and potentially the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose were breached. The buyer can demand his money back from Herman. Herman can, in turn, seek **reimbursement from the manufacturer** who shipped the wrong steel.

---

### Question 2: Wanda's Destroyed Goods

**The Setup**: Wanda purchased certain bulky goods from Selma. The goods were identified and marked with Wanda's name. Wanda was to pick up the goods within a week and make full payment. During the week, a fire broke out at Selma's place (NOT Selma's negligence) and the goods were totally destroyed.

**a. Did title pass to Wanda?**
Probably YES. The goods were identified and marked with Wanda's name, and Selma apparently intended title to pass. The assumption is that Selma could pass good title.

**b. Who bears the risk of loss -- must Wanda pay for the goods?**
**Wanda does NOT have to pay.** The question of who has title does NOT affect the risk of loss in this case. Under the UCC, risk of loss follows title EXCEPT when identified goods are at the seller's place of business and the buyer is to pick them up. Since **Selma appears to be a merchant** (she has a place of business), risk of loss does NOT pass to the buyer until the buyer **receives** the goods. Wanda never received them, so Selma bears the loss.

### Question 3: John Doe's Endorsements

Identify the endorsement type and liabilities for each:

**a. "Pay to the order of Howard Roe /s/ John Doe"**
- **Special endorsement** -- gives Howard Roe all rights and liabilities associated with the check
- John Doe has **secondary liability** (unqualified endorser)

**b. "Without recourse /s/ John Doe"**
- **Qualified endorsement** -- John Doe has **avoided secondary liability**
- But the check remains negotiable (blank endorsement makes it payable to bearer)

**c. "Pay to the order of Mary Bright on delivery of 250 barrels of oil /s/ John Doe"**
- **Special endorsement** with a **condition** -- gives Mary Bright the rights and liabilities associated with this check, conditional upon delivery of 250 barrels of oil
- However, conditions on endorsements generally do NOT affect negotiability

**d. "For deposit /s/ John Doe"**
- **Restrictive endorsement** -- limits the check to **deposit only** by John Doe
- Cannot be cashed; can only be deposited into John Doe's account

### Question 4: Ken's Note and Joyce (HDC)

**The Setup**: Ken signed a note as maker, but he thought he was signing a receipt for goods. Ellen distracted him and her friend substituted a promissory note for the receipt. The note came into Joyce's hands as a good-faith purchaser for value and HDC. Joyce demands payment.

**Answer**: Ken's defense is **fraud in the execution** (also called "fraud in the factum") -- he didn't know he was signing a negotiable instrument. This is a **real defense** that is good even against a holder in due course. HOWEVER, the validity of this defense depends on **factual findings**: Was Ken truly unaware, or was he simply careless in not reading what he signed? If he was negligent in failing to read the document, the real defense fails, and Joyce as HDC takes the note free of personal defenses. Even though Ken was tricked, if he could have discovered the substitution through reasonable care, Joyce prevails.

### Question 5: Manufacturer's Security Interest in TV Sets

**The Setup**: A manufacturer sold TVs on credit to George's TV shop. They signed a security agreement, and a financing statement was properly filed. George sold a TV covered by the agreement to Sidney, a consumer. When George didn't pay, the manufacturer repossessed the TV from Sidney.

**a. Did the manufacturer have a perfected security interest in the set?**
YES. The manufacturer **filed a financing statement**, which created a perfected security interest.

**b. Was the manufacturer entitled to possession of the set sold to Sidney?**
NO. The manufacturer was **not entitled** to repossess from Sidney. Under UCC Article 9, a consumer who buys goods for **personal purposes** from a retailer buys the goods **free of the seller's (manufacturer's) security interest**, even if that interest is perfected. Sidney, as a first buyer for personal use, takes the TV free and clear.

## EXAM TIPS & COMMON TRAPS

### Must-Know Distinctions

1. **FOB Shipment vs. FOB Destination** -- This is the #1 tested shipping concept. Shipment = buyer's risk once on the truck. Destination = seller's risk until arrival.

2. **Merchant vs. Nonmerchant** -- Implied warranty of merchantability ONLY applies to merchants. Nonmerchants selling used goods at a garage sale? No merchantability warranty.

3. **Special vs. Blank Endorsement** -- Special names someone ("Pay to Jane"). Blank is just a signature (payable to bearer = DANGEROUS).

4. **Personal vs. Real Defenses** -- HDC beats personal defenses (breach of contract, fraud in inducement). HDC does NOT beat real defenses (forgery, fraud in execution, minority).

5. **Perfected vs. Unperfected Security Interest** -- Perfected (filed financing statement) wins over unperfected. But consumer buyers take free of security interests.

6. **Liquidation vs. Reorganization** -- Chapter 7 = sell everything. Chapter 11 = restructure and keep going. Chapter 13 = individual repayment plan.

- **UCC Article 2** = Sales of Goods
- **UCC Article 3** = Negotiable Instruments
- **UCC Article 9** = Secured Transactions

## QUICK-REFERENCE SUMMARY

### Sales Contracts (UCC Article 2)
- Governs sale of **goods** (tangible, movable property)
- Nonconforming goods are NOT always a breach (accommodation / cure)
- **Express warranties**: Specific factual claims by seller
- **Implied warranty of merchantability**: Goods fit for ordinary purpose (MERCHANTS ONLY)
- **Implied warranty of fitness**: Buyer relies on seller's expertise for particular purpose
- Warranties extend to buyer's family, household, and guests

### Shipping Terms
- **FOB Shipment**: Risk shifts at seller's location when goods reach carrier
- **FOB Destination**: Risk stays with seller until goods reach buyer
- **CIF**: Seller pays cost + insurance + freight
- **CAF**: Seller pays cost + freight (no insurance)
- **COD**: Buyer pays on delivery, no inspection right

### Risk of Loss
- Under the UCC, risk of loss does NOT simply follow title -- the UCC deliberately separates the two
- **Carrier involved (shipment contract)**: Risk passes when goods delivered to carrier
- **Goods held by bailee**: Risk passes when buyer receives document of title
- **No carrier, seller IS a merchant**: Risk stays with seller until buyer actually receives goods
- **No carrier, seller NOT a merchant**: Risk passes when seller tenders delivery
- Key exam point: Identified goods at seller's merchant location awaiting buyer pickup -- risk stays with merchant seller until buyer receives

### Negotiable Instruments (UCC Article 3)
- Must be: Signed, unconditional promise/order, certain sum in money, payable on demand/definite time, payable to order/bearer
- **HDC**: Takes for value, in good faith, without notice -- free of personal defenses
- Endorsements: Special (names payee) > Blank (bearer) > Restrictive (limits use) > Qualified (no recourse)

### Secured Transactions (UCC Article 9)
- Perfect by filing financing statement or taking possession
- Perfected beats unperfected
- Exceptions: HDC of negotiable instrument, artisan's liens
- Consumer buyers take goods free of seller's security interest

### Consumer Law
- McCarran-Ferguson: States regulate insurance; federal antitrust exemption
- Magnuson-Moss: Full vs. limited warranties
- TILA: Disclose finance charges
- FCRA: Limits on consumer reports
- FDCPA: No harassment, threats, or abusive collection practices

### Bankruptcy Priority (Liquidation)
1. Administrative expenses
2. Unsecured business debts
3. Wage claims (limited)
4. Employee benefit plan contributions
5. Unsecured individual claims
6. Government claims (taxes)
