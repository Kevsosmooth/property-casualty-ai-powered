## 1. Employment-at-Will Doctrine

### What It Is (Plain English)

**Employment at will** means an employer can fire an employee at any time, for any reason (or no reason at all), and the employee can quit at any time too. There is no guaranteed job security unless you have a contract saying otherwise.

> **Think of it this way**: Without a written contract, your job is like a month-to-month apartment lease -- either side can end the relationship whenever they want.

### The Two Major Exceptions

Even though employment-at-will is the default rule, fired employees can fight back through two types of legal claims:

### A. Contract Claims

**Wrongful discharge** = Being fired in violation of an employment contract (express or implied).

- An **express contract** is a written or oral agreement spelling out the terms of employment
- An **implied contract** can arise from company handbooks, policies, or verbal promises

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example:**

> **The Setup:** When Ed started at Plumbing Company 20 years ago, the owner told him, "If you're a good plumber, you can work for us as long as we're in business."
>
> **What Happens:** Ed's boss fires him, saying "business has fallen off." Ed later learns they hired a younger plumber to replace him.
>
> **The Result:** Ed MAY have a contract claim based on the owner's verbal promise. However, enforcing such an oral promise is very difficult in employment-at-will states. Courts rarely enforce employment contracts when business necessity dictates cost-cutting.

**Key point**: Even in at-will states, a clear oral promise of continued employment can potentially create an enforceable contract -- but it is hard to prove and harder to enforce.

### B. Tort Claims

A dismissed employee can make tort claims based on **four causes of action**:

1. **Wrongful discharge** -- Firing that violates public policy (e.g., firing someone for filing a workers' comp claim or refusing to commit a crime)
2. **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** -- The firing was conducted in an extremely outrageous manner
3. **Defamation** -- The employer made false statements about the employee to others (e.g., telling other employers the person was fired for theft when they were not)
4. **Invasion of privacy** -- The employer intruded into the employee's private affairs in an unreasonable way

## 2. Antidiscrimination Laws

### A. Discrimination Based on Age

#### Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA)

- **Who it protects**: Workers age **40 and older**
- **Who it applies to**: Employers with **20+ employees**, labor unions with **25+ members**, employment agencies
- **What it prohibits**: Discrimination in hiring, firing, compensation, or terms/conditions of employment based on age
- **Mandatory retirement**: The ADEA **bans mandatory retirement** at any age for most workers

> **Plain English**: A company cannot force you to retire just because you turned 65 (or any age). There are very narrow exceptions (e.g., certain high-level executives or public safety officers like firefighters/police).

**BFOQ Exception**: Age CAN be a **bona fide occupational qualification (BFOQ)** -- meaning age is genuinely necessary for the job. This is a very narrow exception.

> **Example**: An airline could potentially argue that pilots over a certain age pose safety risks. But a law firm cannot refuse to hire a 55-year-old attorney just because they prefer younger lawyers.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The ADEA has been interpreted more broadly in recent decades. Check for Supreme Court decisions after 2008 that may have changed the burden of proof in age discrimination cases.

#### Older Workers Benefit Protection Act

- Prevents employers from using employee benefit plans to justify refusing to hire older workers
- Restricts employers from reducing benefits for older workers unless cost-justified
- Sets strict rules for **waivers** -- if an employer asks an older worker to waive their ADEA rights (e.g., in a severance package), strict requirements must be met

### B. Discrimination Based on Sex, Race, Color, Religion, or National Origin

#### Civil Rights Acts of 1866 and 1871

- **Section 1981** (from 1866 Act): Prohibits racial discrimination in making and enforcing contracts, including employment contracts
- These are among the OLDEST federal anti-discrimination laws
- Apply to both private and government employers

#### Civil Rights Act of 1964 -- Title VII

This is the **BIG ONE** for employment discrimination. Know this cold for the exam.

- **Who it applies to**: Employers with **15+ employees**, labor unions, employment agencies
- **What it prohibits**: Discrimination based on **sex, race, color, religion, or national origin** in all aspects of employment

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: In **Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)**, the U.S. Supreme Court held that Title VII's prohibition on discrimination "because of sex" includes discrimination based on **sexual orientation and gender identity**. This landmark ruling means LGBTQ+ employees are protected under Title VII. The 2008 textbook does not reflect this.

- **BFOQ defense**: An employer can discriminate ONLY if the characteristic is a **bona fide occupational qualification** reasonably necessary for the business
  - Race is **NEVER** a BFOQ -- there is no situation where race can be a job requirement
  - Sex CAN be a BFOQ in very narrow cases (e.g., hiring a female attendant for a women's locker room)
  - Religion CAN be a BFOQ for religious organizations

**Two Theories of Discrimination Under Title VII:**

| Theory | What Employee Must Prove | Employer's Intent | Example |
|--------|-------------------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Disparate Treatment** | Employer intentionally treated them differently because of protected class | Intent IS the issue -- employer meant to discriminate | "We don't hire women for management positions" |
| **Disparate Impact** | A neutral policy has a disproportionate negative effect on a protected class | Intent does NOT matter -- even unintentional discrimination counts | A height requirement of 5'10" eliminates most women and many minorities |

> **Exam Trap**: Disparate treatment requires PROOF OF INTENT. Disparate impact does NOT require intent -- just proof that a neutral policy disproportionately excludes a protected group.

#### Sexual Harassment (Two Types)

| Type | What It Is | Key Feature |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Quid Pro Quo** | Job benefits or advancement conditioned on submission to sexual advances | "Sleep with me or you're fired" -- direct exchange |
| **Hostile Environment** | Abusive conduct or hostile situation in the workplace based on sex | Pervasive offensive jokes, touching, displays -- no direct "exchange" required |

**Constructive discharge** = When the work environment becomes so intolerable that a reasonable person would feel forced to quit. The employee technically "resigned" but it is treated as a firing because the employer created impossible conditions.

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** Maria's supervisor makes daily sexual comments, displays offensive materials, and assigns her the worst shifts whenever she refuses his advances.
>
> **What Happens:** Maria cannot take it anymore and quits.
>
> **The Result:** This is **constructive discharge** -- even though Maria quit, the law treats it as if she was fired. The employer is liable for hostile environment sexual harassment.

#### Civil Rights Act of 1991

This law strengthened Title VII significantly:

- Allows plaintiffs to recover **compensatory AND punitive damages** (not just back pay)
- Broadens the Civil Rights Act of 1866 to cover **all aspects** of the employment relationship
- Changes **burden of proof** in disparate impact cases
- Makes **jury trials** available for compensatory/punitive damage claims under Title VII or ADA
- Allows prevailing parties to recover **expert witness fees**

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Damage caps under the 1991 Act may have been adjusted. Originally capped at $50,000-$300,000 depending on employer size.

#### Executive Order 11246

- Applies to **federal government contractors**
- Requires **affirmative action plans** -- employers must take proactive steps to recruit, hire, and promote minorities and women
- Not just "don't discriminate" -- must actively work to increase diversity

> **Plain English**: If your company has a contract with the federal government, you must have a written plan showing how you will increase representation of minorities and women. Just not discriminating is not enough.

#### Equal Pay Act

- Requires **equal pay for equal work** regardless of sex
- Jobs must be substantially equal in skill, effort, responsibility, and working conditions
- Exceptions: Pay differences are allowed based on seniority, merit, quantity/quality of production, or any factor other than sex

#### Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986

- Employers must verify the **identity and right to work** of all employees
- Uses **Form I-9** to document verification
- Prohibits discrimination based on national origin or citizenship status (for authorized workers)

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] Immigration enforcement and E-Verify requirements have changed significantly since 2008. Many states now require E-Verify for some or all employers.

#### Pregnancy Discrimination Act (1978)

**Pregnancy Discrimination Act (1978)**: An amendment to Title VII that prohibits discrimination on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions. Employers must treat pregnant employees the same as other employees who are similar in their ability or inability to work. Example: An employer cannot fire or refuse to hire a woman because she is pregnant, and must provide the same leave benefits available for other temporary disabilities.

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** Lisa works as an accountant and tells her employer she is 3 months pregnant.
>
> **What Happens:** Her employer says "We can't have you out on maternity leave during tax season" and terminates her.
>
> **The Result:** This violates the Pregnancy Discrimination Act. The employer must treat Lisa the same as any other temporarily disabled employee. If the company allows medical leave for other conditions (broken leg, surgery recovery), it must allow the same for pregnancy.

### C. Discrimination Based on Disability

#### Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

The ADA covers much more than just physical disabilities:

- **Physical disabilities** (mobility, vision, hearing impairments)
- **Mental illness**
- **Infectious diseases** (including HIV/AIDS)
- **Drug addiction** (recovering addicts are protected; current illegal drug users are not)
- **Alcoholism**

**Key ADA concepts:**
- **Reasonable accommodation** -- Employers must make reasonable modifications to enable disabled workers to perform essential job functions (e.g., modified workstation, flexible schedule, assistive technology)
- **Undue hardship** -- An employer does not have to provide accommodation if it causes significant difficulty or expense
- The ADA applies to employers with **15+ employees**

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** A research company's two-story office building has steps at every entrance and no elevator.
>
> **What Happens:** A qualified wheelchair-using applicant applies for a job on the second floor.
>
> **The Result:** The company likely violates the ADA. They must provide **reasonable accommodation** -- which could mean installing a ramp, relocating the position to the first floor, or installing an elevator if financially feasible.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA) significantly broadened the definition of "disability." The textbook was published before this law took effect. The ADAAA makes it much easier for employees to prove they have a disability under the ADA.

#### Rehabilitation Act of 1973

- Similar to the ADA but applies specifically to **federal government** and **federal contractors**
- Requires affirmative action for qualified individuals with disabilities
- **Section 503**: Requires federal contractors with contracts over $10,000 to take affirmative action to employ and advance qualified individuals with disabilities
- **Section 504**: Prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in any program or activity receiving federal financial assistance

**How it differs from the ADA**: The Rehabilitation Act applies to the federal government and its contractors; the ADA applies to private employers with 15+ employees. Together, they ensure disability protections cover virtually all workplaces.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: A defense contractor with $50 million in government contracts rejects a qualified software engineer who uses a wheelchair.
- **What Happens**: The engineer applies under both the ADA and the Rehabilitation Act.
- **The Result**: Under Section 503, the defense contractor is REQUIRED to take affirmative action to employ qualified disabled individuals -- not just avoid discrimination, but actively recruit and accommodate them. The engineer has claims under both laws.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: The **Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008)** prohibits employers from discriminating based on genetic information, including family medical history. Employers cannot request, require, or purchase genetic information about employees or their family members. This law was enacted the same year as the textbook and is not covered in the original material.

#### Retaliation Claims

**Retaliation Claims**: Federal antidiscrimination laws (Title VII, ADA, ADEA) also prohibit **retaliation** against employees who file discrimination complaints, participate in investigations, or oppose discriminatory practices. Retaliation is now the MOST COMMON charge filed with the EEOC. Example: An employee files a sexual harassment complaint. The employer then demotes the employee "for performance reasons." This could constitute illegal retaliation.

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** James, a warehouse worker, files a formal complaint with HR alleging racial discrimination by his supervisor.
>
> **What Happens:** Two weeks later, James is transferred to the night shift, given the worst assignments, and written up for minor infractions that were previously ignored.
>
> **The Result:** Even if the original discrimination claim is hard to prove, the employer's retaliatory actions after the complaint are independently illegal. James has a strong retaliation claim. Courts look at whether a reasonable employee would be discouraged from filing a complaint by this treatment.

### D. Other Areas Affected by Antidiscrimination Laws

#### Military Service Protection

- **Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA)**: Protects employees who leave for military service -- they must be reemployed in the same or comparable position upon return

**Key USERRA Protections**:
- **Reemployment rights**: Employee must be restored to the position they would have held if they had not left for military service (the "escalator principle" -- they move UP the career ladder as if they never left)
- **Benefits continuation**: Health insurance must be continued for up to 24 months during military service (employee may have to pay up to 102% of the full premium)
- **No discrimination**: Employers cannot deny hiring, promotion, or any benefit based on military service or obligation
- **Protected period**: Covers up to 5 cumulative years of military service with the same employer

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Sarah, a claims adjuster earning $65,000/year, is called up for a 12-month deployment with the National Guard.
- **What Happens**: While she is gone, her employer promotes her colleagues and restructures the department. When Sarah returns, the employer offers her a lower-level position at $55,000 saying "your old role was eliminated."
- **The Result**: Under USERRA, Sarah must be reemployed in the position she WOULD have held had she not left -- including any promotions she would have received. If colleagues with similar qualifications were promoted, Sarah must be given equivalent advancement. The employer cannot use the deployment as an excuse to demote or underpay her.

#### Jury Service Protection

- Federal and state laws prohibit employers from firing or penalizing employees for serving on a jury

#### Wage Garnishment Protection

- **Consumer Credit Protection Act**: Prohibits an employer from **discharging an employee** because of garnishment of wages for **indebtedness**
- Sets limits on the amount of wages that can be garnished
- **Maximum garnishment**: Generally limited to 25% of disposable earnings OR the amount by which weekly earnings exceed 30 times the federal minimum wage, whichever is LESS
- **Exception**: Higher limits apply for child support (up to 50-65% of disposable earnings)
- **Protection**: Employer cannot fire employee for garnishment due to **any one debt** (protection is only for the first garnishment; multiple garnishments from different debts may not be protected)

> **Plain English**: If a court orders that part of your paycheck goes to pay a debt, your employer cannot fire you just because of that garnishment.

**Setup -> What Happens -> Result Example**:
- **The Setup**: Tom, an underwriter at an insurance company, falls behind on credit card payments. A creditor gets a court judgment and garnishes Tom's wages.
- **What Happens**: Tom's supervisor sees the garnishment notice and says, "We can't have employees with financial problems handling insurance policies." Tom is fired.
- **The Result**: Tom's employer violated the Consumer Credit Protection Act. An employer CANNOT discharge an employee because of a single garnishment. Tom can be reinstated and recover lost wages. However, if Tom later gets a SECOND garnishment from a different creditor, the federal protection may not apply (though some state laws provide broader protection).

## 4. Labor-Management Relations

### A. Collective-Bargaining Relationships

**Collective bargaining** = The process by which a union and an employer negotiate the terms and conditions of employment for a group of workers.

**Historical context (know the evolution):**

- **Yellow dog contract** = A now-illegal agreement where an employee promises not to join a union as a condition of employment. Named because workers felt treated "like dogs" for signing them.

**Key Federal Labor Laws:**

- **Norris-LaGuardia Act (1932)**: Limited courts' power to issue injunctions in labor disputes; outlawed yellow dog contracts
- **National Labor Relations Act / Wagner Act (1935)**: Established the right to organize and bargain collectively; created the **National Labor Relations Board (NLRB)**
- **Taft-Hartley Act (1947)**: Balanced power by also restricting union practices; listed unfair labor practices by UNIONS (not just employers)
- **Landrum-Griffin Act (1959)**: Regulated internal union affairs; required financial transparency

### B. Collective-Bargaining Process

**Steps to establish a collective-bargaining relationship:**

1. Union organizers **pass out authorization cards** to employees and request signatures
2. Union asks the employer to **bargain collectively**; if employer refuses, union can file an **unfair labor practices charge**
3. Alternatively, the union files a **representation petition** with the NLRB
4. NLRB holds an **election** -- if majority votes yes, the union is certified
5. Union and employer **negotiate** a collective bargaining agreement

**Mandatory subjects** for collective bargaining (MUST be discussed):
- Wages, hours, and conditions of employment
- Merit wage increases
- Pensions
- Seniority rights
- Grievance procedures
- Management function clauses

**Nonmandatory subjects** (can be discussed but not required):
- Right to subcontract work
- Ballot clause requiring a secret ballot of all employees to consider the employer's last offer before calling a strike

### C. Economic Pressure

**Employee weapons:**
- **Strikes** -- Workers refuse to work (the most powerful tool)
- **Boycotts** -- Workers encourage public not to buy the employer's products
- **Picketing** -- Workers march outside the business with signs

**Special strike types:**
- **Sit-down strike** = Workers occupy the workplace but refuse to work (generally ILLEGAL)
- **Sympathy strike** = Workers strike to support another group of workers, not over their own dispute

**Employer weapons:**
- **Replacement workers** -- Hiring temporary or permanent replacements during a strike
- **Lockouts** -- Employer shuts down operations and prevents workers from working

## 5. Regulation of Employee Safety and Health

### The Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSH Act)

**Purpose**: Ensure safe and healthy working conditions for all employees.

**Relationship to workers' comp**: The OSH Act and workers' compensation system work together -- OSH Act tries to PREVENT injuries; workers' comp provides compensation AFTER injuries occur. The workers' comp system provides an incentive for employers and insurers to invest in workplace safety.

### A. Duties of Employers and Employees

**Employer duties:**
- Comply with all OSHA safety and health standards
- Maintain workplaces free from **recognized hazards** (the **general duty clause**)
- Keep records of employee injuries and illnesses

**Employee duties:**
- Comply with all OSHA safety and health standards, regulations, and orders

> **The General Duty Clause** is critical -- even if there is no specific OSHA standard covering a hazard, employers must still keep the workplace free from recognized hazards that could cause death or serious harm.

### B. Safety and Health Standards

- OSHA sets specific standards for workplace hazards (chemicals, machinery, noise, etc.)
- Standards are set through a formal rulemaking process

### C. Enforcement and Penalties

- Enforced through **workplace inspections and investigations** by OSHA compliance officers
- OSHA can conduct inspections without advance notice
- **Penalties** range from citations and fines to criminal prosecution for willful violations causing death

## 6. Regulation of Wages and Hours

### A. Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

The FLSA establishes three major requirements:

1. **Minimum wage** -- Sets a federal floor for hourly wages
2. **Overtime compensation** -- Requires time-and-a-half (1.5x regular rate) for hours worked over **40 per week**
3. **Equal pay for men and women** -- Reinforces the Equal Pay Act

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The federal minimum wage was $6.55/hour in 2008 (rising to $7.25 in 2009). As of 2026, the federal minimum wage is still $7.25/hour, but many states and cities have minimum wages of $15/hour or higher. Check current rates.

**Key distinction:**

- **Nonexempt employee** = Covered by FLSA minimum wage and overtime rules. These are typically hourly workers. They MUST receive overtime pay.
- **Exempt employee** = NOT covered by overtime rules. These are typically salaried professionals, executives, and administrators who meet specific criteria.

> **Exam Trap**: Just because someone is paid a salary does NOT automatically make them exempt. They must meet specific duties tests AND salary thresholds to qualify as exempt.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The DOL has significantly updated the exempt/nonexempt salary thresholds multiple times since 2008. The salary threshold for exemption has increased substantially.

#### Posting and Recordkeeping

- Employers must display FLSA notices in the workplace
- Must keep detailed records of hours worked, wages paid, etc.

### B. Other Federal Minimum Wage Acts

- **Davis-Bacon Act**: Requires prevailing wages on federal construction projects
- **Walsh-Healey Act**: Sets wage/hour standards for federal supply contracts

### C. Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA)

**Who it covers**: Employers with **50+ employees** within 75 miles

**What it provides**: Up to **12 weeks of unpaid leave** per year for:
- Birth, adoption, or foster care placement of a child
- Care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition
- The employee's own serious health condition

**Key rules:**
- Leave is **unpaid** (but employer can require use of accrued paid leave)
- Employee must be restored to the **same or equivalent position** upon return
- Employer must continue **group health insurance** during the leave

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** Sarah works for a company with 200 employees. She just had a baby.
>
> **What Happens:** Sarah requests 12 weeks off to care for her newborn.
>
> **The Result:** The employer MUST grant the leave under FMLA. Sarah's job (or equivalent) must be waiting when she returns, and her health insurance continues during the leave. The leave is unpaid unless she has accrued PTO.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] FMLA was expanded in later years. Check for updates regarding military caregiver leave (up to 26 weeks) and qualifying exigency leave added by later amendments.

## 7. Regulation of Employee Benefits

### A. Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA)

**Critical point**: ERISA does **NOT** require employers to provide benefits. It only regulates benefits IF the employer chooses to offer them.

Once an employer sets up a benefit plan, ERISA requires:
- **Fiduciary duty** -- Plan managers must act solely in the interest of plan participants
- **Reporting and disclosure** -- Plans must provide regular reports to participants and the government
- **Vesting requirements** -- Employees gain ownership rights to employer contributions over time
- **Funding standards** -- Pension plans must be adequately funded

### B. Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (COBRA)

**What it does**: Allows employees (and their dependents) to **continue group health insurance coverage** after losing their job.

**Key details:**
- Applies to employers with **20+ employees** working on 50% of the employer's working days in the preceding calendar year
- **Qualified beneficiary** = Employee, spouse, or dependent child who was covered under the plan
- **Qualifying event** = Event that causes loss of coverage (termination, death of employee, divorce, etc.)
- Coverage continues for up to **18 months** after termination (36 months for some events)
- The former employee **pays the full premium** (employer contribution + employee contribution) plus up to 2% administrative fee

> **Setup -> What Happens -> Result:**
>
> **The Setup:** Ed is fired from Plumbing Company, which has 25 employees and provides group health insurance.
>
> **What Happens:** Ed loses his job and wants to keep his health coverage.
>
> **The Result:** Under COBRA, Ed can remain on the group health policy for **18 months** after termination -- but he must pay the **full premium** himself. After 18 months, he loses COBRA coverage.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The ACA (Affordable Care Act) of 2010 created health insurance exchanges/marketplaces as an alternative to COBRA. COBRA is still available but employees now have additional options.

## 8. Employee Privacy

### A. Employer Privacy Statutes (Federal Law)

Federal law addresses several specific areas of employee privacy:

#### 1. Drugs and Alcohol
- **Drug-Free Workplace Act**: Requires federal contractors to maintain drug-free workplaces
- Employers can generally require drug testing, but specific rules vary by state

#### 2. Polygraph Tests
- **Employee Polygraph Protection Act (EPPA)**: Generally **prohibits** employers from requiring or using lie detector tests
- Exceptions exist for government employers, pharmaceutical companies, and security firms

#### 3. Searches, Surveillance, and Background Checks
- The **Fourth Amendment** protects against unreasonable government searches but does NOT apply to private employers
- **Fair Credit Reporting Act**: Regulates employer use of background checks and credit reports
- Employers must get employee consent before running background checks

### B. Common-Law Invasion of Privacy

Even without specific statutes, employees can sue for invasion of privacy under common law (tort law) in cases of:
- Unreasonable intrusion into private affairs
- Public disclosure of private facts
- Placing someone in a false light
- Using someone's name or likeness without consent

### C. Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

- Protects the privacy of employee **health information**
- Restricts how employers can use and disclose employee medical records
- Requires safeguards for electronic health information

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] HIPAA regulations have been significantly strengthened since 2008. Additionally, state privacy laws (like California's CCPA/CPRA) now provide additional employee privacy protections. The landscape of employee monitoring, email/internet surveillance, and social media privacy has changed dramatically.

## 9. Key Terms Glossary

**Employment at will** (p. 13.4) -- The legal doctrine that an employer can fire an employee at any time for any reason (or no reason), and the employee can quit at any time. The default rule in most states unless there is a contract or law saying otherwise.

**Wrongful discharge** (p. 13.4) -- Firing an employee in a way that violates either a contract (express or implied) or public policy. The employee can sue for damages.

**Bona fide occupational qualification (BFOQ)** (p. 13.6) -- A characteristic (like sex, age, or religion) that is genuinely necessary to perform a particular job. Allows what would otherwise be illegal discrimination. RACE IS NEVER A BFOQ.

**Disparate treatment theory** (p. 13.10) -- A theory of employment discrimination where the employee must prove the employer INTENTIONALLY treated them differently because of their protected class (sex, race, religion, etc.).

**Disparate impact theory** (p. 13.10) -- A theory of employment discrimination where a neutral employment policy disproportionately excludes members of a protected class. Employer intent does NOT matter -- only the discriminatory EFFECT matters.

**Quid pro quo sexual harassment** (p. 13.11) -- Sexual harassment where job benefits are conditioned on submission to sexual advances. "Do this for me sexually, or lose your job/promotion."

**Hostile environment sexual harassment** (p. 13.11) -- Sexual harassment where abusive conduct creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment, even without direct demands for sexual favors.

**Constructive discharge** (p. 13.11) -- When an employer makes working conditions so intolerable that a reasonable person would feel forced to quit. Legally treated as if the employer fired the employee.

**Affirmative action plan** (p. 13.13) -- A written plan detailing how an employer will proactively recruit, hire, and promote minorities and women. Required for federal contractors under Executive Order 11246.

**Yellow dog contract** (p. 13.17) -- A now-illegal agreement where an employee promises not to join a union as a condition of being hired. Outlawed by the Norris-LaGuardia Act of 1932.

**Collective bargaining** (p. 13.17) -- The process by which representatives of a union and an employer negotiate the terms and conditions of employment (wages, hours, benefits, working conditions) for a group of workers.

**Sit-down strike** (p. 13.19) -- A strike where workers stay at their workplace but refuse to work. Generally considered ILLEGAL because it involves trespass on employer property.

**Sympathy strike** (p. 13.19) -- A strike by workers to support another group of workers' dispute, not over their own employment conditions.

**General duty clause** (p. 13.22) -- The provision in the OSH Act requiring employers to keep the workplace free from recognized hazards that could cause death or serious physical harm, even when no specific OSHA standard addresses the hazard.

**Nonexempt employee** (p. 13.24) -- An employee who IS covered by the FLSA's minimum wage and overtime requirements. They MUST receive overtime pay for hours over 40 per week. Typically hourly workers.

**Exempt employee** (p. 13.24) -- An employee who is NOT covered by the FLSA's overtime requirements. Typically salaried professionals, executives, and administrators who meet specific salary and duties tests.

**Qualified beneficiary** (p. 13.28) -- Under COBRA, a person who was covered under a group health plan on the day before a qualifying event (employee, spouse, or dependent child).

**Qualifying event** (p. 13.28) -- Under COBRA, an event that causes loss of group health coverage, such as termination of employment, death of the covered employee, divorce, or a dependent child aging out of coverage.

## 10. Review Questions & Answers

### Q1: What tort claims can a dismissed employee make?

**Answer:** A dismissed employee can make tort claims based on four causes of action:
1. **Wrongful discharge** -- Firing that violates public policy
2. **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** -- Outrageous conduct by the employer
3. **Defamation** -- False statements made about the employee
4. **Invasion of privacy** -- Unreasonable intrusion into personal affairs

### Q2: Can a company legally set a mandatory retirement age?

**Answer:** No. The ADEA bans mandatory retirement at any age for most workers. There are very narrow exceptions for certain high-level executives and public safety positions (like police and firefighters), but as a general rule, mandatory retirement ages are illegal.

### Q3: Which civil rights acts prohibit discrimination based on sex? On race?

**Answer:**
- **Sex discrimination**: Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Civil Rights Act of 1991
- **Race discrimination**: Section 1981 of the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964

### Q4: Distinguish between the disparate treatment and disparate impact theories of employment discrimination.

**Answer:**
- **Disparate treatment**: The employee must establish that the employer INTENTIONALLY treated individuals differently solely because of their sex, race, color, religion, or national origin. Intent is the key issue.
- **Disparate impact**: The employer's intent is NOT an issue. The employee must show that apparently neutral employment practices applied equally to all individuals excluded a disproportionate number of a protected class under Title VII. It is about the EFFECT, not the motive.

### Q5: Distinguish between quid pro quo and hostile environment types of sexual harassment claims.

**Answer:**
- **Quid pro quo**: Deals with situations where the job or job advancement is conditioned on submission to sexual advances. Direct exchange: "sexual favors for job benefits."
- **Hostile environment**: Occurs when the workplace is permeated with discriminatory intimidation, ridicule, or insult that is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment. It does NOT require refusal of sexual advances -- it can exist based on offensive jokes, slurs, intimidation, or other conduct based on a protected characteristic. No direct exchange -- just a pervasively offensive workplace.

### Q6: Are sex-based insurance rates legal?

**Answer:** Sex-based insurance rates have been subject to legal challenge, but the law does not prohibit them entirely. Courts have generally allowed actuarially justified sex-based differences in insurance pricing, though this remains a contested area.

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The ACA (2010) prohibits sex-based pricing in health insurance. Some states have also banned sex-based pricing in auto and other lines. The EU banned sex-based pricing in insurance in 2012.

### Q7: What are the most important features of the Civil Rights Act of 1991?

**Answer:**
- Amends Title VII and ADA to allow plaintiffs to recover **compensatory and punitive damages** in discrimination suits
- **Broadens** the scope of the Civil Rights Act of 1866 to apply to all aspects of the employment relationship
- Changes the **burden of proof** placed on each litigant in disparate-impact cases brought under Title VII or ADA
- Makes **jury trials** available when plaintiffs seek compensatory or punitive damages under Title VII or ADA
- Allows prevailing parties to recover **expert witness fees**

### Q8: What does Executive Order 11246 require?

**Answer:** Executive Order 11246 requires government contracts to include specific **nondiscrimination provisions**. Federal contractors must develop and implement **affirmative action plans** to proactively recruit, hire, and promote minorities and women.

### Q9: What does the Immigration Reform and Control Act require that employers verify with respect to their employees?

**Answer:** The Act requires that employers verify the **identities** of their employees and their **right to work** (legal authorization to work in the United States).

### Q10: What conditions, other than physical disabilities, does the Americans with Disabilities Act cover?

**Answer:** The ADA covers:
- **Infectious diseases** (including HIV/AIDS)
- **Mental illness**
- **Drug addiction** (recovering addicts; NOT current illegal drug users)
- **Alcoholism**

These are all considered disabilities within the ADA's terms.

### Q11: What law prohibits an employer from discharging an employee because of garnishment of wages for indebtedness?

**Answer:** The **Consumer Credit Protection Act** prohibits an employer from discharging an employee because of garnishment of wages for indebtedness.

### Q12: Outline the process for establishment of the collective-bargaining relationship.

**Answer:** To establish a collective-bargaining relationship, the representatives of both sides must:
- Meet at **reasonable times**
- Confer in **good faith** regarding employment conditions
- If the subject matter is **mandatory**, refusal to bargain shows **bad faith**
- Collective bargaining should result in a labor contract creating **legally enforceable obligations** between the parties

### Q13: What are examples of nonmandatory subjects for collective bargaining?

**Answer:**
- **Right to subcontract work** (outsourcing)
- **Ballot clause** requiring a secret ballot of all employees to consider the employer's last offer before calling a strike

### Q14: How can a group of employees apply economic pressure on an employer to strengthen its position?

**Answer:** Employees can use **strikes, boycotts, and picketing** to apply economic pressure on the employer.

### Q15: How can an employer apply economic pressure to employees?

**Answer:** Employers can apply economic pressure to employees through:
- **Replacement workers** -- Hiring replacement employees during strikes
- **Lockouts** -- Shutting down operations and preventing workers from working

### Q16: How does the OSH Act relate to the workers' compensation system?

**Answer:** The workers' compensation system provides an **incentive** for employers and insurers to engage in workplace safety activities. The OSH Act is a comprehensive law that provides for **safety and health standards** for the workplace and for **employee protection**, thereby reinforcing the workers' comp system. They work together -- OSH Act prevents injuries; workers' comp compensates for injuries.

### Q17: What are employer and employee duties under the OSH Act?

**Answer:**
- **Employers**: Must comply with safety and health standards promulgated under the act, maintain workplaces that are free from recognized hazards, and maintain records of employee injuries and illnesses
- **Employees**: Must comply with occupational safety and health standards and all applicable OSH Act rules, regulations, and orders

### Q18: How is the OSH Act enforced?

**Answer:** The OSH Act is enforced through **workplace inspections and investigations** by OSHA compliance officers. These can be conducted without advance notice to the employer.

### Q19: What are the basic requirements of the Fair Labor Standards Act?

**Answer:** The FLSA establishes employment requirements relating to:
- **Minimum wage**
- **Overtime compensation** (time-and-a-half for hours over 40/week)
- **Equal pay** for men and women
- **Child labor** restrictions

### Q20: Identify the rights of an employee under the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993.

**Answer:** The FMLA provides up to **12 weeks of UNPAID, job-protected leave** per year. The key benefit is **job protection, NOT wage compensation** -- FMLA does NOT require the employer to pay the employee during leave. Qualifying reasons include:
- Birth, adoption, or foster care placement of a child
- Care for the employee's own **serious health condition**
- Care for the serious health condition of the employee's **spouse, child, or parent**
- **Job restoration** to the same or equivalent position upon return
- **Continuation of group health insurance** during leave

> **Exam Trap**: FMLA provides UNPAID leave. The employee may use accrued paid leave (vacation, sick time) concurrently, but the FMLA itself does not provide wage compensation.

### Q21: Does ERISA require employers to provide benefits to employees? Explain.

**Answer:** **No.** ERISA does NOT require employers to provide benefits to employees. An employer's obligations under ERISA arise **only once** the employer provides (establishes) such a plan. ERISA regulates HOW plans are managed, not WHETHER they must exist.

### Q22: What does COBRA enable qualified individuals to elect?

**Answer:** COBRA enables qualified individuals to elect **continuation of group health insurance coverage** for themselves and their dependents after a qualifying event (like job loss or divorce).

### Q23: What aspects of employee privacy are addressed by federal law?

**Answer:** Federal law extends some protection to employees regarding:
- **Drug and alcohol** testing
- **Polygraph testing** (generally prohibited by EPPA)
- **Searches, surveillance, and background checks**
- **Health information privacy** (HIPAA)

## 11. Application Questions & Answers

### Application Question 1: Ed the Plumber

**Scenario:** Ed, a 38-year-old plumber, worked for Plumbing Company for 20 years with no written contract. When he started, the owner said, "If you're a good plumber, you can work for us as long as we're in business." Ed was fired and replaced with a 30-year-old plumber. The state follows employment-at-will doctrine.

**a. Does Ed have a contract claim against Plumbing Company?**

Ed MAY have a contract claim despite the employment-at-will nature of the state law. The owner's promise did not have to be in writing under the statute of frauds because it was possible that the contract could be performed within a year (the business could have ended within a year). However, even if Ed has a contract claim, his chances of success are questionable. Few courts would enforce an employment contract when business necessity dictates cost-cutting. In employment-at-will states, it is difficult to enforce any kind of employment contract except one that is very clear-cut.

**b. Does Ed have any other common-law claims against Plumbing Company?**

There is no evidence of any common-law claim Ed could make other than a contract claim unless Ed can prove the tort of **intentional infliction of emotional distress**. No facts are provided that would support that tort.

**c. Does Ed have grounds for a discrimination lawsuit?**

Ed is a male and has no cause of action for sex discrimination. He is **38 years old** and does NOT fall within the **40-and-over** age requirement for alleged age discrimination under the ADEA. Therefore, Ed does not have grounds for age discrimination.

**d. Will Ed lose group health insurance benefits that the company had been providing for him?**

Under **COBRA**, Ed can remain on the group health policy for **18 months** after termination if he pays the premium. COBRA applies only to companies having **20 or more employees** working on 50% of an employer's working days during the preceding calendar year. If Plumbing Company meets this threshold, Ed qualifies for COBRA continuation coverage.

### Application Question 2: Theatrical Company BFOQs

**Scenario:** A theatrical company is casting for "Goldilocks and the Three Bears," "Little Red Riding Hood," and "The Gingerbread Man." What parts might legally require males or females as BFOQs?

**Answer:** **None** of these acting parts can legally require males or females as BFOQs. Actors of each gender can play each role with costumes and makeup. The BFOQ exception for sex is very narrow and would not apply here since any character can be portrayed by any gender with appropriate costuming.

### Application Question 3: Mark's Insurance Investigation of Research Company

**Scenario:** Mark works for an insurance company and discovers the following conditions at Research Company while prescreening them for commercial liability insurance:

**a. Research Company plans to lay off employees closest to retirement first.**
- This could violate the **Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA)** and the **Older Workers Benefit Protection Act** -- targeting employees closest to retirement is essentially targeting older workers.

**b. The second group to be laid off are those with least seniority, most of whom are female and/or minority group members.**
- **Title VII** could be applicable regarding alleged discrimination against women workers and racial discrimination against minorities (disparate impact theory).

**c. The main office building has steps at each entrance and one two-story building has no elevator.**
- The **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** and **Rehabilitation Act of 1973** would apply if the limited access situation causes problems for people with disabilities.

**d. Research Company requires all employees to work five days a week on varying schedules spanning weekends.**
- **Title VII** would apply to alleged discrimination based on **religion** -- some employees' religious practices require Sabbath observance, and the employer may need to provide reasonable accommodation.

### Application Question 4: Mary and Al at Garment Company

**Scenario:** Mary and Al work for Garment Company (300+ other workers). They are dissatisfied with wages, hours, and benefits.

**a. What must Mary and Al do to establish a collective bargaining unit?**

Mary and Al must take the following steps:
- Pass out **authorization cards** to employees and request signatures
- Ask the employer to **bargain collectively**. If the employer refuses, the union can file an **unfair labor practices charge**
- Alternatively, the union can file a **representation petition** with NLRB, and the NLRB will hold an election to certify the union
- Mary and Al would also conduct a campaign to influence the workers

**b. What subjects must Garment Company discuss with Mary and Al if they negotiate a collective bargaining agreement?**

Mandatory subjects include:
- **Wages, hours, and conditions** of employment
- **Merit wage increases**
- **Pensions**
- **Seniority rights**
- **Grievance procedures**
- **Management function clauses**

**c. If Garment Company is resistant, what actions can the employees take to exert economic pressure?**

The employees can exert economic pressure via **strikes, boycotts, and picketing**.

### Application Question 5: Mary and Al Without a Union

**Scenario:** Mary and Al fail to form a union but still want to improve conditions.

**a. Mary is paid less than Al for substantially equivalent work. Can she challenge this?**

Yes, Mary can challenge the practice under the **Equal Pay Act**. If her work is substantially equivalent to Al's in skill, effort, responsibility, and working conditions, she should receive equal pay regardless of sex.

**b. They believe conditions are unsafe (no fire exits on upper floors, fire hazard from materials storage). What legal actions can they take?**

They can report the conditions to **OSHA**, requesting an inspection. Under the OSH Act, employers must maintain workplaces free from recognized hazards (general duty clause). The lack of fire exits and hazardous materials storage would likely violate OSHA standards.

**c. Employees receive $4.50/hour after $0.75 in mandatory fee deductions. They work 10-12 hours/day with only a half-hour lunch and two 10-minute breaks. What can they do?**

They can call the **Department of Labor** and report possible violations of the **Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)**:
- The company may be paying **below minimum wage** after deductions
- They may be violating **overtime provisions** (hours over 40/week must be paid at 1.5x)
- The mandatory deductions may illegally reduce wages below the minimum wage floor

[2026 UPDATE NEEDED] The federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour (since 2009) would definitely make $4.50/hour a violation. Many states have much higher minimums.

## 12. Exam Tips

### Common Exam Traps

1. **ADEA age threshold**: Protects workers age **40 and older** ONLY. A 38-year-old cannot bring an ADEA claim even if replaced by someone younger.

2. **Race is NEVER a BFOQ**: Sex, religion, and age can be BFOQs in narrow circumstances. Race can NEVER be a BFOQ under any circumstances.

3. **Disparate treatment vs. disparate impact**: Treatment requires INTENT. Impact does NOT require intent -- only discriminatory EFFECT matters.

4. **ERISA does NOT mandate benefits**: It only regulates benefits once an employer chooses to offer them. This is a favorite exam trick.

5. **COBRA duration**: 18 months after termination (not 12, not 24). 36 months for some other qualifying events.

6. **COBRA employer size**: Applies to employers with 20+ employees. Smaller employers are NOT required to offer COBRA.

7. **FMLA employer size**: Applies to employers with 50+ employees. Much larger threshold than COBRA or Title VII.

8. **Title VII employer size**: 15+ employees. Different from ADEA (20+) and FMLA (50+).

9. **Employment-at-will is the DEFAULT**: Unless there is a contract, statute, or public policy exception, employers can fire at will.

10. **Sit-down strikes are ILLEGAL**: Regular strikes are legal; sit-down strikes (occupying the workplace) are generally illegal.

11. **Yellow dog contracts are ILLEGAL**: Outlawed by Norris-LaGuardia Act of 1932.

12. **Polygraph tests generally PROHIBITED**: EPPA bans most employer use of lie detectors, with narrow exceptions.

### Key Numbers to Memorize

- **40** -- Age threshold for ADEA protection
- **15** -- Minimum employees for Title VII and ADA to apply
- **20** -- Minimum employees for ADEA and COBRA to apply
- **25** -- Minimum union members for ADEA to apply to unions
- **50** -- Minimum employees for FMLA to apply
- **12 weeks** -- Maximum unpaid FMLA leave per year
- **18 months** -- Standard COBRA continuation period after termination
- **36 months** -- Extended COBRA period for certain qualifying events
- **40 hours/week** -- Overtime threshold under FLSA

### What to Focus On

- **Discrimination theories** (disparate treatment vs. disparate impact) -- know the difference cold
- **Sexual harassment types** (quid pro quo vs. hostile environment) -- commonly tested
- **COBRA specifics** -- qualifying events, duration, who pays
- **FMLA requirements** -- who qualifies, how long, what conditions
- **OSH Act general duty clause** -- employers must eliminate recognized hazards even without specific OSHA standards
- **Economic pressure tools** -- know what employees and employers can each do

## 13. Quick-Reference Summary

### Employment-at-Will
- Default rule: either party can end employment at any time
- Exceptions: contract claims (express/implied) and tort claims (wrongful discharge, emotional distress, defamation, invasion of privacy)

### Antidiscrimination Laws at a Glance
- **ADEA**: Age 40+, employers with 20+ employees, no mandatory retirement
- **Title VII**: Sex/race/color/religion/national origin, 15+ employees, BFOQ defense (never race)
- **Civil Rights Act 1991**: Added compensatory/punitive damages, jury trials
- **ADA**: Physical/mental disabilities, infectious diseases, drug addiction, alcoholism; reasonable accommodation required
- **Executive Order 11246**: Federal contractors must have affirmative action plans
- **Equal Pay Act**: Equal pay for substantially equal work regardless of sex
- **Immigration Reform Act**: Employers must verify identity and work authorization

### Labor-Management Relations
- Yellow dog contracts = ILLEGAL
- Wagner Act created NLRB and right to organize
- Mandatory bargaining subjects: wages, hours, conditions, pensions, seniority, grievance procedures
- Employee pressure: strikes, boycotts, picketing
- Employer pressure: replacement workers, lockouts

### Workplace Safety (OSH Act)
- General duty clause: keep workplace free from recognized hazards
- Employer must comply with standards, maintain records
- Enforced through OSHA inspections

### Wages and Hours (FLSA)
- Minimum wage, overtime (1.5x over 40 hrs/week), equal pay
- Exempt vs. nonexempt employee distinction is critical

### Employee Benefits
- ERISA: regulates (does NOT mandate) employee benefit plans
- COBRA: 18 months continuation of group health after termination; employer 20+ employees

### Employee Privacy
- Polygraph tests generally prohibited (EPPA)
- Drug testing allowed but regulated
- HIPAA protects health information
- Background checks require consent (FCRA)

### FMLA
- 50+ employees, 12 weeks unpaid leave, job protection, health insurance continues
