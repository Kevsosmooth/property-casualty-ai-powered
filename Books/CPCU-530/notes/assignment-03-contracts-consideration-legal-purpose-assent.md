### PART 1: CONSIDERATION (Third Element of an Enforceable Contract)

Remember: A valid contract requires 4 elements: (1) Agreement, (2) Capacity, (3) **Consideration**, (4) Legal Purpose. This assignment covers elements 3 and 4, plus the concept of genuine assent.

#### What Is Consideration?

**Consideration** is something of value bargained for and exchanged by the parties to a contract. In plain English: each side must give something up or promise something in return for what they get. Without consideration, a promise is just a gift -- and gifts are not enforceable contracts.

> **Setup:** Your neighbor says, "I'll mow your lawn every Saturday."
> **What Happens:** You say, "Great, thanks!" but promise nothing in return.
> **Result:** This is a **gratuitous promise** (a gift). If your neighbor stops mowing, you cannot sue -- there was no consideration from your side.

---

#### A. Types of Consideration

##### 1. Valuable Consideration

**Valuable consideration** is the consideration necessary and sufficient to support a valid contract. It can take many forms:

- **A return promise** -- "I'll pay you $500 if you paint my house" (your promise to pay is consideration for the painter's promise to paint)
- **An act performed** -- Actually doing something (painting the house)
- **A forbearance from acting** -- Agreeing NOT to do something you have a legal right to do

> **Setup:** Maria owes Jim $5,000. Jim says, "If you pay me $4,000 right now, I'll forgive the rest."
> **What Happens:** Maria pays $4,000 immediately.
> **Result:** Jim's promise to forgive $1,000 is supported by Maria's act of paying early. Both sides gave something up -- this is valid consideration.

**Key distinction -- Valuable vs. Good Consideration:**
- **Valuable consideration** = Something with actual legal value (money, services, promises, forbearance). This SUPPORTS a valid contract.
- **Good consideration** = Based on natural love, affection, or moral duty. This is NOT sufficient to support a contract.

> **Setup:** A father promises his daughter $10,000 as a wedding gift "because I love you."
> **What Happens:** The father later changes his mind.
> **Result:** The daughter cannot enforce this promise. Love and affection = "good consideration" only, which is NOT legally sufficient. This is a **gratuitous promise**.

##### 2. Forbearance

**Forbearance** means giving up a legal right as your part of the bargain. If you agree NOT to do something you're legally entitled to do, that counts as consideration.

> **Setup:** An employee discovers their employer has been underpaying them by $2,000. The employer says, "I'll pay you $1,500 right now if you agree not to sue."
> **What Happens:** The employee agrees and accepts $1,500.
> **Result:** The employee's forbearance (giving up the right to sue) is valid consideration supporting the employer's promise to pay $1,500.

##### 3. Present, Past, and Future Consideration

- **Present consideration** -- Given at the time of the contract (e.g., paying cash now)
- **Future consideration** -- A promise to do something later (e.g., "I'll pay you next month")
- **Past consideration** -- Something already done BEFORE the promise was made. **PAST CONSIDERATION IS NOT VALID CONSIDERATION.**

> **Setup:** Your neighbor shovels your driveway during a snowstorm without being asked. Afterward, you say, "Thanks so much! I'll pay you $50 for that."
> **What Happens:** You later refuse to pay.
> **Result:** Your neighbor CANNOT enforce your promise. The shoveling was already done (past consideration) before you promised to pay. There was no bargained-for exchange.

##### 4. Binding Promise

A **binding promise** is one that is legally enforceable because it is supported by consideration. Both sides must be bound -- if only one side is obligated, there is no consideration (this is called an **illusory promise**).

> **Setup:** A company offers you a "contract" that says, "We may buy goods from you if we feel like it."
> **What Happens:** They never order anything.
> **Result:** This is an **illusory promise** -- the company never actually committed to anything. No consideration, no enforceable contract.

##### 5. Promise to Perform an Existing Obligation

A promise to do something you're **already legally obligated to do** is NOT valid consideration. You're not giving up anything new.

> **Setup:** A police officer says to a crime victim, "Pay me $500 and I'll investigate your case."
> **What Happens:** The victim pays.
> **Result:** The officer was already obligated by law to investigate crimes. This promise is not supported by new consideration. The victim could recover the $500.

##### 6. Compromise and Release of Claims

**Accord and satisfaction** -- An agreement to substitute a different performance than what was originally required in a contract, plus the carrying out of that agreement.

A debtor's paying only part of a debt can be binding on a creditor to forgive the entire debt under these circumstances:
- In **bona fide disputes** when each party agrees to surrender a claim
- **Payment before** a debt is due
- **Accord and satisfaction** (substituting a different obligation)
- **Composition of creditors** (multiple creditors agree to accept partial payment)

> **Setup:** You owe a credit card company $10,000. You dispute some charges. You offer $7,000 as full settlement.
> **What Happens:** The company accepts $7,000 and signs a release.
> **Result:** This is a valid accord and satisfaction. Because there was a bona fide dispute, the partial payment counts as consideration for the release of the remaining debt.

---

#### B. Adequacy of Consideration

Courts generally do NOT examine whether the consideration exchanged is "fair" or "adequate." As long as something of legal value is exchanged, courts will not second-guess whether the deal was a good one.

**The exception:** Courts may intervene if the consideration is so inadequate that it suggests **fraud, duress, or unconscionability**.

> **Setup:** A collector sells a rare painting worth $50,000 for $100.
> **What Happens:** The collector later claims the deal was unfair.
> **Result:** Generally, $100 is technically valid consideration. BUT if evidence shows the buyer knew the seller was confused, mentally impaired, or under duress, a court might void the contract.

---

#### C. Exceptions to the Consideration Requirement

There are situations where a promise can be enforced even WITHOUT traditional consideration:

##### 1. Promissory Estoppel

**Promissory estoppel** is a legal principle that permits enforcement of a promise made without consideration in order to preserve justice. Three elements must exist:

1. A party makes a promise **expecting the other party to act or forbear** in reliance on that promise
2. The other party **justifiably relies** on the promise by acting or forbearing
3. **Only enforcement** of the promise will achieve justice

In plain English: If someone makes you a promise, you reasonably rely on it and change your position, and then they break the promise -- a court can enforce it even without formal consideration.

> **Setup:** Darrell, Peter's father-in-law, promises Peter $10,000 to help with a house down payment. Peter and his wife buy the house relying on this promise.
> **What Happens:** Darrell loses money in investments and backs out.
> **Result:** Even though Peter's promise to "be a good husband" is not valid consideration (it's an existing obligation), **promissory estoppel** may apply because: (1) Darrell expected Peter to rely on the promise, (2) Peter did rely by buying the house, and (3) justice requires enforcement.

**Insurance Example of Promissory Estoppel:**

> **Setup:** An insurance agent tells a restaurant owner, "Don't worry, your new policy covers flood damage -- I'll have it added." The owner relies on this and cancels their standalone flood policy.
> **What Happens:** A flood destroys the restaurant. The insurer denies the claim because flood coverage was never actually added to the policy.
> **Result:** The restaurant owner may invoke **promissory estoppel** against the agent/insurer. The agent made a clear promise, the owner reasonably relied on it by canceling other coverage, and injustice can only be avoided by enforcing the promise. Courts have held agents liable in similar situations.

##### 2. Charitable Subscriptions

Promises to donate to charities are often enforced without consideration, especially when the charity has relied on the pledge (e.g., started building a new wing based on the pledge).

##### 3. Exceptions Under the UCC

The Uniform Commercial Code has specific situations where consideration is not required (e.g., firm offers by merchants to keep offers open).

##### 4. State Statutory Exceptions

Some states have enacted laws that make certain promises enforceable without consideration.

---

#### D. Consideration in Insurance Contracts

This is a **critical distinction for the exam**:

**Property-Casualty Insurance:**
- Prepaying the premium is NOT a condition necessary to make the contract valid
- If an insured suffers a loss before paying the premium at the start of a policy period, the insurer CANNOT refuse to pay based on failure of consideration
- However, payment of the entire premium becomes an obligation as soon as coverage begins
- The insurer's promise to pay claims IS the consideration; the insured's premium IS the consideration

**Life Insurance:**
- A life insurance policy or application typically provides that the insurance will NOT take effect until the purchaser pays the first full premium
- Nonpayment can result in **forfeiture of policy rights**
- This is a stricter requirement than P&C insurance

> **Setup:** A business owner gets a commercial property policy effective January 1. The premium is $5,000/year, billed quarterly. On January 15, before paying any premium, a fire destroys the building.
> **What Happens:** The owner files a claim.
> **Result:** The insurer MUST pay the claim. In P&C insurance, prepayment is not required for the contract to be valid. The insurer can then pursue the unpaid premium separately.

> **Setup:** John applies for a life insurance policy but has not yet paid the first premium. He dies in a car accident.
> **What Happens:** His beneficiaries file a claim.
> **Result:** The insurer can likely DENY the claim. Life insurance contracts typically require payment of the first premium before coverage begins.

### PART 2: LEGAL PURPOSE (Fourth Element of an Enforceable Contract)

For a contract to be enforceable, it must have a **legal purpose**. Contracts that involve illegal activities or that violate public policy are generally void or unenforceable.

---

#### A. Types of Illegal Contracts

##### 1. Contracts to Commit Crimes or Torts

Any contract that requires a party to commit a crime or tort is illegal and void.

> **Setup:** A business owner hires someone to commit arson on a competitor's warehouse.
> **What Happens:** The arsonist completes the job but doesn't get paid.
> **Result:** The arsonist cannot sue for payment. The contract is illegal and completely unenforceable. Both parties could face criminal charges.

##### 2. Contracts Harmful to the Public Interest

Contracts that harm the public interest are unenforceable, even if they don't involve outright crimes. Examples include contracts to influence government officials or bribe voters.

##### 3. Usury Contracts

**Usury** is the charging of an illegally high rate of interest on a loan. Each state sets maximum interest rates. Contracts charging above these rates are partially or wholly unenforceable.

> **Setup:** A lender offers a personal loan at 75% annual interest in a state where the maximum is 25%.
> **What Happens:** The borrower defaults and the lender sues.
> **Result:** A court may void the entire contract or reduce the interest to the legal maximum, depending on state law.

##### 4. Wagering Contracts

Gambling contracts are generally illegal and unenforceable, except where specifically authorized by state law (e.g., state lotteries, licensed casinos).

**Important distinction:** Insurance is NOT a wagering contract because the insured must have an **insurable interest** -- they must stand to lose something. A wager creates a NEW risk; insurance covers an EXISTING risk.

##### 5. Contracts With Unlicensed Practitioners

If a profession requires a license (doctors, lawyers, insurance agents), contracts made by unlicensed practitioners may be void.

##### 6. Contracts Violating Sunday Laws

Some states have "blue laws" restricting business on Sundays. Contracts made in violation may be unenforceable, though these laws are becoming increasingly rare. [2026 UPDATE NEEDED: Many states have repealed or significantly modified Sunday closing laws.]

##### 7. Contracts to Transfer Liability for Negligence (Exculpatory Clauses)

**Exculpatory clauses** are contractual provisions that attempt to excuse a party from liability resulting from negligence or an otherwise wrongful act. They are:
- Often, but NOT always, illegal
- More likely to be struck down when a party is at a **bargaining disadvantage**
- Courts interpret them **narrowly** against the party trying to limit their own liability

> **Setup:** A parking garage has a sign saying "Not responsible for any damage to vehicles." A garage attendant negligently crashes your car.
> **What Happens:** You sue the garage.
> **Result:** The court will likely enforce your claim despite the exculpatory clause, because courts interpret these narrowly and the garage had superior bargaining power.

##### 8. Contracts in Restraint of Marriage

Contracts that discourage or prevent marriage are generally unenforceable as against public policy.

##### 9. Contracts in Restraint of Trade

**Noncompetition agreements** are valid when they:
- Contain restrictions **necessary to protect** the parties
- Impose **no undue hardship** on the restricted party

Courts examine the reasonableness of the geographic scope, duration, and activities restricted.

> **Setup:** A software engineer signs a noncompete agreeing not to work for any tech company worldwide for 20 years.
> **What Happens:** The engineer quits and takes a job at another tech company.
> **Result:** The noncompete is likely unenforceable -- worldwide scope and 20-year duration impose undue hardship and are unreasonably broad.

> [2026 UPDATE NEEDED]: Noncompete law has changed dramatically since 2008. The FTC proposed a near-total ban on noncompetes in 2023 (later struck down by federal court in 2024, but the issue remains in flux). California, Minnesota, Oklahoma, and North Dakota effectively ban most noncompetes. Colorado, Illinois, and other states have imposed significant salary thresholds and restrictions. Always check current state law.

##### 10. Unconscionable Bargains

A contract is **unconscionable** when one party has overwhelming bargaining power and the terms are grossly unfair. Courts may refuse to enforce all or part of such contracts.

---

#### B. Illegality in Insurance Contracts

Three examples of possible illegality in insurance contracts:

1. **Coverage of contraband** -- A policy covering illegal drugs or stolen goods
2. **Lack of insurable interest** -- The insured has no insurable interest in the property or life covered (this turns insurance into a wager)
3. **Contracts that allow an insured to profit from wrongful conduct** -- A policy that would pay the insured for losses resulting from their own intentional criminal acts

> **Setup:** A person takes out a life insurance policy on a stranger with no family or business connection.
> **What Happens:** The stranger dies.
> **Result:** The policy is unenforceable because the policyholder lacks an insurable interest. This is essentially a wager on someone's life.

---

#### C. Qualifications to Illegal Contract Rules

Not all illegal contracts are completely void. Some may still be **totally or partially enforceable** under these conditions:

1. **Applicability of protective laws** -- When a law is designed to protect one party (like a consumer), courts may enforce the contract to protect that party while penalizing the other
2. **In pari delicto agreements** -- An illegal transaction in which both parties are equally at fault. Generally, neither party can enforce the contract. BUT if the parties are NOT equally at fault, the less-guilty party may get relief.
3. **Severable contracts** -- A contract that includes 2 or more promises, each of which a court can enforce separately. If one part is illegal, the legal parts may still be enforced.

> **Setup:** A contract contains 10 provisions. Provision #7 is an illegal restraint of trade, but the other 9 are perfectly legal.
> **What Happens:** A party seeks to enforce the legal provisions.
> **Result:** If the contract is **severable**, the court can strike provision #7 and enforce the remaining 9 provisions.

### PART 3: GENUINE ASSENT

**Genuine assent** means the contracting parties' actual assent to form a contract, or their indication of intent to contract by their words and actions. Even if all four elements of a contract exist (agreement, capacity, consideration, legal purpose), the contract may still be voidable if genuine assent is lacking.

Genuine assent may be absent in these situations:
1. **Fraud**
2. **Mistake**
3. **Duress**
4. **Undue influence**
5. **Innocent misrepresentation**

---

#### A. Fraud

**Fraud** is an intentional misrepresentation resulting in harm to a person or organization. It makes a contract **voidable** at the option of the defrauded party.

The 6 elements of fraud (ALL must be present):

##### 1. False Representation
There must be a false statement of fact (not opinion).

##### 2. Of a Material Fact
The misleading statement must involve a **material fact** -- a fact that a party would consider important in deciding on a course of action. Not every false statement counts; it must be significant enough to influence the decision.

> **Setup:** A seller says their used car "runs great" when it needs a new transmission.
> **What Happens:** The buyer relies on this and purchases the car.
> **Result:** The condition of the transmission is a material fact that would influence a reasonable buyer's decision. This element is satisfied.

##### 3. Knowingly Made
The person making the false statement must know it is false or make it with reckless disregard for the truth.

##### 4. With Intent to Deceive
The person must intend to mislead the other party.

##### 5. On Which the Other Party Has Placed Justifiable Reliance
The deceived party must actually rely on the false statement, and that reliance must be reasonable.

##### 6. To His or Her Detriment
The deceived party must suffer actual harm or loss as a result.

##### 7. Remedies for Fraud
- **Rescission** -- The complete unmaking of a contract. The defrauded party can cancel the contract and be restored to their original position.
- **Damages** -- The defrauded party can sue for monetary damages.
- The defrauded party can choose EITHER rescission or damages, but generally not both.

##### 8. Fraud in Insurance Contracts

Two important situations of fraud in insurance:

**Collusion** -- An agreement by two or more people to defraud another person. Example: An insured and a repair shop conspire to inflate a claim.

**Concealment** -- A misrepresentation by silence. This is particularly important in insurance because applicants have a duty to disclose material facts.

Concealment is a defense to an insurance contract under the following circumstances:
- The insured **knew** that the fact concealed was material
- The insured **concealed the fact with the intent to defraud**

> **Setup:** When applying for homeowners insurance, the applicant knows the house has severe foundation damage but says nothing about it on the application.
> **What Happens:** The foundation collapses a month later.
> **Result:** The insurer can void the policy based on concealment if it can prove the applicant knew the foundation issue was material AND intentionally concealed it to get coverage.

---

#### B. Mistake

A **mistake** is a perception that doesn't agree with the facts. Mistakes in contracts can affect enforceability.

##### 1. Unilateral Mistake

A **unilateral mistake** is a perception by ONE party to a contract that doesn't agree with the facts.

**General rule: A unilateral mistake is ordinarily NOT a defense to a contract.**

The exception: If the other party **knew or should have known** about the mistake, the contract may be voidable.

> **Setup:** A contractor accidentally bids $50,000 on a project instead of $500,000 (missed a zero). The client accepts immediately.
> **What Happens:** The contractor realizes the error and wants out.
> **Result:** If the client knew or should have known that $50,000 was an obvious mistake (the bid was 10x lower than all others), the contractor may be able to avoid the contract. Otherwise, the contractor is stuck.

##### 2. Bilateral Mistake

A **bilateral mistake** is when BOTH parties share the same mistaken perception. This is more likely to make a contract voidable.

> **Setup:** Both buyer and seller believe a ring is costume jewelry worth $200. They complete the sale.
> **What Happens:** The ring turns out to be worth $50,000.
> **Result:** Because BOTH parties were mistaken about a material fact, the seller may be able to rescind the contract.

##### 3. Mistakes in Insurance Contracts

**Mistakes of law** -- Mistakes of law generally do NOT allow a party to void a contract. The principle is that everyone is presumed to know the law -- ignorance of the law is not an excuse, even when the law is ambiguous or complex.

**Mistakes of fact** -- Can be grounds for reformation or rescission depending on the circumstances.

**Reformation** -- An equitable remedy by which a court rewrites, or reforms, a contract to reflect the parties' actual intentions. Used when a written contract doesn't accurately reflect what the parties agreed to.

> **Setup:** An insurance policy is supposed to cover a building at 123 Main Street, but due to a clerical error, the policy says 132 Main Street.
> **What Happens:** The building at 123 Main Street is damaged.
> **Result:** A court can use **reformation** to correct the address and enforce coverage at the intended location.

> **Setup:** An insurer sends a quote for $125 premium due to a clerical error (should have been $1,295). The applicant accepts and requests a policy at $125.
> **What Happens:** The insurer discovers the error.
> **Result:** The insurer likely is NOT bound to the $125 premium. The mistake should be obvious from the correspondence, and the offeree (applicant) knew or should have known it was a mistake. An offeree may not profit by taking advantage of an obvious mistake.

---

#### C. Duress

**Duress** is the use of violence, restraint, or threats to compel a party to act contrary to his or her wishes. Duress can permit avoidance of a contract when the wrongdoer deprived the plaintiff of **free will** in entering the agreement.

Types of duress include:
- Physical force or threats of physical harm
- Threats of criminal prosecution
- Threats of economic harm (in some cases)

> **Setup:** A contractor threatens to abandon a half-built house during winter unless the homeowner pays triple the agreed price.
> **What Happens:** The homeowner, fearing pipes will freeze and the structure will be ruined, agrees to pay.
> **Result:** The homeowner signed under economic duress and may be able to void the price increase.

**Insurance Claims Duress Example:**

> **Setup:** After a serious car accident, an insurance adjuster visits the injured claimant in the hospital and says, "Sign this release for $5,000 right now, or we'll deny your entire claim and you'll get nothing."
> **What Happens:** The claimant, in pain and worried about medical bills, signs the release.
> **Result:** The release may be **voidable due to duress**. The adjuster used the claimant's vulnerable position and fear of losing everything to pressure a quick settlement far below the claim's value. Courts scrutinize releases signed under these circumstances.

---

#### D. Undue Influence

**Undue influence** is the improper use of power or trust to deprive a person of free will, substituting another's objectives, resulting in lack of genuine assent to a contract.

Undue influence can permit avoidance of a contract when it results in lack of genuine assent, usually in **confidential relationships** where one party exercises some control and influence over the other.

Common confidential relationships:
- Attorney and client
- Doctor and patient
- Guardian and ward
- Trustee and beneficiary
- Parent and child (especially elderly parent)

> **Setup:** An elderly, confused woman's caregiver convinces her to sign over her house "for safekeeping."
> **What Happens:** The caregiver takes ownership and refuses to return it.
> **Result:** The transfer can be voided due to undue influence. The caregiver held a position of trust and exploited the confidential relationship.

**Insurance Agent Undue Influence Example:**

> **Setup:** An insurance agent visits a recently widowed 82-year-old woman weekly, bringing groceries and helping with errands. After gaining her complete trust, the agent convinces her to cash out her late husband's $500,000 whole life insurance policy and purchase a new annuity that pays the agent a massive commission but has a 15-year surrender charge.
> **What Happens:** The woman's children discover the transaction and realize the annuity is completely unsuitable -- she may not live to see the surrender charges expire, and she lost the death benefit.
> **Result:** The transaction may be voided due to **undue influence**. The agent established a confidential relationship, exploited the widow's trust and vulnerability, and substituted his own financial interest for her well-being. State insurance regulators also have suitability rules that this likely violates.

---

#### E. Innocent Misrepresentation

**Innocent misrepresentation** occurs when a false statement of material fact is made WITHOUT knowledge of its falsity and WITHOUT intent to deceive. The key difference from fraud: there is no intentional deception.

If an innocent misrepresentation relates to a **material fact** and results in a lack of genuine assent, the victim can ask a court to **rescind** the contract.

**Important limitation:** With innocent misrepresentation, the remedy is typically **rescission only** -- not damages. With fraud, both rescission AND damages may be available.

---

#### F. Important Insurance Distinction -- Representations vs. Warranties

A **representation** is a statement made by the insurance applicant that is substantially true. Minor inaccuracies do not void the policy -- the insurer must show the misrepresentation was **material** (would have changed the insurer's decision) to avoid the contract.

A **warranty** is a statement in the insurance contract that must be **exactly true**. Any breach, even minor, can technically void the policy.

**Why This Matters**: Most states now treat insurance policy statements as **representations** rather than warranties, to protect policyholders. This means the insurer must prove a false statement was material before it can deny a claim.

**Example**:
- **The Setup**: An applicant for homeowners insurance states on the application that the home has a burglar alarm. In reality, the alarm was installed but has not worked for 6 months.
- **What Happens**: The home is damaged by a fire (unrelated to the alarm).
- **The Result**: If treated as a **warranty**, the insurer could void the policy for any breach (even though the alarm had nothing to do with the fire). If treated as a **representation**, the insurer would need to show the non-working alarm was material to the risk -- and since the loss was a fire, not a burglary, the insurer likely cannot deny the claim.

> **Exam Trap**: The representations vs. warranties distinction is heavily tested. Remember: representations = substantially true standard (modern majority rule). Warranties = strictly true standard (older, harsher rule).

> **Setup:** A homeowner genuinely believes their house was built in 1990 and tells the buyer this. Records later show it was built in 1975.
> **What Happens:** The buyer discovers the true age and wants out.
> **Result:** If the construction date was a material factor in the decision, the buyer may be able to rescind the contract. However, the buyer likely CANNOT collect damages because the seller had no intent to deceive.

## Review Questions & Answers

### Q1: What types of consideration can make a promise enforceable? (p. 3.3)
**Answer:** The consideration necessary to make a promise enforceable can be one of the following:
- A return promise
- An act performed
- A forbearance from acting

### Q2: In what situations can a debtor's paying only part of a debt be binding on the creditor to forgive the entire debt? (pp. 3.7-3.8)
**Answer:** A debtor's paying only part of a debt can be binding on a creditor under the following circumstances:
- In **bona fide disputes** when each party agrees to surrender a claim
- **Payment before** a debt is due
- **Accord and satisfaction**
- **Composition of creditors**

### Q3: Identify exceptions to the consideration requirement for contracts. (p. 3.8)
**Answer:** Exceptions to the consideration requirement include:
- **Promissory estoppel**
- **Charitable subscriptions**
- **Specific exceptions under the UCC**
- **State statutory exceptions**

### Q4: Distinguish the kind of valuable consideration that will support a property-casualty insurance contract from that which will support a life insurance policy. (pp. 3.10-3.11)
**Answer:**
- **Property-casualty insurance:** Prepaying the premium is NOT a condition necessary to make the contract valid. If an insured suffers a loss before paying the premium at the outset of a policy period, an insurer cannot refuse to pay damages based on failure of consideration. However, payment of the entire premium becomes an obligation as soon as coverage begins.
- **Life insurance:** A life insurance policy or application provides that the insurance will NOT take effect until the purchaser pays the first full premium. Nonpayment can result in forfeiture of policy rights.

### Q5: List types of illegal contracts. (pp. 3.11-3.16)
**Answer:** Types of illegal contracts:
- Contracts to commit crimes or torts
- Contracts harmful to the public interest
- Usury contracts
- Wagering contracts
- Contracts by unlicensed practitioners
- Contracts violating Sunday laws
- Contracts attempting to transfer one's negligence liability
- Contracts restraining marriage
- Contracts restraining trade
- Unconscionable bargains

### Q6: Are exculpatory clauses always illegal? Explain. (p. 3.14)
**Answer:** Exculpatory clauses are often, but NOT always, illegal, particularly when a party is at a bargaining disadvantage. Courts interpret them narrowly against the parties attempting to limit their own liability.

### Q7: Under what circumstances are noncompetition agreements valid? (p. 3.15)
**Answer:** Noncompetition agreements are valid when they contain restrictions that are **necessary to protect the parties** and if they impose **no undue hardship** on the restricted party.

### Q8: Give three examples of possible illegality in insurance contracts. (pp. 3.16-3.17)
**Answer:**
1. The contract covers **contraband** (illegal goods)
2. The insured has **no insurable interest** in the property or life covered
3. The insured is **allowed to profit** from his or her wrongful conduct

### Q9: List the conditions under which an illegal contract might still be totally or partially enforceable. (pp. 3.17-3.18)
**Answer:**
- **Applicability of protective laws** (laws designed to protect one party)
- **In pari delicto** agreements (if parties are NOT equally at fault)
- **Severable contracts** (illegal portions can be separated from legal portions)

### Q10: List the situations in which genuine assent to contract may be absent. (pp. 3.18-3.19)
**Answer:** Genuine assent may be absent in the following situations:
- **Fraud**
- **Mistake**
- **Duress**
- **Undue influence**
- **Innocent misrepresentation**

### Q11: Explain the material fact element of fraud. (p. 3.20)
**Answer:** The material fact element of fraud requires that the misleading statement involve a **material fact** -- that is, a fact that a party would consider important in deciding on a course of action.

### Q12: Describe two situations of fraud in insurance contracts. (pp. 3.22-3.23)
**Answer:**
1. **Collusion** -- An agreement by two or more people to defraud another person
2. **Concealment** -- A misrepresentation by silence (not disclosing material information)

### Q13: Under what circumstances is concealment a defense to an insurance contract? (p. 3.23)
**Answer:** Concealment is a defense to an insurance contract under the following circumstances:
- The insured **knew that the fact concealed was material**
- The insured **concealed the fact with the intent to defraud**

### Q14: Is a unilateral mistake ordinarily a defense to a contract? (pp. 3.26-3.27)
**Answer:** **No**, a unilateral mistake is ordinarily NOT a defense to a contract. Exception: If the non-mistaken party knew or should have known about the mistake, the contract may be voidable.

### Q15: What is reformation? (p. 3.28)
**Answer:** Reformation is an **equitable remedy** by which a court rewrites, or reforms, a contract to reflect the parties' actual intentions.

### Q16: What effect do mistakes of law have on the binding nature of a contract? (p. 3.28)
**Answer:** Mistakes of law, whether unilateral or bilateral, do **NOT** affect the binding nature of a contract, particularly when the law is not clear.

### Q17: Under what circumstances may duress permit the avoidance of a contract? (pp. 3.28-3.29)
**Answer:** Duress can permit avoidance of a contract when the wrongdoer **deprived the plaintiff of free will** in entering the agreement.

### Q18: Under what circumstances may undue influence permit the avoidance of a contract? (pp. 3.29-3.30)
**Answer:** Undue influence can permit avoidance of a contract when it results in **lack of genuine assent** to a contract, usually in **confidential relationships** in which one party exercises some control and influence over the other.

### Q19: What is the effect of an innocent misrepresentation relating to a contract? (p. 3.30)
**Answer:** If an innocent misrepresentation relates to a material fact and results in a lack of genuine assent, the victim can ask a court to **rescind** the contract. (Note: typically rescission only -- not damages.)

## Application Questions & Answers

### Application Question 1: Darrell's Promise to Peter

**Facts:** Peter and his wife wanted to buy a house. They had the 5% down payment but hesitated because of high monthly payments. Darrell, Peter's father-in-law, said, "Son, if you promise to be a good husband to my daughter, I'll give you $10,000 so your payments can come down to a reasonable amount." Peter agreed enthusiastically. Two days later, Peter and his wife entered a binding agreement to purchase the house, put down 5%, agreed to pay an additional $10,000 within thirty days, and planned to get a mortgage for the balance. One week later, Darrell lost money in investments and backed out of his promise.

**Q1a: Was there consideration for Darrell's promise? Fully explain what is meant by consideration.**

**Answer:** Peter's response that he would be a good husband to Darrell's daughter is a promise to perform an **existing obligation** -- the obligation of Peter's marriage. This promise would be **insufficient consideration** to support a contract. Being a good spouse is already a duty within the marriage, so Peter is not giving up anything new.

**Q1b: Assuming there is no consideration, is there any other legal doctrine that might make Darrell's promise binding?**

**Answer:** Yes -- **promissory estoppel**. Darrell's promise is, in effect, a promise to make a gift (a gratuitous promise). Gratuitous promises are generally unenforceable. BUT under the doctrine of promissory estoppel, a contract can be enforceable without consideration if three elements exist:
1. A party makes a promise expecting the other party to act or forbear in reliance on that promise -- **Darrell expected Peter and his wife to purchase the house in reliance on receiving $10,000**
2. The other party justifiably relies on the promise by acting or forbearing from acting -- **Peter and his wife acted on Darrell's promise when they contracted to buy the house**
3. Only enforcement of the promise will achieve justice -- **this is probably the case in this situation**

### Application Question 2: Ted's Eroding Bluff Property

**Facts:** Ted, an experienced builder, owned a house on a bluff above Lake Michigan. Erosion from wind-swept waves was causing the bluff to collapse. Ted and neighbors had unsuccessfully sought government help. Frank, through a broker, inquired about buying the property. Frank asked Ted whether the house was too close to the bluff edge and whether the bluff was eroding. Ted replied he had lived there many years and the place was perfectly safe. Frank bought the property. The following year, further erosion caused the bluff to collapse completely, taking the house with it. Frank sues Ted.

**Answer:** The legal issues raised include:
- **Did genuine assent exist in this agreement?** -- Questionable, given Ted's misrepresentations
- **Was there fraud?** -- Ted, an experienced builder, likely knew the bluff was eroding and the property was at risk. His statement that the place was "perfectly safe" appears to be a false representation of a material fact, knowingly made, with intent to deceive, relied upon by Frank, to Frank's detriment
- **Is Ted liable to Frank?** -- Yes, likely liable for fraud
- **Is the broker liable to Frank?** -- Possibly, depending on what the broker knew
- **What damages may Frank seek?** -- Rescission or compensatory damages

### Application Question 3: Jill's Age Misrepresentation

**Facts:** Jill, age seventy, purchased a car on credit and applied for auto insurance and credit life insurance. In the applications for both policies, Jill listed her age as sixty. While driving the car, Jill suffered a heart attack and died, causing an accident.

**Answer:**
- **Auto insurance:** Jill's misrepresentation of her age may NOT be material to the auto policy. It is difficult to predict whether a seventy-year-old has a higher risk of auto accidents than a sixty-year-old. (Studies probably vary on this issue.)
- **Life/credit insurance:** Age IS material in life insurance with regard to mortality rates. Jill's misrepresentation may permit the insurer to **avoid the policy**.

### Application Question 4: Stuart's $125 Premium

**Facts:** Stuart has an insurance policy at $1,295/year. While shopping for cheaper coverage, he found three companies at similar prices but a fourth company quoted $125. Stuart immediately sent a letter asking the fourth company to write his policy on those terms.

**Answer:** This case involves a **mistake** by the insurer on its correspondence, and the mistake should be obvious to Stuart. When an offeree knows or has reason to know that an offer is a mistake, the offeree may **not profit by taking advantage** of the offer. The company should not be bound to the $125 premium.

### Application Question 5: Jean's Life Insurance Application

**Facts:** Jean, applying for life insurance, faces three questions she'd rather not answer. (1) Medical history -- she answers untruthfully. (2) Physician visits -- she tells the agent truthfully she visits every six months, and the agent says "keep quiet about those visits." (3) Drug use -- she admits occasional marijuana use, and the agent says "They're not interested in that."

**Answer:**
- **a. Medical history:** Jean's misrepresentation is **material** in a life insurance policy application and may be grounds for **avoidance** by the insurer.
- **b. Physician visits:** Both the agent AND Jean have made a misrepresentation. The agent's advice to conceal doctor visits is material in a life insurance contract and may be grounds for avoidance. (Note: the insurer may be bound by the agent's actions under agency law -- this creates a complex situation.)
- **c. Marijuana use:** The application asks about drug use, and even casual use of a drug may be material on a life insurance application. This misrepresentation could also be grounds for avoidance if it proves to increase the risk on the policy.

## Exam Tips

### Critical Distinctions to Memorize

1. **Valuable consideration vs. Good consideration** -- Only VALUABLE consideration supports a contract. Love, affection, and moral duty ("good consideration") do NOT.

2. **Past consideration is NOT consideration** -- Something already done before a promise is made cannot be the consideration for that promise.

3. **P&C vs. Life Insurance consideration** -- P&C does NOT require premium prepayment for coverage to be valid; Life insurance DOES require first premium payment.

4. **Unilateral mistake vs. Bilateral mistake** -- Unilateral generally is NOT a defense; bilateral IS more likely to be a defense.

5. **Fraud vs. Innocent misrepresentation** -- Fraud allows rescission OR damages; innocent misrepresentation allows rescission ONLY.

6. **Concealment requires TWO things** -- (1) Knowledge that the fact was material AND (2) intent to defraud. If the insured didn't KNOW the fact was material, concealment does not apply.

### Common Exam Traps

- **Trap 1: "Good consideration supports a contract"** -- FALSE. Only VALUABLE consideration does. "Good" consideration (love, affection) is legally insufficient.

- **Trap 2: "A unilateral mistake always voids a contract"** -- FALSE. Unilateral mistakes are generally NOT a defense. The exception is when the other party knew or should have known about the mistake.

- **Trap 3: "Exculpatory clauses are always illegal"** -- FALSE. They are OFTEN but NOT ALWAYS illegal. Courts interpret them narrowly.

- **Trap 4: "All illegal contracts are completely void"** -- FALSE. Some illegal contracts may be partially enforceable through severable contracts, protective laws, or when parties are not equally at fault.

- **Trap 5: "Innocent misrepresentation allows for damages"** -- FALSE. Innocent misrepresentation typically allows for RESCISSION only, not damages.

- **Trap 6: "Any mistake in an insurance policy can void it"** -- FALSE. Mistakes of LAW do not affect the binding nature of a contract. Only certain mistakes of FACT may lead to reformation or rescission.

- **Trap 7: Confusing duress and undue influence** -- Duress involves violence, restraint, or threats. Undue influence involves abuse of a confidential/trust relationship. Different mechanisms, similar result (lack of genuine assent).

### High-Probability Test Topics

- The 6 elements of fraud (expect a scenario question testing whether all elements are met)
- Promissory estoppel (3 elements)
- P&C vs. life insurance consideration rules
- Types of illegal contracts (especially exculpatory clauses and noncompete agreements)
- Concealment as a defense in insurance
- Reformation vs. rescission
- When a unilateral mistake IS a defense (exception to the general rule)

1. False representation
2. Of a material fact
3. Knowingly made
4. With intent to deceive
5. Justifiable reliance by the other party
6. To his or her detriment

- **Collusion** = 2+ people conspiring to defraud
- **Concealment** = misrepresentation by silence (requires: knew fact was material + intent to defraud)

### Mistake Rules
- **Unilateral** = generally NOT a defense (unless other party knew/should have known)
- **Bilateral** = likely a defense (both parties share same wrong perception)
- **Mistakes of law** = do NOT affect binding nature
- **Reformation** = court rewrites contract to match actual intent

### Remedies Comparison
- **Fraud:** Rescission OR damages
- **Innocent misrepresentation:** Rescission ONLY
- **Bilateral mistake:** Rescission or reformation
- **Duress/Undue influence:** Avoidance of contract
