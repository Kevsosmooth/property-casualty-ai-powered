PAP Part A: Liability Coverage

Protection When YOU Cause an Accident

Overview Part A: Liability Part B: Medical Part C: UM/UIM Part D: Physical Parts E & F

What Does Liability Coverage Pay For?

Liability = When YOU Are At Fault

This coverage protects you when your car is involved in an accident and YOU are legally responsible for hurting someone or damaging their property.

The Policy Will Pay For:

1. Bodily Injury & Property Damage

Pays for injuries to others and damage to their property due to an accident for which you are legally responsible.

Example: You run a red light and hit another car. You pay for their medical bills and car repairs.

2. Defense Costs

Pays for your legal defense as long as the policy covers the particular loss.

Important: Defense costs are paid IN ADDITION to the policy limits!

3. Settlement Costs

Costs for settling or defending you as the insurer deems appropriate.

These costs END when the limits of insurance are exhausted.

Who Is an Insured Under Part A?

Key Concept:

Any person qualifies as an insured while using a covered auto, as defined in Part A - Liability Coverage. Click each category to see detailed examples!

For the ownership, maintenance, or use of any auto or trailer.

What does this mean?

You and your family members are covered while driving ANY car - yours, a rental, a friend's car, any car at all.

Example:

Your teenage son (who lives with you) borrows a friend's car and crashes. Your PAP covers him because he's a "family member" driving "any auto."

Anyone you give permission to drive YOUR car is covered under YOUR policy.

Example - COVERED:

The Setup: Your friend asks to borrow your car to run to the store. You say "sure, go ahead."

What Happens: Your friend crashes your car and injures someone.

Who Pays? YOUR PAP covers your friend because they had your permission to use your covered auto.

Example - NOT COVERED:

The Setup: Your neighbor's kid takes your car keys without asking and goes for a joyride.

What Happens: They crash your car.

Who Pays? YOUR PAP does NOT cover them - they didn't have permission!

This covers someone who gets sued because of what YOU did while driving YOUR car.

What does this mean in plain English?

If someone gets dragged into a lawsuit because of YOUR accident in YOUR car, your policy helps protect them too.

Example - COVERED:

The Setup: You have a PAP on your Honda Civic. You work for ABC Company.

What Happens: While driving YOUR Honda Civic to deliver documents for work, you crash and injure someone. The injured person sues both you AND ABC Company, claiming "He was on company business!"

Who Pays? YOUR PAP covers ABC Company. Why? They're being sued because of YOUR actions in YOUR covered auto.

Key Point:

This only applies when you're driving YOUR OWN covered auto. For borrowed cars, see #4 below.

This is similar to #3, but for when you're driving someone else's car (not your covered auto).

Let's break this down step by step:

This covers a person or organization who gets sued because of what YOU did - but in a car that ISN'T yours.

The catch: The person/organization being covered can't OWN or HIRE that other auto.

Example - COVERED:

The Setup: You have your own PAP (your own car insurance policy on your own car at home). Today, you borrow your neighbor's car to run a work errand for your employer.

What Happens: You crash your neighbor's car and injure someone. The injured person sues your employer, saying "He was on a work errand, so the company is responsible!"

Who Pays? YOUR PAP (your personal policy) will help cover your employer's legal defense. Why? Because your employer is getting sued for YOUR actions, and your employer doesn't own or hire your neighbor's car.

Key Point: Your neighbor's car has nothing to do with your employer - they just got dragged into a lawsuit because of YOU. So YOUR insurance helps protect them.

Example - NOT COVERED:

The Setup: Same situation - you have your own PAP. But this time, you're driving a company car (a vehicle your employer owns or leases).

What Happens: You crash the company car. Someone sues your employer.

Who Pays? YOUR PAP will NOT cover your employer. Why? Because your employer OWNS or HIRED that vehicle - they should have their own Business Auto Policy covering it.

Key Point: Your personal insurance isn't meant to cover vehicles that belong to businesses. If a company owns/hires a vehicle, THEY need to insure it.

Why does this rule exist?

Your personal policy extends protection to innocent third parties who get sued because of YOUR actions. But if that third party owns or controls the vehicle, they're not "innocent" - they should have insured that vehicle themselves!

Remember:

This coverage only kicks in if YOU have a PAP. It's YOUR policy extending protection to others who get sued because of YOUR driving in someone else's car.

Liability Exclusions - What's NOT Covered

Know These for the Exam!

The liability coverage will NOT pay for these situations. Click each one to see examples!

Damage or injury that you cause on purpose is never covered.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You get in an argument with someone in a parking lot and deliberately ram your car into theirs. Your PAP will NOT cover the damage because you did it intentionally.

Compare - COVERED:

You accidentally back into someone's car in a parking lot. This IS covered because it was an accident, not intentional.

Liability coverage doesn't pay for damage to property YOU own or property YOU'RE carrying in your car.

Why? Liability = damage to OTHER people's stuff. Your own stuff needs property coverage, not liability.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You're transporting your new $2,000 laptop in your car. You get in an accident and the laptop is destroyed. Your liability coverage will NOT pay for your laptop - it's YOUR property being transported.

Another Example - NOT COVERED:

You crash into your own fence at home. Liability won't cover it because YOU own the fence.

Compare - COVERED:

You crash into your NEIGHBOR'S fence. That IS covered by liability because it's someone else's property.

Property you're renting, using, or have in your care/custody is excluded - EXCEPT a home or private garage.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You borrow your friend's trailer to haul stuff. While driving, you damage the trailer. Your liability won't cover it because the trailer was "in your care."

Exception - COVERED:

You crash your car into your rented apartment building's wall. This IS covered because there's an exception for residence premises and private garages.

Injuries to your employee during the course of employment aren't covered - that's what Workers Compensation is for!

Example - NOT COVERED:

You own a small business. Your employee is riding with you to a job site when you crash. Your PAP won't cover their injuries because they're your employee during work hours - Workers Comp applies instead.

Exception - Domestic Employees:

If you have a nanny or housekeeper and your state doesn't require Workers Comp for domestic employees, your PAP WOULD cover their injuries.

Using your car as a taxi or to transport passengers for a fee is excluded.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You start an informal taxi service, charging neighbors $10 for rides to the airport. If you crash, your PAP won't cover it because you're operating a livery service.

Exceptions - COVERED:

Carpools: Sharing gas money with coworkers for a regular carpool IS covered.
Charity: Driving elderly neighbors to church (volunteer) IS covered.

When you're logged into the Uber/Lyft app as a driver, you're NOT covered - even if you don't have a passenger yet!

Example - NOT COVERED:

You're logged into the Uber app waiting for a ride request. While waiting, you get in an accident. Your personal PAP won't cover it - you need rideshare insurance!

Key Point:

It doesn't matter if you have passengers or not. The moment you're "available for hire" on the app, your personal PAP is OFF.

If you work in the auto business (selling, repairing, servicing, storing, or parking vehicles), your PAP won't cover you while working on CUSTOMER vehicles.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You work at a car dealership. While test-driving a customer's trade-in, you crash it. Your personal PAP won't cover this - the dealership needs Garage Coverage.

Exception - YOUR Own Car:

If you drive YOUR personal covered auto to work at the dealership and crash it, that IS still covered by your PAP.

Using commercial-type vehicles (big trucks, buses) for business purposes requires commercial insurance, not a PAP.

Example - NOT COVERED:

You own a food truck. While driving it to an event, you crash. Your personal PAP won't cover the food truck - you need a Business Auto Policy.

If someone takes your car without your permission, they're NOT covered under your policy.

Example - NOT COVERED:

Your neighbor's teenager "borrows" your car without asking and crashes it. The teenager is NOT covered by your PAP because they didn't have permission.

Compare - With Permission:

You let your neighbor borrow your car for errands. They crash. They ARE covered because they had your permission.

Anyone covered under a nuclear energy liability policy is excluded from PAP coverage.

Why This Exists:

Nuclear facilities have their own special liability insurance. If a nuclear plant worker causes an accident, the nuclear policy covers it - not their personal auto policy. This prevents duplicate coverage.

Limits of Liability - Split vs Combined

Split Limit Coverage

Uses 3 dollar amounts, always expressed in this order:

1st Number: BI Per Person

Max for bodily injury for EACH person

2nd Number: BI Per Accident

Max for ALL bodily injuries in one accident

3rd Number: PD Per Accident

Max for property damage per accident

Example: 25/50/25

  • $25,000 BI per person
  • $50,000 BI per accident (all people)
  • $25,000 property damage per accident

Combined Single Limit (CSL)

Uses 1 dollar amount for everything:

One Number Covers It All

Maximum coverage for ALL losses - regardless of number of people or amount of property damage

Example: $100,000 CSL

$100,000 total available for all BI and PD combined in one accident

Financial Responsibility

When the policy is used to satisfy any financial responsibility requirements, the policy will comply with the law to the extent required.

Out-of-State Coverage

What Happens If You Have an Accident in Another State?

If your covered auto is involved in an accident in any state or Canadian province OTHER than where your car is principally garaged:

If the Other State Has Higher Limits:

Your policy will provide the higher limits required by that state's financial responsibility law.

If the State Requires Certain Coverage for Non-Residents:

Your policy will provide at least the required minimum amounts and types of coverage.

No Duplicate Payments

No one is entitled to duplicate payments for the same loss.

Key Point:

Your limits don't change when you travel state to state. BUT if you have an accident, the limits adjust UP to meet the minimum limits of the state where the accident occurred (if those are higher).

Other Insurance Clause

If Other Insurance Exists:

If there is other insurance available to pay for a covered loss, the insurer will only pay its share of the loss.

The insurer's share = the proportion that its limit bears to the total of all available limits.

For Nonowned Vehicles:

The insurance will be considered EXCESS over any other collectible insurance.

Example: You borrow a friend's car. Their insurance pays first; your insurance only kicks in if their limits are exhausted.

Exam Trap Alerts

!

Split limits order: Always BI per person / BI per accident / PD. Never changes!

!

$250 bail bonds, $200/day lost earnings: Memorize these supplementary payment limits.

!

Defense costs: Paid IN ADDITION to policy limits (they don't reduce your coverage).

!

Rideshare exclusion: Uber/Lyft drivers are NOT covered while logged in - even without passengers!

!

Nonowned vehicle: Your insurance is EXCESS (pays after the car owner's insurance).

!

Carpool exception: Using car for carpool is NOT excluded (only commercial livery is excluded).
